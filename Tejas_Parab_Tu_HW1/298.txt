colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a href="#Religions">Indigenous</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>Ta\xc3\xadno</b> people were among the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous people</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>. At the time of European contact in the late 15th century, they were the principal inhabitants of most of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> (<a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, the northern <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>, they were known as the <a href="/wiki/Lucayans" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucayans">Lucayans</a>. They spoke the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_language" title="Ta\xc3\xadno language">Ta\xc3\xadno language</a> (one of the <a href="/wiki/Arawakan_languages" title="Arawakan languages">Arawakan languages</a>).</p>\n<p>The ancestors of the Ta\xc3\xadno entered the Caribbean from South America and their culture is closely linked to that of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamericans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> At the time of contact, the Ta\xc3\xadno were divided into three broad groups, known as the <i>Western Ta\xc3\xadno</i> (Jamaica, most of Cuba, and the Bahamas), the <i>Classic Ta\xc3\xadno</i> (Hispaniola and Puerto Rico) and the <i>Eastern Ta\xc3\xadno</i> (northern Lesser Antilles). Ta\xc3\xadno groups were in conflict with the <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Caribs</a> of the southern Lesser Antilles.</p>\n<p>At the time of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Columbus</a>\' arrival in 1492, there were five Ta\xc3\xadno <a href="/wiki/Chiefdom" title="Chiefdom">chiefdoms</a> and territories on Hispaniola, each led by a principal <i><a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">Cacique</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Tribal_chief" title="Tribal chief">chieftain</a>), to whom tribute was paid. <i>Ayiti</i> ("land of high mountains") was the indigenous Ta\xc3\xadno name for the island of Hispaniola, which (on the Western side) has retained its name as <i>Ha\xc3\xafti</i> in French.</p>\n<p>Cuba, the largest island of the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a>, was originally divided into 29 chiefdoms. Most of the native settlements later became the site of Spanish colonial cities retaining the original Ta\xc3\xadno names, including <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bataban%C3%B3,_Cuba" title="Bataban\xc3\xb3, Cuba">Bataban\xc3\xb3</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camag%C3%BCey" title="Camag\xc3\xbcey">Camag\xc3\xbcey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baracoa" title="Baracoa">Baracoa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bayamo" title="Bayamo">Bayamo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The name <i>Cuba</i> comes from the Ta\xc3\xadno language, although the exact meaning of the name is unclear. It can be translated as "where fertile land is abundant" (cubao), or a "great place" (coabana).</p>\n<p>Puerto Rico also was divided into chiefdoms. As the hereditary head chief of Ta\xc3\xadno nation, the cacique received significant tribute. At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">Spanish conquest</a>, the largest Ta\xc3\xadno population centers may have had more than 3,000 people each.<sup id="cite_ref-Ta.C3.ADnoMuseum_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ta.C3.ADnoMuseum-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Ta\xc3\xadno were historically enemies of the neighboring <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Carib</a> nations, a different group which also had its origins in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and lived mainly in the Lesser Antilles.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The relationship between the rival groups has been the subject of many studies. For much of the 15th century, the Ta\xc3\xadno tribe was being driven to the northeast in the Caribbean because of raids by the Carib. Women were taken as captives, resulting in many Carib women speaking Ta\xc3\xadno.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> who arrived in the Bahamas, Cuba, and Hispaniola in 1492, and later in Puerto Rico, did not bring women on their first expeditions. Since the arrival of the conquistadores, Taino women were stolen and some became commodities for Spaniards to trade. The rape of Ta\xc3\xadno women in <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">Hispaniola</a> by the Spanish was common, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">mestizo</a> children.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEL.C3.A9ger190723_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEL.C3.A9ger190723-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAccilien_et_al.200312_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAccilien_et_al.200312-8">[8]</a></sup> Scholars suggest there was also substantial <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">mestizaje</a> (racial and cultural mixing) in <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, and several Indian pueblos survived into the 19th century.</p>\n<p>The Ta\xc3\xadno became nearly extinct as a culture following settlement by Spanish colonists, primarily due to <a href="/wiki/Infectious_diseases" class="mw-redirect" title="Infectious diseases">infectious diseases</a> for which they had no <a href="/wiki/Immunity_(medical)" title="Immunity (medical)">immunity</a>. The first recorded <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> outbreak in Hispaniola was in either December 1518 or January 1519.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a> killed almost 90% of the Native Americans who had not already perished.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAbbot2010_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAbbot2010-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/War" title="War">Warfare</a> and harsh enslavement by the colonists also caused many deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChrisp200634_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChrisp200634-11">[11]</a></sup> By 1548, the Ta\xc3\xadno population had declined to fewer than 500. Starting in about 1840, there have been attempts to create a quasi-indigenous Ta\xc3\xadno identity in rural areas of Cuba, the Dominican Republic, and Puerto Rico. This trend accelerated among the Puerto Rican community in the mainland United States in the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-Aikhenvald_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aikhenvald-12">[12]</a></sup> At the 2010 U.S. census, 1,098 people in Puerto Rico identified themselves as "Puerto Rican Indian," 1,410 identified as "Spanish American Indian," and 9,399 identified as "Ta\xc3\xadno." In total, 35,856 Puerto Ricans considered themselves Native American.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div was derived from the Arawakan word for <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a> flour, a staple of their diet. The Ta\xc3\xadno people, or Ta\xc3\xadno culture, has been classified by some authorities as belonging to the Arawak, as their language was considered to belong to the Arawak language family, the languages of which were present throughout the Caribbean, and much of Central and South America. The early ethnohistorian <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Garrison_Brinton" title="Daniel Garrison Brinton">Daniel Garrison Brinton</a> called the Ta\xc3\xadno people the "Island Arawak".<sup id="cite_ref-DGB1871_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DGB1871-14">[14]</a></sup> Nevertheless, contemporary scholars have recognized that the Ta\xc3\xadno had developed a distinct language and culture.</p>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno and Arawak appellations have been used with numerous and contradictory meanings by writers, travelers, historians, linguists, and anthropologists. Often they were used interchangeably; "Ta\xc3\xadno" has been applied to the Greater Antillean nation only, or including the Bahamian nations, or adding the <a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands" title="Leeward Islands">Leeward Islands</a> nations, or all those excluding the Puerto Rican and Leeward nations. Similarly, "Island Ta\xc3\xadno" has been used to refer to those living in the <a href="/wiki/Windward_Islands" title="Windward Islands">Windward Islands</a> only, to the northern Caribbean inhabitants only, as well as to the population of the entire Caribbean.</p>\n<p>Modern historians, linguists and anthropologists now hold that the term Ta\xc3\xadno should refer to all the Ta\xc3\xadno/Arawak nations except for the <a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Caribs</a>, who are not seen to belong to the same people. Linguists continue to debate whether the Carib language is an Arawakan <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialect</a> or <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a>, or perhaps an individual language, with an Arawakan <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a> used for communication purposes.</p>\n<p>Rouse classifies as Ta\xc3\xadno all inhabitants of the Greater Antilles (except the western tip of Cuba), the Bahamian <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>, and the northern Lesser Antilles. He subdivides the Ta\xc3\xadno into three main groups: Classic Ta\xc3\xadno, mostly from Haiti, Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic; Western Ta\xc3\xadno, or sub-Ta\xc3\xadno, for population from Jamaica, Cuba (except for the western tip) and the Bahamian archipelago; and Eastern Ta\xc3\xadno for those from the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands" title="Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> to <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERouse19927_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERouse19927-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Two schools of thought have emerged regarding the origin of the indigenous people of the Caribbean.</p>\n<ul>\n<li>One group contends that the ancestors of the Ta\xc3\xadno came from the center of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Basin">Amazon Basin</a>, and are related to the <a href="/wiki/Yanomama" class="mw-redirect" title="Yanomama">Yanomama</a>. This is indicated by linguistic, cultural and ceramic evidence. They migrated to the <a href="/wiki/Orinoco" title="Orinoco">Orinoco</a> valley on the north coast. From there they reached the Caribbean by way of what is now <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> into <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>, proceeding along the Lesser Antilles to Cuba and the Bahamian archipelago. Evidence that supports this theory includes the tracing of the ancestral cultures of these people to the Orinoco Valley and their languages to the Amazon Basin.<sup id="cite_ref-Origins_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-native_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Madrigal_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Madrigal-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li>The alternate theory, known as the circum-Caribbean theory, contends that the ancestors of the Ta\xc3\xadno diffused from the <a href="/wiki/Andean_Region_of_Colombia" class="mw-redirect" title="Andean Region of Colombia">Colombian Andes</a>. Julian H. Steward, who originated this concept, suggests a migration from the Andes to the Caribbean and a parallel migration into <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and into the <a href="/wiki/Guianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Guianas">Guianas</a>, Venezuela, and the Amazon Basin of South America.<sup id="cite_ref-Origins_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Origins-16">[16]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno culture as documented is believed to have developed in the Caribbean. The Ta\xc3\xadno creation story says that they emerged from caves in a sacred mountain on present-day Hispaniola.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In Puerto Rico, 21st century studies have shown a high proportion of people having <a href="/wiki/Amerindian" class="mw-redirect" title="Amerindian">Amerindian</a> <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">MtDNA</a>. Of the two major <a href="/wiki/Haplotypes" class="mw-redirect" title="Haplotypes">haplotypes</a> found, one does not exist in the Ta\xc3\xadno ancestral group, so other Native American people are also part of this genetic ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-native_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-native-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Duho.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Duho.jpg/220px-Duho.jpg" width="220" height="105" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Duho.jpg/330px-Duho.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Duho.jpg/440px-Duho.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="491" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Duho.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<i>Dujo</i>, a wooden chair crafted by Ta\xc3\xadnos.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno society was divided into two classes: <i>naborias</i> (commoners) and <i>nita\xc3\xadnos</i> (nobles). These were governed by male chiefs known as <i>caciques</i>, who were advised by priests/healers known as <i>bohiques</i>. Caciques enjoyed the privilege of wearing golden pendants called <i>guan\xc3\xadn</i>, living in square <i>boh\xc3\xados,</i> instead of the round ones of ordinary villagers, and sitting on wooden stools to be above the guests they received.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Bohiques were extolled for their healing powers and ability to speak with gods. They were consulted and granted the Ta\xc3\xadno permission to engage in important tasks.</p>\n<p>The Ta\xc3\xadno had a <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal">matrilineal</a> system of <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a>, descent and inheritance. When a male heir was not present, the inheritance or succession would go to the oldest male child of the deceased\'s sister. The Ta\xc3\xadno had <a href="/wiki/Avunculism" class="mw-redirect" title="Avunculism">avunculocal</a> post-marital residence, meaning a newly married couple lived in the household of the maternal uncle. He was more important in the lives of his niece\'s children than their biological father; the uncle introduced the boys to men\'s societies. Some Ta\xc3\xadno practiced <a href="/wiki/Polygamy" title="Polygamy">polygamy</a>. Men, and sometimes women, might have two or three spouses. A few caciques had as many as 30 wives.</p>\n<p>The Ta\xc3\xadno women were highly skilled in agriculture. The people depended on it, but the men also fished and hunted. They made fishing nets and ropes from <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palm_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm tree">palm</a>. Their dugout <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">canoes</a> (<i>kanoa</i>) were made in various sizes, which could hold from 2 to 150 people. An average-sized canoe would hold about 15\xe2\x80\x9320 people. They used bows and arrows for hunting, and developed the use of poisons on their arrowheads.</p>\n<p>A frequently worn hair style for women featured bangs in front and longer hair in back. They sometimes wore gold jewelry, paint, and/or shells. Ta\xc3\xadno men and unmarried women were usually naked although women wore a small cotton apron called a <i>nagua</i> after <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> The Ta\xc3\xadno lived in settlements called <i>yucayeques</i>, which varied in size depending on the location. Those in Puerto Rico and Hispaniola were the largest, and those in the Bahamas were the smallest. In the center of a typical village was a central plaza, used for various social activities such as games, festivals, religious <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">rituals</a>, and public ceremonies. These plazas had many shapes, including oval, rectangular, and narrow and elongated. Ceremonies where the deeds of the ancestors were celebrated, called <i>areitos</i>, were performed here.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Often, the general population lived in large circular buildings (<i>bohios</i>), constructed with wooden poles, woven straw, and palm leaves. These houses, built surrounding the central plaza, could hold 10-15 families each. The cacique and his family lived in rectangular buildings (<i>caney</i>) of similar construction, with wooden porches. Ta\xc3\xadno home furnishings included cotton hammocks (<i>hamaca</i>), sleeping and sitting mats made of palms, wooden chairs (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Dujo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dujo (page does not exist)">dujo</a> or duho) with woven seats, platforms, and cradles for children.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg/220px-Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg/330px-Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg/440px-Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="600" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parco_Cerimoniale_Indigeno_di_Caguana.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCaguana Ceremonial ball court (<i>batey</i>), outlined with stones.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The Ta\xc3\xadno played a ceremonial ball game called <i><a href="/wiki/Batey_(game)" title="Batey (game)">batey</a></i>. Opposing teams had 10 to 30 players per team and used a solid <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> ball. Normally, the teams were composed of men, but occasionally women played the game as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Alegr.C3.ADa.2C_p.348_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alegr.C3.ADa.2C_p.348-23">[23]</a></sup> The Classic Ta\xc3\xadno played in the village\'s center plaza or on especially designed rectangular ball courts called <i>batey</i>. Games on the <i>batey</i> are believed to have been used for conflict resolution between communities. The most elaborate ball courts are found at chiefdoms\' boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Often, chiefs made wagers on the possible outcome of a game.<sup id="cite_ref-Alegr.C3.ADa.2C_p.348_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alegr.C3.ADa.2C_p.348-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno spoke an <a href="/wiki/Arawakan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arawakan language">Arawakan language</a> and used an early form of writing <a href="/wiki/Proto-writing" title="Proto-writing">Proto-writing</a> in the form of <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyph</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> Some of the words used by them, such as <i>barbacoa</i> ("barbecue"), <i>hamaca</i> ("hammock"), <i>kanoa</i> ("canoe"), <i>tabaco</i> ("tobacco"), <i><a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">yuca</a></i>, <i>batata</i> ("sweet potato"), and <i><a href="/wiki/Jurac%C3%A1n" title="Jurac\xc3\xa1n">jurac\xc3\xa1n</a></i> ("hurricane"), have been incorporated into Spanish and English.</p>\n<p>For warfare, the men made wooden war clubs, which they called a <i><a href="/wiki/Macana" title="Macana">macana</a>.</i> It was about one inch thick and was similar to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coco_macaque&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Coco macaque (page does not exist)">coco macaque</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Food_and_agriculture">Food and agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Food and agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg/220px-Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg/330px-Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg/440px-Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manihot_esculenta_dsc07325.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCassava (<i>yuca</i>) roots, the Ta\xc3\xadnos\' main crop</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno staples included vegetables, fruit, meat, and fish. There were no large animals native to the Caribbean, but they captured and ate small animals, such as <a href="/wiki/Hutia" title="Hutia">hutias</a> and other mammals, <a href="/wiki/Earthworms" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthworms">earthworms</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lizards" class="mw-redirect" title="Lizards">lizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turtles" class="mw-redirect" title="Turtles">turtles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Birds" class="mw-redirect" title="Birds">birds</a>. <a href="/wiki/Manatees" class="mw-redirect" title="Manatees">Manatees</a> were speared and fish were caught in nets, speared, trapped in <a href="/wiki/Weir" title="Weir">weirs</a>, or caught with hook and line. Wild <a href="/wiki/Parrots" class="mw-redirect" title="Parrots">parrots</a> were decoyed with domesticated birds, and <a href="/wiki/Iguanas" class="mw-redirect" title="Iguanas">iguanas</a> were taken from trees and other <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">vegetation</a>. The Ta\xc3\xadno stored live animals until they were ready to be consumed: fish and turtles were stored in weirs, and hutias and dogs were stored in corrals.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.13_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.13-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Due to this lack of large game, the Ta\xc3\xadno people became very skilled <a href="/wiki/Fishermen" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishermen">fishermen</a>. One technique was to hook a <a href="/wiki/Remora" title="Remora">remora</a>, also known as a suckerfish, to a line secured to a canoe and wait for the fish to attach itself to a larger fish or even a sea turtle. Once this happened, men would jump into the water and bring in their assisted catch. Another method used by the Ta\xc3\xadnos was to take shredded stems and roots of poisonous <a href="/wiki/Senna_(plant)" title="Senna (plant)">senna</a> shrubs and throw them into nearby streams or rivers. Upon eating the bait, the fish were stunned just long enough to allow the fishermen to gather them in. This poison did not affect the edibility of the fish. Ta\xc3\xadno youth, mostly young boys, also collected <a href="/wiki/Mussel" title="Mussel">mussels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oysters</a> in shallow waters and within the <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangroves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Francine_Jacobs_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Francine_Jacobs-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno groups in the more developed islands, such as Puerto Rico, Hispaniola, and Jamaica, relied more on agriculture (farming and other jobs). Fields for important <a href="/wiki/Root_crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Root crops">root crops</a>, such as the staple <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">yuca</a>, were prepared by heaping up mounds of soil, called <i>conucos.</i> This improved soil drainage and fertility as well as delaying erosion, allowing for longer storage of crops in the ground. Less important crops such as corn were raised in simple clearings created by <a href="/wiki/Slash_and_burn" class="mw-redirect" title="Slash and burn">slash and burn</a> technique. Typically, <i>conucos</i> were three feet high and nine feet in circumference and were arranged in rows.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.12_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.12-28">[28]</a></sup> The primary root crop was yuca/<a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">cassava</a>, a woody <a href="/wiki/Shrub" title="Shrub">shrub</a> cultivated for its edible and <a href="/wiki/Starch" title="Starch">starchy</a> <a href="/wiki/Tuberous_root" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuberous root">tuberous root</a>. It was planted using a <i>coa</i>, a kind of <a href="/wiki/Hoe_(tool)" title="Hoe (tool)">hoe</a> made completely from wood. Women processed the poisonous variety of cassava by squeezing it to extract the toxic juices. Then they would grind the roots into flour for baking bread. <i>Batata</i> (<a href="/wiki/Sweet_potato" title="Sweet potato">sweet potato</a>) was the next most important root crop.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.12_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.12-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Contrary to mainland practices, corn was not ground into flour and baked into bread, but was cooked and eaten off the cob. Corn bread becomes moldy faster than cassava bread in the high humidity of the Caribbean. Corn was also used to make an alcoholic beverage known as <a href="/wiki/Chicha" title="Chicha">chicha</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Ta\xc3\xadno grew <a href="/wiki/Squash_(fruit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Squash (fruit)">squash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beans" class="mw-redirect" title="Beans">beans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Capsicum" title="Capsicum">peppers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">peanuts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapples</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">Tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calabash" title="Calabash">calabashes</a> (West Indian pumpkins) and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> were grown around the houses. Other fruits and vegetables, such as <a href="/wiki/Arecaceae" title="Arecaceae">palm nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">guavas</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Zamia" title="Zamia">Zamia</a></i> roots, were collected from the wild.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.12_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.12-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spirituality">Spirituality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spirituality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg/220px-Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg" width="220" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg/330px-Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg/440px-Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1552" data-file-height="1800" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Taino_-_Zemi_-_Walters_20056_-_Left_Side.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTa\xc3\xadno zem\xc3\xad sculpture from <a href="/wiki/Walters_Art_Museum" title="Walters Art Museum">Walters Art Museum</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno spirituality centered on the worship of <a href="/wiki/Zemi" title="Zemi">zem\xc3\xads</a>. A zem\xc3\xad is a spirit or ancestor (the word "god" is a misnomer but will be used henceforth for better understanding). The major Ta\xc3\xadno gods are <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%BAcahu" title="Y\xc3\xbacahu">Y\xc3\xbacahu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atabey_(goddess)" title="Atabey (goddess)">Atabey</a>. Y\xc3\xbacahu,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> which means spirit of cassava, was the god of cassava&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the Ta\xc3\xadnos\' main crop&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 and the sea. Atabey,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> mother of Y\xc3\xbacahu, was the <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">moon</a>, fresh waters and fertility.</p>\n<p>The minor Ta\xc3\xadno gods related to the growing of cassava, the process of life, creation and death. Baibrama was a minor god worshiped for his assistance in growing cassava and curing people from its poisonous juice. Boinayel and his twin brother M\xc3\xa1rohu were the gods of rain and fair weather, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.119_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.119-32">[32]</a></sup> Guabancex was the non-nurturing aspect of the goddess Atabey who had control over natural disasters. Jurac\xc3\xa1n is often identified as the god of storms but the word simply means hurricane in the Ta\xc3\xadno language. Guabancex had two assistants: Guataub\xc3\xa1, a messenger who created hurricane winds, and Coatrisquie who created <a href="/w/index.php?title=Floodwaters&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Floodwaters (page does not exist)">floodwaters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Maquetaurie Guayaba or Maketaori Guayaba was the god of Coaybay or Coabey, the land of the dead. Opiyelguabir\xc3\xa1n\', a dog-shaped god, watched over the dead. Demin\xc3\xa1n Caracaracol, a male cultural hero from which the Ta\xc3\xadno believed themselves to be descended, was worshipped as a zem\xc3\xad.<sup id="cite_ref-Rouse.2C_p.119_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rouse.2C_p.119-32">[32]</a></sup> Macocael was a cultural hero worshipped as a god, who had failed to guard the mountain from which <a href="/wiki/Human_beings" class="mw-redirect" title="Human beings">human beings</a> arose. He was punished by being turned into stone, or a bird, a <a href="/wiki/Frog" title="Frog">frog</a>, or a reptile, depending on interpretation of the <a href="/wiki/Myth" title="Myth">myth</a>.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lombards_Museum_053.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Lombards_Museum_053.JPG/170px-Lombards_Museum_053.JPG" width="170" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Lombards_Museum_053.JPG/255px-Lombards_Museum_053.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Lombards_Museum_053.JPG/340px-Lombards_Museum_053.JPG 2x" data-file-width="776" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lombards_Museum_053.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nZem\xc3\xad, a physical object housing a god, spirit, or ancestor<br />\n<i>Lombards Museum</i></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Zem\xc3\xad was also the name the people gave to their physical representations of the gods, whether objects or drawings. They were made in many forms and materials and have been found in a variety of settings. The majority of zem\xc3\xads were crafted from wood but stone, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">shell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, and cotton were also used.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Zem\xc3\xad <a href="/wiki/Petroglyph" title="Petroglyph">petroglyphs</a> were carved on rocks in streams, ball courts, and on <a href="/wiki/Stalagmite" title="Stalagmite">stalagmites</a> in caves. <i>Cem\xc3\xad</i> <a href="/wiki/Pictograph" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictograph">pictographs</a> were found on secular objects such as pottery, and on <a href="/wiki/Tattoos" class="mw-redirect" title="Tattoos">tattoos</a>. Yucah\xc3\xba, the god of cassava, was represented with a three-pointed zem\xc3\xad, which could be found in <i>conucos</i> to increase the yield of cassava. Wood and stone zem\xc3\xads have been found in <a href="/wiki/Caves" class="mw-redirect" title="Caves">caves</a> in Hispaniola and Jamaica.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> <i>Cem\xc3\xads</i> are sometimes represented by <a href="/wiki/Toads" class="mw-redirect" title="Toads">toads</a>, turtles, fishes, <a href="/wiki/Snakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Snakes">snakes</a>, and various abstract and human-like faces.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taino._Cohoba_Spoon,_1200-1500.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg/220px-Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg" width="220" height="507" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg/330px-Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg/440px-Taino._Cohoba_Spoon%2C_1200-1500.jpg 2x" data-file-width="666" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div title="Brooklyn Museum">Brooklyn Museum</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Petroglyph_at_Caguana.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/16/Petroglyph_at_Caguana.jpg" width="180" height="260" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="260" /></a>\n<div title="Petroglyph">petroglyph</a> overlaid with chalk in the Caguana Indigenous Ceremonial Center in Utuado, Puerto Rico.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Some zem\xc3\xads are accompanied by a small table or tray, which is believed to be a receptacle for <a href="/wiki/Hallucinogen" title="Hallucinogen">hallucinogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Herbal_smokeless_tobacco" title="Herbal smokeless tobacco">snuff</a> called <i><a href="/wiki/Cohoba" title="Cohoba">cohoba</a>,</i> prepared from the beans of a species of <i><a href="/wiki/Piptadenia" title="Piptadenia">Piptadenia</a></i> tree. These trays have been found with ornately carved snuff tubes. Before certain ceremonies, Ta\xc3\xadnos would purify themselves, either by inducing vomiting with a swallowing stick or by <a href="/wiki/Fasting" title="Fasting">fasting</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> After communal bread was served, first to the zem\xc3\xad, then to the cacique, and then to the common people, the people would sing the village epic to the accompaniment of <a href="/wiki/Maraca" title="Maraca">maraca</a> and other instruments.</p>\n<p>One Ta\xc3\xadno <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">oral tradition</a> explains that the Sun and Moon come out of caves. Another story tells of people who once lived in caves and only came out at night, because it was believed that the Sun would transform them. The Ta\xc3\xadno believed they were descended from the union of Demin\xc3\xa1n Caracaracol and a female turtle. The origin of the oceans is described in the story of a huge flood, which occurred when a father murdered his son (who was about to murder the father). The father put the son\'s bones into a <a href="/wiki/Gourd" title="Gourd">gourd</a> or <a href="/wiki/Calabash" title="Calabash">calabash</a>. When the bones turned into fish, the gourd broke, and all the water of the world came pouring out.</p>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadnos believed that Jupias, the souls of the dead, would go to Coaybay, the underworld, and there they rest by day. At night they would assume the form of bats and eat the <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">guava</a> fruit.</p>\n<h2><span id="Spaniards_and_Ta\xc3\xadno"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spaniards_and_Ta.C3.ADno">Spaniards and Ta\xc3\xadno</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spaniards and Ta\xc3\xadno">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Columbus and his crew, landing on an island in the Bahamas on October 12, 1492, were the first <a href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="European ethnic groups">Europeans</a> to encounter the Ta\xc3\xadno people. Columbus described the Ta\xc3\xadnos as a physically tall, well-proportioned people, with a noble and kind personality.</p>\n<p>Columbus wrote:</p>\n<blockquote class="templatequote">\n<p>They traded with us and gave us everything they had, with good will&#160;... they took great delight in pleasing us&#160;... They are very gentle and without knowledge of what is evil; nor do they murder or steal...Your highness may believe that in all the world there can be no better people&#160;... They love their neighbours as themselves, and they have the sweetest talk in the world, and are gentle and always laughing.</p>\n<div class="templatequotecite">\xe2\x80\x94\xe2\x80\x89<cite><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></cite></div>\n</blockquote>\n<p>At this time, the neighbors of the Ta\xc3\xadno were the <a href="/wiki/Guanahatabey" title="Guanahatabey">Guanahatabeys</a> in the western tip of Cuba, the Island-Caribs in the Lesser Antilles from <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> to <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Calusa" title="Calusa">Calusa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ais_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ais (tribe)">Ais</a> nations of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. The Ta\xc3\xadno called the island Guanahan\xc3\xad which Columbus renamed as <i>San Salvador</i> (Spanish for "Holy Savior"). Columbus called the Ta\xc3\xadno "Indians", a reference that has grown to encompass all the indigenous peoples of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>. A group of Ta\xc3\xadno people accompanied Columbus on his return voyage back to Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On Columbus\' second voyage, he began to require tribute from the Ta\xc3\xadno in Hispaniola. According to <a href="/wiki/Kirkpatrick_Sale" title="Kirkpatrick Sale">Kirkpatrick Sale</a>, each adult over 14 years of age was expected to deliver a hawks bell full of gold every three months, or when this was lacking, twenty-five pounds of spun cotton. If this tribute was not brought, the Spanish cut off the hands of the Ta\xc3\xadno and left them to bleed to death.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> These cruel practices inspired many revolts by the Ta\xc3\xadno and campaigns against the Spanish \xe2\x80\x94some being successful, some not.</p>\n<p>In 1511, several caciques in Puerto Rico, such as <a href="/wiki/Ag%C3%BCeyban%C3%A1_II" title="Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II">Ag\xc3\xbceyban\xc3\xa1 II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arasibo" title="Arasibo">Arasibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hayuya" title="Hayuya">Hayuya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jumacao" title="Jumacao">Jumacao</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urayo%C3%A1n" title="Urayo\xc3\xa1n">Urayo\xc3\xa1n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guarionex" title="Guarionex">Guarionex</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orocobix" title="Orocobix">Orocobix</a>, allied with the Carib and tried to oust the Spaniards. The revolt was suppressed by the Indio-Spanish forces of Governor <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n">Juan Ponce de Le\xc3\xb3n</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hatuey" title="Hatuey">Hatuey</a>, a Ta\xc3\xadno chieftain who had fled from Hispaniola to Cuba with 400 natives to unite the Cuban natives, was burned at the stake on February 2, 1512.</p>\n<p>In Hispaniola, a Ta\xc3\xadno chieftain named <a href="/wiki/Enriquillo" title="Enriquillo">Enriquillo</a> mobilized over 3,000 Ta\xc3\xadno in a successful rebellion in the 1520s. These Ta\xc3\xadno were accorded land and a charter from the royal administration. Despite the small Spanish military presence in the region, they often used diplomatic divisions and, with help from powerful native allies, controlled most of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In exchange for a seasonal salary, religious and language education, the Ta\xc3\xadno were required to work for Spanish and Indian land owners. This system of labor was part of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encomienda" title="Encomienda">encomienda</a></i>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Women">Women</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Women">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno society was based on a <a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal</a> system, meaning that descent was traced through the mother and that women lived together with other women and their children apart from the men. Because of this Ta\xc3\xadno women seem to have had a lot of control over their lives, their co-villagers and their bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Since they lived separately from men, they were able to decide when they wanted to involve in sexual contact. This is in part what shaped the views of <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistadors</a> who came in contact with Ta\xc3\xadno culture. They reportedly perceived women as \xe2\x80\x9cmacho women\xe2\x80\x9d who had strong control over the men.</p>\n<p>Most historical evidence suggests that, although unclear, it seems that Ta\xc3\xadno gender roles were non exclusive to most of the activities done in their community.</p>\n<p>Ta\xc3\xadno women played an important role in intercultural interaction between <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">Spaniards</a> and the Taino people. When Ta\xc3\xadno men were fighting intervention from other groups, women were left back home turning into the primary food producers or ritual specialists.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> Women seem to have participated in all levels of the Ta\xc3\xadno political hierarchy, they went up to occupy roles as high up as being <i><a href="/wiki/Cacique" title="Cacique">caciques</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> This meant that Ta\xc3\xadno women could potentially give permission to other Ta\xc3\xadno men and women to take on important tasks and that they could too make important choices for the village.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> There is evidence that suggests that the women who were wealthier among the tribe collected crafted goods that they would then use for trade or as gifts.</p>\n<p>Despite women being seemingly independent in Ta\xc3\xadno society, coming into the era of contact Spaniards took Ta\xc3\xadno women as an exchange item, putting them in a non-autonomous position. Dr. Chanca, a physician who traveled with <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>, reported in a letter that Spaniards took as many women as they possibly could and kept them as <a href="/wiki/Concubinage" title="Concubinage">concubines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> Some sources report that, despite women being free and powerful before the contact era, they became the first commodities up for Spaniards to trade, or often steal. This marked the beginning of a lifetime of theft and abuse of Ta\xc3\xadno women.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depopulation">Depopulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Depopulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Early population estimates of Hispaniola, probably the most populous island inhabited by Ta\xc3\xadnos, range from 100,000 to 1,000,000 people. The maximum estimates for Jamaica and Puerto Rico are 600,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> The Spanish priest <a href="/wiki/Bartolom%C3%A9_de_las_Casas" title="Bartolom\xc3\xa9 de las Casas">Bartolom\xc3\xa9 de las Casas</a> (who had lived in <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">Santo Domingo</a>) wrote in his 1561 multi-volume <i>History of the Indies</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>\n<blockquote class="templatequote">\n<p>There were 60,000 people living on this island [when I arrived in 1508], including the Indians; so that from 1494 to 1508, over three million people had perished from war, <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slavery</a> and the mines. Who in future generations will believe this?</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Researchers today doubt Las Casas\' figures for the pre-contact levels of the Ta\xc3\xadno population, considering them an exaggeration. For example, Anderson C\xc3\xb3rdova estimates a maximum of 500,000 people inhabiting the island.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> The Ta\xc3\xadno population estimates vary a great deal, from a few hundred thousand up to 8,000,000.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> They had no resistance to <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">Old World</a> diseases, notably <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> The encomienda system brought many Ta\xc3\xadno to work in the fields and mines in exchange for Spanish <a href="/wiki/Civil_disorder" title="Civil disorder">protection</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">education</a>, and a seasonal salary.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Under the pretense of searching for gold and other materials,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> many Spaniards took advantage of the regions now under control of the <i><a href="/wiki/Indian_auxiliaries" title="Indian auxiliaries">anaborios</a></i> and Spanish <i>encomenderos</i> to exploit the native population by seizing their <a href="/wiki/Land" title="Land">land</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wealth" title="Wealth">wealth</a>. It would take some time before the Ta\xc3\xadno revolted against their oppressors \xe2\x80\x94 both Indian and Spanish alike \xe2\x80\x94 and many military campaigns before <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Charles V</a> eradicated the <i>encomienda</i> system as a form of slavery.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In thirty years, between 80% and 90% of the Ta\xc3\xadno population died.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> Because of the increased number of people (Spanish) on the island, there was a higher demand for food. Ta\xc3\xadno cultivation was converted to Spanish methods. In hopes of frustrating the Spanish, some Ta\xc3\xadnos refused to plant or harvest their crops. The supply of food became so low in 1495 and 1496 that some 50,000 died from the severity of the famine.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> Historians have determined that the massive decline was due more to infectious disease outbreaks than any warfare or direct attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> By 1507 their numbers had shrunk to 60,000. Scholars believe that <a href="/wiki/Pandemic" title="Pandemic">epidemic</a> disease (<a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">influenza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Measles" title="Measles">measles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a>) was the overwhelming cause of the population decline of the indigenous people.<sup id="cite_ref-Watts_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Watts-63">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Schimmer_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schimmer-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Raudzens_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raudzens-65">[65]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="Ta\xc3\xadno_heritage_in_modern_times"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ta.C3.ADno_heritage_in_modern_times">Ta\xc3\xadno heritage in modern times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ta\xc3\xadno heritage in modern times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Groups of people currently identify as Ta\xc3\xadno, most notably among the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans" title="Puerto Ricans">Puerto Ricans</a> and <a href="/wiki/People_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="People of the Dominican Republic">Dominicans</a>, both on the islands and on <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> mainland.</p>\n<p>Some scholars, such as Jalil Sued Badillo, an ethnohistorian at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Puerto_Rico" title="University of Puerto Rico">University of Puerto Rico</a>, assert that although the official Spanish histories speak of the disappearance of the Ta\xc3\xadnos, many survivors left descendants usually by intermarrying with other <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic groups">ethnic groups</a>. Recent research revealed a high percentage of mixed or tri-racial ancestry in Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic. Those claiming Ta\xc3\xadno ancestry also have Spanish ancestry or African ancestry, and often both.</p>\n<p>Frank Moya Pons, a Dominican historian, documented that Spanish colonists intermarried with Ta\xc3\xadno women. Over time, some of their mixed descendants intermarried with Africans, creating a tri-racial <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">Creole</a> culture. 1514 census records reveal that 40% of Spanish men on the island of Hispaniola had Ta\xc3\xadno wives. Ethnohistorian Lynne Guitar writes that the Ta\xc3\xadno were declared extinct in Spanish documents as early as the 16th century; however, individual Ta\xc3\xadnos continued to appear in wills and legal records for several decades after the arrival of the Spaniards.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuitar2000_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuitar2000-66">[66]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Evidence suggests that some Ta\xc3\xadno men and African women inter-married and lived in relatively isolated <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">Maroon</a> communities in the interior of the islands, where they evolved into a hybrid rural or campesino population with little or no interference from the Spanish authorities. Scholars also note that contemporary rural Dominicans retain Ta\xc3\xadno linguistic features, agricultural practices, food ways, <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a>, fishing practices, <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture" title="Architecture">architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">oral history</a>, and religious views. However, these cultural traits are often looked down upon by urbanites as backwards.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGuitar2000_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGuitar2000-66">[66]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Sixteen \xe2\x80\x9cautosomal\xe2\x80\x9d studies of peoples in the Spanish-speaking Caribbean and its diaspora (mostly Puerto Ricans) have shown that between 10-20% of their DNA is indigenous, with some individuals having slightly higher scores and others having lower scores or no indigenous DNA at all.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> A recent study of a population in eastern Puerto Rico where the majority of persons tested claimed Ta\xc3\xadno ancestry and pedigree showed that they had 61% mtDNA (distant maternal ancestry) and 0% y-chromosome DNA (distant paternal ancestry) demonstrating as expected that this is a hybrid creole population.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Groups, such as the Jatibonicu Taino Tribal Nation of Boriken Puerto Rico (1970), the Ta\xc3\xadno Nation of the Antilles N.Y.C. (1993), United Confederation of Ta\xc3\xadno People N.Y.C (1998) and El Pueblo Guatu Ma-Cu A Borik\xc3\xa9n Puerto Rico (2000), have been established to foster Ta\xc3\xadno culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Ta\xc3\xadno activists have created two unique writing scripts. The scripts are used to write Spanish, not a retained language from pre-Columbian ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> The organization <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guaka-k%C3%BA&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Guaka-k\xc3\xba (page does not exist)">Guaka-k\xc3\xba</a> teaches and uses their script among their own members. The <a href="/w/index.php?title=LGTK&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="LGTK (page does not exist)">LGTK</a> (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Liga_Guak%C3%ADa_Ta%C3%ADna-k%C3%A9&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Liga Guak\xc3\xada Ta\xc3\xadna-k\xc3\xa9 (page does not exist)">Liga Guak\xc3\xada Ta\xc3\xadna-k\xc3\xa9</a>) has promoted teaching their script among elementary and middle school students to strengthen their interest in Ta\xc3\xadno identity.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ta%C3%ADno&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2;
