href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a>: \xe0\xa6\xac\xe0\xa6\x99\xe0\xa7\x8d\xe0\xa6\x97\xe0\xa7\x8b\xe0\xa6\xaa\xe0\xa6\xb8\xe0\xa6\xbe\xe0\xa6\x97\xe0\xa6\xb0 [b\xc9\x94\xc5\x8bgopo\xca\x83ago\xc9\xbe], <a href="/wiki/Hindi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi language">Hindi</a>: \xe0\xa4\xac\xe0\xa4\x82\xe0\xa4\x97\xe0\xa4\xbe\xe0\xa4\xb2 \xe0\xa4\x95\xe0\xa5\x80 \xe0\xa4\x96\xe0\xa4\xbe\xe0\xa4\xa1\xe0\xa4\xbc\xe0\xa5\x80 [b\xc9\x99\xc5\x8bg\xc9\x91\xcb\x90l k\xc9\xaa\xcb\x90 k\xca\xb0\xc9\x91\xcb\x90\xc9\xbd\xc9\xaa\xcb\x90])</i> is the largest <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bay</a> in the world with waters flowing straight out of the Himalayas through Bangladesh. Roughly triangular, it is bordered by Bangladesh to the North, Myanmar to the East, Sri Lanka and India to the west. <a href="/wiki/Countries_dependent_on_the_Bay_of_Bengal" title="Countries dependent on the Bay of Bengal">Countries dependent on the Bay of Bengal</a> straddle both <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>.</p>\n<p>The Bay of Bengal occupies an area of 2,172,000 square kilometres (839,000&#160;sq&#160;mi). A number of large rivers&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a> and three major rivers of Bangladesh the <a href="/wiki/Padma_River" title="Padma River">Padma</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Jamuna_River_(Bangladesh)" title="Jamuna River (Bangladesh)">Jamuna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meghna_River" title="Meghna River">Meghna</a>, other rivers such as the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_River" title="Irrawaddy River">Irrawaddy River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Godavari" class="mw-redirect" title="Godavari">Godavari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahanadi" title="Mahanadi">Mahanadi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishna_River" title="Krishna River">Krishna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaveri" title="Kaveri">Kaveri</a> flow into the Bay of Bengal. Among the important ports are <a href="/wiki/Chennai" title="Chennai">Chennai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Kolkata" title="Port of Kolkata">Kolkata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paradip" title="Paradip">Paradip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuticorin" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuticorin">Tuticorin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visakhapatnam" title="Visakhapatnam">Visakhapatnam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishnapatnam_Port" title="Krishnapatnam Port">Krishnapatnam Port</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chittagong" title="Chittagong">Chittagong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombo" title="Colombo">Colombo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Mongla" title="Port of Mongla">Mongla</a>. Among the smaller ports are the <a href="/wiki/Dhamra_Port" title="Dhamra Port">Dhamra Port</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kakinada_Port" title="Kakinada Port">Kakinada Port</a>, <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Payra" title="Port of Payra">Payra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yangon" title="Yangon">Yangon</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The bay gets its name from the historical <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">Bengal</a> region (The Indian state of <a href="/wiki/West_Bengal" title="West Bengal">West Bengal</a> and modern-day Bangladesh). In ancient scriptures, this water body may have been referred to as \'<i>Mahodadhi\'</i> (<a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>: \xe0\xa4\xae\xe0\xa4\xb9\xe0\xa5\x8b\xe0\xa4\xa6\xe0\xa4\xa7\xe0\xa4\xbf, lit. <i>great water receptacle</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-Kuttan_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuttan-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-indiatourism4u_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indiatourism4u-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="Citation is a random company website; there must be more scholarly sources for this. (August 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup> while it appears as <i>Sinus Gangeticus</i> or <i>Gangeticus Sinus</i>, meaning "Gulf of the Ganges", in ancient maps.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The other Sanskrit names for Bay of Bengal are \'<i>Vangopasagara</i>\' (Sanskrit: \xe0\xa4\xb5\xe0\xa4\x99\xe0\xa5\x8d\xe0\xa4\x97\xe0\xa5\x8b\xe0\xa4\xaa\xe0\xa4\xb8\xe0\xa4\xbe\xe0\xa4\x97\xe0\xa4\xb0, lit. <i>Bengal\'s Bay</i>), also simply called as \'<i>Vangasagara</i>\' (Sanskrit: \xe0\xa4\xb5\xe0\xa4\x99\xe0\xa5\x8d\xe0\xa4\x97\xe0\xa4\xb8\xe0\xa4\xbe\xe0\xa4\x97\xe0\xa4\xb0, lit. <i>Bengal Sea</i>) and \'<i>Purvapayodhi\'</i> (Sanskrit: \xe0\xa4\xaa\xe0\xa5\x82\xe0\xa4\xb0\xe0\xa5\x8d\xe0\xa4\xb5\xe0\xa4\xaa\xe0\xa4\xaf\xe0\xa5\x8b\xe0\xa4\xa7\xe0\xa4\xbf, lit. <i>Eastern Ocean</i>). Even today in Bengali, it is known as "Bongoposagor".</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rivers">Rivers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rivers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many major <a href="/wiki/Rivers_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Rivers of India">Rivers of India</a> flow west to east before draining into the Bay of Bengal. The <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganga</a> is the northernmost of these. Its main channel enters and flows through Bangladesh, where it is known as the <a href="/wiki/Padma_River" title="Padma River">Padma River</a>, before joining the <a href="/wiki/Meghna_River" title="Meghna River">Meghna River</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra_River" title="Brahmaputra River">Brahmaputra River</a> flows from east to west in <a href="/wiki/Assam" title="Assam">Assam</a> before turning south and entering Bangladesh where it is called the <a href="/wiki/Jamuna_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Jamuna River">Jamuna River</a>. This joins the Padma where upon the Padma joins the <a href="/wiki/Meghna_River" title="Meghna River">Meghna River</a> that finally drains into Bay of Bengal. The <a href="/wiki/Sundarbans" title="Sundarbans">Sundarbans</a> mangrove of forest of Bangladesh is a forest at the delta of the Padma, Jamuna and Meghna rivers lies partly in West Bengal and mostly in Bangladesh. The Brahmaputra at 2,948&#160;km (1,832&#160;mi) is the 28th <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_by_length" title="List of rivers by length">longest River</a> in the world. It originates in <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Hooghly_River" title="Hooghly River">Hooghly River</a>, another channel of the <a href="/wiki/Ganga" class="mw-redirect" title="Ganga">Ganga</a> that flows through <a href="/wiki/Calcutta" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcutta">Calcutta</a> drains into Bay of Bengal.</p>\n<p>The Padma\xe2\x80\x93Meghna-Jamuna rivers deposit nearly 1000 million tons of <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> per year. The sediment from these three rivers form the Bengal Delta and the <a href="/wiki/Submarine_fan" class="mw-redirect" title="Submarine fan">submarine fan</a>, a vast structure that extends from Bangladesh to south of the Equator, is up to 16.5 kilometres (10.3&#160;mi) thick, and contains at least 1,130 trillion tonnes of sediment, which has accumulated over the last 17 million years at an average rate of 665 million tons per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The Bay of Bengal used to be deeper than the <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Trench" title="Mariana Trench">Mariana Trench</a>, the present deepest ocean point.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The fan has buried organic carbon at a rate of nearly 1.1 trillion mol/yr (13.2 million t/yr) since the early <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> period. The three rivers currently contribute nearly 8% of the <a href="/wiki/Total_organic_carbon" title="Total organic carbon">total organic carbon</a> (TOC) deposited in the world\'s oceans. Due to high TOC accumulation in the deep sea bed of the Bay of Bengal, the area is rich in oil and natural gas and <a href="/wiki/Gas_hydrate" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas hydrate">gas hydrate</a> reserves. Bangladesh can <a href="/wiki/Land_reclamation" title="Land reclamation">reclaim land</a> substantially and economically gain from the sea area by constructing sea <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">dikes</a>, bunds, <a href="/wiki/Causeways" class="mw-redirect" title="Causeways">causeways</a> and by trapping the sediment from its rivers.</p>\n<p>Further southwest of Bangladesh, the <a href="/wiki/Mahanadi_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahanadi River">Mahanadi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Godavari_River" title="Godavari River">Godavari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishna_River" title="Krishna River">Krishna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kaveri_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaveri River">Kaveri Rivers</a> also flow from west to east in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and drain into the Bay of Bengal. Many small rivers also drain directly into the Bay of Bengal; the shortest of them is the <a href="/wiki/Cooum_River" title="Cooum River">Cooum River</a> at 64&#160;km (40&#160;mi).</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_River" title="Irrawaddy River">Irrawaddy (or Ayeyarwady) River</a> in Myanmar flows into the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Sea" title="Andaman Sea">Andaman Sea</a> of the Bay of Bengal and once had thick <a href="/wiki/Myanmar_Coast_mangroves" class="mw-redirect" title="Myanmar Coast mangroves">mangrove</a> forests of its own.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" class="mw-headline" id="Plate_tectonics">Plate tectonics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Plate tectonics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">lithosphere</a> of the earth is broken up into what are called <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">tectonic plates</a>. Underneath the Bay of Bengal, which is part of the great <a href="/wiki/Indo-Australian_Plate" title="Indo-Australian Plate">Indo-Australian Plate</a> and is slowly moving north east. This plate meets the <a href="/wiki/Burma_Plate" title="Burma Plate">Burma Microplate</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Trench" title="Sunda Trench">Sunda Trench</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_Islands" title="Nicobar Islands">Nicobar Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a> are part of the <a href="/wiki/Burma_Plate" title="Burma Plate">Burma Microplate</a>. The India Plate subducts beneath the Burma Plate at the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Trench" title="Sunda Trench">Sunda Trench</a> or Java Trench. Here, the pressure of the two plates on each other increase pressure and temperature resulting in the formation of volcanoes such as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_volcanoes_in_Myanmar" title="List of volcanoes in Myanmar">volcanoes in Myanmar</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_arc" title="Volcanic arc">volcanic arc</a> called the <a href="/wiki/Sunda_Arc" title="Sunda Arc">Sunda Arc</a>. The <a href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami">Sumatra-Andaman earthquake and Asian tsunami</a> was a result of the pressure at this zone causing a <a href="/wiki/Submarine_earthquake" title="Submarine earthquake">submarine earthquake</a> which then resulted in a destructive tsunami.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marine_geology">Marine geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Marine geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A zone 50 m wide extending from the island of Ceylon and the Coromandel coast to the head of the bay, and thence southwards through a strip embracing the Andaman and Nicobar islands, is bounded by the 100 fathom line of sea bottom; some 50 m. beyond this lies the 500-fathom limit. Opposite the mouth of the Ganges, however, the intervals between these depths are very much extended by deltaic influence.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Swatch_of_No_Ground" class="mw-redirect" title="Swatch of No Ground">Swatch of No Ground</a> is a 14&#160;km-wide deep sea canyon of the Bay of Bengal. Glory of Bengal cone (<i><a href="/wiki/Conus_bengalensis" title="Conus bengalensis">Conus bengalensis</a></i>) is just one of the seashells which can be photographed along beaches of the Bay of Bengal.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Endangered Species Act">endangered species</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Olive_ridley_sea_turtle" title="Olive ridley sea turtle">olive ridley sea turtle</a> can survive because of the nesting grounds made available at the Gahirmatha Marine Wildlife Sanctuary, <a href="/wiki/Gahirmatha_Beach" title="Gahirmatha Beach">Gahirmatha Beach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Odisha" title="Odisha">Odisha</a>, India. <a href="/wiki/Marlin" title="Marlin">Marlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barracuda" title="Barracuda">barracuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Skipjack_tuna" title="Skipjack tuna">skipjack tuna, (<i>Katsuwonus pelamis</i>)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellowfin_tuna" title="Yellowfin tuna">yellowfin tuna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indo-Pacific_hump-backed_dolphin" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Pacific hump-backed dolphin">Indo-Pacific humpbacked dolphin (<i>Sousa chinensis</i>)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bryde%27s_whale" title="Bryde\'s whale">Bryde\'s whale (<i>Balaenoptera edeni</i>)</a> are a few of the marine animals. Bay of Bengal hogfish (<i><a href="/wiki/Bodianus_neilli" title="Bodianus neilli">Bodianus neilli</a></i>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Wrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Wrass">wrass</a> which live in turbid lagoon reefs or shallow coastal reefs. Schools of dolphins can be seen, whether they are the <a href="/wiki/Bottle_nose_dolphin" class="mw-redirect" title="Bottle nose dolphin">bottle nose dolphin (<i>Tursiops truncatus</i>)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantropical_spotted_dolphin" title="Pantropical spotted dolphin">pantropical spotted dolphin (<i>Stenella attenuata</i>)</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Spinner_dolphin" title="Spinner dolphin">spinner dolphin (<i>Stenella longirostris</i>)</a>. <a href="/wiki/Tuna" title="Tuna">Tuna</a> and dolphins usually reside in the same waters. In shallower and warmer coastal waters the <a href="/wiki/Irrawaddy_dolphin" title="Irrawaddy dolphin">Irrawaddy dolphins (<i>Orcaella brevirostris</i>)</a> can be found.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Nicobar_Biosphere_Reserve" title="Great Nicobar Biosphere Reserve">Great Nicobar Biosphere Reserve</a> provides sanctuary to many animals some of which include the <a href="/wiki/Saltwater_crocodile" title="Saltwater crocodile">saltwater crocodile (<i>Crocodylus porosus</i>)</a>, giant <a href="/wiki/Leatherback_sea_turtle" title="Leatherback sea turtle">leatherback sea turtle (<i>Dermochelys coriacea</i>)</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cuora_amboinensis" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuora amboinensis">Malayan box turtle (<i>Cuora amboinensis kamaroma</i>)</a> to name a few.</p>\n<p>Another endangered species <a href="/wiki/Bengal_tiger" title="Bengal tiger">royal Bengal tiger</a> is supported by <a href="/wiki/Sundarbans_National_Park" title="Sundarbans National Park">Sundarbans</a> a large estuarine delta that holds a mangrove area in the <a href="/wiki/Ganges_Delta" title="Ganges Delta">Ganges River Delta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_oceanography">Chemical oceanography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Chemical oceanography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Coastal" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal">Coastal</a> regions bordering the Bay of Bengal are rich in minerals. Sri Lanka, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Serendib</a>, or <i>Ratna&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Dweepa</i> which means Gem Island. <a href="/wiki/Amethyst" title="Amethyst">Amethyst</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryl" title="Beryl">beryl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ruby" title="Ruby">ruby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sapphire" title="Sapphire">sapphire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Topaz" title="Topaz">topaz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Garnet" title="Garnet">garnet</a> are just some of the <a href="/wiki/Gems_of_Sri_Lanka" title="Gems of Sri Lanka">gems of Sri Lanka</a>. Garnet and other precious gems are also found in abundance in the <a href="/wiki/States_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="States of India">Indian states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Odisha" title="Odisha">Odisha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">Andhra Pradesh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="Physical_oceanography_\xe2\x80\x93_climate"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_oceanography_.E2.80.93_climate">Physical oceanography \xe2\x80\x93 climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Physical oceanography \xe2\x80\x93 climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From January to October, the current is northward flowing, and the clockwise circulation pattern is called the "East Indian Current". The Bay of Bengal <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_India" title="Climate of India">monsoon</a> moves in a northwest direction striking the <a href="/wiki/Nicobar_Islands" title="Nicobar Islands">Nicobar Islands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Andaman_Islands" title="Andaman Islands">Andaman Islands</a> first end of May, then the north eastern coast of India by end of June.</p>\n<p>The remainder of the year, the counterclockwise current is southwestward flowing, and the circulation pattern is called the East Indian Winter Jet. September and December see very active weather, season varsha (or monsoon), in the Bay of Bengal producing severe cyclones which affect eastern India. This would connect India from east to west without the necessity of going around Sri Lanka.</p>\n<p>Thoni and catamaran fishing boats of fishing villages thrive along the Bay of Bengal shorelines. It is considered to be a combination of vehicle exhaust, smoke from cooking fires, and industrial discharges.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transboundary_issues_affecting_the_marine_ecosystem">Transboundary issues affecting the marine ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Transboundary issues affecting the marine ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">\n<tr>\n<td class="mbox-image">\n<div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div>\n</td>\n<td class="mbox-text">\n<div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>. <span class="hide-when-compact">Please help <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.</span> <small><i>(December 2012)</i></small> <small class="hide-when-compact"><i>(<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>A transboundary issue is defined as an environmental problem in which either the cause of the problem and/or its impact is separated by a national boundary; or the problem contributes to a global environmental problem and finding regional solutions is considered to be a global environmental benefit. The eight Bay of Bengal countries have (2012) identified three major transboundary problems (or areas of concern) affecting the health of the Bay, that they can work on together. With the support of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_Of_Bengal_Large_Marine_Ecosystem_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bay Of Bengal Large Marine Ecosystem Project (page does not exist)">Bay Of Bengal Large Marine Ecosystem Project</a> (BOBLME), the eight countries are now (2012) developing responses to these issues and their causes, for future implementation as the Strategic Action Programme.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overexploitation_of_fisheries">Overexploitation of fisheries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Overexploitation of fisheries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg/250px-Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg" width="250" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg/375px-Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg/500px-Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2688" data-file-height="1520" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bay_of_Bengal_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSome small fishing boats are catching fish &amp; sell them in local coastal markets</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Fisheries">Fisheries</a> production in the Bay of Bengal is six million tonnes per year, more than seven percent of the world\'s catch. The major transboundary issues relating to shared fisheries are: a decline in the overall availability of fish resources; changes in species composition of catches; the high proportion of juvenile fish in the catch; and changes in <a href="/wiki/Marine_biodiversity" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine biodiversity">marine biodiversity</a>, especially through loss of vulnerable and <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>. The transboundary nature of these issues are: that many <a href="/wiki/Fish_stocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Fish stocks">fish stocks</a> are shared between BOBLME countries through the transboundary migration of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Ichthyoplankton" title="Ichthyoplankton">larvae</a>. <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">Fishing</a> overlaps national jurisdictions, both legally and illegally&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 overcapacity and <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a> in one location forces a migration of fishers and vessels to other locations. All countries (to a greater or lesser degree) are experiencing difficulties in implementing <a href="/wiki/Fisheries_management" title="Fisheries management">fisheries management</a>, especially the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem_approach_to_fisheries" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecosystem approach to fisheries">ecosystem approach to fisheries</a>. Bay of Bengal countries contribute significantly to the global problem of loss of <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered species</a>.</p>\n<p>The main causes of the issues are: open access to fishing grounds; Government emphasis on increasing fish catches; inappropriate government subsidies provided to fishers; increasing fishing effort, especially from <a href="/wiki/Fishing_trawler" title="Fishing trawler">trawlers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Purse_seiners" class="mw-redirect" title="Purse seiners">purse seiners</a>; high consumer demand for fish, including for seed and <a href="/wiki/Fishmeal" class="mw-redirect" title="Fishmeal">fishmeal</a> for <a href="/wiki/Aquaculture" title="Aquaculture">aquaculture</a>; ineffective fisheries management; and illegal and destructive fishing.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Degradation_of_critical_habitats">Degradation of critical habitats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Degradation of critical habitats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Bay of Bengal is an area of high biodiversity, with many <a href="/wiki/Endangered_species" title="Endangered species">endangered</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vulnerable_species" title="Vulnerable species">vulnerable species</a>. The major transboundary issues relating to habitats are: the loss and degradation of <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove habitats</a>; <a href="/wiki/Environmental_issues_with_coral_reefs" title="Environmental issues with coral reefs">degradation of coral reefs</a>; and the loss of, and damage to, <a href="/wiki/Seagrasses" class="mw-redirect" title="Seagrasses">seagrasses</a>. The transboundary nature of these major issues are: that all three critical habitats occur in all BOBLME countries. <a href="/wiki/Coastal_development_hazards" title="Coastal development hazards">Coastal development</a> for several varying uses of the land and sea are common in all BOBLME countries. Trade in products from all the habitats is transboundary in nature. <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> impacts are shared by all BOBLME countries. The main causes of the issues are: <a href="/wiki/Food_security" title="Food security">food security</a> needs of the coastal poor; lack of <a href="/wiki/Coastal_management" title="Coastal management">coastal development plans</a>; increasing trade in products from coastal habitats; coastal development and industrialization; ineffective <a href="/wiki/Marine_protected_area" title="Marine protected area">marine protected areas</a> and lack of enforcement; upstream development that affects water-flow; <a href="/wiki/Nonpoint_source_pollution" title="Nonpoint source pollution">intensive upstream agricultural practices</a>; and increasing tourism.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution_and_water_quality">Pollution and water quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bay_of_Bengal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Pollution and water quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The major transboundary issues relating to <a href="/wiki/Marine_pollution" title="Marine pollution">pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_quality" title="Water quality">water quality</a> are: sewage-borne pathogens and organic load; solid waste/marine litter; increasing nutrient inputs; oil pollution; persistent organic pollutants (<a href="/wiki/Persistent_organic_pollutant" title="Persistent organic pollutant">POPs</a>) and persistent toxic substances (PTSs); sedimentation; and heavy metals. The transboundary nature of these issues are: discharge of untreated/partially treated <a href="/wiki/Sewage" title="Sewage">sewage</a> being a common problem. Sewage and organic discharges from the <a href="/wiki/Ganges" title="Ganges">Ganges</a>-<a href="/wiki/Brahmaputra" class="mw-redirect" title="Brahmaputra">Brahmaputra</a>-<a href="/wiki/Meghna" class="mw-redirect" title="Meghna">Meghna</a> River are likely to be transboundary. Plastics and derelict fishing gear can be transported long distances across national boundaries. High nutrient discharges from rivers could intensify largescale hypoxia. Atmospheric transport of nutrients is inherently transboundary. Differences between countries with regard to regulation and enforcement of shipping discharges may drive discharges across boundaries. <a href="/wiki/Tarball_(oil)" title="Tarball (oil)">Tar balls</a> are transported long distances. POPs/PTSs and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">mercury</a>, including organo-mercury, undergo long-range transport. <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">Sedimentation</a> and most heavy metal contamination tend to be localized and lack a strong transboundary dimension. The main causes of the issues are: increasing coastal population density and urbanization; higher consumption, resulting in more waste generated per person; insufficient funds allocated to waste management; migration of industry into BOBLME countries; and proliferation of small industries.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
