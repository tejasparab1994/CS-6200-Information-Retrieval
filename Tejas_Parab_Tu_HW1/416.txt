href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale</a> in the <a Hurricanes of such intensity occur once every three years in this region on average.</p>\n<p>Only six times\xe2\x80\x94in the <a href="/wiki/1932_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1932 Atlantic hurricane season">1932</a>, <a href="/wiki/1933_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1933 Atlantic hurricane season">1933</a>, <a href="/wiki/1961_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1961 Atlantic hurricane season">1961</a>, <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005</a>, <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2007 Atlantic hurricane season">2007</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a> hurricane seasons\xe2\x80\x94has more than one Category&#160;5 recorded hurricane formed. Only in 2005 have more than two Category&#160;5 hurricanes formed, and only in 2007 and 2017 did more than one make landfall at Category&#160;5 strength.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-0" Atlantic hurricanes between 1851 and 2014</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A Category&#160;5 Atlantic hurricane is one that is considered by the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC), to have had sustained wind speeds greater than 136 knots (157&#160;mph; 252&#160;km/h; 70&#160;m/s) on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a>. The NHC considers sustained wind speeds to be those that occur over a one-minute period at 10 metres (32.8&#160;ft) above ground. These wind speeds are estimated by using a blend of data from a variety of sources, which include observations from nearby ships, reconnaissance aircraft, or automatic weather stations and pictures from various satellites.</p>\n<p>Between <a href="/wiki/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1924 Atlantic hurricane season">1924</a> and <a href="/wiki/2017_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2017 Atlantic hurricane season">2017</a>, 33 hurricanes were recorded at Category&#160;5 strength. No Category&#160;5 hurricanes were observed officially before 1924. It can be presumed that earlier storms reached Category&#160;5 strength over open waters, but the strongest winds were not measured. The <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a>, a device used for measuring wind speed, was invented in 1846. However, during major hurricane strikes the instruments as a whole were often blown away, leaving the hurricane\'s peak intensity unrecorded. For example, as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Beaufort_Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Beaufort Hurricane">Great Beaufort Hurricane</a> of 1879 struck North Carolina, the anemometer cups were blown away when indicating 138&#160;mph (222&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-Hudgins_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hudgins-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_reanalysis" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic hurricane reanalysis">reanalysis</a> of weather data is ongoing by researchers who may upgrade or downgrade other Atlantic hurricanes currently listed at Categories 4 and 5.<sup id="cite_ref-reanalysis_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reanalysis-3">[3]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/1825_Santa_Ana_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="1825 Santa Ana hurricane">1825 Santa Ana hurricane</a> is suspected to have reached Category 5 strength.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Furthermore, <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">paleotempestological</a> research aims to identify past major hurricanes by comparing sedimentary evidence of recent and past hurricane strikes. For example, a "giant hurricane" significantly more powerful than <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hattie" title="Hurricane Hattie">Hurricane Hattie</a> (Category 5) has been identified in <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belizean</a> sediment, having struck the region sometime <a href="/wiki/Pre-1600_Atlantic_hurricane_seasons" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-1600 Atlantic hurricane seasons">before 1500</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mccloskey2009_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mccloskey2009-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png/220px-1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png/330px-1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png/440px-1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="633" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1924_Atlantic_hurricane_10_track.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAn <a href="/wiki/1924_Cuba_hurricane" title="1924 Cuba hurricane">October Category&#160;5</a> that hit Cuba in <a href="/wiki/1924_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1924 Atlantic hurricane season">1924</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Officially, the decade with the most Category&#160;5 hurricanes is 2000\xe2\x80\x932009, with eight Category&#160;5 hurricanes having occurred: Isabel (2003), Ivan (2004), Emily (2005), Katrina (2005), Rita (2005), Wilma (2005), Dean (2007), and Felix (2007). The previous decades with the most Category&#160;5 hurricanes were the 1930s and 1960s, with six occurring between 1930 and 1939 (before naming began).<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Nine Atlantic hurricanes\xe2\x80\x94<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Camille</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Allen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Andrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Isabel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ivan" title="Hurricane Ivan">Ivan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dean" title="Hurricane Dean">Dean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Felix" title="Hurricane Felix">Felix</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irma" title="Hurricane Irma">Irma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Maria</a>\xe2\x80\x94reached Category&#160;5 intensity on more than one occasion; that is, by reaching Category&#160;5 intensity, weakening to a Category&#160;4 or lower, and then becoming a Category&#160;5 again. Such hurricanes have their dates shown together. Camille, Andrew, Dean, Felix, Irma, and Maria each attained Category 5 status twice during their lifespans. Allen, Isabel, and Ivan reached Category 5 intensity on three separate occasions. However, no Atlantic hurricane has reached Category&#160;5 intensity more than three times during its lifespan. The November 1932 Cuba hurricane holds the record for most time spent as a Category&#160;5 (although it took place before satellite or aircraft reconnaissance, so this record may be somewhat suspect). Irma holds the record for longest continuous span as a Category 5 storm in the satellite era.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Andrew_reanalysis_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andrew_reanalysis-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Thirty-three Category&#160;5s have been recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a> since 1851, when records began. Only one Category 5 has been recorded in July, eight in August, nineteen in September, five in October, and one in November. There have been no officially recorded June or off-season Category&#160;5 hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The July and August Category&#160;5s reached their high intensities in both the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>. These are the areas most favorable for tropical cyclone development in those months.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-climatology_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-climatology-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>September sees the most Category&#160;5 hurricanes. This coincides with the climatological peak of the Atlantic hurricane season, which occurs in early September.<sup id="cite_ref-September_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-September-8">[8]</a></sup> September Category&#160;5s reached their strengths in any of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, and open Atlantic. These places are where September tropical cyclones are likely to form.<sup id="cite_ref-climatology_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-climatology-7">[7]</a></sup> Many of these hurricanes are either <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_hurricane" title="Cape Verde hurricane">Cape Verde-type storms</a>, which develop their strength by having a great deal of open water; or so-called Bahama busters, which intensify over the warm <a href="/wiki/Loop_Current" title="Loop Current">Loop Current</a> in the Gulf of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-CapeVerde_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CapeVerde-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>All six Category&#160;5s in October and November reached their intensities in the western Caribbean, a region that Atlantic hurricanes strongly Most Category&#160;5 hurricanes in the Atlantic make landfall because of their proximity to land in the Caribbean and Gulf of Mexico, where the usual synoptic weather patterns carry them towards land, as opposed to the westward, oceanic mean track of Eastern Pacific hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-meanTrack_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meanTrack-50">[50]</a></sup> Sixteen of the storms made landfall while at Category&#160;5 intensity;<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup> 2007 and 2017 are the only years in which two storms made landfall at this intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Many of these systems made landfall shortly after weakening from a Category&#160;5. This weakening can be caused by dry air near land, shallower waters due to <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">shelving</a>, interaction with land, or cooler waters near shore.<sup id="cite_ref-Katrina_TCR_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Katrina_TCR-35">[35]</a></sup> In southern Florida, the return period for a Category&#160;5 hurricane is roughly once
