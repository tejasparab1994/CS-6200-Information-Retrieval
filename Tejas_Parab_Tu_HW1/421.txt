class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sub-basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Bay of Bengal, located in the northeast of the Indian Ocean, is responsible for the formation of some of the strongest and deadliest tropical cyclones in the world. The basin is abbreviated <i>BOB</i> by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD), the official <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> of the basin. The Bay of Bengal\'s coast is shared among <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> and western part of <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>. The strongest storm in the bay was the <a href="/wiki/1999_Odisha_cyclone" title="1999 Odisha cyclone">1999 Odisha cyclone</a>. As the remnants impact <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> sometimes, the <a href="/wiki/National_Meteorological_Center_of_CMA" title="National Meteorological Center of CMA">National Meteorological Center of CMA</a> also issues full advisories to cyclonic storms in this basin.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Arabian Sea is a sea located in the northwest of the Indian Ocean. Tropical cyclones in the basin are abbreviated <i>ARB</i> by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD), the official <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre</a> (RSMC) of the basin. The Arabian Sea\'s coast is shared among <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">Maldives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mahalo.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mahalo.com-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Monsoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Monsoons">Monsoons</a> are characteristic of the Arabian Sea and responsible for the yearly cycling of its waters. In summer, strong winds blow from the southwest to the northeast, bringing rain to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. During the winter, the winds are milder and blow in the opposite direction, from the northeast to the southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-mahalo.com_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mahalo.com-2">[2]</a></sup> Cyclones are rare in the Arabian Sea, but the basin can produce strong tropical cyclones. <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu" title="Cyclone Gonu">Super Cyclonic Storm Gonu</a> was the strongest recorded tropical cyclone in the basin.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> However, storms typically do not reach a high intensity in the Arabian Sea due to the small amount of water, dry air coming from the desert of the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, and unfavorable wind shear from the monsoon.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_basin">History of the basin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Indian_Ocean_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of the basin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The systematic scientific studies of tropical systems in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> was started during the 19th century by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Piddington" title="Henry Piddington">Henry Piddington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup> Piddington utilised meteorological logs of vessels that navigated the seas and published a series of memoirs, in the \xe2\x80\x9cJournal of the Asiatic Society of Bengal\xe2\x80\x9d between 1839 and 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup> These memoirs gave accounts and tracks of individual storms in the Bay of Bengal and the Arabian Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_Report_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT_Report-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the 2004 post monsoon season the IMD started to name tropical cyclones within the basin, with the first one named <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Onil" title="Cyclone Onil">Cyclone Onil</a> during September 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_2004_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_2004-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During 2015 a modification to the intensity scale took place, with the IMD and WMO calling a system with 3-minute maximum sustained wind speeds between 90 knots (165&#160;km/h; 105&#160;mph) and 120 knots (220&#160;km/h; 140&#160;mph) an extremely severe cyclonic storm.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Water temperatures in the Arabian Sea are typically warm enough to allow for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> year round, although strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> prevents formation in the summer months and limits intensity other times of the year. An increase in air pollution since the 1930s caused a decrease in the wind shear, allowing storms to have become stronger since 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Years">Years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a analyzing the spring season of tropical cyclones in the Bay of Bengal<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[15]</a></sup> found increases in both premonsoon precipitation and The deepened monsoon trough in the Bay of Bengal not only affects cyclone frequency and timing, but also acts to direct more cyclones towards Myanmar. Increased <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/jgrd.50396/abstract">anthropogenic aerosols</a> likely contributed to such a regional climate change.</p>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-intensity-12"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-7"><sup><i><b>h</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-8"><sup><i><b>i</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-9"><sup><i><b>j</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-10"><sup><i><b>k</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-11"><sup><i><b>l</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-intensity_12-12"><sup><i><b>m</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Each column refers to how many Storms developed during the season with D=Depressions, DD=Deep Depressions, CS=Cyclonic Storms, SCS=Severe Cyclonic Storm, VSCS=Very Severe Cyclonic Storm, ESCS=Extremely Severe Cyclonic Storm, SUCS=Super Cyclonic Storm.
