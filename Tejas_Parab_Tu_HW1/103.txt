For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Clockwise_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Clockwise (disambiguation)">Clockwise (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clockwise_arrow.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Clockwise_arrow.svg/220px-Clockwise_arrow.svg.png" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Clockwise_arrow.svg/330px-Clockwise_arrow.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Clockwise_arrow.svg/440px-Clockwise_arrow.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="329" data-file-height="312" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clockwise_arrow.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe clockwise direction</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">Rotation</a> can occur in two possible directions. A <b>clockwise</b> (typically abbreviated as <b>CW</b>) motion is one that proceeds in the same direction as a <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a>\'s hands: from the top to the right, then down and then to the left, and back up to the top. The opposite sense of <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">revolution</a> is (in <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">North American English</a>) <b>counterclockwise</b> (<b>CCW</b>) or (in <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth English">Commonwealth English</a>) <b>anticlockwise</b> (<b>ACW</b>).</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Before clocks were commonplace, the terms "<a href="/wiki/Sunwise" title="Sunwise">sunwise</a>" and "deasil", "deiseil" and even "deocil" from the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a> language and from the same root as the Latin "dexter" ("right") were used for clockwise. "<a href="/wiki/Widdershins" title="Widdershins">Widdershins</a>" or "withershins" (from <a href="/wiki/Middle_Low_German" title="Middle Low German">Middle Low German</a> "weddersinnes", "opposite course") was used for counterclockwise.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Counterclockwise_arrow.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Counterclockwise_arrow.svg/220px-Counterclockwise_arrow.svg.png" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Counterclockwise_arrow.svg/330px-Counterclockwise_arrow.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Counterclockwise_arrow.svg/440px-Counterclockwise_arrow.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="329" data-file-height="312" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Counterclockwise_arrow.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe counterclockwise or anticlockwise direction</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The terms clockwise and counterclockwise can only be applied to a rotational motion once a side of the rotational plane is specified, from which the rotation is observed. For example, the daily rotation of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> is clockwise when viewed from above the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a>, and counterclockwise when viewed from above the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a>.</p>\n<p>Clocks traditionally follow this sense of rotation because of the clock\'s predecessor: the <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">sundial</a>. Clocks with hands were first built in the Northern Hemisphere (see <i><a href="/wiki/Clock#A_new_mechanism" title="Clock">main article</a></i>), and they were made to work like sundials. In order for a horizontal sundial to work (in the Northern Hemisphere), it must be placed looking northward. Then, when the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> moves in the sky (east to south to west), the shadow cast on the opposite side of the sundial moves with the same sense of rotation (west to north to east). This is why hours were drawn in sundials in that manner, and why modern clocks have their numbers set in the same way. Note, however, that on a vertical sundial (such as those placed on the walls of buildings), the shadow moves in the opposite direction, and some clocks were constructed to mimic this. The best-known surviving example is the astronomical clock in the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCnster_Cathedral" title="M\xc3\xbcnster Cathedral">M\xc3\xbcnster Cathedral</a>, whose hands move counterclockwise.</p>\n<p>Occasionally, clocks whose hands revolve counterclockwise are nowadays sold as a novelty. Historically, some <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> clocks were built that way, for example in some synagogue towers in Europe, to accord with right-to-left reading in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew language</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In 2014 under <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivian</a> president <a href="/wiki/Evo_Morales" title="Evo Morales">Evo Morales</a>, the clock outside the Legislative Assembly in <a href="/wiki/Plaza_Murillo" title="Plaza Murillo">Plaza Murillo</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Paz,_Bolivia" class="mw-redirect" title="La Paz, Bolivia">La Paz</a>, was shifted to counterclockwise motion to promote indigenous values.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shop-work">Shop-work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shop-work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Typical <a href="/wiki/Nut_(hardware)" title="Nut (hardware)">nuts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">screws</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screw#Bolt" title="Screw">bolts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bottle_cap" title="Bottle cap">bottle caps</a>, and jar lids are tightened (moved away from the observer) clockwise and loosened (moved towards the observer) counterclockwise in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">right-hand rule</a>.</p>\n<p>To apply the right-hand rule, place one\'s loosely clenched right hand above the object with the thumb pointing in the direction one wants the screw, nut, bolt, or cap ultimately to move, and the curl of the fingers, from the palm to the tips, will indicate in which way one needs to turn the screw, nut, bolt or cap to achieve the desired result. Almost all threaded objects obey this rule except for a few left-handed exceptions described below.</p>\n<p>The reason for the clockwise standard for most screws and bolts is that <a href="/wiki/Supination" class="mw-redirect" title="Supination">supination</a> of the arm, which is used by a right-handed person to tighten a screw clockwise, is generally stronger than <a href="/wiki/Pronation" class="mw-redirect" title="Pronation">pronation</a> used to loosen.</p>\n<p>Sometimes the opposite (left-handed, counterclockwise, reverse) sense of threading is used for a special reason. A thread might need to be left-handed to prevent operational stresses from loosening it. For example, some older <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">cars</a> and trucks had right-handed <a href="/wiki/Lug_nut" title="Lug nut">lug nuts</a> on the right wheels and left-handed ones on the left wheels, so that, as the vehicle moved forward, the lug nuts tended to tighten rather than loosen. For <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_pedal" title="Bicycle pedal">bicycle pedals</a>, the one on the left must be reverse-threaded to prevent it unscrewing during use. Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Spinning_wheel#Treadle_wheel" title="Spinning wheel">flyer whorl</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Spinning_wheel" title="Spinning wheel">spinning wheel</a> uses a left-hand thread to keep it from loosening. A <a href="/wiki/Turnbuckle" title="Turnbuckle">turnbuckle</a> has right-handed threads on one end and left-handed threads on the other. Some gas fittings are left-handed to prevent disastrous misconnections: oxygen fittings are right-handed, but <a href="/wiki/Acetylene" title="Acetylene">acetylene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a>, and other flammable gases are unmistakably distinguished by left-handed fittings.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematics">Mathematics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mathematics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">trigonometry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> in general, plane <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">angles</a> are conventionally measured counterclockwise, starting with 0\xc2\xb0 or 0 <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">radians</a> pointing directly to the right (or east), and 90\xc2\xb0 pointing straight up (or north). However, in <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">navigation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Compass" title="Compass">compass</a> headings increase clockwise around the compass face, starting with 0\xc2\xb0 at the top of the compass (the northerly direction), with 90\xc2\xb0 to the right (east).</p>\n<p>A circle defined parametrically in a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#Orientation_and_.22handedness.22" title="Cartesian coordinate system">positive Cartesian plane</a> by the equations <span class="nowrap"><i>x</i> = cos <i>t</i></span> and <span class="nowrap"><i>y</i> = sin <i>t</i></span> is traced counterclockwise as the angle <i>t</i> increases in value, from the right-most point at <span class="nowrap"><i>t</i> = 0</span>. An alternative formulation with sin and cos swapped gives a clockwise trace from the upper-most point.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games_and_activities">Games and activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Games and activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In general, most card games, board games, parlor games and multiple team sports play in a clock-wise turn rotation in Western Countries and Latin America with a notable resistance to playing in the opposite direction (counter-clockwise). Traditionally (and still continued for the most part) turns pass counter-clockwise in many Asian countries. In Western countries when speaking and discussion activities take part in a circle, turns tend to naturally pass in a clockwise motion even though there is no obligation to do so. Curiously, unlike with games, there is usually no objection when the activity uncharacteristically begins in a counter-clockwise motion.</p>\n<p>Notably, the game of baseball is played counter-clockwise.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_humans">In humans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: In humans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Most left-handed people prefer to draw circles and circulate in buildings clockwise, while most right-handed people prefer to draw circles and circulate in buildings counterclockwise. This is believed to result from dominant brain hemispheres,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> though some attribute it to muscle mechanics.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Handedness" title="Handedness">Handedness</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Chirality_(physics)" title="Chirality (physics)">Chirality (physics)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chirality_(chemistry)" title="Chirality (chemistry)">Chirality (chemistry)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Inner/outer_orientation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inner/outer orientation">Inner/outer orientation</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Optical_isomerism" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical isomerism">Optical isomerism</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" class="mw-redirect" title="Retrograde motion">Retrograde motion</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Relative_direction" title="Relative direction">Relative direction</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clockwise&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
