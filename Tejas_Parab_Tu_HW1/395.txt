class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StormNovel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b7/StormNovel.jpg/220px-StormNovel.jpg" House</a>)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><i><b>Storm</b></i> is a novel written by <a href="/wiki/George_Rippey_Stewart" class="mw-redirect" title="George Rippey Stewart">George Rippey Stewart</a> and published in 1941. The book became a <a href="/wiki/Best-seller" class="mw-redirect" title="Best-seller">best-seller</a> and helped lead to the naming of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-islandnet_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet-1">[1]</a></sup> even though the titular storm is <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_J4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_J4-2">[2]</a></sup> The book is divided into twelve chapters: one chapter for each day of the storm\'s existence.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In January 1935, a cyclone develops in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> near <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, and becomes a significant storm as it moves toward <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The storm, named "Maria" by the (unnamed) Junior <a href="/wiki/Meteorologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorologist">Meteorologist</a> at the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> Weather Bureau Office, becomes a blizzard that threatens the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">Sierra Nevada</a> range with snowfall amounts of 20&#160;feet (6.1&#160;m). The storm\'s beneficial effects include averting a <a href="/wiki/Locust" title="Locust">locust</a> plague and ending a drought. Its harmful effects include flooding a valley near Sacramento, endangering a plane, stalling a train, and leading to the deaths of 16 people. It spawns a new cyclone, which significantly affects <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>This book was the inspiration for the Lerner and Loewe song "<a href="/wiki/They_Call_the_Wind_Maria" title="They Call the Wind Maria">They Call the Wind Maria</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-islandnet_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-islandnet-1">[1]</a></sup> performed in the musical <i><a href="/wiki/Paint_Your_Wagon_(musical)" title="Paint Your Wagon (musical)">Paint Your Wagon</a></i>. It also prompted the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> to use personal names to designate storms.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p><i>Storm</i> was dramatized as <i>A Storm Called Maria</i> on the November 2, 1959 episode of ABC\'s <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_anthology_television_series" title="Walt Disney anthology television series"><i>Walt Disney Presents</i></a>. Co-produced by Ken Nelson Productions, it blended newsreel footage of several different storms to represent the mega-storm in the novel and traced the storm from its origins in Japan to the coast of California. The cast included non-actors, among them the dam superintendent George Kritsky, the telephone lineman Walt Bowen, and the highway superintendent Leo Quinn.</p>\n<p>American singer, songwriter and producer <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a> was named after this storm.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sequel">Sequel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm_(novel)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sequel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Stewart\'s novel <i>Fire</i> (1948) was a sequel to <i>Storm,</i> again featuring the life of the (former) Junior Meteorologist, who was now a <a href="/wiki/World_War_Two" class="mw-redirect" title="World War Two">World War Two</a> veteran and had been promoted. Dealing with a California <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfire</a>, it also used the backdrop of an environmental catastrophe to disclose the personal struggles and triumphs of individual human beings.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
