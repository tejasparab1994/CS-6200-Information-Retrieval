surface temperature and flows.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Sea surface temperature</b> (SST) is the water <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> close to the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a>\'s surface. The exact meaning of <i>surface</i> varies according to the measurement method used, but it is between 1 millimetre (0.04&#160;in) and 20 metres (70&#160;ft) below the <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a> surface. <a href="/wiki/Air_mass" title="Air mass">Air masses</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> are highly modified by sea surface temperatures within a short distance of the shore. Localized areas of heavy <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> can form in <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">bands</a> downwind of warm water bodies within an otherwise cold air mass. Warm sea surface temperatures are known to be a cause of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s oceans. Tropical cyclones can also cause a cool wake, due to turbulent mixing of the upper 30 metres (100&#160;ft) of the ocean. SST changes diurnally, like the air above it, but to a lesser degree. There is less SST variation on breezy days than on calm days. In addition, <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean currents</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a> (AMO), can effect SST\'s on multi-decadal time scales,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> a major impact results from the global <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermohaline">thermohaline</a> circulation, which affects average SST significantly throughout most of the world\'s oceans.</p>\n<p>Coastal SSTs can cause offshore winds to generate <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">upwelling</a>, which can significantly cool or warm nearby landmasses, but shallower waters over a <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> are often warmer. Onshore winds can cause a considerable warm-up even in areas where upwelling is fairly constant, such as the northwest coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Its values are important within <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> as the SST influences the atmosphere above, such as in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breezes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advection_fog#Types" class="mw-redirect" title="Advection fog">sea fog</a>. It is also used to calibrate measurements from <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div (a) at night and (b) during the day</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>There are a variety of techniques for measuring this parameter that can potentially yield different results because different things are actually being measured. Away from the immediate sea surface, general temperature measurements are accompanied by a reference to the specific depth of measurement. This is because of significant differences encountered between measurements made at different depths, especially during the daytime when low wind speed and high sunshine conditions may lead to the formation of a warm layer at the ocean\'s surface and strong vertical temperature gradients (a diurnal <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">thermocline</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-space_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space-2">[2]</a></sup> Sea surface temperature measurements are confined to the top portion of the ocean, known as the near-surface layer.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermometers">Thermometers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Thermometers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>SST was one of the first oceanographic variables to be measured. <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> suspended a <a href="/wiki/Mercury_thermometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury thermometer">mercury thermometer</a> from a ship while travelling between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in his survey of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_stream" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf stream">Gulf stream</a> in the late eighteenth century. SST was later measured by dipping a <a href="/wiki/Thermometer" title="Thermometer">thermometer</a> into a bucket of water that was manually drawn from the sea surface. The first automated technique for determining SST was accomplished by measuring the temperature of water in the intake port of large ships, which was underway by 1963. These observations have a warm bias of around 0.6&#160;\xc2\xb0C (1&#160;\xc2\xb0F) due to the heat of the engine room.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> This bias has led to changes in the perception of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> since 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Fixed <a href="/wiki/Weather_buoy" title="Weather buoy">weather buoys</a> measure the water temperature at a depth of 3 metres (9.8&#160;ft). Measurements of SST have had inconsistencies over the last 130 years due to the way they were taken. In the nineteenth century, measurements were taken in a bucket off of a ship. However, there was a slight variation in temperature because of the differences in buckets. Samples were collected in either a wood or an uninsulated canvas bucket, but the canvas bucket cooled quicker than the wood bucket. The sudden change in temperature between 1940 and 1941 was the result of an undocumented change in procedure. The samples were taken near the engine intake because it was too dangerous to use lights to take measurements over the side of the ship at night.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Many different drifting buoys exist around the world that vary in design, and the location of reliable temperature sensors varies. These measurements are beamed to satellites for automated and immediate data distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-buoy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-buoy-7">[7]</a></sup> A large network of coastal buoys in U.S. waters is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/National_Data_Buoy_Center" title="National Data Buoy Center">National Data Buoy Center</a> (NDBC).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Between 1985 and 1994, an extensive array of moored and drifting buoys was deployed across the equatorial Pacific Ocean designed to help monitor and predict the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation#Effects_of_ENSO.27s_warm_phase_.28El_Ni.C3.B1o.29" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> phenomenon.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weather_satellites">Weather satellites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Weather satellites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">Weather satellite</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MODIS_sst.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/MODIS_sst.png/220px-MODIS_sst.png" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/MODIS_sst.png/330px-MODIS_sst.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/MODIS_sst.png/440px-MODIS_sst.png 2x" data-file-width="2205" data-file-height="1229" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MODIS_sst.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n2003\xe2\x80\x932011 SST based on <a href="/wiki/Moderate-Resolution_Imaging_Spectroradiometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Moderate-Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer">MODIS</a> Aqua data.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Weather satellites have been available to determine sea surface temperature information since 1967, with the first global composites created during 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Since 1982,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> have been increasingly utilized to measure SST and have allowed its <a href="/wiki/Spatial_variability" title="Spatial variability">spatial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">temporal</a> variation to be viewed more fully. Satellite measurements of SST are in reasonable agreement with <a href="/wiki/In_situ" title="In situ">in situ</a> temperature measurements.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The satellite measurement is made by sensing the ocean <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a> in two or more wavelengths within the <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">electromagnetic spectrum</a> or other parts of the spectrum which can then be empirically related to SST.<sup id="cite_ref-John_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John-13">[13]</a></sup> These wavelengths are chosen because they are:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>within the peak of the <a href="/wiki/Blackbody_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackbody radiation">blackbody radiation</a> expected from the Earth,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and</li>\n<li>able to transmit adequately well through the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n</ol>\n<p>The satellite-measured SST provides both a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic view</a> of the ocean and a high frequency of repeat views,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> allowing the examination of basin-wide upper <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> dynamics not possible with ships or buoys. <span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nasa.gov/">NASA\'s</a></span> (National Aeronautic and Space Administration) <span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://modis.gsfc.nasa.gov/">Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS)</a></span> SST satellites have been providing global SST data since 2000, available with a one-day lag. NOAA\'s <span class="plainlinks"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.goes.noaa.gov/">GOES (Geostationary Orbiting Earth Satellites)</a></span> satellites are <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geo-stationary</a> above the Western Hemisphere which enables to them to deliver SST data on an hourly basis with only a few hours of lag time.</p>\n<p>There are several difficulties with satellite-based absolute SST measurements. First, in infrared remote sensing methodology the radiation emanates from the <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_microlayer" title="Sea surface microlayer">top "skin" of the ocean</a>, approximately the top 0.01 <a href="/wiki/Millimetre" title="Millimetre">mm</a> or less, which may not represent the <a href="/wiki/Bulk_temperature" title="Bulk temperature">bulk temperature</a> of the upper meter of ocean due primarily to effects of solar surface heating during the daytime, reflected radiation, as well as sensible heat loss and surface evaporation. All these factors make it somewhat difficult to compare satellite data to measurements from buoys or shipboard methods, complicating ground truth efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Secondly, the satellite cannot look through clouds, creating a cool bias in satellite-derived SSTs within cloudy areas.<sup id="cite_ref-space_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space-2">[2]</a></sup> However, passive microwave techniques can accurately measure SST and penetrate cloud cover.<sup id="cite_ref-John_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John-13">[13]</a></sup> Within atmospheric sounder channels on <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a>, which peak just above the ocean\'s surface, knowledge of the sea surface temperature is important to their calibration.<sup id="cite_ref-space_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_variation">Local variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Local variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Upwelling" title="Upwelling">Upwelling</a></div>\n<p>The SST has a <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnal range</a>, just like the Earth\'s atmosphere above, though to a lesser degree due to its greater specific heat.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> On calm days, the temperature can vary by 6&#160;\xc2\xb0C (10&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-space_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space-2">[2]</a></sup> The temperature of the ocean at depth lags the Earth\'s atmosphere temperature by 15 days per 10 metres (33&#160;ft), which means for locations like the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, temperatures near its bottom reach a maximum in December and a minimum in May and June.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Near the coastline, offshore winds move the warm waters near the surface offshore, and replace them with cooler water from below in the process known as <a href="/wiki/Ekman_transport" title="Ekman transport">Ekman transport</a>. This pattern increases nutrients for marine life in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Offshore <a href="/wiki/River_delta" title="River delta">river deltas</a>, freshwater flows over the top of the denser seawater, which allows it to heat faster due to limited vertical mixing.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Remotely sensed SST can be used to detect the surface temperature signature due to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. In general, an SST cooling is observed after the passing of a hurricane primarily as the result of mixed layer deepening and surface heat losses.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_Cooling_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Cooling-22">[22]</a></sup> In the wake of several day long <a href="/wiki/Mineral_dust#Saharan_dust" title="Mineral dust">Saharan dust</a> outbreaks across the adjacent northern Atlantic Ocean, sea surface temperatures are reduced 0.2 C to 0.4&#160;C (0.3 to 0.7&#160;F).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Other sources of short-term SST fluctuation include <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, rapid influxes of <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glacial</a> fresh water<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and concentrated <a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">phytoplankton</a> blooms<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> due to seasonal cycles or agricultural run-off.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a> (AMO) is important for how external forcings are linked with North Atlantic SSTs.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variation">Regional variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regional variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/200px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/300px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg/400px-1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2550" data-file-height="2548" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1997_El_Nino_TOPEX.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe 1997 El Ni\xc3\xb1o observed by <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">TOPEX/Poseidon</a>. The white areas off the tropical coasts of South and North America indicate the pool of warm water.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation</a></div>\n<p>El Ni\xc3\xb1o is defined by prolonged differences in Pacific Ocean surface temperatures when compared with the average value. The accepted definition is a warming or cooling of at least 0.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (0.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F) averaged over the east-central tropical Pacific Ocean. Typically, this anomaly happens at irregular intervals of 2\xe2\x80\x937 years and lasts nine months to two years.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The average period length is 5 years. When this warming or cooling occurs for only seven to nine months, it is classified as El Ni\xc3\xb1o/La Ni\xc3\xb1a "conditions"; when it occurs for more than that period, it is classified as El Ni\xc3\xb1o/La Ni\xc3\xb1a "episodes".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The sign of an El Ni\xc3\xb1o in the sea surface temperature pattern is when warm water spreads from the west Pacific and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the east Pacific. It takes the rain with it, causing extensive drought in the western Pacific and rainfall in the normally dry eastern Pacific. El Ni\xc3\xb1o\'s warm rush of nutrient-poor tropical water, heated by its eastward passage in the Equatorial Current, replaces the cold, nutrient-rich surface water of the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a>. When El Ni\xc3\xb1o conditions last for many months, extensive ocean warming and the reduction in Easterly Trade winds limits upwelling of cold nutrient-rich deep water and its economic impact to local fishing for an international market can be serious.<sup id="cite_ref-deadfish_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deadfish-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_surface_temperature&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Importance to the Earth\'s atmosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Snow_Clouds_in_Korea.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" oceans</a></div>\n<p>Sea surface temperature affects the behavior of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">Earth\'s atmosphere</a> above, so their initialization into <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">atmospheric models</a> is important. While sea surface temperature is important for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>, it is also important in determining the formation of sea fog and sea breezes.<sup id="cite_ref-space_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-space-2">[2]</a></sup> Heat from underlying warmer waters can significantly modify an air mass over distances as short as 35 kilometres (22&#160;mi) to 40 kilometres (25&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> For example, southwest of Northern Hemisphere <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, curved cyclonic flow bringing cold air across relatively warm water bodies can lead to narrow <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake-effect snow</a> (or sea effect) bands. Those bands bring strong localized <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, since large water bodies such as lakes efficiently store heat that results in significant temperature differences\xe2\x80\x94larger than 13&#160;\xc2\xb0C (23&#160;\xc2\xb0F)\xe2\x80\x94between the water surface and the air above.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Because of this temperature difference, warmth and moisture are transported upward, condensing into vertically oriented clouds which produce snow showers. The temperature decrease with height and cloud depth are directly affected by both the water temperature and the large-scale environment. The stronger the temperature decrease with height, the taller the clouds get, and the greater the precipitation rate becomes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a peaks of tropical cyclone activity worldwide</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif" class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mean_sst_equatorial_pacific.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAverage equatorial Pacific temperatures</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical 26.5<a href="/wiki/Degrees_Celsius" class="mw-redirect" title="Degrees Celsius">\xc2\xb0C</a> (79.7<a href="/wiki/Degrees_Fahrenheit" class="mw-redirect" title="Degrees Fahrenheit">\xc2\xb0F</a>) spanning through at minimum a 50-<a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a> depth is one of the precursors needed to maintain a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> (a type of <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-A15_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A15-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SST_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SST-36">[36]</a></sup> These warm waters are needed to maintain the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Mechanics" title="Tropical cyclone">warm core</a> that fuels tropical systems. This value is well above 16.1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (60.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F), the long term global average surface temperature of the oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-SSTMEAN_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SSTMEAN-37">[37]</a></sup> However, this requirement can be considered only a general baseline because it assumes that the ambient atmospheric environment surrounding an area of disturbed weather presents average conditions. Tropical cyclones have intensified when SSTs were slightly below this standard temperature.</p>\n<p>Tropical cyclones are known to form even when normal conditions are not met. For example, cooler air temperatures at a higher altitude (e.g., at the 500&#160;<a href="/wiki/HPa" class="mw-redirect" title="HPa">hPa</a> level, or 5.9&#160;km) can lead to tropical cyclogenesis at lower water temperatures, as a certain <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a> is required to force the atmosphere to be <a href="/wiki/Baroclinic_instability" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroclinic instability">unstable</a> enough for convection. In a moist atmosphere, this lapse rate is 6.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C/km, while in an atmosphere with less than 100% <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a>, the required lapse rate is 9.8&#160;\xc2\xb0C/km.<sup id="cite_ref-TCS_EESC_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCS_EESC-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At the 500&#160;hPa level, the air temperature averages \xe2\x88\x927&#160;\xc2\xb0C (18&#160;\xc2\xb0F) within the tropics, but air in the tropics is normally dry at this height, giving the air room to <a href="/wiki/Wet-bulb_temperature" title="Wet-bulb temperature">wet-bulb</a>, or cool as it moistens, to a more favorable temperature that can then support convection. A wetbulb temperature at 500&#160;hPa in a tropical atmosphere of \xe2\x88\x9213.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (8.2&#160;\xc2\xb0F) is required to initiate convection if the water temperature is 26.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (79.7&#160;\xc2\xb0F), and this temperature requirement increases or decreases proportionally by 1&#160;\xc2\xb0C in the sea surface temperature for each 1&#160;\xc2\xb0C change at 500&#160;hpa. Inside a <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold cyclone</a>, 500&#160;hPa temperatures can fall as low as \xe2\x88\x9230&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9222&#160;\xc2\xb0F), which can initiate convection even in the driest atmospheres. This also explains why moisture in the mid-levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, roughly at the 500&#160;hPa level, is normally a requirement for development. However, when dry air is found at the same height, temperatures at 500&#160;hPa need to be even colder as dry atmospheres require a greater lapse rate for instability than moist atmospheres.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LANDSEACLI_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LANDSEACLI-40">[40]</a></sup> At heights near the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, the 30-year average temperature (as measured in the period encompassing 1961 through 1990) was \xe2\x88\x9277&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x92132&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-UAIRtropics_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UAIRtropics-41">[41]</a></sup> A recent example of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that maintained itself over cooler waters was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Epsilon_(2005)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Epsilon (2005)">Epsilon</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a
