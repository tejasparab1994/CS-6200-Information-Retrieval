class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSignificant track errors still occur on occasion, as seen in this <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ernesto_(2006)" title="Hurricane Ernesto (2006)">Ernesto (2006)</a> early forecast. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">NHC</a> official forecast is light blue, while the storm\'s actual track is the white line over <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>tropical cyclone forecast model</b> is a computer program that uses <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> data to <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecast</a> aspects of the future state of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. There are three types of models: statistical, dynamical, or combined statistical-dynamic.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup> Dynamical models utilize powerful <a href="/wiki/Supercomputer" title="Supercomputer">supercomputers</a> with sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">mathematical modeling</a> software and meteorological data to <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">calculate future weather conditions</a>. <a href="/wiki/Statistical_model" title="Statistical model">Statistical models</a> forecast the evolution of a tropical cyclone in a simpler manner, by extrapolating from historical datasets, and thus can be run quickly on platforms such as <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a>. Statistical-dynamical models use aspects of both types of forecasting. Four primary types of forecasts exist for tropical cyclones: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">track</a>, intensity, <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_climatology" title="Tropical cyclone rainfall climatology">rainfall</a>. Dynamical models were not developed until the 1970s and the 1980s, with earlier efforts focused on the storm surge problem.</p>\n<p>Track models did not show <a href="/wiki/Forecast_skill" title="Forecast skill">forecast skill</a> when compared to statistical models until the 1980s. Statistical-dynamical models were used from the 1970s into the 1990s. Early models use data from previous model runs while late models produce output after the official hurricane forecast has been sent. The use of consensus, ensemble, and superensemble forecasts lowers errors more than any individual forecast model. Both consensus and superensemble forecasts can use the guidance of global and regional models runs to improve the performance more than any of their respective components. Techniques used at the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> indicate that superensemble forecasts are a very powerful tool for track forecasting.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Statistical_guidance"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Statistical guidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabel2003rcliper.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Isabel2003rcliper.jpg/220px-Isabel2003rcliper.jpg" width="220" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Isabel2003rcliper.jpg/330px-Isabel2003rcliper.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Isabel2003rcliper.jpg/440px-Isabel2003rcliper.jpg 2x" data-file-width="452" data-file-height="396" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabel2003rcliper.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nr-CLIPER for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a> (2003)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The first statistical guidance used by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> was the Hurricane Analog Technique (HURRAN), which was available in 1969. It used the newly developed <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">North Atlantic tropical cyclone database</a> to find storms with similar tracks. It then shifted their tracks through the storm\'s current path, and used location, direction and speed of motion, and the date to find suitable analogs. The method did well with storms south of the <a href="/wiki/25th_parallel_north" title="25th parallel north">25th parallel</a> which had not yet turned northward, but poorly with systems near or after recurvature.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup> Since 1972, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Climatology_and_Persistence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Climatology and Persistence (page does not exist)">Climatology and Persistence</a> (CLIPER) statistical model has been used to help generate tropical cyclone track forecasts. In the era of skillful dynamical forecasts, CLIPER is now being used as the baseline to show model and forecaster skill.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The Statistical Hurricane Intensity Forecast (SHIFOR) has been used since 1979 for tropical cyclone intensity forecasting. It uses climatology and persistence to predict future intensity, including the current <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">Julian day</a>, current cyclone intensity, the cyclone\'s intensity 12&#160;hours ago, the storm\'s initial latitude and longitude, as well as its zonal (east-west) and meridional (north-south) components of motion.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A series of statistical-dynamical models, which used regression equations based upon CLIPER output and the latest output from <a href="/wiki/Primitive_equations" title="Primitive equations">primitive equation</a> models run at the National Meteorological Center, then <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Prediction" title="National Centers for Environmental Prediction">National Centers for Environmental Prediction</a>, were developed between the 1970s and 1990s and were named NHC73, NHC83, NHC90, NHC91, and NHC98.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-4">[4]</a></sup> Within the field of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">tropical cyclone track forecasting</a>, despite the ever-improving dynamical model guidance which occurred with increased computational power, it was not until the decade of the 1980s when <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> showed <a href="/wiki/Forecast_skill" title="Forecast skill">skill</a>, and until the 1990s when it consistently outperformed statistical or simple dynamical models.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In 1994, a version of SHIFOR was created for the northwest Pacific Ocean for <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> forecasting, known as the Statistical Typhoon Intensity Forecast (STIFOR), which used the 1971\xe2\x80\x931990 data for that region to develop intensity forecasts out to 72&#160;hours into the future.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In regards to intensity forecasting, the Statistical Hurricane Intensity Prediction Scheme (SHIPS) utilizes relationships between environmental conditions from the <a href="/wiki/Global_Forecast_System" title="Global Forecast System">Global Forecast System</a> (GFS) such as vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>, climatology, and persistence (storm behavior) via multiple regression techniques to come up with an intensity forecast for systems in the northern Atlantic and northeastern Pacific oceans.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup> A similar model was developed for the northwest Pacific Ocean and Southern Hemisphere known as the Statistical Intensity Prediction System (STIPS), which accounts for land interactions through the input environmental conditions from the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Operational_Global_Prediction_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy Operational Global Prediction System">Navy Operational Global Prediction System</a> (NOGAPS) model.<sup id="cite_ref-STIPS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STIPS-7">[7]</a></sup> The version of SHIPS with an inland decay component is known as Decay SHIPS (DSHIPS). The Logistic Growth Equation Model (LGEM) uses the same input as SHIPS but within a simplified dynamical prediction system.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup> Within <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_rainfall_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting">tropical cyclone rainfall forecasting</a>, the Rainfall Climatology and Persistence (r-CLIPER) model was developed using microwave rainfall data from polar orbiting satellites over the ocean and first-order rainfall measurements from the land, to come up with a realistic rainfall distribution for tropical cyclones based on the National Hurricane Center\'s track forecast. It has been operational since 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A statistical-parametric wind radii model has been developed for use at the National Hurricane Center and Joint Typhoon Warning Center which uses climatology and persistence to predict wind structure out to five days into the future.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamical_guidance">Dynamical guidance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dynamical guidance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sloshrun.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Sloshrun.gif/220px-Sloshrun.gif" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e8/Sloshrun.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="328" data-file-height="312" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sloshrun.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nExample of a SLOSH run</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_numerical_weather_prediction" title="History of numerical weather prediction">History of numerical weather prediction</a></div>\n<p>During 1972, the first model to forecast storm surge along the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a> of the United States was developed, known as the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Special_Program_to_List_the_Amplitude_of_Surges_from_Hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Special Program to List the Amplitude of Surges from Hurricanes (page does not exist)">Special Program to List the Amplitude of Surges from Hurricanes</a> (SPLASH).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1978, the first hurricane-tracking model based on <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dynamics#Dynamic_meteorology" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric dynamics">atmospheric dynamics</a> \xe2\x80\x93 the movable fine-mesh (MFM) model \xe2\x80\x93 began operating.<sup id="cite_ref-Shuman_W.26F_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shuman_W.26F-10">[10]</a></sup> The Quasi-Lagrangian Limited Area (QLM) model is a multi-level primitive equation model using a <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">Cartesian</a> grid and the <a href="/wiki/Global_Forecast_System" title="Global Forecast System">Global Forecast System</a> (GFS) for boundary conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup> In the early 1980s, the assimilation of satellite-derived winds from water vapor, infrared, and visible satellite imagery was found to improve tropical cyclones track forecasting.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_Fluid_Dynamics_Laboratory" title="Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory">Geophysical Fluid Dynamics Laboratory</a> (GFDL) hurricane model was used for research purposes between 1973 and the mid-1980s. Once it was determined that it could show skill in hurricane prediction, a multi-year transition transformed the research model into an operational model which could be used by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> for both track and intensity forecasting in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 1985, the Sea Lake and Overland Surges from Hurricanes (SLOSH) Model had been developed for use in areas of the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and near the United States\' East coast, which was more robust than the SPLASH model.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beta_Advection_Model&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beta Advection Model (page does not exist)">Beta Advection Model</a> (BAM) has been used operationally since 1987 using steering winds averaged through the 850 hPa to 200 hPa layer and the Beta effect which causes a storm to drift northwest due to differences in the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolis effect">coriolis effect</a> across the tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The larger the cyclone, the larger the impact of the beta effect is likely to be.<sup id="cite_ref-NAVY_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY-15">[15]</a></sup> Starting in 1990, three versions of the BAM were run operationally: the BAM shallow (BAMS) average winds in an 850 hPa to 700 hPa layer, the BAM Medium (BAMM) which uses average winds in an 850 hPa to 400 hPa layer, and the BAM Deep (BAMD) which is the same as the pre-1990 BAM.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-4">[4]</a></sup> For a weak hurricane without well-developed central thunderstorm activity, BAMS works well, because weak storms tend to be steered by low-level winds.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup> As the storm grows stronger and associated thunderstorm activity near its center gets deeper, BAMM and BAMD become more accurate, as these types of storms are steered more by the winds in the upper-level. If the forecast from the three versions is similar, then the forecaster can conclude that there is minimal uncertainty, but if the versions vary by a great deal, then the forecaster has less confidence in the track predicted due to the greater uncertainty.<sup id="cite_ref-ensbook_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ensbook-16">[16]</a></sup> Large differences between model predictions can also indicate wind shear in the atmosphere, which could affect the intensity forecast as well.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tested in 1989 and 1990, The Vic Ooyama Barotropic (VICBAR) model used a <a href="/wiki/Spline_(mathematics)" title="Spline (mathematics)">cubic-B spline</a> representation of variables for the objective analysis of observations and solutions to the shallow-water prediction equations on nested domains, with the boundary conditions defined as the global forecast model.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> It was implemented operationally as the Limited Area Sine Transform Barotropic (LBAR) model in 1992, using the GFS for boundary conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup> By 1990, Australia had developed its own storm surge model which was able to be run in a few minutes on a personal computer.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> (JMA) developed its own Typhoon Model (TYM) in 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and in 1998, the agency began using its own dynamic <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> model.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Irene13.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Irene13.gif/220px-Irene13.gif" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Irene13.gif/330px-Irene13.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Irene13.gif/440px-Irene13.gif 2x" data-file-width="895" data-file-height="716" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Irene13.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA NOAA prediction for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2011)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Irene (2011)">Hurricane Irene</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Weather_Research_and_Forecasting_model" title="Hurricane Weather Research and Forecasting model">Hurricane Weather Research and Forecasting</a> (HWRF) model is a specialized version of the <a href="/wiki/Weather_Research_and_Forecasting_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Weather Research and Forecasting model">Weather Research and Forecasting</a> (WRF) model and is used to <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecast</a> the track and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">intensity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. The model was developed by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA), the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="United States Naval Research Laboratory">U.S. Naval Research Laboratory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Rhode_Island" title="University of Rhode Island">University of Rhode Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_University" title="Florida State University">Florida State University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> It became operational in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA_Magazine_Article_2885_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_Magazine_Article_2885-22">[22]</a></sup> Despite improvements in track forecasting, predictions of the intensity of a tropical cyclone based on numerical weather prediction continue to be a challenge, since statiscal methods continue to show higher skill over dynamical guidance.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Other than the specialized guidance, global guidance such as the GFS, <a href="/wiki/Unified_Model" title="Unified Model">Unified Model</a> (UKMET), NOGAPS, Japanese Global Spectral Model (GSM), <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts</a> model, France\'s Action de Recherche Petite Echelle Grande Echelle (ARPEGE) and Aire Limit\xc2\xb4ee Adaptation Dynamique Initialisation (ALADIN) models, India\'s <a href="/wiki/National_Centre_for_Medium_Range_Weather_Forecasting" title="National Centre for Medium Range Weather Forecasting">National Centre for Medium Range Weather Forecasting</a> (NCMWRF) model, Korea\'s Global Data Assimilation and Prediction System (GDAPS) and Regional Data Assimilation and Prediction System (RDAPS) models, Hong Kong/China\'s Operational Regional Spectral Model (ORSM) model, and Canadian <a href="/wiki/Global_Environmental_Multiscale_Model" title="Global Environmental Multiscale Model">Global Environmental Multiscale Model</a> (GEM) model are used for track and intensity purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-models_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-models-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timeliness">Timeliness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Timeliness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Some models do not produce output quickly enough to be used for the forecast cycle immediately after the model starts running (including HWRF, GFDL, and FSSE). Most of the above track models (except CLIPER) require data from <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">global weather models</a>, such as the GFS, which produce output about four hours after the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Synoptic_time&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Synoptic time (page does not exist)">synoptic times</a> of 0000, 0600, 1200, and 1800 Universal Coordinated Time (UTC). For half of their forecasts, the NHC issues forecasts only three hours after that time, so some "early" models \xe2\x80\x94 NHC90, BAM, and LBAR \xe2\x80\x94 are run using a 12-hour-old forecast for the current time. "Late" models, such as the GFS and GFDL, finish after the advisory has already been issued. These models are <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">interpolated</a> to the current storm position for use in the following forecast cycle \xe2\x80\x94 for example, GFDI, the interpolated version of the GFDL model.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2005_Verification_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2005_Verification-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consensus_methods">Consensus methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consensus methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WRF_rita_spread2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/WRF_rita_spread2.jpg/260px-WRF_rita_spread2.jpg" width="260" height="421" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d5/WRF_rita_spread2.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="311" data-file-height="504" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WRF_rita_spread2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<i>Top</i>: WRF model simulation of Hurricane Rita tracks. <i>Bottom</i>: The spread of NHC multi-model ensemble forecast.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Using a consensus of forecast models reduces forecast error.<sup id="cite_ref-TBK_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-25">[25]</a></sup> Trackwise, the GUNA model is a consensus of the interpolated versions of the GFDL, UKMET with quality control applied to the cyclone tracker, United States Navy NOGAPS, and GFS models. The version of the GUNA corrected for model biases is known as the CGUN. The TCON consensus is the GUNA consensus plus the Hurricane WRF model. The version of the TCON corrected for model biases is known as the TCCN. A lagged average of the last two runs of the members within the TCON plus the ECMWF model is known as the TVCN consensus. The version of the TVCN corrected for model biases is the TVCC consensus.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In early 2013, The <a href="/wiki/NAVGEM" class="mw-redirect" title="NAVGEM">NAVGEM</a> replaced the NOGAPS as the Navy\'s primary operational global forecast model. For the 2013 season, and until model verification can occur, it is not being utilized in the development of any consensus forecasts.</p>\n<p>For intensity, a combination of the LGEM, interpolated GFDL, interpolated HWRF, and DSHIPS models is known as the ICON consensus. The lagged average of the last two runs of models within the ICON consensus is called the IVCN consensus.<sup id="cite_ref-NHCmodel_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHCmodel-1">[1]</a></sup> Across the northwest Pacific and Southern Hemisphere, a ten-member STIPS consensus is formed from the output of the NOGAPS, GFS, the Japanese GSM, the Coupled Ocean/Atmosphere Mesoscale Prediction System (COAMPS), the UKMET, the Japanese TYM, the GFDL with NOGAPS boundary conditions, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Weather_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Air Force Weather Agency">Air Force Weather Agency</a> (AFWA) Model, the Australian Tropical Cyclone Local Area Prediction System, and the Weber Barotropic Model.<sup id="cite_ref-STIPS_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STIPS-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ensemble_methods">Ensemble methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ensemble methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>No model is ever perfectly accurate because it is impossible to learn exactly everything about the atmosphere in a timely enough manner, and atmospheric measurements that are taken are not completely accurate.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The use of the ensemble method of forecasting, whether it be a multi-model ensemble, or numerous ensemble members based on the global model, helps define the uncertainty and further limit errors.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The JMA has produced an 11-member ensemble forecast system for typhoons known as the Typhoon Ensemble Prediction System (TEPS) since February 2008, which is run out to 132&#160;hours into the future. It uses a lower resolution version (with larger grid spacing) of its GSM, with ten perturbed members and one non-perturbed member. The system reduces errors by an average of 40 kilometres (25&#160;mi) five days into the future when compared to its higher resolution GSM.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Florida State Super Ensemble (FSSE) is produced from a suite of models which then uses statistical regression equations developed over a training phase to reduce their biases, which produces forecasts better than the member models or their mean solution. It uses 11&#160;global models, including five developed at <a href="/wiki/Florida_State_University" title="Florida State University">Florida State University</a>, the Unified Model, the GFS, the NOGAPS, the United States Navy NOGAPS, the Australian Bureau of Meteorology Research Centre (BMRC) model, and Canadian <a href="/wiki/Recherche_en_Pr%C3%A9vision_Num%C3%A9rique" title="Recherche en Pr\xc3\xa9vision Num\xc3\xa9rique">Recherche en Pr\xc3\xa9vision Num\xc3\xa9rique</a> (RPN) model. It shows significant skill in track, intensity, and rainfall predictions of tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Systematic Approach Forecast Aid (SAFA) was developed by the Joint Typhoon Warning Center to create a selective consensus forecast which removed more erroneous forecasts at a 72\xe2\x80\x91hour time frame from consideration using the United States Navy NOGAPS model, the GFDL, the Japan Meteorological Agency\'s global and typhoon models, as well as the UKMET. All the models improved during SAFA\'s five-year history and removing erroneous forecasts proved difficult to do in operations.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sunspot_theory">Sunspot theory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sunspot theory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A 2010 report correlates low <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">sunspot</a> activity with high <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">hurricane</a> activity. Analyzing historical data, there was a 25% chance of at least one hurricane striking the continental United States during a peak sunspot year; a 64% chance during a low sunspot year. In June 2010, the hurricanes predictors in the US were not using this information.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_forecast_model_accuracy">Hurricane forecast model accuracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hurricane forecast model accuracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The accuracy of hurricane forecast models can vary significantly from storm to storm. For some storms the factors affecting the hurricane track are relatively straightforward, and the models are not only accurate but they produce similar forecasts, while for other storms the factors affecting the hurricane track are more complex and different models produce very different forecasts.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<div
