href="/wiki/Meteorological_phenomenon" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorological phenomenon">phenomenon</a> defined by the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>\'s glossary as "a large-scale circulation of winds around a central region of high atmospheric pressure, clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere, counterclockwise in the Southern Hemisphere".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Effects of surface-based anticyclones include clearing skies as well as cooler, drier air. Fog can also form overnight within a region of higher pressure. Mid-tropospheric systems, such as the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>, deflect tropical cyclones around their periphery and cause a <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">temperature inversion</a> inhibiting <a href="/wiki/Free_convection" class="mw-redirect" title="Free convection">free convection</a> near their center, building up surface-based <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> under their base. Anticyclones aloft can form within warm core lows such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, due to descending cool air from the backside of upper troughs such as polar highs, or from large scale sinking such as the subtropical ridge.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Sir_Francis_Galton" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Francis Galton">Sir Francis Galton</a> first discovered anticyclones in the 1860s. Preferred areas within a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> flow pattern in higher levels of the hydrosphere are beneath the western side of troughs, or dips in the <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby wave</a> pattern.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2014)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> High-pressure systems are alternatively referred to as anticyclones. Their circulation is sometimes referred to as <a href="/wiki/Cum_sole" title="Cum sole">cum sole</a>. Subtropical high pressure zones form under the descending portion of the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> circulation. Upper-level high-pressure areas lie over tropical cyclones due to their warm core nature.</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Siberian_high" class="mw-redirect" title="Siberian high">Siberian high</a></div>\n<p>Surface anticyclones form due to downward motion through the troposphere, the atmospheric layer where weather occurs. Preferred areas within a <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a> flow pattern in higher levels of the troposphere are beneath the western side of troughs. On weather maps, these areas show converging winds (isotachs), also known as <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a>, or converging height lines near or above the level of non-divergence, which is near the 500 hPa pressure surface about midway up the troposphere.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Because they weaken with height, these high-pressure systems are cold.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtropical_ridge">Subtropical ridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subtropical ridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/250px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" width="250" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/375px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg/500px-Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg 2x" data-file-width="622" data-file-height="282" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Subtropicalridge2000091412.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe subtropical ridge shows up as a large area of black (dryness) on this water vapor satellite image from September 2000.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">Subtropical ridge</a></div>\n<p>Heating of the earth near the equator forces upward motion and convection along the <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a>. The divergence over the near-equatorial trough leads to air rising and moving away from the equator aloft. As air moves towards the mid-latitudes, it cools and sinks leading to subsidence near the 30\xc2\xb0 parallel of both hemispheres. This circulation known as the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> forms the subtropical ridge.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Many of the world\'s deserts are caused by these climatological <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure areas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Because these anticyclones strengthen with height, they are known as warm core ridges.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_aloft">Formation aloft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation aloft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The development of anticyclones aloft occurs in warm core cyclones such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> when latent heat caused by the formation of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a> is released aloft increasing the air temperature; the resultant thickness of the atmospheric layer increases high pressure aloft which evacuates their outflow.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strike">Strike</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Strike">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In the absence of rotation, the <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> tends to blow from areas of high pressure to <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">areas of low pressure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The stronger the pressure difference (pressure gradient) between a high-pressure system and a low-pressure system, the stronger the wind. The <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s rotation gives winds within high-pressure systems their clockwise circulation in the northern hemisphere (as the wind moves outward and is deflected right from the center of high pressure) and anticlockwise circulation in the southern hemisphere (as the wind moves outward and is deflected left from the center of high pressure). Friction with land slows down the wind flowing out of high-pressure systems and causes wind to flow more outward (more <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">ageostrophically</a>) from the center.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surface-based_systems">Surface-based systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surface-based systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg/250px-GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg" width="250" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg/375px-GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg/500px-GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1777" data-file-height="1120" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GGB_in_fog_2007_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Golden_Gate_Bridge" title="Golden Gate Bridge">Golden Gate Bridge</a> in fog</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>High-pressure systems are frequently associated with light winds at the surface and <a href="/wiki/Subsidence_(atmosphere)" title="Subsidence (atmosphere)">subsidence</a> of air from higher portions of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. Subsidence will generally warm an air mass by <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> (compressional) heating.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Thus, high pressure typically brings clear skies.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Because no clouds are present to reflect sunlight during the day, there is more incoming <a href="/wiki/Solar_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar radiation">solar radiation</a> and temperatures rise rapidly near the surface. At night, the absence of clouds means that <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave radiation">outgoing longwave radiation</a> (i.e. heat energy from the surface) is not blocked, giving cooler <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation" title="Diurnal temperature variation">diurnal low temperatures</a> in all seasons. When surface winds become light, the subsidence produced directly under a high-pressure system can lead to a buildup of particulates in urban areas under the high pressure, leading to widespread <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> If the surface level <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> rises towards 100 percent overnight, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> can form.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The movement of continental arctic air masses to lower latitudes produces strong but vertically shallow high-pressure systems.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The surface level, sharp temperature <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> can lead to areas of persistent <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratus cloud</a>, colloquially known as anticyclonic gloom. The type of weather brought about by an anticyclone depends on its origin. For example, extensions of the Azores high pressure may bring about anticyclonic gloom during the winter because they pick up moisture as they move over the warmer oceans. High pressures that build to the north and move southwards often bring clear weather because they are cooled at the base (as opposed to warmed) which helps prevent clouds from forming.</p>\n<p>Once arctic air moves over an unfrozen ocean, the air mass modifies greatly over the warmer water and takes on the character of a maritime air mass, which reduces the strength of the high-pressure system.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> When extremely cold air moves over relatively warm oceans, <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a> can develop.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, warm and moist (or maritime tropical) air masses which move poleward from tropical sources are slower to modify than arctic air masses.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-tropospheric_systems">Mid-tropospheric systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mid-tropospheric systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Subtropridgejulyna.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/250px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif" width="250" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/375px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Subtropridgejulyna.gif/500px-Subtropridgejulyna.gif 2x" data-file-width="625" data-file-height="445" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Subtropridgejulyna.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMean July subtropical ridge position in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The circulation around mid-level (altitude) ridges, and the air subsidence at their center, act to steer tropical cyclones around their periphery. Due to the subsidence within this type of system, a <i><a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">cap</a></i> can develop which inhibits free convection and hence mixing of the lower with the middle level troposphere. This limits thunderstorm activity near their centers and traps low-level pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> as <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> under their base, which is a significant problem in large urban centers during summer months such as <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles, California</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_tropospheric_systems">Upper tropospheric systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Upper tropospheric systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The existence of upper-level (altitude) high pressure allows upper level <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">divergence</a> which leads to surface <a href="/wiki/Convergence_zone" title="Convergence zone">convergence</a>. If a capping mid-level ridge does not exist, this leads to <a href="/wiki/Free_convection" class="mw-redirect" title="Free convection">free convection</a> and the development of showers and thunderstorms if the lower atmosphere is humid. Because a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> loop develops between the convective tropical cyclone and the upper level high, the two system are strengthened. This loop stops once ocean temperatures cool to below 26.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (79.7&#160;\xc2\xb0F),<sup id="cite_ref-A15_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A15-16">[16]</a></sup> reducing the thunderstorm activity, which then weakens the upper level high pressure system.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance_to_global_monsoon_regimes">Importance to global monsoon regimes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Importance to global monsoon regimes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a></div>\n<p>When the subtropical ridge in the Northwest Pacific is stronger than normal, it leads to a wet <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> season for <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The subtropical ridge position is linked to how far northward monsoon moisture and <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> extend into the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Typically, the subtropical ridge across <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> migrates far enough northward to begin monsoon conditions across the <a href="/wiki/Desert_Southwest" class="mw-redirect" title="Desert Southwest">Desert Southwest</a> from July to September.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> When the subtropical ridge is farther north than normal towards the <a href="/wiki/Four_Corners" title="Four Corners">Four Corners</a>, monsoon thunderstorms can spread northward into <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>. When suppressed to the south, the atmosphere dries out across the Desert Southwest, causing a break in the monsoon regime.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depiction_on_weather_maps">Depiction on weather maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Depiction on weather maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Surface_analysis.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Surface_analysis.gif/250px-Surface_analysis.gif" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Surface_analysis.gif/375px-Surface_analysis.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Surface_analysis.gif/500px-Surface_analysis.gif 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Surface_analysis.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA surface weather analysis for the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> on October 21, 2006</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">Weather map</a></div>\n<p>On weather maps, high-pressure centers are associated with the letter H in English,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> within the <a href="/wiki/Isobar_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isobar (meteorology)">isobar</a> with the highest pressure value. On constant-pressure upper-level charts, anticyclones are located within the highest height line contour.<sup id="cite_ref-AMS_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_versions">Extraterrestrial versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anticyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On Jupiter, there are two examples of an extraterrestrial anticyclonic storm; the <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> and the recently formed <a href="/wiki/Oval_BA" class="mw-redirect" title="Oval BA">Oval BA</a>. They are powered by smaller storms merging<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> unlike any typical anticyclonic storm that happens on Earth where water powers them. Another theory is that warmer gases rise in a column of cold air, creating a vortex as is the case of other storms that include <a href="/wiki/Anne%27s_Spot" title="Anne\'s Spot">Anne\'s Spot</a> on <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot" title="Great Dark Spot">Great Dark Spot</a> on Neptune. Anticyclones have been detected near the poles of Venus.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
