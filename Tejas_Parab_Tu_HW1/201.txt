href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force</a> command located in <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Harbor" title="Pearl Harbor">Pearl Harbor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>. The JTWC is responsible for the issuing of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> warnings in the North West Pacific Ocean, South Pacific Ocean and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> for all branches of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> and other U.S. government agencies. Their warnings are intended for the protection of primarily military ships and aircraft as well as military installations jointly operated with other countries around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-MISSION_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MISSION-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Cobra,_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/250px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" width="250" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/375px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/500px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="707" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Cobra,_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRadar image of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Cobra" title="Typhoon Cobra">Typhoon Cobra</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The origins of the Joint Typhoon Warning Center <small>(JTWC)</small> can be traced back to June 1945, when the Fleet Weather Center/Typhoon Tracking Center was established on the island of <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, after multiple typhoons, including Typhoon Cobra of December 1944 and June 1945, had caused a significant loss of men and ships.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_50th_Prog_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_50th_Prog-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form-4">[4]</a></sup> At this time the centre was one of three Navy and two Air Force units responsible for tropical cyclone reconnaissance and warnings in the Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_50th_Prog_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_50th_Prog-3">[3]</a></sup> Over the next few years the coordination of tropical warnings between the centres was at times difficult or impossible due to various communication problems.<sup id="cite_ref-1959_ATCR_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1959_ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> During 1958, the United States <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> weather services and the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">Weather Bureau</a> formed the Joint Meteorology Committee to the Pacific Command and proposed the formation of a joint Navy and Air Force center for typhoon analysis and forecasting.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_50th_Prog_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_50th_Prog-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form2-6">[6]</a></sup> A committee was subsequently set up to study the issue which issued a report during January 1959, which gave recommendation that the center be set up.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form2-6">[6]</a></sup> Based on the report and the conclusions reached at the March 1959 Annual Tropical Cyclone Conference, the Joint Meteorology Committee formally urged, The Commander in Chief, US Pacific Command (CINCPAC) to establish a Joint Typhoon Warning Center.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form2-6">[6]</a></sup> The CINCPAC subsequently petitioned the Joint Chiefs of Staff, who gave permission for the centre to be set up effective May 1, 1959, under the command of the Fleet Weather Center\'s commander.<sup id="cite_ref-1959_ATCR_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1959_ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form2_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form2-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The JTWC initially consisted of ten people with two officers and three enlisted personnel provided by each service.<sup id="cite_ref-1959_ATCR_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1959_ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> It was required to provide warnings on all tropical cyclones, between the Malay Peninsular and the International Dateline for US government agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-1959_ATCR_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1959_ATCR-5">[5]</a></sup> They also had to determine reconnaissance requirements, prepare annual typhoon summaries, and conduct research into tropical cyclone forecasting and detection.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_50th_Prog_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_50th_Prog-3">[3]</a></sup> In November 1962, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Karen" title="Typhoon Karen">Typhoon Karen</a> destroyed the building housing the Fleet Weather Center/Joint Typhoon Warning Center. It relocated in a more typhoon-proof building in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Between 1971 and 1976, CINCPAC gradually expanded out the JTWC\'s area of responsibility, to include the area between the International Dateline and the African coasts. In October 1978, the Fleet Weather Center/JTWC became the Navy Oceanographic Command Center/Joint Typhoon Warning Center and responsible for the whole oceanic environment, from the bottom of the ocean to the top of the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-reallignment_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reallignment-8">[8]</a></sup> The JTWC subsequently started issuing warnings for the Southern Hemisphere between the African coast and the International Dateline during October 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-reallignment_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reallignment-8">[8]</a></sup> It was relocated to Pearl Harbor on January 1, 1999 due to the <a href="/wiki/1995_Base_Realignment_and_Closure_Commission" title="1995 Base Realignment and Closure Commission">1995 Base Realignment and Closure Commission</a> round. During October 2011, the JTWC\'s name changed to from the \xe2\x80\x9cNaval Maritime Forecast Center/Joint Typhoon Warning Center\xe2\x80\x9d to just the Joint Typhoon Warning Center, as it became a stand-alone command for the first time in its 52-year history.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards_and_practices">Standards and practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Standards and practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Automated_Tropical_Cyclone_Forecasting_System" title="Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System">Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System</a></div>\n<p>A more modernized method for forecasting tropical cyclones had become apparent by the 1980s. Prior to the development of ATCF, the tools used by the Department of Defense to forecast tropical cyclone track were acetate, grease pencils, and disparate computer programs.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF1-10">[10]</a></sup> The ATCF software was developed by the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a> for the JTWC beginning in 1986,<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF2-11">[11]</a></sup> and used since 1988. It was adapted for use at the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>JTWC adheres to the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization\'s</a> (WMO) rules for storm names and adheres to acknowledged guidelines for intensity of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storms" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storms">tropical storms</a>, with the exception of using the U.S. standard of measuring sustained winds for 1-min instead of the 10-min span recommended by the WMO (see <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>). The JTWC is not one of the WMO designated <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centres</a>, nor one of its <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre#Tropical_cyclone_warning_centers" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Tropical cyclone warning centres</a>, as its main mission is to support the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> government agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Product_Notice_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Product_Notice-12">[12]</a></sup> JTWC monitors, analyzes, and forecasts tropical cyclone formation, development, and movement year round.<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ-13">[13]</a></sup> Its area of responsibility covers 89% of the world\'s tropical cyclone activity.<sup id="cite_ref-DoDnews_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoDnews-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Staffing">Staffing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Staffing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The Center is manned by about 37 <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> personnel.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCR2013_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR2013-15">[15]</a></sup> The JTWC uses several satellite systems and sensors, radar, surface and upper level synoptic data as well as atmospheric models to complete its mission.<sup id="cite_ref-MISSION_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MISSION-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
