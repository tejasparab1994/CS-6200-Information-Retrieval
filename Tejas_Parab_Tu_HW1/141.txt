src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Ana_aug_12_2009_1335Z.jpg/220px-Ana_aug_12_2009_1335Z.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ana_aug_12_2009_1335Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Ana_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Ana (2009)">Tropical Storm Ana (2009)</a> with its small CDO</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>central dense overcast</b>, or <b>CDO</b>, of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> or strong <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclone</a> is the large central area of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> surrounding its circulation center, caused by the formation of its eyewall. It can be round, angular, oval, or irregular in shape. This feature shows up in tropical cyclones of tropical storm or hurricane strength. How far the center is embedded within the CDO, and the temperature difference between the cloud tops within the CDO and the cyclone\'s eye, can help determine a tropical cyclone\'s intensity. Locating the center within the CDO can be a problem for strong tropical storms and with systems of minimal hurricane strength as its location can be obscured by the CDO\'s high cloud canopy. This center location problem can be resolved through the use of microwave satellite imagery.</p>\n<p>After a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> reaches hurricane intensity, an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> appears at the center of the CDO, defining its center of <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low pressure</a> and its cyclonic wind field. Tropical cyclones with changing intensity have more lightning within their CDO than <a href="/wiki/Steady_state" title="Steady state">steady state</a> storms. Tracking cloud features within the CDO, using frequently updated <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a>, can also be used to determine its intensity. The highest <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a> within a tropical cyclone, as well as its heaviest rainfall, are usually located under the coldest cloud tops in the CDO.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Characteristics"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_dense_overcast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/220px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/330px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png/440px-Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="600" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Winston_2016-02-12_1200Z.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSouthern hemisphere tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Winston" title="Cyclone Winston">cyclone Winston</a> with a large CDO surrounding its eye</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>It is a large region of thunderstorms surrounding the center of stronger tropical and subtropical cyclones which shows up brightly (with cold cloud tops) on <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CDO_AMS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDO_AMS-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_CDO_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_CDO-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The CDO forms due to the development of an eyewall within a tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Its shape can be round, oval, angular, or irregular.<sup id="cite_ref-Vern_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vern-5">[5]</a></sup> Its development can be preceded by a narrow, dense, C-shaped <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">convective band</a>. Early in its development, the CDO is often angular or oval in shape, which rounds out, increases in size, and appears more smooth as a tropical cyclone intensifies.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Rounder CDO shapes occur in environments with low levels of vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_CDO_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_CDO-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The strongest winds within <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> tend to be located under the deepest <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> within the CDO, which is seen on satellite imagery as the coldest cloud tops.<sup id="cite_ref-HSU_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSU-7">[7]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Radius_of_maximum_wind" title="Radius of maximum wind">radius of maximum wind</a> is usually collocated with the coldest cloud tops within the CDO,<sup id="cite_ref-HSU_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSU-7">[7]</a></sup> which is also the area where a tropical cyclone\'s rainfall reaches its maximum intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> For mature tropical cyclones that are steady state, the CDO contains nearly no <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> activity, though lightning is more common within weaker tropical cyclones and for systems fluctuating in intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eye">Eye</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_dense_overcast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Eye">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">Eye (cyclone)</a></div>\n<p>The eye is a region of mostly calm <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> at the center of the CDO of strong <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. The eye of a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> is a roughly circular area, typically 30\xe2\x80\x9365&#160;km (20\xe2\x80\x9340&#160;miles) in <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>. It is surrounded by the eyewall, a ring of towering <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> surrounding its center of circulation. The cyclone\'s lowest <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> occurs in the eye, and can be as much as 15% lower than the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> outside the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-10">[10]</a></sup> In weaker tropical cyclones, the eye is less well-defined, and can be covered by high cloudiness caused by <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus cloud</a> outflow from the surrounding central dense overcast.<sup id="cite_ref-FAQ_eye_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FAQ_eye-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_a_tropical_cyclone_strength_indicator">Use as a tropical cyclone strength indicator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_dense_overcast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Use as a tropical cyclone strength indicator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png/220px-Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png/330px-Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png 1.5x, class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dvorak1984DevelopmentalPatterns.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCommon developmental patterns seen during tropical cyclone development, and their Dvorak-assigned intensities</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a></div>\n<p>Within the Dvorak satellite strength estimate for tropical cyclones, there are several <a href="/wiki/Pattern_recognition" title="Pattern recognition">visual patterns</a> that a cyclone may take on which define the upper and lower bounds on its intensity. The central dense overcast (CDO) pattern is one of those patterns. The central dense overcast utilizes the size of the CDO. The CDO pattern intensities start at T2.5, equivalent to minimal tropical storm intensity, 40&#160;mph (64&#160;km/h). The shape of the central dense overcast is also considered. The farther the center is tucked into the CDO, the stronger it is deemed.<sup id="cite_ref-Vern_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vern-5">[5]</a></sup> Banding features can be utilized to objectively determine the tropical cyclone\'s center, using a ten degree <a href="/wiki/Logarithmic_spiral" title="Logarithmic spiral">logarithmic spiral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-evolution_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-evolution-11">[11]</a></sup> Using the 85\xe2\x80\x9392&#160;GHz channels of polar-orbiting microwave satellite imagery can definitively locate the center within the CDO.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tropical cyclones with maximum sustained winds between 65&#160;mph (105&#160;km/h) and 100&#160;mph (160&#160;km/h) can have their center of circulations obscured by cloudiness within visible and infrared satellite imagery, which makes diagnosis of their intensity a challenge.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> Winds within tropical cyclones can also be estimated by tracking features within the CDO using rapid scan <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite#Geostationary" title="Weather satellite">geostationary satellite imagery</a>, whose pictures are taken minutes apart rather than every half-hour.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_dense_overcast&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
