class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Teletype games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Text-based_game" title="Text-based game">Text-based game</a></div>\n<p>The earliest form of computer game to achieve any degree of mainstream use was the text-based <a href="/wiki/Teleprinter" title="Teleprinter">Teletype</a> game. Teletype games lack video display screens and instead present the game to the player by printing a series of characters on paper which the player reads as it emerges from the <a href="/wiki/Platen" title="Platen">platen</a>. Practically this means that each action taken will require a line of paper and thus a hard-copy record of the game remains after it has been played. This naturally tends to reduce the size of the gaming universe or alternatively to require a great amount of paper. As <a href="/wiki/Visual_display_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Visual display unit">computer screens</a> became standard during the rise of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_computing_hardware#Post-1960:_third_generation_and_beyond" title="History of computing hardware">third generation computer</a>, text-based <a href="/wiki/Command-line_interface" title="Command-line interface">command line</a>-driven language parsing Teletype games transitioned into visual <a href="/wiki/Interactive_fiction" title="Interactive fiction">interactive fiction</a> allowing for greater depth of gameplay and reduced paper requirements. This transition was accompanied by a simultaneous shift from the <a href="/wiki/Mainframe_computer" title="Mainframe computer">mainframe</a> environment to the <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a>.</p>\n<p>Examples of text-based Teletype games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Oregon_Trail_(video_game)" title="The Oregon Trail (video game)">The Oregon Trail</a></i> (1971) - the earliest versions</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Trek73" title="Trek73">Trek73</a></i> (1973)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dungeon_(video_game)" title="Dungeon (video game)">Dungeon</a></i> (1975)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Super_Star_Trek" class="mw-redirect" title="Super Star Trek">Super Star Trek</a></i> (1975)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Colossal_Cave_Adventure" title="Colossal Cave Adventure">Adventure</a></i> (1976) - the earliest versions</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Zork" title="Zork">Zork</a></i> (1977) - the earliest versions</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_handhelds">Electronic handhelds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Electronic handhelds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Handheld_electronic_game" title="Handheld electronic game">Handheld electronic game</a></div>\n<p>The earliest form of <a href="/wiki/Dedicated_console" title="Dedicated console">dedicated console</a>, handheld electronic games are characterized by their size and portability. Used to play interactive <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">games</a>, handheld electronic games are often miniaturized versions of <a href="/wiki/Video_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Video games">video games</a>. The controls, display and speakers are all part of a single unit, and rather than a general-purpose screen made up of a grid of small <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">pixels</a>, they usually have custom displays designed to play one game. This simplicity means they can be made as small as a <a href="/wiki/Digital_watch" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital watch">digital watch</a>, which they sometimes are. The visual output of these games can range from a few small light bulbs or <a href="/wiki/Light-emitting_diode" title="Light-emitting diode">LED</a> lights to calculator-like alphanumerical screens; later these were mostly displaced by <a href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid crystal display">liquid crystal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_fluorescent_display" title="Vacuum fluorescent display">Vacuum fluorescent display</a> screens with detailed images and in the case of VFD games, color. Handhelds were at their most popular from the late 1970s into the early 1990s. They are both the precursors to and inexpensive alternatives to the <a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">handheld game console</a>.</p>\n<p>Examples of handheld electronic games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mattel_Auto_Race" title="Mattel Auto Race">Mattel Auto Race</a></i> (1976)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Simon_(game)" title="Simon (game)">Simon</a></i> (1978)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Merlin_(game)" title="Merlin (game)">Merlin</a></i> (1978)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Game_%26_Watch" title="Game &amp; Watch">Game &amp; Watch</a></i> (1979)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Bandai_LCD_Solarpower" title="Bandai LCD Solarpower">Bandai LCD Solarpower</a></i> (1982)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Entex_Adventure_Vision" title="Entex Adventure Vision">Entex Adventure Vision</a></i> (1982)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Lights_Out_(game)" title="Lights Out (game)">Lights Out</a></i> (1995)</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pinball_machines_and_similar_devices">Pinball machines and similar devices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pinball machines and similar devices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pinball#Solid-state_era" title="Pinball">Pinball \xc2\xa7&#160;Solid-state era</a></div>\n<p>Since the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Electromechanics" title="Electromechanics">electromechanics</a> to the pinball machine in 1933\'s <i>Contact</i>, pinball has become increasingly dependent on <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> as a means to keep score on the <a href="/wiki/Pinball#Backglass" title="Pinball">backglass</a> and to provide quick impulses on the <a href="/wiki/Pinball#Playfield" title="Pinball">playfield</a> (as with <a href="/wiki/Pinball#Bumpers" title="Pinball">bumpers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pinball#Flippers" title="Pinball">flippers</a>) for exciting gameplay. Unlike games with electronic visual displays, pinball has retained a physical display that is viewed on a table through glass. Similar forms of game such as <a href="/wiki/Pachinko" title="Pachinko">pachinko</a> have also become increasingly dependent on electronics in modern times.</p>\n<p>Examples of pinball games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Addams_Family_(pinball)" title="The Addams Family (pinball)">The Addams Family</a></i> (1991)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones:_The_Pinball_Adventure" title="Indiana Jones: The Pinball Adventure">Indiana Jones: The Pinball Adventure</a></i> (1993)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation_(pinball)" title="Star Trek: The Next Generation (pinball)">Star Trek: The Next Generation</a></i> (1993)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_pinball_machines" title="List of pinball machines">List of pinball machines</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redemption_games_and_merchandisers">Redemption games and merchandisers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Redemption games and merchandisers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Redemption_game" title="Redemption game">Redemption game</a></div>\n<p>Redemption games such as <i>Skee Ball</i> have been around since the days of the <a href="/wiki/Carnival_game" title="Carnival game">carnival game</a> - well earlier than the development of the electronic game, however with modern advances many of these games have been re-worked to employ electronic scoring and other game mechanics. The use of electronic scoring mechanisms has allowed carnival or arcade attendants to take a more passive role, simply exchanging prizes for electronically dispensed coupons and occasionally emptying out the coin boxes or <a href="/wiki/Currency_detector#Banknote_acceptors" title="Currency detector">banknote acceptors</a> of the more popular games.</p>\n<p>Merchandisers such as the <i>Claw Crane</i> are more recent electronic games in which the player must accomplish a seemingly simple task (e.g. remotely controlling a mechanical arm) with sufficient ability to earn a reward.</p>\n<p>Examples of redemption games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Whac-A-Mole" title="Whac-A-Mole">Whac-A-Mole</a></i> (1976)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Skee_Ball" class="mw-redirect" title="Skee Ball">Skee Ball</a></i> - modern electric versions</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Examples or merchandisers include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Claw_crane" title="Claw crane">Claw crane</a></i> (1980)</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Slot_machines">Slot machines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Slot machines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Slot_machine" title="Slot machine">Slot machine</a></div>\n<p>The slot machine is a <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">casino</a> gambling machine with three or more <a href="/wiki/Reel" title="Reel">reels</a> which spin when a button is pushed. Though slot machines were originally operated mechanically by a <a href="/wiki/Lever" title="Lever">lever</a> on the side of the machine (the one arm) instead of an electronic button on the front panel as used on today\'s models, many modern machines still have a "legacy lever" in addition to the button on the front. Slot machines include a <a href="/wiki/Currency_detector" title="Currency detector">currency detector</a> that validates the <a href="/wiki/Coin" title="Coin">coin</a> or money inserted to play. The machine pays off based on patterns of symbols visible on the front of the machine when it stops. Modern computer technology has resulted in many variations on the slot machine concept.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Audio_games">Audio games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Audio games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Audio_game" title="Audio game">Audio game</a></div>\n<p>An audio game is a <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">game</a> played on an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_device" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic device">electronic device</a> such as\xe2\x80\x94but not limited to\xe2\x80\x94a <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a>. It is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> save that the only feedback device is audible rather than visual. Audio games originally started out as \'blind accessible\'-games, but recent interest in audio games has come from sound artists, <a href="/wiki/Game_accessibility" title="Game accessibility">game accessibility</a> researchers, mobile <a href="/wiki/Video_game_developer" title="Video game developer">game developers</a>, and mainstream <a href="/wiki/Video_game_player" class="mw-redirect" title="Video game player">video gamers</a>. Most audio games run on a <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> platform, although there are a few audio games for handhelds and <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game consoles</a>. Audio games feature the same variety of genres as video games, such as <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Racing_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Racing game">racing games</a>, etc.</p>\n<p>Examples of audio games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Real_Sound:_Kaze_no_Regret" title="Real Sound: Kaze no Regret">Real Sound: Kaze no Regret</a></i> (1997)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Chillingham_(game)" title="Chillingham (game)">Chillingham</a></i> (2004)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/BBBeat" title="BBBeat">BBBeat</a></i> (2005)</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">Video game</a></div>\n<p>A video game is a <a href="/wiki/Game" title="Game">game</a> that involves interaction with a <a href="/wiki/User_interface" title="User interface">user interface</a> to generate visual feedback on a <a href="/wiki/Display_device" title="Display device">video device</a>. The word <i>video</i> in <i>video game</i> traditionally referred to a <a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">raster</a> display device.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> However, with the popular use of the term "video game", it now implies any type of <a href="/wiki/Display_device" title="Display device">display device</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arcade_games">Arcade games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Arcade games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">Arcade game</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_of_arcade_video_games" title="Golden age of arcade video games">Golden age of arcade video games</a></div>\n<p>Electronic video arcade games make extensive use of solid state <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">integrated circuits</a>. In the past coin-operated arcade video games generally used custom per-game hardware often with multiple <a href="/wiki/Central_processing_unit" title="Central processing unit">CPUs</a>, highly specialized sound and graphics chips and/or boards, and the latest in computer graphics display technology. Recent arcade game hardware is often based on modified video game console hardware or high end pc components. Arcade games may feature specialized ambiance or control accessories, including fully enclosed dynamic cabinets with <a href="/wiki/Force_feedback" class="mw-redirect" title="Force feedback">force feedback</a> controls, dedicated <a href="/wiki/Lightgun" class="mw-redirect" title="Lightgun">lightguns</a>, rear-projection displays, reproductions of car or plane cockpits and even motorcycle or horse-shaped controllers, or even highly dedicated controllers such as <a href="/wiki/Dance_pad" title="Dance pad">dancing mats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing_rod" title="Fishing rod">fishing rods</a>. These accessories are usually what set modern arcade games apart from PC or console games, and they provide an experience that some <a href="/wiki/Gamer" title="Gamer">gamers</a> consider more immersing and realistic.</p>\n<p>Examples of arcade games include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Galaxy_Game" title="Galaxy Game">Galaxy Game</a></i> (1971)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pong" title="Galaxian">Galaxian</a></i> (1979)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a></i> (1980)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Battlezone_(1980_video_game)" title="Battlezone (1980 video game)">Battlezone</a></i> (1980)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Donkey_Kong_(arcade_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="Donkey Kong (arcade game)">Donkey Kong</a></i> (1981)</li>\n<li><i><a Fighters">King of Fighters</a></i> (1994\xe2\x80\x932005)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Time_Crisis" title="Time Crisis">Time Crisis</a></i> (1995)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Dance_Dance_Revolution" href="/wiki/The_House_of_the_Dead_(arcade_game)" class="mw-redirect" title="The House of the Dead (arcade game)">House of the Dead</a></i> (1998)</li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computer_video_games">Computer video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Computer video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/PC_game" title="PC game">PC game</a></div>\n<p>A personal computer <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> (also known as a <b>computer game</b> or simply <b>PC game</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> played on a <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computer</a>, rather than on a <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game console</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arcade_machine" class="mw-redirect" title="Arcade machine">arcade machine</a>. The vast majority of computer games today are <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>, and since the earliest days of the medium, visual displays such as the <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a> have been used to relay game information.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Console_games">Console games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Console games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Console_game" title="Console game">Console game</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">Video game console</a></div>\n<p>A console game is a form of interactive <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia</a> used for entertainment. The game consists of manipulable images (and usually sounds) generated by a <a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">video game console</a>, and displayed on a <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> or similar audio-video system. The game itself is usually controlled and manipulated using a handheld device connected to the console called a <a href="/wiki/Game_controller" title="Game controller">controller</a>. The controller generally contains a number of buttons and directional controls (such as <a href="/wiki/Joysticks" class="mw-redirect" title="Joysticks">analog joysticks</a>) each of which has been assigned a purpose for interacting with and controlling the images on the screen. The display, speakers, console, and controls of a console can also be incorporated into one small object known as a <a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">handheld game console</a>.</p>\n<p>Console games are most frequently differentiated between by their compatibility with consoles belonging in the following categories:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Video_game_console" title="Video game console">Traditional console</a>, also called "home console" - A multi-game system that uses the screen of a television to produce graphics.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Handheld_game_console" title="Handheld game console">Handheld game console</a> - A multi-game system the screen and controls of which are compacted into a single handheld device.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dedicated_console" title="Dedicated console">Dedicated console</a> - A (typically) single game system of either the <a href="/wiki/Plug_and_play" title="Plug and play">Plug and play</a> variety or the <a href="/wiki/LCD_game" class="mw-redirect" title="LCD game">LCD game</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Educational_console&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Educational console (page does not exist)">Educational console</a> - A multi-game console supporting primarily <a href="/wiki/Educational_game" title="Educational game">educational games</a>. These consoles are often simplified for use by children.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Within these categories the systems are differentiated between by their manufacturer and generation (corresponding to the year of their release). Console games are also often differentiated by <a href="/wiki/Video_game_genres" class="mw-redirect" title="Video game genres">game genre</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrid_or_Combined_Games">Hybrid or Combined Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hybrid or Combined Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Game hybridization refers to the integration of an interactive, electronic component into a game. A <b>"hybrid"</b> or <b>"combined game"</b> is any tabletop game where an electronic device and/or application is an element crucial to the gameplay.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> These games are a catalyst for creating new game mechanics. Important consequences of this technology are: the possible substitution of the gamemaster or person who leads a game for an application or device, which can be more fair, with less room for bias, cheating or favouritism, and can be intelligently randomised; the possibility of using <a href="/wiki/Artificial_intelligence" title="Artificial intelligence">artificial intelligence</a> and machine learning in games; greater randomisation of events; possibility of conducting fast and advanced mathematical calculations, making some complex games easier or available to a wider group of consumers; and enhanced player immersion with the aid of various stimuli like sound or animation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p><b>One may categorize hybrid games as follows:</b></p>\n<ul>\n<li><b>Purpose-built devices:</b> Where the game uses a device that has certain functions (like an electronic dice or spinner) to play out the game. An example would be "Monopoly: Electronic Banking" (2007) by Hasbro, <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> where all players have an electronic card which carries virtual Monopoly currency, and use a machine to increase and deduct their earnings.</li>\n<li><b>Beacon technology games</b> are mobile, digital games that employ <a href="/wiki/Bluetooth_low_energy_beacons" class="mw-redirect" title="Bluetooth low energy beacons">BLE (Bluetooth Low Energy) Beacons</a> to track and control players movements and actions. An example: Artifact Technologies created a mobile, digital board game, called BattleKasters <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> where players physically walk around to cast spells or unlock cards.</li>\n<li><b>Augmented Reality:</b> board games that interact with a mobile device and immerse the player in the virtual environment through animation, sound and/or vibrations. Example: Roar! by Trefl,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> which utilises sound as part of the gameplay.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Electronics have been adapted for use in a wide range of applications. Board games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Tower_(game)" title="Dark Tower (game)">Dark Tower</a></i>, for instance, rely heavily upon electronics. Non-traditional electronic games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rubik%27s_Revolution" title="Rubik\'s Revolution">Rubik\'s Revolution</a></i> or electronic toys which blur the boundaries between games and toys such as the <i>Electronic <a href="/wiki/Magic_8_Ball" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic 8 Ball">Magic 8 Ball</a> Date Ball</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> or the <i>Electronic <a href="/wiki/Ouija" title="Ouija">Ouija</a> Board</i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> are often considered electronic games as well.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electro-mechanical_games">Electro-mechanical games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Electro-mechanical games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game#Electro-mechanical_games" title="Arcade game">Arcade game: Electro-mechanical games</a></div>\n<p>These were types of <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade games</a> similar to arcade <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a> but relying on <a href="/wiki/Electro-mechanical" class="mw-redirect" title="Electro-mechanical">electro-mechanical</a> components to produce sounds or images rather than a <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">cathode ray tube</a> screen.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> These were popular during the 1960s and 1970s, but video games eventually overtook them in popularity during the <a href="/wiki/Golden_age_of_video_arcade_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden age of video arcade games">golden age of video arcade games</a> that began in 1978.</p>\n<p>A popular early example was <a href="/wiki/Sega" title="Sega">Sega</a>\'s <i>Periscope</i> in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Kent_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kent-10">[10]</a></sup> It was an early <a href="/wiki/Submarine_simulator" title="Submarine simulator">submarine simulator</a> and <a href="/wiki/Light_gun_shooter" title="Light gun shooter">light gun shooter</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> which used lights and plastic waves to simulate sinking ships from a submarine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Sega later produced gun games which resemble <a href="/wiki/First-person_shooter" title="First-person shooter">first-person shooter</a> video games, but were in fact electro-mechanical games that used <a href="/wiki/Rear_projection_effect" title="Rear projection effect">rear</a> <a href="/wiki/Image_projector" class="mw-redirect" title="Image projector">image projection</a> in a manner similar to the ancient <a href="/wiki/Zoetrope" title="Zoetrope">zoetrope</a> to produce moving <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animations</a> on a <a href="/wiki/Projection_screen" title="Projection screen">screen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-9">[9]</a></sup> An early example of this was the <a href="/wiki/Light_gun" title="Light gun">light gun</a> game <i><a href="/wiki/Light_gun_shooter#History" title="Light gun shooter">Duck Hunt</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-Duck_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duck-13">[13]</a></sup> which Sega released in 1969;<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> it featured animated moving targets on a screen, printed out the player\'s <a href="/wiki/Score_(game)" title="Score (game)">score</a> on a ticket, and had sound effects that were volume controllable.<sup id="cite_ref-Duck_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duck-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_games">Non-human games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Non-human games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>With the development of technology geared toward electronic entertainment of animals (typically pets), video games for pets have also been created. Since the majority of animals lack <a href="/wiki/Thumb#Importance_of_the_opposable_thumb" title="Thumb">opposable thumbs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Fine_motor_skill" title="Fine motor skill">fine motor skills</a> required for use in most gaming is unavailable to these animals. Furthermore, the <a href="/wiki/Visual_perception" title="Visual perception">visual perception</a> of many animals is influenced by a different <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">visible spectrum</a> than humans.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Techniques that de-emphasize manual control and visual components have been developed to circumvent these problems beginning with the development of television for pets.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> From this point, developers have branched out into the realm of electronic games with such products as <i>Mice Arena</i> (for <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mice</a>), <i>Chicken Petman</i>, and <i>Cyberpounce</i> (for <a href="/wiki/Cat" title="Cat">cats</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Electronic_game&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.freepatentsonline.com/3659285.html">"TELEVISION GAMING APPARATUS AND METHOD"</a>. <i>United States Patents</i><span class="reference-accessdate">.
