phenomenon">meteorological phenomenon</a> common in <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid" class="mw-redirect" title="Semi-arid">semi-arid</a> regions. Dust storms arise when a <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">gust front</a> or other strong wind blows loose sand and <a href="/wiki/Dirt" title="Dirt">dirt</a> from a dry surface. Fine particles are transported by <a href="/wiki/Saltation_(geology)" title="Saltation (geology)">saltation</a> and suspension, a process that moves soil from one place and <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(sediment)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition (sediment)">deposits</a> it in another.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Drylands" title="Drylands">Drylands</a> around <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabian peninsula">Arabian peninsula</a> are the main terrestrial sources of airborne dust. Also with some contributions from <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China\'s</a> significant storms deposit dust in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific">Pacific</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> It has been argued that recently<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup>, poor management of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s drylands, such as neglecting the <a href="/wiki/Fallow" class="mw-redirect" title="Fallow">fallow</a> system, are increasing dust storms size and frequency from desert margins and changing both the local and global climate, and also impacting local economies.<sup id="cite_ref-vrs_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vrs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The term <i>sandstorm</i> is used most often in the context of <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> sandstorms, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, or places where sand is a more prevalent soil type than dirt or rock, when, in addition to fine particles obscuring visibility, a considerable amount of larger sand particles are blown closer to the surface. The term <i>dust storm</i> is more likely to be used when finer particles are blown long distances, especially when the dust storm affects <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Causes"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Causes</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Physical_and_environmental_effects"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Physical and environmental effects</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-3"><a href="#Economic_impact"><span class="tocnumber">2.1</span> <span class="toctext">Economic impact</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Extraterrestrial_dust_storms"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Extraterrestrial dust storms</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the force of wind passing over loosely held particles increases, particles of sand first start to vibrate, then to <a href="/wiki/Saltation_(geology)" title="Saltation (geology)">saltate</a> ("leaps"). As they repeatedly strike the ground, they loosen and break off smaller particles of dust which then begin to travel in suspension. At wind speeds above that which causes the smallest to suspend, there will be a population of dust grains moving by a range of mechanisms: suspension, saltation and <a href="/wiki/Downhill_creep" title="Downhill creep">creep</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vrs_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vrs-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif/290px-Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif" width="290" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif/435px-Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="256" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Global_movement_of_dust_from_an_Asian_dust_storm_animation.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAnimation showing the global movement of dust from an Asian dust storm.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A study from 2008 finds that the initial saltation of sand particles induces a static electric field by friction. Saltating sand acquires a negative charge relative to the ground which in turn loosens more sand particles which then begin saltating. This process has been found to double the number of particles predicted by previous theories.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Particles become loosely held mainly due to drought or arid conditions, and varied wind causes. Gust fronts may be produced by the outflow of rain-cooled air from an intense thunderstorm. Or, the wind gusts may be produced by a dry <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, that is, a cold front that is moving into a dry air mass and is producing no <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>\xe2\x80\x94the type of dust storm which was common during the <a href="/wiki/Dust_Bowl" title="Dust Bowl">Dust Bowl</a> years in the U.S. Following the passage of a dry cold front, <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> instability resulting from cooler air riding over heated ground can maintain the dust storm initiated at the front.</p>\n<p>In desert areas, dust and sand storms are most commonly caused by either thunderstorm outflows, or by strong pressure gradients which cause an increase in wind velocity over a wide area. The vertical extent of the dust or sand that is raised is largely determined by the stability of the atmosphere above the ground as well as by the weight of the particulates. In some cases, dust and sand may be confined to a relatively shallow layer by a low-lying <a href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperature inversion">temperature inversion</a>. In other instances, dust (but not sand) may be lifted as high as 20,000&#160;feet (6,100&#160;m) high.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">Drought</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> contribute to the emergence of dust storms, as do poor <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a> and grazing practices by exposing the dust and sand to the wind.</p>\n<p>One poor farming practice which contributes to dust storms is <a href="/wiki/Dryland_farming" title="Dryland farming">dryland farming</a>. Particularly poor dryland farming techniques are <a href="/wiki/Tillage#Intensive_tillage" title="Tillage">intensive tillage</a> or not having established <a href="/wiki/Crop" title="Crop">crops</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cover_crop" title="Cover crop">cover crops</a> when storms strike at particularly vulnerable times prior to <a href="/wiki/Revegetation" title="Revegetation">revegetation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> In a semi-arid climate, these practices increase susceptibility to dust storms. However, <a href="/wiki/Soil_conservation" title="Soil conservation">soil conservation</a> practices may be implemented to control wind erosion.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_environmental_effects">Physical and environmental effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical and environmental effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:267px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg/265px-Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg" width="265" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg/398px-Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg/530px-Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1720" data-file-height="1160" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iraqi_Sandstorm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA convoy endures a dust storm in Iraq during the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">invasion of 2003</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A sandstorm can transport and carry large volumes of sand unexpectedly. Dust storms can carry large amounts of dust, with the leading edge being composed of a wall of thick dust as much as 1.6&#160;km (0.99&#160;mi) high. Dust and sand storms which come off the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a> are locally known as a <a href="/wiki/Simoom" title="Simoom">simoom</a> or simoon (s\xc3\xaem\xc5\xabm, s\xc3\xaem\xc5\xabn). The <i><a href="/wiki/Haboob" title="Haboob">haboob</a></i> (h\xc9\x99b\xc5\xabb) is a sandstorm prevalent in the region of <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> around <a href="/wiki/Khartoum" title="Khartoum">Khartoum</a>, with occurrences being most common in the summer.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert is a key source of dust storms, particularly the <a href="/wiki/Bod%C3%A9l%C3%A9_Depression" title="Bod\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa9 Depression">Bod\xc3\xa9l\xc3\xa9 Depression</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> and an area covering the confluence of <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">Mali</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Saharan dust storms have increased approximately 10-fold during the half-century since the 1950s, causing topsoil loss in <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">Chad</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">Burkina Faso</a>. In Mauritania there were just two dust storms a year in the early 1960s, but there are about 80 a year today, according to Andrew Goudie, a professor of geography at Oxford University.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Levels of Saharan dust coming off the east coast of Africa in June 2007 were five times those observed in June 2006, and were the highest observed since at least 1999, which may have cooled Atlantic waters enough to slightly reduce hurricane activity in late 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Dust storms have also been shown to increase the spread of disease across the globe.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Virus spores in the ground are blown into the atmosphere by the storms with the minute particles and interact with urban air pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Short-term effects of exposure to desert dust include immediate increased <a href="/wiki/Symptoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Symptoms">symptoms</a> and worsening of the <a href="/wiki/Lung_function" class="mw-redirect" title="Lung function">lung function</a> in individuals with <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> increased <a href="/wiki/Mortality_rate" title="Mortality rate">mortality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morbidity" class="mw-redirect" title="Morbidity">morbidity</a> from long-transported dust from both Saharan<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and Asian dust storms<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> suggesting that long-transported dust storm particles adversely affects the circulatory system. <a href="/wiki/Dust_pneumonia" title="Dust pneumonia">Dust pneumonia</a> is the result of large amounts of dust being inhaled.</p>\n<p>Prolonged and unprotected exposure of the <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_system" title="Respiratory system">respiratory system</a> in a dust storm can also cause <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">silicosis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> which, if left untreated, will lead to <a href="/wiki/Asphyxia" title="Asphyxia">asphyxiation</a>; silicosis is an incurable condition that may also lead to <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a>. There is also the danger of <a href="/wiki/Keratoconjunctivitis_sicca" class="mw-redirect" title="Keratoconjunctivitis sicca">keratoconjunctivitis sicca</a> ("dry eyes") which, in severe cases without immediate and proper treatment, can lead to <a href="/wiki/Blindness" class="mw-redirect" title="Blindness">blindness</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_impact">Economic impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Economic impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Dust storms cause soil loss from the dry lands, and worse, they preferentially remove organic matter and the nutrient-rich lightest particles, thereby reducing agricultural productivity. Also the abrasive effect of the storm damages young crop plants. Dust storms also reduced visibility affecting aircraft and road transportation. In addition dust storms also create problems due to complications of breathing in dust.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Dust can also have beneficial effects where it deposits: Central and South American rain forests get most of their mineral nutrients from the Sahara; iron-poor ocean regions get iron; and dust in Hawaii increases <a href="/wiki/Plantain_(cooking)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plantain (cooking)">plantain</a> growth. In northern China as well as the mid-western U.S., ancient dust storm deposits known as <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">loess</a> are highly fertile soils, but they are also a significant source of contemporary dust storms when soil-securing vegetation is disturbed.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2016)">verification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (May 2016)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_dust_storms">Extraterrestrial dust storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial dust storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hellas_basin.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Hellas_basin.gif/190px-Hellas_basin.gif" width="190" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Hellas_basin.gif 1.5x" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="250" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hellas_basin.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n2001 Hellas Basin dust storm.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Mars#Effect_of_dust_storms" title="Climate of Mars">Climate of Mars \xc2\xa7&#160;Effect of dust storms</a></div>\n<p>Dust storms are not limited to Earth and have been known to form on other planets such as <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> These dust storms can extend over larger areas than those on Earth, sometimes encircling the planet, with wind speeds as high as 60 miles per hour (97&#160;km/h). However, given Mars\' much lower atmospheric pressure (roughly 1% that of Earth\'s), the intensity of Mars\' storms could never reach the kind of hurricane-force winds experienced on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Martian dust storms are formed when solar heating warms the Martian atmosphere and causes the air to move, lifting dust off the ground. The chance for storms is increased when there are great temperature variations like those seen at the equator during the Martian summer.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dust_storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div>\n<table class="multicol" role="presentation" style="&quot;border-collapse:">\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align: left;
