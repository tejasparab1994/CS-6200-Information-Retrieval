Often producing damaging winds, it may be confused with a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>, where high-velocity winds circle a central area, and air moves inward and upward; by contrast, in a downburst, winds are directed downward and then outward from the surface landing point.</p>\n<p>Downbursts are created by an area of significantly rain-cooled air that, after reaching ground level, spreads out in all directions producing strong winds. <b>Dry downbursts</b> are associated with <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> with very little rain, while <b>wet downbursts</b> are created by thunderstorms with high amounts of rainfall. <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">Microbursts</a> and <b>macrobursts</b> are downbursts at very small and larger scales respectively. Another variety, the <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">heat burst</a>, is created by vertical currents on the backside of old outflow boundaries and <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> where rainfall is lacking. Heat bursts generate significantly higher temperatures due to the lack of rain-cooled air in their formation. Downbursts create vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> or <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">microbursts</a>, which is dangerous to aviation.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Definition"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> (Source <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A downburst is created by a column of sinking air that after hitting ground level, spreads out in all directions and is capable of producing damaging straight-line winds of over 240&#160;km/h (150&#160;mph), often producing damage similar to, but distinguishable from, that caused by <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. This is because the physical properties of a downburst are completely different from those of a tornado. Downburst damage will radiate from a central point as the descending column spreads out when hitting the surface, whereas tornado damage tends towards convergent damage consistent with rotating winds. To differentiate between tornado damage and damage from a downburst, the term <b>straight-line winds</b> is applied to damage from microbursts.</p>\n<p>Downbursts are particularly strong <a href="/wiki/Updraft_and_Downdraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Updraft and Downdraft">downdrafts</a> from thunderstorms. Downbursts in air that is <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> free or contains <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> are known as <b>dry downbursts</b>;<sup id="cite_ref-micro_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-micro-1">[1]</a></sup> those accompanied with precipitation are known as <b>wet downbursts</b>. Most downbursts are less than 4&#160;km (2.5&#160;mi) in extent: these are called <a href="/wiki/Microburst" title="Microburst">microbursts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Macroburst_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macroburst-2">[2]</a></sup> Downbursts larger than 4&#160;km (2.5&#160;mi) in extent are sometimes called <b>macrobursts</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Macroburst_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macroburst-2">[2]</a></sup> Downbursts can occur over large areas. In the extreme case, a <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derecho</a> can cover a huge area more than 320&#160;km (200&#160;mi) wide and over 1,600&#160;km (1,000&#160;mi) long, lasting up to 12 hours or more, and is associated with some of the most intense straight-line winds,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but the generative process is somewhat different from that of most downbursts.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Straight-line_winds">Straight-line winds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Straight-line winds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">Derecho</a></div>\n<p><b>Straight-line winds</b> (also known as <b>thundergusts</b> and <b>hurricanes of the prairie</b>) are very strong winds that can produce damage, demonstrating a lack of the rotational damage pattern associated with <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Straight-line winds are common with the <a href="/wiki/Gust_front" class="mw-redirect" title="Gust front">gust front</a> of a thunderstorm or originate with a downburst from a thunderstorm. These events can cause considerable damage, even in the absence of a tornado. The winds can gust to 210&#160;km/h (130&#160;mph)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and winds of 93&#160;km/h (58&#160;mph) or more can last for more than twenty minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In the United States, such straight-line wind events are most common during the spring when instability is highest and weather fronts routinely cross the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Straight-line wind events in the form of <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a> can take place throughout the Eastern half of the U.S.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Straight-line winds may be damaging to marine interests. Small ships, cutters and sailboats are at risk from this meteorological phenomenon.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The formation of a downburst starts with <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> or large <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">raindrops</a> falling through drier air. Hailstones melt and raindrops evaporate, pulling <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> from surrounding air and cooling it considerably. Cooler air has a higher density than the warmer air around it, so it sinks to the ground. As the cold air hits the ground it spreads out and a <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">front</a> can be observed as a <a href="/wiki/Gust_front" class="mw-redirect" title="Gust front">gust front</a>. Areas under and immediately adjacent to the downburst are the areas which receive the highest winds and rainfall, if any is present. Also, because the rain-cooled air is descending from the middle troposphere, a significant drop in temperatures is noticed. Due to interaction with the ground, the downburst quickly loses strength as it fans out and forms the distinctive "curl shape" that is commonly seen at the periphery of the microburst (see image). Downbursts usually last only a few minutes and then dissipate, except in the case of squall lines and derecho events. However, despite their short lifespan, microbursts are a serious hazard to aviation and property and can result in substantial damage to the area.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_bursts">Heat bursts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heat bursts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">Heat burst</a></div>\n<p>A special, and much rarer, kind of downburst is a <a href="/wiki/Heat_burst" title="Heat burst">heat burst</a>, which results from precipitation-evaporated air compressionally heating as it descends from very high altitude, usually on the backside of a dying <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a> or outflow boundary.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Heat bursts are chiefly a nocturnal occurrence, can produce winds over 160&#160;km/h (100&#160;mph), are characterized by exceptionally dry air, and can suddenly raise the surface temperature to 38&#160;\xc2\xb0C (100&#160;\xc2\xb0F) or more, sometimes persisting for several hours.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Danger_to_aviation">Danger to aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Danger to aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Microburst#Danger_to_aircraft" title="Microburst">Microburst \xc2\xa7&#160;Danger to aircraft</a></div>\n<p>Downbursts, particularly microbursts, are exceedingly dangerous to aircraft which are taking off or landing due to the strong vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> caused by these events. A number of fatal crashes have been attributed to downbursts.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">Bow echo</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Convective_storm_detection" title="Convective storm detection">Convective storm detection</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Haboob" title="Haboob">Haboob</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">Line echo wave pattern</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_derecho_events" title="List of derecho events">List of derecho events</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_microbursts" title="List of microbursts">List of microbursts</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mesovortex" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesovortex">Mesovortex</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Downburst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-micro-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-micro_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Fernando Caracena, Ronald L.
