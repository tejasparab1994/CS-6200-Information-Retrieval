space</b> (also: <b>3-space</b> or, rarely, <b>tri-dimensional space</b>) is a geometric setting in which three values (called <a href="/wiki/Parameter" title="Parameter">parameters</a>) are required to determine the position of an element (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Point_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Point (mathematics)">point</a>). This is the informal meaning of the term <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a>.</p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">sequence</a> of <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span> <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">numbers</a> can be understood as a location in <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i></span>-dimensional space. When <span class="texhtml"><i>n</i> = 3</span>, the set of all such locations is called <span id="3D_EUCLIDEAN_SPACE"></span><b>three-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">Euclidean space</a></b>. It is commonly represented by the symbol <span class="texhtml">\xe2\x84\x9d<sup>3</sup></span>. This serves as a three-parameter model of the physical <a href="/wiki/Universe" title="Universe">universe</a> (that is, the spatial part, without considering time) in which all known <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">matter</a> exists. However, this space is only one example of a large variety of spaces in three dimensions called <a href="/wiki/3-manifold" title="3-manifold">3-manifolds</a>. In this classical example, when the three values refer to measurements in different directions (<a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinates</a>), any three directions can be chosen, provided that <a href="/wiki/Vector_space" title="Vector space">vectors</a> in these directions do not all lie in the same <a href="/wiki/Two-dimensional_space" title="Two-dimensional space">2-space</a> (<a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">plane</a>). Furthermore, in this case, these three values can be labeled by any combination of three chosen from the terms <i><a href="/wiki/Width" class="mw-redirect" title="Width">width</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Height" title="Height">height</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">depth</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Breadth" class="mw-redirect" title="Breadth">breadth</a></i>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a system</a></div>\n<p>In mathematics, <a href="/wiki/Analytic_geometry" title="Analytic geometry">analytic geometry</a> (also called Cartesian geometry) describes every point in three-dimensional space by means of three coordinates. Three <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_axis" class="mw-redirect" title="Coordinate axis">coordinate axes</a> are given, each perpendicular to the other two at the <a href="/wiki/Origin_(mathematics)" title="Origin (mathematics)">origin</a>, the point at which they cross. They are usually labeled <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i>, <i>y</i></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>z</i></span>. Relative to these axes, the position of any point in three-dimensional space is given by an ordered triple of <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">real numbers</a>, each number giving the distance of that point from the <a href="/wiki/Origin_(mathematics)" title="Origin (mathematics)">origin</a> measured along the given axis, which is equal to the distance of that point from the plane determined by the other two axes.<sup id="cite_ref-Hughes_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hughes-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Other popular methods of describing the location of a point in three-dimensional space include <a href="/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates" class="mw-redirect" title="Cylindrical coordinates">cylindrical coordinates</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spherical_coordinates" class="mw-redirect" title="Spherical coordinates">spherical coordinates</a>, though there are an infinite number of possible methods. href="/w/index.php?title=Three-dimensional_space&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lines and planes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Two distinct points always determine a (straight) <a href="/wiki/Line_(geometry)" title="Line (geometry)">line</a>. Three distinct points are either <a href="/wiki/Collinear" class="mw-redirect" title="Collinear">collinear</a> or determine a unique plane. Four distinct points can either be collinear, <a href="/wiki/Coplanar" class="mw-redirect" title="Coplanar">coplanar</a> or determine the entire space.</p>\n<p>Two distinct lines can either intersect, be <a href="/wiki/Parallel_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel line">parallel</a> or be <a href="/wiki/Skew_lines" title="Skew lines">skew</a>. Two parallel lines, or <a href="/wiki/Two_intersecting_lines" class="mw-redirect" title="Two intersecting lines">two intersecting lines</a>, lie in a unique plane, so skew lines are lines that do not meet and do not lie in a common plane.</p>\n<p>Two distinct planes can either meet in a common line or are parallel (do not meet). Three distinct planes, no pair of which are parallel, can either meet in a common line, meet in a unique common point or have no point in common. In the last case, the three lines of intersection of each pair of planes are mutually parallel.</p>\n<p>A line can lie in a given plane, intersect that plane in a unique point or be parallel to the plane. In the last case, there will be lines in the plane that are parallel to the given line.</p>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Hyperplane" title="Hyperplane">hyperplane</a> is a subspace of one dimension less than the dimension of the full space. The hyperplanes of a three-dimensional space are the two-dimensional subspaces, that is, the planes. In terms of cartesian coordinates, the points of a hyperplane satisfy a single <a href="/wiki/Linear_equation" title="Linear equation">linear equation</a>, so planes in this 3-space are described by linear equations. A line can be described by a pair of independent linear equations, each representing a plane having this line as a common intersection.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Varignon%27s_theorem" title="Varignon\'s theorem">Varignon\'s theorem</a> states that the midpoints of any quadrilateral in \xe2\x84\x9d<sup>3</sup> form a <a href="/wiki/Parallelogram" title="Parallelogram">parallelogram</a>, and so, are coplanar.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spheres_and_balls">Spheres and balls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a The plane curve is called the <i>generatrix</i> of the surface. A section of the surface, made by intersecting the surface with a plane that is perpendicular (orthogonal) to the axis, is a circle.</p>\n<p>Simple examples occur when the generatrix is a line. If the generatrix line intersects the axis line, the surface of revolution is a right circular <a href="/wiki/Cone_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cone (mathematics)">cone</a> with vertex (apex) the point of intersection. Space has three dimensions because the length of a <a href="/wiki/Cuboid" title="Cuboid">box</a> is independent of its width or breadth.
