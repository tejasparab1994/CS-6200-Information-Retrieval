dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the class of poisonous substances. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Toxin_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Toxin (disambiguation)">Toxin (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<p>A <b>toxin</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient&#160;Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">\xcf\x84\xce\xbf\xce\xbe\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xcf\x8c\xce\xbd</span>&#160;<span title="Ancient Greek transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i><a href="/wiki/Arrow_poison" title="Arrow poison">toxikon</a></i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisonous</a> substance produced within living cells or organisms;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-urltoxin_-_Definition_from_the_Merriam-Webster_Online_Dictionary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urltoxin_-_Definition_from_the_Merriam-Webster_Online_Dictionary-2">[2]</a></sup> synthetic <a href="/wiki/Toxicant" title="Toxicant">toxicants</a> created by artificial processes are thus excluded. The term was first used by organic chemist Ludwig Brieger (1849\xe2\x80\x931919).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Toxins can be <a href="/wiki/Small_molecule" title="Small molecule">small molecules</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a> that are capable of causing disease on contact with or absorption by <a href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological tissue">body tissues</a> interacting with biological <a href="/wiki/Macromolecule" title="Macromolecule">macromolecules</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Enzyme" title="Enzyme">enzymes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry)" title="Receptor (biochemistry)">cellular receptors</a>. Toxins vary greatly in their <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxicity</a>, ranging from usually minor (such as a <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bee</a> <a href="/wiki/Stinger" title="Stinger">sting</a>) to almost immediately deadly (such as <a href="/wiki/Botulinum_toxin" title="Botulinum toxin">botulinum toxin</a>).</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Toxins are often distinguished from other chemical agents by their method of production\xe2\x80\x94the word toxin does not specify method of delivery (compare with <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> and the broader meaning of <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poison</a>\xe2\x80\x94all substances that can also cause disturbances to organisms). It simply means it is a biologically produced poison. There was an ongoing terminological dispute between <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> over whether to call a toxin a <a href="/wiki/Biological_agent" title="Biological agent">biological</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_agent" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical agent">chemical agent</a>, in which the NATO opted for biological agent, and the Warsaw Pact, like most other countries in the world, for chemical agent.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>According to an <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_of_the_Red_Cross" title="International Committee of the Red Cross">International Committee of the Red Cross</a> review of the <a href="/wiki/Biological_Weapons_Convention" title="Biological Weapons Convention">Biological Weapons Convention</a>, "Toxins are poisonous products of organisms; unlike biological agents, they are inanimate and not capable of reproducing themselves", and "Since the signing of the Constitition, there have been no disputes among the parties regarding the definition of biological agents or toxins".<sup id="cite_ref-urlThe_Biological_Weapons_Convention_-_An_overview_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlThe_Biological_Weapons_Convention_-_An_overview-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Title_18_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 18 of the United States Code">Title 18 of the United States Code</a>, "... the term "toxin" means the <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> material or product of <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> (including, but not limited to, <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">viruses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fungi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fungi">fungi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rickettsiae" class="mw-redirect" title="Rickettsiae">rickettsiae</a> or <a href="/wiki/Protozoa" title="Protozoa">protozoa</a>), or infectious substances, or a <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant</a> or synthesized molecule, whatever their origin and method of production..."<sup id="cite_ref-urlU.S._Code_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlU.S._Code-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A rather informal terminology of individual toxins relates them to the anatomical location where their effects are most notable:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hemotoxin" title="Hemotoxin">Hemotoxin</a>, causes destruction of <a href="/wiki/Red_blood_cells" class="mw-redirect" title="Red blood cells">red blood cells</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hemolysis" title="Hemolysis">hemolysis</a>)</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Phototoxin" title="Phototoxin">Phototoxin</a>, causes dangerous photosensitivity</li>\n</ul>\n<p>On a broader scale, toxins may be classified as either <a href="/wiki/Exotoxin" title="Exotoxin">exotoxins</a>, being excreted by an organism, or <a href="/wiki/Endotoxin" class="mw-redirect" title="Endotoxin">endotoxins</a>, that are released mainly when bacteria are <a href="/wiki/Lysis" title="Lysis">lysed</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biotoxins">Biotoxins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biotoxins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "biotoxin" is sometimes used to explicitly confirm the biological origin.<sup id="cite_ref-urlbiotoxin_-_Definition_from_the_Merriam-Webster_Online_Dictionary_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-urlbiotoxin_-_Definition_from_the_Merriam-Webster_Online_Dictionary-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Biotoxins are further classified into fungal biotoxins, or short <a href="/wiki/Mycotoxin" title="Mycotoxin">mycotoxins</a>, microbial biotoxins, plant biotoxins, short <a href="/wiki/Phytotoxin" title="Phytotoxin">phytotoxins</a> and animal biotoxins.</p>\n<p>Toxins produced by <a href="/wiki/Microorganism" title="Microorganism">microorganisms</a> are important <a href="/wiki/Virulence" title="Virulence">virulence</a> determinants responsible for microbial <a href="/wiki/Pathogenicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Pathogenicity">pathogenicity</a> and/or evasion of the host <a href="/wiki/Immune_response" title="Immune response">immune response</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ProftT_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ProftT-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Biotoxins vary greatly in purpose and mechanism, and can be highly complex (the <a href="/wiki/Venom" title="Venom">venom</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cone_snail" title="Cone snail">cone snail</a> contains dozens of small <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">proteins</a>, each targeting a specific nerve channel or receptor), or relatively small protein.</p>\n<p>Biotoxins in nature have two primary functions:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">Predation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Spider" title="Spider">spider</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jellyfish" title="Jellyfish">jellyfish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasp</a></li>\n<li>Defense in the <a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ant" title="Ant">ant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">termite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honeybee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wasp" title="Wasp">wasp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poison_dart_frog" title="Poison dart frog">poison dart frog</a></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Some of the more well known types of biotoxins include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cyanotoxin" title="Cyanotoxin">Cyanotoxins</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dinotoxin" title="Dinotoxin">Dinotoxins</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellate" title="Dinoflagellate">Dinoflagellates</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Necrosis" title="Necrosis">Necrotoxins</a> cause necrosis (i.e., death) in the cells they encounter and destroy all types of <a href="/wiki/Biological_tissue" class="mw-redirect" title="Biological tissue">tissue</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Necrotoxins spread through the bloodstream<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. In humans, <a href="/wiki/Skin" title="Skin">skin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muscle" title="Muscle">muscle</a> tissues are most sensitive to necrotoxins<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Organisms that possess necrotoxins include:\n<ul>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Brown_recluse_spider" title="Brown recluse spider">brown recluse</a> or "fiddle back" spider</li>\n<li>Most <a href="/wiki/Rattlesnake" title="Rattlesnake">rattlesnakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vipers" class="mw-redirect" title="Vipers">vipers</a> produce <a href="/wiki/Phospholipase" title="Phospholipase">phospholipase</a> and various <a href="/wiki/Trypsin" title="Trypsin">trypsin</a>-like <a href="/wiki/Serine_proteases" class="mw-redirect" title="Serine proteases">serine proteases</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Bitis_arietans" title="Bitis arietans">Puff Adder</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Necrotizing_fasciitis" title="Necrotizing fasciitis">Necrotizing fasciitis</a> (caused by the "flesh eating" bacterium <a href="/wiki/Streptococcus_pyogenes" title="Streptococcus pyogenes">Streptococcus pyogenes</a>) - produces a <a href="/wiki/Pore-forming_toxin" title="Pore-forming toxin">pore forming toxin</a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Neurotoxin" title="Neurotoxin">Neurotoxins</a> primarily affect the nervous systems of animals. The group neurotoxins generally consists of <a href="/wiki/Category:Ion_channel_toxins" title="Category:Ion channel toxins">ion channel toxins</a> that disrupt ion channel conductance. Organisms that possess neurotoxins include:\n<ul>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Latrodectus" title="Latrodectus">black widow</a> spider.</li>\n<li>Most <a href="/wiki/Scorpion" title="Scorpion">scorpions</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Box_jellyfish" title="Box jellyfish">box jellyfish</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Elapidae" title="Elapidae">Elapid</a> snakes</li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Cone_snail" title="Cone snail">cone snail</a></li>\n<li>The <a href="/wiki/Blue-ringed_octopus" title="Blue-ringed octopus">Blue-ringed octopus</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Venomous_fish" title="Venomous fish">Venomous fish</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Frogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Frogs">Frogs</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Palythoa" title="Palythoa">Palythoa</a> <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">coral</a></li>\n<li>Various different types of <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyanobacteria" title="Cyanobacteria">cyanobacteria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellates" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinoflagellates">dinoflagellates</a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Myotoxin" title="Myotoxin">Myotoxins</a> are small, basic <a href="/wiki/Peptide" title="Peptide">peptides</a> found in <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">snake</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lizard" title="Lizard">lizard</a> <a href="/wiki/Venom_(poison)" class="mw-redirect" title="Venom (poison)">venoms</a>, They cause muscle tissue damage by a non enzymatic receptor based mechanism. Organisms that possess myotoxins include:\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rattlesnake" title="Rattlesnake">rattlesnakes</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Eastern_bearded_dragon" title="Eastern bearded dragon">eastern bearded dragon</a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cytotoxic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cytotoxic">Cytotoxins</a> are toxic at the level of individual cells, either in a non-specific fashion or only in certain types of living cells:\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ricin" title="Ricin">Ricin</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Castor_bean" class="mw-redirect" title="Castor bean">castor beans</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Apitoxin" title="Apitoxin">Apitoxin</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Honey_bee" title="Honey bee">honey bees</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/T-2_mycotoxin" title="T-2 mycotoxin">T-2 mycotoxin</a>, from certain toxic <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">mushrooms</a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_toxins">Environmental toxins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Environmental toxins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The term "environmental toxin" can sometimes explicitly include synthetic contaminants<sup id="cite_ref-pmid14977703_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14977703-9">[9]</a></sup> such as industrial <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a> and other artificially made <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> substances. As this contradicts most formal definitions of the term "toxin", it is important to confirm what the researcher means when encountering the term outside of microbiological contexts.</p>\n<p>Environmental toxins from <a href="/wiki/Food_chain" title="Food chain">food chains</a> that may be dangerous to human health include:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Paralytic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Paralytic shellfish poisoning">Paralytic shellfish poisoning</a> (PSP)<sup id="cite_ref-iviv_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iviv-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dlpsp_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dlpsp-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-psptp_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-psptp-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Amnesic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Amnesic shellfish poisoning">Amnesic shellfish poisoning</a> (ASP)<sup id="cite_ref-aspt_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aspt-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ocfl_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ocfl-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Diarrheal_shellfish_poisoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Diarrheal shellfish poisoning">Diarrheal shellfish poisoning</a> (DSP)<sup id="cite_ref-dmto_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dmto-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ehed_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehed-16">[16]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Neurotoxic_shellfish_poisoning" title="Neurotoxic shellfish poisoning">Neurotoxic shellfish poisoning</a> (NSP)<sup id="cite_ref-nspb_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nspb-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bnba_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bnba-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bigm_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bigm-19">[19]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finding_information_about_toxins">Finding information about toxins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Finding information about toxins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Toxicology and Environmental Health Information Program (TEHIP)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Library_of_Medicine" title="United States National Library of Medicine">United States National Library of Medicine</a> (NLM) maintains a comprehensive toxicology and environmental health web site that includes access to toxins-related resources produced by TEHIP and by other government agencies and organizations. This web site includes links to databases, bibliographies, tutorials, and other scientific and consumer-oriented resources. TEHIP also is responsible for the Toxicology Data Network (TOXNET),<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> an integrated system of toxicology and environmental health databases that are available free of charge on the web.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/TOXMAP" title="TOXMAP">TOXMAP</a> is a Geographic Information System (GIS) that is part of TOXNET. TOXMAP uses maps of the United States to help users visually explore data from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a>\'s (EPA) <a href="/wiki/Toxics_Release_Inventory" title="Toxics Release Inventory">Toxics Release Inventory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superfund_Basic_Research_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Superfund Basic Research Program">Superfund Basic Research Programs</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computational_resources_for_prediction_of_toxic_peptides_and_proteins">Computational resources for prediction of toxic peptides and proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Computational resources for prediction of toxic peptides and proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>One of the bottlenecks in peptide/protein-based therapy is their toxicity. Recently, <i>in silico</i> models for predicting toxicity of peptides and proteins, developed by <a href="/wiki/Gajendra_Pal_Singh_Raghava" title="Gajendra Pal Singh Raghava">Gajendra Pal Singh Raghava</a>\'s group,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> predict toxicity with reasonably good accuracy. The prediction models are based on machine learning technique and quantitative matrix using various properties of peptides. The prediction tool is freely accessible to public in the form of web server.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Misuse_of_the_term">Misuse of the term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toxin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Misuse of the term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>When used non-technically, the term "toxin" is often applied to any <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> substance, even though the term <a href="/wiki/Toxicant" title="Toxicant">toxicant</a> would be more appropriate. Toxic substances not directly of biological origin are also termed <a href="/wiki/Poison" title="Poison">poisons</a> and many non-technical and lifestyle journalists follow this usage to refer to <a href="/wiki/Toxicity" title="Toxicity">toxic</a> substances in general.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Quackery" title="Quackery">quackery</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alternative_medicine" title="Alternative medicine">alternative medicine</a>, the term "toxin" is used to refer to any substance alleged to cause ill health. This could range from trace amounts of potentially dangerous <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, to supposedly harmful substances produced in the body by intestinal fermentation (<a href="/wiki/Colon_cleansing" title="Colon cleansing">auto-intoxication</a>), to food ingredients such as <a href="/wiki/Sucrose" title="Sucrose">table sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monosodium_glutamate" title="Monosodium glutamate">monosodium glutamate</a> (MSG), and <a href="/wiki/Aspartame" title="Aspartame">aspartame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
