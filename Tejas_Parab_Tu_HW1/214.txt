href="/wiki/Hovm%C3%B6ller_diagram" title="Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagram">Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagram</a> of the 5-day running mean of <a href="/wiki/Outgoing_longwave_radiation" title="Outgoing longwave Time increases from top to bottom in the figure, so contours that are oriented from upper-left to lower-right represent movement from west to east.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation</b> (<b>MJO</b>) is the largest element of the intraseasonal (30- to 90-day) variability in the tropical atmosphere. It was discovered in 1971 by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Roland_Madden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Roland Madden (page does not exist)">Roland Madden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Julian_(meteorologist)" title="Paul Julian (meteorologist)">Paul Julian</a> of the American <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR). It is a large-scale coupling between atmospheric circulation and tropical <a href="/wiki/Deep_convection" class="mw-redirect" title="Deep convection">deep convection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-agu1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-agu1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UEA1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UEA1-2">[2]</a></sup> Unlike a standing pattern like the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO), the Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation is a traveling pattern that propagates eastward at approximately 4 to 8&#160;m/s (14 to 29&#160;km/h, 9 to 18&#160;mph), through the atmosphere above the warm parts of the Indian and Pacific oceans. This overall circulation pattern manifests itself most clearly as anomalous <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>.</p>\n<p>The Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation is characterized by an eastward progression of large regions of both enhanced and suppressed tropical rainfall, observed mainly over the Indian and Pacific Ocean. The anomalous rainfall is usually first evident over the western Indian Ocean, and remains evident as it propagates over the very warm ocean waters of the western and central tropical Pacific. This pattern of tropical rainfall then generally becomes nondescript as it moves over the cooler ocean waters of the eastern Pacific (except over the region of warmer water off the west coast of Central America) but occasionally reappears at low amplitude over the tropical Atlantic and higher amplitude over the Indian Ocean. The wet phase of enhanced convection and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> is followed by a dry phase where <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity is suppressed. Each cycle lasts approximately 30\xe2\x80\x9360 days. Because of this pattern, The Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation is also known as the <b>30- to 60-day oscillation</b>, <b>30- to 60-day wave</b>, or <b>intraseasonal oscillation</b>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Behavior"><span class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behavior">Behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Distinct patterns of lower-level and upper-level atmospheric circulation anomalies accompany the MJO-related pattern of enhanced or decreased tropical rainfall across the tropics. These circulation features extend around the globe and are not confined to only the eastern hemisphere. The Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation moves eastward at between 4&#160;m/s (14&#160;km/h, 9&#160;mph) and 8&#160;m/s (29&#160;km/h, 18&#160;mph) across the tropics, crossing the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> in 30 to 60 days\xe2\x80\x94with the active phase of the MJO tracked by the degree of outgoing long wave radiation, which is measured by <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a>-sensing <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">geostationary</a> <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">weather satellites</a>. The lower the amount of outgoing long wave radiation, the stronger the thunderstorm complexes, or convection, is within that region.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Enhanced surface (upper level) westerly winds occur near the west (east) side of the active convection.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Ocean currents, up to 100 metres (330&#160;ft) in depth from the ocean surface, follow in phase with the east-wind component of the surface winds. In advance, or to the east, of the MJO enhanced activity, winds aloft are westerly. In its wake, or to the west of the enhanced rainfall area, winds aloft are easterly. These wind changes aloft are due to the divergence present over the active thunderstorms during the enhanced phase. Its direct influence can be tracked poleward as far as 30&#160;degrees latitude from the equator in both northern and southern hemispheres, propagating outward from its origin near the equator at around 1&#160;degree latitude, or 111 kilometres (69&#160;mi), per day.<sup id="cite_ref-MJOreview_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MJOreview-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_effects">Local effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_the_monsoon">Connection to the monsoon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Connection to the monsoon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/220px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png" width="220" height="249" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/330px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg/440px-India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1639" data-file-height="1852" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:India_southwest_summer_monsoon_onset_map_en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nOnset dates and prevailing wind currents of the southwest summer monsoon.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">Monsoon trough</a></div>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> summer season the MJO-related effects on the Indian and West African summer <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> are well documented. MJO-related effects on the North American summer monsoon also occur, though they are relatively weaker. MJO-related impacts on the North American summer precipitation patterns are strongly linked to meridional (i.e. north\xe2\x80\x93south) adjustments of the precipitation pattern in the eastern tropical Pacific. A strong relationship between the leading <a href="/wiki/Mode_of_variability" class="mw-redirect" title="Mode of variability">mode of intraseasonal variability</a> of the North American Monsoon System, the MJO and the points of origin of tropical cyclones is also present.</p>\n<p>A period of warming sea surface temperatures are found five to ten days prior to a strengthening of MJO-related precipitation across southern Asia. A break in the Asian monsoon, normally during the month of July, has been attributed to the Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation, after its enhanced phase moves off to the east of the region into the open tropical Pacific Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_tropical_cyclogenesis">Influence on tropical cyclogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence on tropical cyclogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>\n<p>Tropical cyclones occur throughout the boreal warm season (typically May\xe2\x80\x93November) in both the north Pacific and the north Atlantic basins\xe2\x80\x94but any given year has periods of enhanced or suppressed activity within the season. Evidence suggests that the Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation modulates this activity (particularly for the strongest storms) by providing a large-scale environment that is favorable (or unfavorable) for development. MJO-related descending motion is not favorable for tropical storm development. However, MJO-related ascending motion is a favorable pattern for thunderstorm formation within the tropics, which is quite favorable for tropical storm development. As the MJO progresses eastward, the favored region for tropical cyclone activity also shifts eastward from the western Pacific to the eastern Pacific and finally to the Atlantic basin.</p>\n<p>An inverse relationship exists between tropical cyclone activity in the western north Pacific basin and the north Atlantic basin, however. When one basin is active, the other is normally quiet, and vice versa. The main reason for this appears to be the phase of the MJO, which is normally in opposite modes between the two basins at any given time.<sup id="cite_ref-Mad-Jul_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mad-Jul-7">[7]</a></sup> While this relationship appears robust, the MJO is one of many factors that contribute to the development of tropical cyclones. For example, sea surface temperatures must be sufficiently warm and vertical wind shear must be sufficiently weak for tropical disturbances to form and persist.<sup id="cite_ref-A15_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A15-8">[8]</a></sup> However, the MJO also influences these conditions that facilitate or suppress tropical cyclone formation. The MJO is monitored routinely by both the USA <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> and the USA <a href="/wiki/Climate_Prediction_Center" title="Climate Prediction Center">Climate Prediction Center</a> during the Atlantic hurricane (<a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>) season to aid in anticipating periods of relative activity or inactivity.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Downstream_effects">Downstream effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Downstream effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Link_to_the_El_Nino-Southern_oscillation">Link to the El Nino-Southern oscillation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Link to the El Nino-Southern oscillation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation</a></div>\n<p>There is strong year-to-year (interannual) variability in Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation activity, with long periods of strong activity followed by periods in which the oscillation is weak or absent. This interannual variability of the MJO is partly linked to the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO) cycle. In the Pacific, strong MJO activity is often observed 6 \xe2\x80\x93 12 months prior to the onset of an <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> episode, but is virtually absent during the maxima of some El Ni\xc3\xb1o episodes, while MJO activity is typically greater during a <a href="/wiki/La_Ni%C3%B1a" title="La Ni\xc3\xb1a">La Ni\xc3\xb1a</a> episode. Strong events in the Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation over a series of months in the western Pacific can speed the development of an El Ni\xc3\xb1o or La Ni\xc3\xb1a but usually do not in themselves lead to the onset of a warm or cold ENSO event.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> However, observations suggest that the 1982-1983 El Ni\xc3\xb1o developed rapidly during July 1982 in direct response to a <a href="/wiki/Kelvin_wave" title="Kelvin wave">Kelvin wave</a> triggered by an MJO event during late May.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Further, changes in the structure of the MJO with the seasonal cycle and ENSO might facilitate more substantial impacts of the MJO on ENSO. For example, the surface westerly winds associated with active MJO convection are stronger during advancement toward El Ni\xc3\xb1o and the surface easterly winds associated with the suppressed convective phase are stronger during advancement toward La Nina.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Globally, the inter annual variability of the MJO is most determined by atmospheric internal dynamics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2009)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_winter_precipitation">North American winter precipitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North American winter precipitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_the_El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Effects of the El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation in the United States">Effects of the El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation in the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_rainfall_climatology" title="United States rainfall climatology">United States rainfall climatology</a></div>\n<p>The strongest impacts of intraseasonal variability on the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> occur during the winter months over the western U.S. During the winter this region receives the bulk of its annual <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. Storms in this region can last for several days or more and are often accompanied by persistent <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a> features. Of particular concern are extreme precipitation events linked to <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>. Strong evidence suggests a link between weather and climate in this region from studies that have related the El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation to regional precipitation variability. In the tropical Pacific, winters with weak-to-moderate cold, or La Nina, episodes or ENSO-neutral conditions are often characterized by enhanced 30- to 60-day Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation activity. A recent example is the winter of 1996\xe2\x80\x9397, which featured heavy flooding in California and in the Pacific Northwest (estimated damage costs of $2.0\xe2\x80\x933.0 billion at the time of the event) and a very active MJO. Such winters are also characterized by relatively small sea surface temperature anomalies in the tropical Pacific compared to stronger warm and cold episodes. In these winters, there is a stronger link between the MJO events and extreme west coast precipitation events.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pineapple_Express_events">Pineapple Express events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pineapple Express events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pineapple_Express" title="Pineapple Express">Pineapple Express</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mjo_north_america_rain.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Mjo_north_america_rain.png/350px-Mjo_north_america_rain.png" width="350" height="471" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Mjo_north_america_rain.png/525px-Mjo_north_america_rain.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a7/Mjo_north_america_rain.png 2x" data-file-width="556" data-file-height="749" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mjo_north_america_rain.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe <i>Pineapple Express</i>, an MJO effect on North American weather patterns.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The typical scenario linking the pattern of tropical rainfall associated with the MJO to extreme precipitation events in the Pacific Northwest features a progressive (i.e. eastward moving) circulation pattern in the tropics and a retrograding (i.e. westward moving) circulation pattern in the mid latitudes of the North Pacific. Typical wintertime weather anomalies preceding heavy precipitation events in the Pacific Northwest are as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<ol>\n<li><i>7\xe2\x80\x9310 days prior to the heavy precipitation event:</i> Heavy tropical rainfall associated with the MJO shifts eastward from the eastern Indian Ocean to the western tropical Pacific. A moisture plume extends northeastward from the western tropical Pacific towards the general vicinity of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian</a> Islands. A strong blocking anticyclone is located in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Alaska" title="Gulf of Alaska">Gulf of Alaska</a> with a strong <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">polar jet stream</a> around its northern flank.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>3\xe2\x80\x935 days prior to the heavy precipitation event:</i> Heavy tropical rainfall shifts eastward towards the date line and begins to diminish. The associated moisture plume extends further to the northeast, often traversing the Hawaiian Islands. The strong blocking high weakens and shifts westward. A split in the North Pacific <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> develops, characterized by an increase in the amplitude and areal extent of the upper tropospheric westerly zonal winds on the southern flank of the block and a decrease on its northern flank. The tropical and extra tropical circulation patterns begin to "phase", allowing a developing mid latitude trough to tap the moisture plume extending from the deep tropics.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li><i>The heavy precipitation event:</i> As the pattern of enhanced tropical rainfall continues to shift further to the east and weaken, the deep tropical moisture plume extends from the subtropical central Pacific into the mid latitude trough now located off the west coast of North America. The <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> at upper levels extends across the North Pacific with the mean jet position entering North America in the northwestern United States. The deep low pressure located near the Pacific Northwest coast can bring up to several days of heavy rain and possible flooding. These events are often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Pineapple_Express" title="Pineapple Express">Pineapple Express</a> events, so named because a significant amount of the deep tropical moisture traverses the Hawaiian Islands on its way towards western North America.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n</ol>\n<p>Throughout this evolution, retrogression of the large-scale atmospheric circulation features is observed in the eastern Pacific\xe2\x80\x93North American sector. Many of these events are characterized by the progression of the heaviest precipitation from south to north along the Pacific Northwest coast over a period of several days to more than one week. However, it is important to differentiate the individual <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic</a>-scale storms, which generally move west to east, from the overall large-scale pattern, which exhibits retrogression.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A coherent simultaneous relationship exists between the longitudinal position of maximum MJO-related rainfall and the location of extreme west coast precipitation events. Extreme events in the Pacific Northwest are accompanied by enhanced precipitation over the western tropical Pacific and the region of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> called by meteorologists the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_Continent" title="Maritime Continent">Maritime Continent</a>, with suppressed precipitation over the Indian Ocean and the central Pacific. As the region of interest shifts from the Pacific Northwest to <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, the region of enhanced tropical precipitation shifts further to the east. For example, extreme rainfall events in southern California are typically accompanied by enhanced precipitation near 170\xc2\xb0E. However, it is important to note that the overall link between the MJO and extreme west coast precipitation events weakens as the region of interest shifts southward along the west coast of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>There is case-to-case variability in the amplitude and longitudinal extent of the MJO-related precipitation, so this should be viewed as a general relationship only.<sup id="cite_ref-PACNW_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PACNW-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 28em; -webkit-column-width: 28em; column-width: 28em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-agu1-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-agu1_1-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web">Zhang, Chidong (2005). <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://dx.doi.org/10.1029/2004RG000158">"Madden-Julian Oscillation"</a><span class="reference-accessdate">.
