href="/wiki/1996_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1996 Pacific hurricane season">1996</a></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>1994 Pacific hurricane season</b> was the final season of the eastern north Pacific\'s most recent active string of hurricane seasons that unofficially started in <a href="/wiki/1981_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1981 Pacific hurricane season">1981</a>. The season officially started on May 15, 1994, in the eastern Pacific, and on June 1, 1994, in the central Pacific, and lasted until November 30, 1994. These dates conventionally delimit the period of each year when most tropical cyclones form in the northeastern Pacific Ocean. The first <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> formed on June 18, while the last system dissipated on October 26.<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup> This season, twenty-two tropical cyclones formed in the north Pacific Ocean east of the dateline, with all but two becoming tropical storms or hurricanes. A total of 10 hurricanes occurred, including five major hurricanes.</p>\n<p>Of note in this season is an unusual spree of very intense storms. Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emilia_(1994)" title="Hurricane Emilia (1994)">Emilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilma_(1994)" title="Hurricane Gilma (1994)">Gilma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">John</a>, and Olivia all reached a pressure below 930&#160;<a href="/wiki/Millibars" class="mw-redirect" title="Millibars">millibars</a>. Longevity-wise, no tropical cyclone of any basin had previously persisted for as long as Hurricane John, which lasted 31 days. Elsewhere, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rosa_(1994)" title="Hurricane Rosa (1994)">Hurricane Rosa</a> caused four casualties in Mexico as the basin\'s lone landfalling tropical storm or hurricane, and later was responsible for flooding in <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li cyclones</a> formed in the north Pacific Ocean east of the dateline. All but two of them became tropical storms or hurricanes. In the Eastern Pacific region (<a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140\xc2\xb0W</a> to North America), nineteen tropical depressions formed, of which seventeen became tropical storms, nine further intensifying into hurricanes, and five ultimately reaching major hurricanes of Category&#160;3 intensity or higher on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir Simpson Scale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup> These numbers are slightly above the long-term averages of fifteen tropical storms, nine hurricanes, and four major hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-EPac_background_info_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPac_background_info-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Area_of_responsibility" title="Area of responsibility">area of responsibility</a> (<a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140\xc2\xb0W</a> to the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>), three depressions, two tropical storms, and one hurricane formed. Overall, there were eleven tropical cyclones, eight tropical storms, five hurricanes, and three major hurricanes that formed or entered the Central Pacific region.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_main_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_main-3">[3]</a></sup> These numbers are well above the long-term average of four tropical cyclones, two hurricanes, one tropical storm, and two depressions.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC2006_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC2006-4">[4]</a></sup> The exceptionally high activity was contributed to by an <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> ongoing at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC1997_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC1997-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The only named storm to make landfall this year was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rosa_(1994)" title="Hurricane Rosa (1994)">Hurricane Rosa</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup> which killed four people in Western Mexico<sup id="cite_ref-Rosa2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa2-6">[6]</a></sup> and forced over 400 to be evacuated.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_Herald_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Herald-7">[7]</a></sup> Other notable storms include Hurricane Olivia, a high-end Category 4 system,<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup> the three Category&#160;5 hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Emilia_(1994)" title="Hurricane Emilia (1994)">Emilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gilma_(1994)" title="Hurricane Gilma (1994)">Gilma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_John_(1994)" title="Hurricane John (1994)">John</a>. Both John and Hurricane Li existed in two of the three <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclone basin">basins</a> (East, and West) of the Pacific Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>This season marked the end of the Northeastern Pacific\'s most recent active period, which began in <a href="/wiki/1982_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1982 Pacific hurricane season">1982</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Padgett_April_2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett_April_2004-9">[9]</a></sup> and includes the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_hurricanes#Highest" title="List of Pacific hurricanes">five most active Pacific hurricane seasons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Padgett_April_2004_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett_April_2004-9">[9]</a></sup> Beginning in <a href="/wiki/1995_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1995 Pacific hurricane season">1995</a>, multi-decadal factors switched to a phase that suppresses Pacific hurricane activity.<sup id="cite_ref-2008_Forecast_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2008_Forecast-10">[10]</a></sup> Since then, Pacific hurricane seasons have generally been below normal;<sup id="cite_ref-Historic_EPac_Seasonal_Activity_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historic_EPac_Seasonal_Activity-11">[11]</a></sup> the lone abnormally active season since then was in <a href="/wiki/1997_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1997 Pacific hurricane season">1997</a>, where a strong El Nino event was observed.<sup id="cite_ref-Padgett_April_2004_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Padgett_April_2004-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The 1994 Pacific hurricane season set several records. First, three hurricanes reached Category&#160;5 intensity on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-8">[8]</a></sup> setting a record later tied in <a href="/wiki/2002_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2002 Pacific hurricane season">2002</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup> Hurricane John lasted longer and spent more time tropical than any other tropical cyclone on Earth in recorded history.<sup id="cite_ref-e6_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e6-12">[12]</a></sup> Eleven tropical cyclones entered or formed in the central Pacific, a record shared with the <a href="/wiki/1992_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1992 Pacific hurricane season">1992 season</a> until <a href="/wiki/2015_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2015 Pacific hurricane season">the 2015 season</a> broke the record.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC1997_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC1997-5">[5]</a></sup> Finally, of the four most intense hurricanes (107)</th>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a> (ACE) is a measure of how active a hurricane season is. It is calculated by squaring the windspeed of a cyclone with at least tropical storm-force winds every six hours, summing the results, and dividing that total by 10<sup>4</sup>. As a tropical cyclone does not have gale-force winds until it becomes a tropical storm, tropical depressions are not included in these tables. For all storms, ACE is given to three <a href="/wiki/Significant_figures" title="Significant figures">significant figures</a>. The ACE in the east Pacific proper (<a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140\xc2\xb0W</a> to North America) is given; the ACE in the central Pacific (the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> to <a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140\xc2\xb0W</a>) is given in brackets. The table includes the ACE for Li and John only during those storm\'s time east of the dateline. Their ACE west of the dateline is part of the totals of the <a href="/wiki/1994_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1994 Pacific typhoon season">1994 typhoon season</a>.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> uses ACE to rank hurricane seasons as above-normal, near-normal, and below-normal. It defines below-normal as having an ACE less than 95*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup> kt<sup>2</sup>; It defines above normal as having an ACE above 150*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup> along with the numbers of any two of the following above average: tropical storms (15), hurricanes (9), or major hurricanes (4); It defines near-normal as having an ACE between 100*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup> and 150*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup>, or an ACE above 150*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup> with fewer than two of the numbers of the following above average: tropical storms (15), hurricanes (9), or major hurricanes (4).<sup id="cite_ref-EPac_background_info_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPac_background_info-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>This season has a total of seventeen tropical storms, nine hurricanes, and five major hurricanes. The total ACE of this season is 185*10<sup>4</sup>&#160;kt<sup>2</sup>. It strengthened to Tropical Storm Aletta the next day. It continued intensifying and reached its peak intensity on June 20. Vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> began to weaken the storm thereafter. The weakening trend continued, weakening Aletta to a depression on June 21. The system dissipated June 23. Aletta\'s remnant low, however, could be tracked on satellite images for days following the storm. The low finally dissipated north of Hawaii.<sup id="cite_ref-Aletta1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aletta1-14">[14]</a></sup> Aletta never affected land, and no damage or casualties were reported.<sup id="cite_ref-Aletta2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aletta2-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Bud">Tropical Storm Bud</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1994_Pacific_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Bud">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; 1003&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>)</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>Tropical Depression Two-E formed on June 27 about 575 miles (925&#160;km) south-southwest of the tip of the <a href="/wiki/Baja_California_Peninsula" title="Baja California Peninsula">Baja California Peninsula</a>. The depression headed west-northwest, gradually turned to the northwest, and strengthened into Tropical Storm Bud on June 27. Early the next day, Bud peaked in intensity. <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Shear</a> caused by a nearby <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">upper level low</a> slowly weakened Bud. Later on June 28, a second center of circulation developed. The two centers started a <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_interaction" class="mw-redirect" title="Fujiwhara interaction">Fujiwhara interaction</a>. The second center then became dominant and the first one vanished. This confused structure is similar to what happened to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Arlene_(1993)" title="Tropical Storm Arlene (1993)">1993\'s Tropical Storm Arlene</a>. This confused structure also weakened Bud to a tropical depression on the afternoon of the same day the second center formed. Bud then headed westward over cool waters and dissipated on June 29.<sup id="cite_ref-Bud1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bud1-16">[16]</a></sup> Tropical Storm Bud spent its entire life over the open ocean far from land areas. It quickly became Tropical Storm Carlotta, and a large <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> became visible. Because of this, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">NHC</a> upgraded the storm to a hurricane. Carlotta peaked in intensity on July 1, as a 105&#160;mph (169&#160;km/h) hurricane. It gradually weakened as it moved into cooler waters, dissipating on July 5. Carlotta did not threaten land.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlotta1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlotta1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Carlotta buffeted <a href="/wiki/Socorro_Island" title="Socorro Island">Socorro Island</a> with sustained winds of 39&#160;mph (63&#160;km/h) on June 30.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlotta1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlotta1-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Carlotta2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlotta2-19">[19]</a></sup> Other than there, Carlotta caused no damage or deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlotta2_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlotta2-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Daniel">Tropical Storm Daniel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1994_Pacific_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Daniel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; It moved west-northwest and strengthened into a hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-Emilia1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emilia1-22">[22]</a></sup> It entered the central Pacific on July 17. It continued intensifying, reaching Category&#160;5 intensity on July 19,<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-8">[8]</a></sup> the first Category&#160;5 Pacific hurricane since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ava_(1973)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Ava (1973)">Ava</a>. It became Tropical Storm Hector quickly, and as it paralleled the coast of Mexico, it began to weaken, dissipating on August 10. A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_watches_and_warnings" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclone watches and warnings">tropical storm watch</a> was issued for part of the peninsula on August 8. It was lifted later the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-Hector2_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hector2-37">[37]</a></sup> Hector\'s most significant impact was rain. The tropical storm dumped rain along a discontinuous zone of coastal and inland Mexico. The highest point maxima were 7.87 inches (200&#160;mm) at Cerro de Ortega/Ixtlahua and 7.60 inches (193&#160;mm) at Caduano/Santiago.<sup id="cite_ref-HPC_Hector_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HPC_Hector-38">[38]</a></sup> No damage or casualties were reported.<sup id="cite_ref-Hector2_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hector2-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Depression_One-C">Tropical Depression One-C</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1994_Pacific_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Tropical Depression One-C">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; It headed generally westward, and was upgraded into a tropical storm twelve hours after it formed and was named John. John fluctuated in strength as it headed west, always managing to stay at tropical storm strength. On August 20, steady intensification began, and John was a major hurricane when it entered the central Pacific. It continued westward, reaching Category&#160;5 intensity on August 23. It passed around 245 miles (394&#160;km) south of Hawaii, and passed just north of Johnston Atoll on August 26.<sup id="cite_ref-John1_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John1-45">[45]</a></sup> John stayed at hurricane intensity until it crossed the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">dateline</a> on August 28, becoming a typhoon of the <a href="/wiki/1994_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1994 Pacific typhoon season">1994 Pacific typhoon season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_8-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-8">[8]</a></sup> After weakening into a tropical storm, John recurved, looped, and recurved again.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWCJohn_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWCJohn-46">[46]</a></sup> It reintensified, and was a hurricane when it recrossed the dateline to reenter the central Pacific. John headed north-northeast until it went extratropical on September 10,<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_8-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-8">[8]</a></sup> thirty one days after it formed.<sup id="cite_ref-best_track_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-best_track-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Ahead of the hurricane, the 1100 people at <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a> <a href="/wiki/Emergency_evacuation" title="Emergency evacuation">evacuated</a>. On the atoll, John caused $15&#160;million (1994&#160;USD; $24.2&#160;million 2017&#160;USD) in damage. No deaths were reported. Other than on Johnston, Hurricane John had minor effects in Hawaii. The disturbance slowly headed westward and it organized into a tropical depression on September 22 while located about 720 miles (1,160&#160;km) south of the southern tip of the <a href="/wiki/Baja_California_Peninsula" title="Baja California Peninsula">Baja California Peninsula</a>. The depression headed west-northwestwards and strengthened into Tropical Storm Olivia on September 22. It steadily intensified and was a hurricane on September 24. It then rapidly strengthened into a powerful major hurricane. It slowly curled to the northwest as it was <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_observation" title="Tropical cyclone observation">observed</a> by NOAA research aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-Olivia1_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olivia1-56">[56]</a></sup> Olivia peaked in intensity on September 25. Meanwhile, a large <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> off the extreme <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">southern part of</a> California induced a northward path. As Olivia started a small anticyclonic loop, <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> began to weaken the hurricane. When Olivia was finished the loop, it had weakened to a tropical storm. It then headed westward. It weakened into a tropical depression on September 28 and dissipated the next day. No impact was reported.<sup id="cite_ref-Olivia2_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olivia2-57">[57]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At the time, Olivia was the third-most intense Pacific hurricane on record. It has since dropped to eleventh. The storm also had the lowest <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_4_Pacific_hurricanes" title="List of Category 4 Pacific hurricanes">Category 4 Pacific hurricane</a> on record. In <a href="/wiki/2001_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Juliette" title="2001 Pacific hurricane season">2001</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juliette" title="Hurricane Juliette">Hurricane Juliette</a> joined Olivia as the most intense Cat. 4 on record. In <a href="/wiki/2014_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Odile" title="2014 Pacific hurricane season">2014</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Odile" title="Hurricane Odile">Hurricane Odile</a> beat both storms when it attained a minimum pressure of 918&#160;mbars. Olivia was also the most intense September hurricane. Since, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Linda">Hurricane Linda</a> beat Olivia. It had trouble organizing, and advisories were discontinued for a while. The cyclone finally became a tropical storm on October 11 and was named Rosa. It moved glacially, but eventually a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> steered Rosa north and then northeast. Rosa intensified quickly, peaking at Category&#160;2 intensity just before landfall near <a href="/w/index.php?title=La_Concepci%C3%B3n,_Veracruz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="La Concepci\xc3\xb3n, Veracruz (page does not exist)">La Concepci\xc3\xb3n</a> on the morning of October 14. Rosa quickly decayed over the mountains of Mexico, and its cloud shield rapidly accelerated northward through the United States, spreading moisture.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosa1_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa1-60">[60]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On October 12, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane watch</a> was issued for the coast from <a href="/wiki/Culiac%C3%A1n" title="Culiac\xc3\xa1n">Culiac\xc3\xa1n</a> to <a href="/wiki/Manzanillo,_Colima" title="Manzanillo, Colima">Manzanillo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baja_California_Peninsula" title="Baja California Peninsula">Baja California Peninsula</a> south of <a href="/wiki/24th_parallel_north" title="24th parallel north">latitude 24\xc2\xb0N</a>. At the same time, a tropical storm warning was issued from Manzanillo to <a href="/wiki/Tepic" title="Tepic">Tepic</a>. On October 14, a hurricane warning was issued for the coast between Culiac\xc3\xa1n and <a href="/wiki/Cabo_Corrientes,_Jalisco" title="Cabo Corrientes, Jalisco">Cabo Corrientes, Jalisco</a>, and a tropical storm warning south of Cabo Corrientes to Manzanillo. All watches and warnings were lifted later that day.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosa5_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa5-61">[61]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Four deaths, two in each of <a href="/wiki/Nayarit" title="Nayarit">Nayarit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Durango" title="Durango">Durango</a>, were reported. Four people were missing in <a href="/wiki/Sinaloa" title="Sinaloa">Sinaloa</a>. All of the deaths were due to <a href="/wiki/Drowning" title="Drowning">drowning</a>. More than 100,000&#160;people had their homes damaged in Nayarit. <a href="/wiki/Telephone_pole" class="mw-redirect" title="Telephone pole">Telephone poles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">power lines</a> were downed in Sinaloa. Rain caused <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash-flooding</a> in mountainous areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Rosa2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rosa2-6">[6]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>, mudslides forced the evacuation of 400 people from two coastal villages.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> The highest rainfall total in Mexico was 14.09 inches (358&#160;mm) at Mesa de Pedro Pablo.<sup id="cite_ref-HPCRosa_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HPCRosa-63">[63]</a></sup> The moisture Rosa sent into the United States was a contributing factor in record rains in parts of southeastern Texas from October 15 to 19. Those rains caused flooding that killed 22 people, destroyed over 3000 homes, and caused US$700&#160;million in damage.<sup id="cite_ref-USGS_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USGS-64">[64]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Storm_Nona">Tropical Storm Nona</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1994_Pacific_hurricane_season&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Tropical Storm Nona">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold;
