href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juliette_(2001)" title="Hurricane Juliette (2001)">Hurricane Juliette</a>, a rare case of triple eyewalls.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Eyewall replacement cycles</b>, also called <b>concentric eyewall cycles</b>, naturally occur in intense <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, generally with winds greater than 185&#160;km/h (115&#160;mph), or major hurricanes (<a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> or above). When tropical cyclones reach this intensity, and the eyewall contracts or is already sufficiently small, some of the outer rainbands may strengthen and organize into a ring of thunderstorms\xe2\x80\x94an outer eyewall\xe2\x80\x94that slowly moves inward and robs the inner eyewall of its needed moisture and <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">angular momentum</a>. Since the strongest winds are in a cyclone\'s <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall</a>, the tropical cyclone usually weakens during this phase, as the inner wall is "choked" by the outer wall. Eventually the outer eyewall replaces the inner one completely, and the storm may re-intensify.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The discovery of this process was partially responsible for the end of the U.S. government\'s hurricane modification experiment <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a>. This project set out to <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">seed clouds</a> outside the eyewall, apparently causing a new eyewall to form and weakening the storm. When it was discovered that this was a natural process due to hurricane dynamics, the project was quickly abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_D8_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_D8-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Almost every intense hurricane undergoes at least one of these cycles during its existence. Recent studies have shown that nearly half of all tropical cyclones, and nearly all cyclones with sustained winds over 204 kilometres per hour (127&#160;mph; 110&#160;kn), undergo eyewall replacement cycles.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1982_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1982-3">[3]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Allen" title="Hurricane Allen">Hurricane Allen</a> in 1980 went through repeated eyewall replacement cycles, fluctuating between Category&#160;5 and Category&#160;3 status on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a> several times. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Juliette_(2001)" title="Hurricane Juliette (2001)">Hurricane Juliette</a> (2001) was a rare documented case of triple eyewalls.<sup id="cite_ref-Triple_Eyewalls_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Triple_Eyewalls-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/1975_Pacific_typhoon_season#Super_Typhoon_June_.28Rosing.29" title="1975 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon June</a> (1975) was the first reported case of triple eyewalls.<sup id="cite_ref-shanmin1987_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shanmin1987-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg/220px-Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg/330px-Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg/440px-Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="521" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Project_Stormfury_crew.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n1966 photo of the crew and personnel of Project Stormfury.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The first tropical system to be observed with concentric eyewalls was <a href="/wiki/1956_Pacific_typhoon_season#Typhoon_Sarah" title="1956 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon Sarah</a> by Fortner in 1956, which he described as "an eye within an eye".<sup id="cite_ref-fortner1956_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortner1956-6">[6]</a></sup> The storm was observed by a reconnaissance aircraft to have an inner eyewall at 6 kilometres (3.7&#160;mi) and an outer eyewall at 28 kilometres (17&#160;mi). During a subsequent flight 8 hours later, the inner eyewall had disappeared, the outer eyewall had reduced to 16 kilometres (9.9&#160;mi) and the maximum sustained winds and hurricane intensity had decreased.<sup id="cite_ref-fortner1956_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortner1956-6">[6]</a></sup> The next hurricane observed to have concentric eyewalls was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Donna" title="Hurricane Donna">Hurricane Donna</a> in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-jordan1961_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jordan1961-7">[7]</a></sup> Radar from reconnaissance aircraft showed an inner eye that varied from 10 miles (16&#160;km) at low altitude to 13 miles (21&#160;km) near the tropopause. In between the two eyewalls was an area of clear skies that extended vertically from 3,000 feet (910&#160;m) to 25,000 feet (7,600&#160;m). The low-level clouds at around 3,000 feet (910&#160;m) were described as stratocumulus with concentric horizontal rolls. The outer eyewall was reported to reach heights near 45,000 feet (14,000&#160;m) while the inner eyewall only extended to 30,000 feet (9,100&#160;m). 12 hours after identifying concentric eyewalls, the inner eyewall had dissipated.<sup id="cite_ref-jordan1961_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jordan1961-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Beulah" title="Hurricane Beulah">Hurricane Beulah</a> in 1967 was the first tropical cyclone to have its eyewall replacement cycle observed from beginning to end.<sup id="cite_ref-hoose1970_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoose1970-8">[8]</a></sup> Previous observations of concentric eyewalls were from aircraft-based platforms. Beulah was observed from the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> land-based radar for 34 hours during which time a double eyewall formed and dissipated. It was noted that Beulah reached maximum intensity immediately prior to undergoing the eyewall replacement cycle, and that it was "probably more than a coincidence."<sup id="cite_ref-hoose1970_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoose1970-8">[8]</a></sup> Previous eyewall replacement cycles had been observed to decrease the intensity of the storm,<sup id="cite_ref-fortner1956_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fortner1956-6">[6]</a></sup> but at this time the dynamics of why it occurred was not known.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>As early as 1946 it was known that the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> ice or <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a> into clouds that contained supercooled water would convert some of the droplets into ice followed by the <a href="/wiki/Bergeron%E2%80%93Findeisen_process" class="mw-redirect" title="Bergeron\xe2\x80\x93Findeisen process">Bergeron\xe2\x80\x93Findeisen process</a> of growth of the ice particles at the expense of the droplets, the water of which would all end up in large ice particles. The increased rate of precipitation would result in dissipation of the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1985_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1985-9">[9]</a></sup> By early 1960, the <a href="/wiki/Scientific_theory" title="Scientific theory">working theory</a> was that the eyewall of a hurricane was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inertial_instability&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inertial instability (page does not exist)">inertially unstable</a> and that the clouds had a large amount of supercooled water. Therefore, seeding the storm outside the eyewall would release more <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> and cause the eyewall to expand. The expansion of the eyewall would be accompanied with a decrease in the maximum wind speed through <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation of angular momentum">conservation of angular momentum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1985_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1985-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Project_Stormfury">Project Stormfury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Project Stormfury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a></div>\n<p>Project Stormfury was an attempt to weaken <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> by flying aircraft into them and <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">seeding</a> with <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a>. The project was run by the United States Government from 1962 to 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-stormfury_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stormfury-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The hypothesis was that the silver iodide would cause <a href="/wiki/Supercooling" title="Supercooling">supercooled</a> <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in the storm to freeze, disrupting the inner structure of the hurricane. This led to the seeding of several Atlantic hurricanes. However, it was later shown that this hypothesis was incorrect.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1985_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1985-9">[9]</a></sup> In reality, it was determined, most hurricanes do not contain enough supercooled water for cloud seeding to be effective. Additionally, researchers found that unseeded hurricanes often undergo the eyewall replacement cycles that were expected from seeded hurricanes. This finding called Stormfury\'s successes into question, as the changes reported now had a natural explanation.<sup id="cite_ref-stormfury_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stormfury-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The last experimental flight was flown in 1971, due to a lack of candidate storms and a changeover in <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>\'s fleet. More than a decade after the last modification experiment, Project Stormfury was officially canceled. Although a failure in its goal of reducing the destructiveness of hurricanes, Project Stormfury was not without merit. The observational data and storm lifecycle research generated by Stormfury helped improve meteorologists\' ability to <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">forecast</a> the movement and intensity of future hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1985_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1985-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secondary_eyewall_formation">Secondary eyewall formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Secondary eyewall formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg/220px-TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg" width="220" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg/330px-TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg/440px-TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg 2x" data-file-width="852" data-file-height="821" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TRMM_Frances_30aug1021_utc_lrg.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nImagery from <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Rainfall_Measuring_Mission" title="Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission">Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission</a> shows the beginning of an eyewall replacement cycle in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Frances" title="Hurricane Frances">Hurricane Frances</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Secondary eyewalls were once considered a rare phenomenon. Since the advent of reconnaissance airplanes and microwave satellite data, it has been observed that over half of all major tropical cyclones develop at least one secondary eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1982_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1982-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hawkins2008_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hawkins2008-11">[11]</a></sup> There have been many hypotheses that attempt to explain the formation of secondary eyewalls. The reason why hurricanes develop secondary eyewalls is not well understood.<sup id="cite_ref-terwey2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terwey2008-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identification">Identification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Identification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Qualitatively identifying secondary eyewalls is easy for a hurricane analyst to do. It involves looking at satellite or radar imagery and seeing if there are two concentric rings of enhanced convection. The outer eyewall is generally almost circular and concentric with the inner eyewall. Quantitative analysis is more difficult since there exists no objective definition of what a secondary eyewall is. Kossin <i>et al.</i>. specified that the outer ring had to be visibly separated from the inner eye with at least 75% closed with a moat region clear of clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-kossin2009_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kossin2009-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While secondary eyewalls have been seen as a tropical cyclone is nearing land, none have been observed while the eye is not over the ocean. July offers the best background environmental conditions for development of a secondary eyewall.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Changes in the intensity of strong hurricanes such as Katrina, Ophelia, and Rita occurred simultaneously with eyewall replacement cycles and comprised interactions between the eyewalls, rainbands and outside environments.<sup id="cite_ref-kossin2009_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kossin2009-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-houze2007_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-houze2007-14">[14]</a></sup> Eyewall replacement cycles, such as occurred in Rita as it approached the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="Gulf Coast of the United States">Gulf Coast of the United States</a>, can greatly increase the size of tropical cyclones while simultaneously decreasing in strength.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the period from 1997\xe2\x80\x932006, 45 eyewall replacement cycles were observed in the tropical North Atlantic Ocean, 12 in the Eastern North Pacific and 2 in the Western North Pacific. 12% of all Atlantic storms and 5% of storms in the Pacific underwent eyewall replacement during this time period. In the North Atlantic, 70% of major hurricanes had at least one eyewall replacement, compared to 33% of all storms. In the Pacific, 33% of major hurricanes and 16% of all hurricanes had an eyewall replacement cycle. Stronger storms have a higher probability of forming a secondary eyewall, with 60% of category 5 hurricanes underwent an eyewall replacement cycle within 12 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-kossin2009_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kossin2009-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the years 1969-1971, 93 storms reached tropical storm strength or greater in the Pacific Ocean. 8 of the 15 that reached super typhoon strength (65&#160;m/s), 11 of the 49 storms that reached typhoon strength (33&#160;m/s), and none of the 29 tropical storms (&lt;33&#160;m/s) developed concentric eyewalls. The authors note that because the reconnaissance aircraft were not specifically looking for double eyewall features, these numbers are likely underestimates.<sup id="cite_ref-willoughby1982_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-willoughby1982-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the years 1949-1983, 1268 typhoons were observed in the Western Pacific. 76 of these had concentric eyewalls. Of all the typhoons that underwent eyewall replacement, around 60% did so only once; 40% had more than one eyewall replacement cycle, with two of the typhoons each experiencing five eyewall replacements. The number of storms with eyewall replacement cycles was strongly correlated with the strength of the storm. Stronger typhoons were much more likely to have concentric eyewalls. There were no cases of double eyewalls where the maximum sustained wind was less than 45&#160;m/s or the minimum pressure was higher than 970 hPa. More than three-quarters of the typhoons that had pressures lower than 970 hPa developed the double eyewall feature. The majority of Western and Central Pacific typhoons that experience double eyewalls do so in the vicinity of Guam.<sup id="cite_ref-shanmin1987_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shanmin1987-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_formation_hypotheses">Early formation hypotheses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Early formation hypotheses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png/220px-Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png/330px-Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png/440px-Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haima_2016-10-19_0340Z.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nConcentric eyewalls seen in <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haima" title="Typhoon Haima">Typhoon Haima</a> as it travels west across the Pacific Ocean.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Since eyewall replacement cycles were discovered to be natural, there has been a strong interest in trying to identify what causes them. There have been many hypotheses put forth that are now abandoned. In 1980, Hurricane Allen crossed the mountainous region of Haiti and simultaneously developed a secondary eyewall. Hawkins noted this and hypothesized that the secondary eyewall may have been caused by topographic forcing.<sup id="cite_ref-hawkins1983_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hawkins1983-16">[16]</a></sup> Willoughby suggested that a resonance between the inertial period and asymmetric friction may be the cause of secondary eyewalls.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Later modeling studies and observations have shown that outer eyewalls may develop in areas uninfluenced by land processes.</p>\n<p>There have been many hypotheses suggesting a link between synoptic scale features and secondary eyewall replacement. It has been observed that radially inward traveling wave-like disturbances have preceded the rapid development of tropical disturbances to tropical cyclones. It has been hypothesized that this synoptic scale internal forcing could lead to a secondary eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-molinari1985_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-molinari1985-18">[18]</a></sup> Rapid deepening of the tropical low in connection with synoptic scale forcing has been observed in multiple storms,<sup id="cite_ref-molinari1989_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-molinari1989-19">[19]</a></sup> but has been shown to not be a necessary condition for the formation of a secondary eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-terwey2008_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terwey2008-12">[12]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Wind-induced_surface_heat_exchange" title="Wind-induced surface heat exchange">wind-induced surface heat exchange</a> (WISHE) is a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> mechanism between the ocean and atmosphere in which a stronger ocean-to-atmosphere heat flux results in a stronger atmospheric circulation, which results in a strong heat flux.<sup id="cite_ref-wishe_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wishe-20">[20]</a></sup> WISHE has been proposed as a method of generating secondary eyewalls.<sup id="cite_ref-nong2003_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nong2003-21">[21]</a></sup> Later work has shown that while WISHE is a necessary condition to amplify disturbances, it is not needed to generate them.<sup id="cite_ref-terwey2008_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terwey2008-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vortex_Rossby_wave_hypothesis">Vortex Rossby wave hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vortex Rossby wave hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the vortex Rossby wave hypothesis, the waves travel radially outward from the inner vortex. The waves amplify angular momentum at a radius that is dependent on the radial velocity matching that of the outside flow. At this point, the two are phase-locked and allow the coalescence of the waves to form a secondary eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-houze2007_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-houze2007-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vortexrossbywave_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vortexrossbywave-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="\xce\xb2-skirt_axisymmetrization_hypothesis"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".CE.B2-skirt_axisymmetrization_hypothesis">\xce\xb2-skirt axisymmetrization hypothesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: \xce\xb2-skirt axisymmetrization hypothesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In a fluid system, \xce\xb2 (beta) is the spatial, usually horizontal, change in the environmental vertical vorticity. \xce\xb2 is maximized in the eyewall of a tropical cyclone. The \xce\xb2-skirt axisymmetrization (BSA) assumes that a tropical cyclone about to develop a secondary eye will have a decreasing, but non-negative \xce\xb2 that extends from the eyewall to approximately 50 kilometres (30&#160;mi) to 100 kilometres (60&#160;mi) from the eyewall. In this region, there is a small, but important \xce\xb2. This area is called the \xce\xb2-skirt. Outward of the skirt, \xce\xb2 is effectively zero.<sup id="cite_ref-terwey2008_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terwey2008-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">Convective available potential energy</a> (CAPE) is the amount of energy a parcel of air would have if lifted a certain distance vertically through the atmosphere. The higher the CAPE, the more likely there will be convection. If areas of high CAPE exist in the \xce\xb2-skirt, the deep convection that forms would act as a source of vorticity and <a href="/wiki/Turbulence_kinetic_energy" title="Turbulence kinetic energy">turbulence kinetic energy</a>. This small-scale energy will upscale into a jet around the storm. The low-level jet focuses the stochastic energy a nearly axisymmetric ring around the eye. Once this low-level jet forms, a positive feedback cycle such as <a href="/wiki/WISHE" class="mw-redirect" title="WISHE">WISHE</a> can amplify the initial perturbations into a secondary eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-terwey2008_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-terwey2008-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-elsberry2008_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elsberry2008-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_the_inner_eyewall">Death of the inner eyewall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death of the inner eyewall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_profile.svg" class="image"><img alt="Hurricane profile.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/220px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/330px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Hurricane_profile.svg/440px-Hurricane_profile.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1367" data-file-height="689" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_profile.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>After the secondary eyewall totally surrounds the inner eyewall, it begins to affect the tropical cyclone dynamics. Hurricanes are fueled by the high ocean temperature. Sea surface temperatures immediately underneath a tropical cyclone can be several degrees cooler than those at the periphery of a storm, and therefore cyclones are dependent upon receiving the energy from the ocean from the inward spiraling winds. When an outer eyewall is formed, the moisture and angular momentum necessary for the maintenance of the inner eyewall is now being used to sustain the outer eyewall, causing the inner eye to weaken and dissipate, leaving the tropical cyclone with one eye that is larger in diameter than the previous eye.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg/220px-Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg/330px-Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg/440px-Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="800" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Phailin_F-17_91H_microwave_pass_12_October_2013_0038z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA microwave pass of <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Phailin" title="Cyclone Phailin">Cyclone Phailin</a> revealing the moat between the inner and outer eyewalls.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In the moat region between the inner and outer eyewall, observations by dropsondes have shown high temperatures and dewpoint depressions. The eyewall contracts because of inertial instability.<sup id="cite_ref-shapiro1982_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro1982-24">[24]</a></sup> Contraction of the eyewall occurs if the area of convection occurs outside the radius of maximum winds. After the outer eyewall forms, subsidence increases rapidly in the moat region.<sup id="cite_ref-rozoff2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rozoff2008-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Once the inner eyewall dissipates, the storm weakens; the central pressure increases and the maximum sustained windspeed decreases. Rapid changes in the intensity of tropical cyclones is a typical characteristic of eyewall replacement cycles.<sup id="cite_ref-rozoff2008_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rozoff2008-25">[25]</a></sup> Compared to the processes involved with the formation of the secondary eyewall, the death of the inner eyewall is fairly well understood.</p>\n<p>Some tropical cyclones with extremely large outer eyewalls do not experience the contraction of the outer eye and subsequent dissipation of the inner eye. Typhoon Winnie (1997) developed an outer eyewall with a diameter of 200 kilometres (120&#160;mi) that did not dissipate until it reached the shoreline.<sup id="cite_ref-lander1999_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lander1999-26">[26]</a></sup> The time required for the eyewall to collapse is inversely related to the diameter of the eyewall which is mostly because inward directed wind decreases asymptotically to zero with distance from the radius of maximum winds, but also due to the distance required to collapse the eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-shapiro1982_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro1982-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Throughout the entire vertical layer of the moat, there is dry descending air. The dynamics of the moat region are similar to the eye, while the outer eyewall takes on the dynamics of the primary eyewall. The vertical structure of the eye has two layers. The largest layer is that from the top of the tropopause to a capping layer around 700 hPa which is described by descending warm air. Below the capping layer, the air is moist and has convection with the presence of stratocumulus clouds. The moat gradually takes on the characteristics of the eye, upon which the inner eyewall can only dissipate in strength as the majority of the inflow is now being used to maintain the outer eyewall. The inner eye is eventually evaporated as it is warmed by the surrounding dry air in the moat and eye. Models and observations show that once the outer eyewall completely surrounds the inner eye, it takes less than 12 hours for the complete dissipation of the inner eyewall. The inner eyewall feeds mostly upon the moist air in the lower portion of the eye before evaporating.<sup id="cite_ref-houze2007_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-houze2007-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution_into_an_annular_hurricane">Evolution into an annular hurricane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Evolution into an annular hurricane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Annular_tropical_cyclone" title="Annular tropical cyclone">Annular tropical cyclone</a></div>\n<p>Annular hurricanes have a single eyewall that is larger and circularly symmetric. Observations show that an eyewall replacement cycle can lead to the development of an annular hurricane. While some hurricanes develop into annular hurricanes without an eyewall replacement, it has been hypothesized that the dynamics leading to the formation of a secondary eyewall may be similar to those needed for development of an annular eye.<sup id="cite_ref-kossin2009_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kossin2009-13">[13]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Daniel_(2006)" title="Hurricane Daniel (2006)">Hurricane Daniel (2006)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Winnie_(1997)" title="Typhoon Winnie (1997)">Typhoon Winnie (1997)</a> were examples where a storm had an eyewall replacement cycle and then turned into an annular hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-knaff2008_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-knaff2008-27">[27]</a></sup> Annular hurricanes have been simulated that have gone through the life cycle of an eyewall replacement. The simulations show that the major rainbands will grow such that the arms will overlap, and then it spiral into itself to form a concentric eyewall. The inner eyewall dissipates, leaving a hurricane with a singular large eye with no rainbands.<sup id="cite_ref-zhou2009_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zhou2009-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eyewall_replacement_cycle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation journal">Sitkowski, Matthew; Kossin, James P.; Rozoff, Christopher M.
