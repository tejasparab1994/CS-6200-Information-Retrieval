Basin</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Tropical cyclone track forecasting</b> involves predicting where a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> is going to track over the next five days, every 6 to 12&#160;hours. The history of tropical cyclone track forecasting has evolved from a single-station approach to a comprehensive approach which uses a variety of meteorological tools and methods to make predictions. The weather of a particular location can show signs of the approaching tropical cyclone, such as increasing swell, increasing cloudiness, falling barometric pressure, increasing tides, <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squalls</a>, and heavy rainfall.</p>\n<p>The forces that affect tropical cyclone steering are the higher-latitude westerlies, the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>, and the beta effect caused by changes of the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">coriolis force</a> within fluids such as the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>. Accurate track predictions depend on determining the position and strength of <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure area">high</a>- and <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure areas</a>, and predicting how those areas will migrate during the life of a tropical system. Computer forecast models are used to help determine this motion as far out as five to seven days in the future.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toclevel-1 tocsection-10"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">8</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The methods through which tropical cyclones are forecast have changed with the passage of time. The first known forecasts in the Western Hemisphere were made by Lt. Col. William Reed of the <a href="/wiki/Corps_of_Royal_Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="Corps of Royal Engineers">Corps of Royal Engineers</a> at Barbados in 1847. Reed mostly utilized <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> measurements as the basis of his forecasts. Benito Vi\xc3\xb1es, <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">S.J.</a>, introduced a forecast and warning system based on cloud cover changes in <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a> during the 1870s. Forecasting hurricane motion was based on tide movements, as well as cloud and barometer changes over time. In 1895, it was noted that cool conditions with unusually high pressure preceded tropical cyclones in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> by several days. Before the early 1900s, most forecasts were done by direct observations at weather stations, which were then relayed to forecast centers via <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a>. It was not until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> in the early twentieth century that observations from ships at sea were available to forecasters. Despite the issuance of hurricane watches and warnings for systems threatening the coast, forecasting the path of tropical cyclones did not occur until 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-WJ_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WJ-1">[1]</a></sup> By 1922, it was known that the winds at 3 kilometres (9,800&#160;ft) to 4 kilometres (13,000&#160;ft) in height above the sea surface within the storms\' right front quadrant were representative of a storm\'s steering, and that hurricanes tended to follow the outermost closed isobar of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wb59_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 1937, radiosondes were used to aide tropical cyclone forecasting.<sup id="cite_ref-wb59_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-2">[2]</a></sup> The next decade saw the advent of aircraft-based reconnaissance by the military, starting with the first dedicated flight into a hurricane in 1943, and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> in 1944. In the 1950s, coastal <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radars</a> began to be used in the United States, and research reconnaissance flights by the precursor of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Oceanographic_and_Meteorological_Laboratory" title="Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratory">Hurricane Research Division</a> began in 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-MWR_NHC_history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWR_NHC_history-3">[3]</a></sup> The launch of the first weather satellite, TIROS-I, in 1960, introduced new techniques to tropical cyclone forecasting that remain important to the present day. In the 1970s, buoys were introduced to improve the resolution of surface measurements, which until that point, were not available at all over sea surfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-MWR_NHC_history_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWR_NHC_history-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Single_station_forecasting_of_a_tropical_cyclone_passage">Single station forecasting of a tropical cyclone passage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Single station forecasting of a tropical cyclone passage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/250px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg" width="250" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/375px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg/500px-Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1788" data-file-height="1168" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_cyclone_eyewall_P-3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nPicture of the sky within the eye of a tropical cyclone</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>About four days in advance of a typical tropical cyclone, an ocean of 1 metre (3.3&#160;ft) in height will roll in about every 10&#160;seconds, moving towards the coast from the direction of the tropical cyclone\'s location. The ocean swell will slowly increase in height and frequency the closer a tropical cyclone gets to land. Two days in advance of the center\'s passage, winds go calm as the tropical cyclone interrupts the environmental wind flow. Within 36&#160;hours of the center passage, the pressure begins to fall and a veil of white cirrus clouds approaches from the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>\'s direction. Within 24&#160;hours of the closest approach to the center, low clouds begin to move in, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Bar_(tropical_cyclone)" title="Bar (tropical cyclone)">bar</a> of a tropical cyclone, as the barometric pressure begins to fall more rapidly and the winds begin to increase. Within 18&#160;hours of the center\'s approach, <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squally</a> weather is common, with sudden increases in wind accompanied by rain showers or thunderstorms. Winds increase within 12&#160;hours of the center\'s approach, occasionally reaching hurricane force. The ocean\'s surface becomes whipped with foam. Small items begin flying in the wind. Within 6&#160;hours of the center\'s arrival, rain becomes continuous and the <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> begins to come inland. Within an hour of the center, the rain becomes very heavy and the highest winds within the tropical cyclone are experienced. When the center arrives with a strong tropical cyclone, weather conditions improve and the sun becomes visible as the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> moves overhead. At this point, the pressure ceases to drop as the lowest pressure within the storm\'s center is reached. This is also when the peak depth of the storm surge occurs. Once the system departs, winds reverse and, along with the rain, suddenly increase. The storm surge retreats as the pressure suddenly rises in the wake of its center. One day after the center\'s passage, the low overcast is replaced with a higher overcast, and the rain becomes intermittent. By 36&#160;hours after the center\'s passage, the high overcast breaks and the pressure begins to level off.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basics">Basics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The large scale <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Synoptic scale">synoptic scale</a> flow determines 70 to 90&#160;percent of a tropical cyclone\'s motion. The deep-layered mean flow through the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> is considered to be the best tool in determining track direction and speed. If storms experience significant vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, use of a lower level wind such as the 700&#160;hPa pressure level (at a height of 3,000 metres (9,800&#160;ft) above sea level) will work out as a better predictor. Knowledge of the beta effect can be used to steer a tropical cyclone, since it leads to a more northwest heading for tropical cyclones in the Northern Hemisphere due to differences in the coriolis force around the cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> For example, the beta effect will allow a tropical cyclone to track poleward and slightly to the right of the deep layer steering flow while the system lies the south of the subtropical ridge. Northwest moving storms move quicker and left, while northeast moving storms move slower and left. The larger the cyclone, the larger the impact of the beta effect is likely to be.<sup id="cite_ref-NAVY_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Binaryinteraction.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Binaryinteraction.svg/250px-Binaryinteraction.svg.png" width="250" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Binaryinteraction.svg/375px-Binaryinteraction.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Binaryinteraction.svg/500px-Binaryinteraction.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="414" data-file-height="247" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Binaryinteraction.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nInteraction of two typhoons</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fujiwhara_effect">Fujiwhara effect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fujiwhara effect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Fujiwhara_effect" title="Fujiwhara effect">Fujiwhara effect</a></div>\n<p>When two or more tropical cyclones are in proximity to one another, they begin to rotate cyclonically around the midpoint between their circulation centers. In the northern hemisphere, this is in a counterclockwise direction, and in the southern hemisphere, a clockwise direction. Usually, the tropical cyclones need to be within 1,450 kilometres (900&#160;mi) of each other for this effect to take place. It is a more common phenomenon in the northern Pacific Ocean than elsewhere, due to the higher frequency of tropical cyclone activity which occurs in that region.<sup id="cite_ref-TBK_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trochoidal_motions">Trochoidal motions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Trochoidal motions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Small wobbles in a tropical cyclone\'s track can occur when the convection is distributed unevenly within its circulation. This can be due to changes in vertical wind shear or inner core structure.<sup id="cite_ref-TBK_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-7">[7]</a></sup> Because of this effect, forecasters use a longer term (6 to 24&#160;hours) motion to help forecast tropical cyclones, which acts to smooth out such wobbles.<sup id="cite_ref-NAVY_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NAVY-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecast_models">Forecast models</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Forecast models">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/250px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png" width="250" height="178" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/375px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/500px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 2x" data-file-width="1282" data-file-height="914" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSignificant errors in track still occur on occasion, as seen in one of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ernesto_(2006)" title="Hurricane Ernesto (2006)">Ernesto\'s (2006)</a> early forecasts. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>\'s official forecast is in light blue.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_prediction_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclone prediction model">Tropical cyclone prediction model</a></div>\n<p>High-speed computers and sophisticated simulation software allow <a href="/wiki/Meteorologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a> to run <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_prediction_model" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclone prediction model">computer models</a> that forecast tropical cyclone tracks based on the future position and strength of high- and low-pressure systems. Combining forecast models with increased understanding of the forces that act on tropical cyclones, and a wealth of data from Earth-orbiting satellites and other sensors, scientists have increased the accuracy of track forecasts over recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_forecast_verifications_models_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_forecast_verifications_models-8">[8]</a></sup> The addition of <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropwindsonde</a> missions around tropical cyclones in what are known as synoptic flow missions in the Atlantic Basin decreased track error by 15\xe2\x80\x9320 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Using a consensus of forecast models, as well as ensemble members of the various models, can help reduce forecast error.<sup id="cite_ref-TBK_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-7">[7]</a></sup> However, regardless how small the average error becomes, large errors within the guidance are still possible.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> An accurate track forecast is important, because if the track forecast is incorrect, forecasts for intensity, rainfall, storm surge, and tornado threat will also be incorrect.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Length_of_forecast_period">Length of forecast period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Length of forecast period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone_warnings" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Atlantic tropical cyclone warnings">History of Atlantic tropical cyclone warnings</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Katrina2005forecast.GIF" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Katrina2005forecast.GIF/250px-Katrina2005forecast.GIF" width="250" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Katrina2005forecast.GIF/375px-Katrina2005forecast.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Katrina2005forecast.GIF/500px-Katrina2005forecast.GIF 2x" data-file-width="895" data-file-height="716" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Katrina2005forecast.GIF" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA three-day <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> track forecast for Katrina in 2005</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Forecasts within hurricane advisories were issued one day into the future in 1954 before being extended to two days into the future in 1961, and three days into the future in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-verify_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verify-11">[11]</a></sup> Starting in the mid to late 1990s, research into tropical cyclones and how forecast models handle the systems led to substantial improvements in track error.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> By 2001, the error had reduced sufficiently to extend track out to 5&#160;days in the future on public advisories. In addition, at 1700&#160;UTC during the hurricane season, a medium-range coordination call takes place between the <a href="/wiki/Hydrometeorological_Prediction_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrometeorological Prediction Center">Hydrometeorological Prediction Center</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> to coordinate tropical cyclone placement on the medium-range pressure forecasts 6 and 7&#160;days into the future for the northeast Pacific and Atlantic basins. Every so often, even at this time range, successful predictions can be made.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In forecasts, the National Hurricane Center uses a track forecast cone for the graphical representation of the uncertainty in its forecasts of a tropical cyclone\'s future location. The cone represents the probable position of a tropical cyclone\'s <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">circulation center</a>, and is made by drawing a set of <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">circles</a> centered at each forecast point\xe2\x80\x9412, 24, 36, 48, and 72 hours for a three-day forecast, as well as 96 and 120 hours for a five-day forecast. The radius of each circle is equal to encompass two-thirds of the historical official forecast errors for the preceding five-year period. The cone is then constructed by drawing a <a href="/wiki/Tangent_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Tangent line">tangent line</a> that connects the outside boundary of all the circles. The National Hurricane Center states that the entire track of the tropical cyclone "can be expected to remain within the cone roughly 60\xe2\x80\x9370% of the time."<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_cone_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_cone-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
