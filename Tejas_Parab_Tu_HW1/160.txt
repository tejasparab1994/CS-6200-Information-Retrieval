As the air cools, it becomes denser and moves towards the ground, as warm air becomes less dense and moves upwards. Subsiding air, when cool, is subject to <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a> warming, which tends to cause the evaporation of any clouds that might be present.</p>\n<p>Subsidence generally causes high <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> as more air moves into the same space: the <a href="/wiki/Polar_high" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar high">polar highs</a> are areas of almost constant subsidence, as are the <a href="/wiki/Horse_latitudes" title="Horse latitudes">horse latitudes</a>, and the areas of subsidence are the sources of much of the world\'s <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_wind" class="mw-redirect" title="Prevailing wind">prevailing wind</a>.</p>\n<p>Subsidence also causes many smaller-scale <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> phenomena, such as morning <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>. An extreme form of subsidence is a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>, which can result in damage similar to that produced by a <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a>.
