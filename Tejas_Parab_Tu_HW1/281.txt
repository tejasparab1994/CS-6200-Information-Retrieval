Meridian.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>180th meridian</b> or <b>antimeridian</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> is the <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> 180\xc2\xb0 east or west of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian#International_prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">Prime Meridian</a>, with which it forms a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a> dividing the earth into the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">Eastern Hemispheres</a>. It is common to both east <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and west longitude. It is used as the basis for the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a> because it mostly passes through the open waters of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. However, the meridian passes through <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.</p>\n<p>Starting at the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> and heading south to the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South
