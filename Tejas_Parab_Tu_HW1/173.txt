It is also known as a <b>bare number</b> or a <b>quantity of dimension one</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Dimensionless quantities are widely used in many fields, such as <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a>. By contrast, examples of quantities with dimensions are <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">length</a>, <a href="/wiki/Time" title="Time">time</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Speed" title="Speed">speed</a>, which are measured in dimensional units, such as <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">metre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">second</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">metre per second</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis#History" title="Dimensional analysis">Dimensional analysis \xc2\xa7&#160;History</a></div>\n<p>Quantities having dimension <b>1</b>, habitually called <i>dimensionless quantities</i>, regularly occur in sciences, and are formally treated within the field of <a href="/wiki/Dimensional_analysis" title="Dimensional analysis">dimensional analysis</a>. In the nineteenth century, <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> mathematician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fourier" title="Joseph Fourier">Joseph Fourier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_people" title="Scottish people">Scottish</a> physicist <a href="/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell" title="James Clerk Maxwell">James Clerk Maxwell</a> led significant developments in the modern concepts of <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unit_(measurement)" class="mw-redirect" title="Unit (measurement)">unit</a>. Later work by <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> physicists <a href="/wiki/Osborne_Reynolds" title="Osborne Reynolds">Osborne Reynolds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Rayleigh">Lord Rayleigh</a> contributed to the understanding of dimensionless numbers in physics. Building on Rayleigh\'s method of dimensional analysis, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Buckingham" title="Edgar Buckingham">Edgar Buckingham</a> proved the <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham \xcf\x80 theorem"><span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span> theorem</a> (independent of French mathematician <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bertrand" title="Joseph Bertrand">Joseph Bertrand</a>\'s previous work) to formalize the nature of these quantities. Numerous other dimensionless numbers, mostly ratios, were coined in the early 1900s, particularly in the areas of <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">fluid mechanics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">heat transfer</a>. Measuring <i>ratios</i> in the (derived) unit <i>dB</i> (<a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">decibel</a>) finds widespread use nowadays. In the early <a href="/wiki/2000s_(decade)" title="2000s (decade)">2000s</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">International Committee for Weights and Measures</a> discussed naming the unit of 1 as the \'uno\', but the idea of just introducing a new SI-name for <b>1</b> was dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pure_numbers">Pure numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pure numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>All <b><a href="/wiki/Number" title="Number">pure numbers</a></b> are dimensionless quantities, for example <a href="/wiki/1_(number)" class="mw-redirect" title="1 (number)">1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_number" title="Imaginary number"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">i</span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi"><span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span></a>, <a href="/wiki/Euler%27s_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Euler\'s number"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">e</span></a>, and <a href="/wiki/Golden_ratio" title="Golden ratio"><span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">\xcf\x86</span></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Units of number such as the <a href="/wiki/Dozen" title="Dozen">dozen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gross_(unit)" title="Gross (unit)">gross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Googol" title="Googol">googol</a>, and Avogadro\'s number may also be considered dimensionless, although <a href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_constant" class="mw-redirect" title="Avogadro\'s constant">Avogadro\'s constant</a> is definitely not.</p>\n<h2><span id="Ratios,_proportions,_and_angles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ratios.2C_proportions.2C_and_angles">Ratios, proportions, and angles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ratios, proportions, and angles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Dimensionless quantities are often obtained as <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">ratios</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a> that are not dimensionless, but whose dimensions cancel out in the mathematical operation.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Examples include calculating <a href="/wiki/Slope" title="Slope">slopes</a> or <a href="/wiki/Conversion_of_units" title="Conversion of units">unit conversion factors</a>. A more complex example of such a ratio is <a href="/wiki/Engineering_strain" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering strain">engineering strain</a>, a measure of physical deformation defined as a change in length divided by the initial length. Since both quantities have the dimension <i>length</i>, their ratio is dimensionless. Another set of examples is <a href="/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry)" title="Mass fraction (chemistry)">mass fractions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fractions</a> often written using <a href="/wiki/Parts-per_notation" title="Parts-per notation">parts-per notation</a> such as ppm (=&#160;10<sup>\xe2\x88\x926</sup>), ppb (=&#160;10<sup>\xe2\x88\x929</sup>), and ppt (=&#160;10<sup>\xe2\x88\x9212</sup>), or more confusingly as ratios of two identical units (<a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">kg</a>/kg or <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">mol</a>/mol). For example, <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_by_volume" title="Alcohol by volume">alcohol by volume</a>, which characterizes the concentration of <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_beverage" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic beverage">alcoholic beverage</a>, could be written as mL / 100 mL.</p>\n<p>Other common proportions are percentages <a href="/wiki/%25" class="mw-redirect" title="%">%</a>&#160;(=&#160;0.01), &#160;<a href="/wiki/Per_mil" class="mw-redirect" title="Per mil">\xe2\x80\xb0</a>&#160;(=&#160;0.001) and angle units such as <a href="/wiki/Radians" class="mw-redirect" title="Radians">radians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gradian" title="Gradian">grads</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">statistics</a> the <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_variation" title="Coefficient of variation">coefficient of variation</a> is the ratio of the <a href="/wiki/Standard_deviation" title="Standard deviation">standard deviation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">mean</a> and is used to measure the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_dispersion" title="Statistical dispersion">dispersion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Statistical_data" class="mw-redirect" title="Statistical data">data</a>.</p>\n<h2><span id="Buckingham_\xcf\x80_theorem"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Buckingham_.CF.80_theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span> theorem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Buckingham \xcf\x80 theorem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham \xcf\x80 theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span> theorem</a> indicates that validity of the laws of physics does not depend on a specific unit system. A statement of this theorem is that any physical law can be expressed as an <a href="/wiki/Identity_(mathematics)" title="Identity (mathematics)">identity</a> involving only dimensionless combinations (ratios or products) of the variables linked by the law (e. g., pressure and volume are linked by <a href="/wiki/Boyle%27s_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Boyle\'s Law">Boyle\'s Law</a> \xe2\x80\x93 they are inversely proportional). If the dimensionless combinations\' values changed with the systems of units, then the equation would not be an identity, and Buckingham\'s theorem would not hold.</p>\n<p>Another consequence of the theorem is that the <a href="/wiki/Function_(mathematics)" title="Function (mathematics)">functional</a> dependence between a certain number (say, <i>n</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Variable_(mathematics)" title="Variable (mathematics)">variables</a> can be reduced by the number (say, <i>k</i>) of <a href="/wiki/Independent_variable" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent variable">independent</a> <a href="/wiki/Dimension" title="Dimension">dimensions</a> occurring in those variables to give a set of <i>p</i> = <i>n</i> \xe2\x88\x92 <i>k</i> independent, dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a>. For the purposes of the experimenter, different systems that share the same description by dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantity</a> are equivalent.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>To demonstrate the application of the <span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span> theorem, consider the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">power</a> consumption of a <a href="/wiki/Stirrer" class="mw-redirect" title="Stirrer">stirrer</a> with a given shape. The power, <i>P</i>, in dimensions [M \xc2\xb7 L<sup>2</sup>/T<sup>3</sup>], is a function of the <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">density</a>, <i>\xcf\x81</i> [M/L<sup>3</sup>], and the <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">viscosity</a> of the fluid to be stirred, <i>\xce\xbc</i> [M/(L \xc2\xb7 T)], as well as the size of the stirrer given by its <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">diameter</a>, <i>D</i> [L], and the <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular speed</a> of the stirrer, <i>n</i> [1/T]. Therefore, we have a total of <i>n</i> = 5 variables representing our example. Those <i>n</i> = 5 variables are built up from <i>k</i> = 3 fundamental dimensions, the length: L (<a href="/wiki/SI" class="mw-redirect" title="SI">SI</a> units: <a href="/wiki/Meters" class="mw-redirect" title="Meters">m</a>), time: T (<a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">s</a>), and mass: M (<a href="/wiki/Kilograms" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilograms">kg</a>).</p>\n<p>According to the <span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span>-theorem, the <i>n</i> = 5 variables can be reduced by the <i>k</i> = 3 dimensions to form <i>p</i> = <i>n</i> \xe2\x88\x92 <i>k</i> = 5 \xe2\x88\x92 3 = 2 independent dimensionless numbers. The resulting <a href="/wiki/System_of_units" class="mw-redirect" title="System of units">system of units</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Natural_units" title="Natural units">natural units</a>. However, not all <a href="/wiki/Physical_constant" title="Physical constant">physical constants</a> can be normalized in this fashion. This ratio is the <a href="/wiki/Rest_mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Rest mass">rest mass</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">proton</a> divided by that of the <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a>. An analogous ratio can be defined for any <a href="/wiki/Elementary_particle" title="Elementary particle">elementary particle</a>;</li>\n<li><i>\xce\xb1</i><sub>s</sub>, a constant characterizing the <a href="/wiki/Strong_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Strong force">strong force</a>;</li>\n<li><i>\xce\xb1</i><sub>G</sub> \xe2\x89\x88 1.75 \xc3\x97 10<sup>\xe2\x88\x9245</sup>, the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_coupling_constant" title="Gravitational coupling constant">gravitational coupling constant</a>, which characterizes the magnitude of the <a href="/wiki/Gravitation" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravitation">gravitational interaction</a> between electrons.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_quantities_produced_by_nondimensionalization">Other quantities produced by nondimensionalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other quantities produced by nondimensionalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_dimensionless_quantities" title="List of dimensionless quantities">List of dimensionless quantities</a></div>\n<p>Physics often uses dimensionless <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">quantities</a> to simplify the characterization of systems with multiple interacting physical phenomena. These may be found by applying the <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_%CF%80_theorem" title="Buckingham \xcf\x80 theorem">Buckingham <span class="texhtml">\xcf\x80</span> theorem</a> or otherwise may emerge from making <a href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" class="mw-redirect" title="Partial differential equations">partial differential equations</a> unitless by the process of <a href="/wiki/Nondimensionalization" title="Nondimensionalization">nondimensionalization</a>. Engineering, economics, and other fields often extend these ideas in <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> and analysis of the relevant systems.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_and_engineering">Physics and engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dimensionless_quantity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Physics and engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fresnel_number" title="Fresnel number">Fresnel number</a> \xe2\x80\x93 wavenumber over distance</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a> is commonly used in fluid mechanics to characterize flow, incorporating both properties of the fluid and the flow. speed of sound in the fluid.</li>\n</ul>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_numbers_in_fluid_mechanics" title="Dimensionless
