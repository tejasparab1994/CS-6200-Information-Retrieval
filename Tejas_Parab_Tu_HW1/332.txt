id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In 1778, Captain <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">James Cook</a> arrived at Waimea Bay, the first European known to have reached the Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ian islands. He named the archipelago after his patron the 6th Earl of Sandwich, George Montagu.<sup id="cite_ref-Kauai_History_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kauai_History-6">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Kamehameha_I" title="Kamehameha I">King Kamehameha</a>, the islands of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i and Ni<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ihau were the last Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ian Islands to join his <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hawaii" title="Kingdom of Hawaii">Kingdom of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i</a>. Their ruler, <a href="/wiki/Kaumualii" title="Kaumualii">Kaumuali<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i</a>, resisted Kamehameha for years. King Kamehameha twice prepared a huge armada of ships and canoes to take the islands by force, and twice failed; once due to a storm, and once due to an <a href="/wiki/Epidemic" title="Epidemic">epidemic</a>. In the face of the threat of a further invasion, however, Kaumuali<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i decided to join the kingdom without bloodshed, and became Kamehameha\'s vassal in 1810. He ceded the island to the Kingdom of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i upon his death in 1824.<sup id="cite_ref-Kauai_History_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kauai_History-6">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="Sch\xc3\xa4ffer_affair"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sch.C3.A4ffer_affair">Sch\xc3\xa4ffer affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sch\xc3\xa4ffer affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%A4ffer_affair" title="Sch\xc3\xa4ffer affair">Sch\xc3\xa4ffer affair</a></div>\n<p>In 1815, a ship from the <a href="/wiki/Russian-American_Company" title="Russian-American Company">Russian-American Company</a> was wrecked on the island. In 1816, an agreement was signed by <a href="/wiki/Kaumuali%CA%BBi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaumuali\xca\xbbi">Kaumuali\xca\xbbi</a> to allow the Russians to build <a href="/wiki/Russian_Fort_Elizabeth" title="Russian Fort Elizabeth">Fort Elizabeth</a>. It was an attempt by Kaumuali\xe2\x80\x99i to gain support from the Russians against <a href="/wiki/Kamehameha_I" title="Kamehameha I">Kamehameha I</a>. Construction was begun in 1817, but in July of that year under mounting resistance of <a href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiians" title="Native Hawaiians">Native Hawaiians</a> and American traders the Russians were expelled.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Sugar_Mill_of_Koloa">Old Sugar Mill of Koloa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Old Sugar Mill of Koloa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Old_Sugar_Mill_of_Koloa" title="Old Sugar Mill of Koloa">Old Sugar Mill of Koloa</a></div>\n<p>In 1835, Old Koloa Town opened a sugar mill.<sup id="cite_ref-Kauai_History_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kauai_History-6">[5]</a></sup> From 1906 to 1934 the office of <a href="/wiki/County_Clerk" class="mw-redirect" active in attempts to restore Queen <a href="/wiki/Liliuokalani" title="Liliuokalani">Liliuokalani</a> to the throne after the United States takeover of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i in 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Garden_Island_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Garden_Island-7">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_language">Etymology and language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Etymology and language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_narrative" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaiian narrative">Hawaiian narrative</a> locates the name\'s origin in the legend of <a href="/wiki/Hawaiiloa" title="Hawaiiloa">Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>iloa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Polynesia" title="Polynesia">Polynesian</a> navigator credited with discovery of the Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ian Islands. The story relates how he named the island of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i after a favorite son; a possible translation of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i is "place around the neck", describing how a father would carry a favorite child. Another possible translation is "food season".<sup id="cite_ref-PukuiElbert1974_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PukuiElbert1974-8">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i was known for its distinct dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">Hawaiian language</a>; this survives on <a href="/wiki/Niihau" title="Niihau">Ni<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ihau</a>. While the standard language today adopts the dialect of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i island</a>, which has the sound <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[k]</span>, the Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i dialect was known for pronouncing this as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[t]</span>. In effect, Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i dialect retained the old pan-Polynesian <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/t/</span>, while "standard" Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i dialect has changed it to the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[k]</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Therefore, the native name for Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i was said as <b>Taua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i</b>, and the major settlement of <a href="/wiki/Kapaa,_Hawaii" title="Kapaa, Hawaii">Kapa<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>a</a> would have been pronounced as <i>Tapa<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>a.</i></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coast_of_Kauai,_Hawaii.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg/220px-Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg/330px-Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg/440px-Coast_of_Kauai%2C_Hawaii.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coast_of_Kauai,_Hawaii.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Kalalau_Beach" title="Kalalau Beach">Kalalau Beach</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s origins are <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanic</a>, the island having been formed by the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Plate" title="Pacific Plate">Pacific Plate</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_hotspot" title="Hawaii hotspot">Hawaii hotspot</a>. At approximately six million years old, it is the oldest of the main islands. The highest peak on this mountainous island is <a href="/wiki/Kawaikini" title="Kawaikini">Kawaikini</a> at 5,243 feet (1,598&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-ElevationRef_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ElevationRef-9">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The second highest peak is <a href="/wiki/Mount_Waialeale" title="Mount Waialeale">Mount Wai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale</a> near the center of the island, 5,148&#160;ft (1,569&#160;m) above sea level. One of the wettest spots on earth, with an annual average rainfall of 460 inches (1,200&#160;cm), is located on the east side of Mount Wai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale. The high annual rainfall has eroded deep valleys in the central mountains, carving out canyons with many scenic waterfalls. On the west side of the island, <a href="/wiki/Waimea,_Kauai_County,_Hawaii" title="Waimea, Kauai County, Hawaii">Waimea</a> town is located at the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Waimea_River_(Hawaii)" title="Waimea River (Hawaii)">Waimea River</a>, whose flow formed Waimea Canyon, one of the world\'s most scenic canyons, which is part of <a href="/wiki/Waimea_Canyon_State_Park" title="Waimea Canyon State Park">Waimea Canyon State Park</a>. At 3,000&#160;ft (914&#160;m) deep, Waimea Canyon is often referred to as "The <a href="/wiki/Grand_Canyon" title="Grand Canyon">Grand Canyon</a> of the Pacific". <a href="/wiki/Kokeo_Point" title="Kokeo Point">Kokeo Point</a> lies on the south side of the island. The Na Pali Coast is a center for recreation in a wild setting, including kayaking past the beaches, or hiking on the trail along the coastal cliffs.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[9]</a></sup> The headland, <a href="/wiki/Kuahonu_Point" title="Kuahonu Point">Kuahonu Point</a>, is on the south-east of the island.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\xe2\x80\x99s climate is tropical, with generally humid and stable conditions year round, although weather phenomena and infrequent storms have caused instances of extreme weather. At the lower elevations the annual precipitation varies from an average of about 50 inches on the windward (northeastern) shore, to less than 20 inches on the (southwestern) leeward side of the island. Average temperature in Lihu\'e, the county seat, ranges from 78&#160;\xc2\xb0F (26&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in February to 85&#160;\xc2\xb0F (29&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in August and September. Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\xe2\x80\x99s mountainous regions offer cooler temperatures and provide a pleasant contrast to the warm coastal areas. At the K\xc5\x8dke\xca\xbbe state park, 3,200\xe2\x80\x934,200&#160;ft (980\xe2\x80\x931,280&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Above_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Above sea level">ASL</a>, day temperatures vary from an average of 45&#160;\xc2\xb0F (7&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in January to 68&#160;\xc2\xb0F (20&#160;\xc2\xb0C) in July. In the winter temperatures have been known to drop down to the 30s and 40s at K\xc5\x8dke\xca\xbbe state park, which holds an unofficial record low of 29&#160;\xc2\xb0F (\xe2\x88\x922&#160;\xc2\xb0C) recorded in February 1986 at Kanaloahuluhulu Meadow.</p>\n<p>Precipitation in Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s mountainous regions averages 50\xe2\x80\x93100&#160;in (1,300\xe2\x80\x932,500&#160;mm) annually. Situated about 10&#160;mi (16&#160;km) southeast of K\xc5\x8dke\xca\xbbe state park at an elevation of 5,075&#160;ft (1,547&#160;m), is the Mt. Wai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale rain gauge.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[10]</a></sup> Mt. Wai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale is often cited in literature as being the wettest spot on earth, although this has been disputed. Based on data for the period from 1931 through 1960 the average yearly precipitation was 460&#160;in (12,000&#160;mm) (U.S. Environmental Science Services Administration, 1968). Between 1949 and 2004 the average yearly precipitation at Mt. Wai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ale was 374&#160;in (9,500&#160;mm).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Not only does Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i hold a record in average yearly precipitation, it also holds a record in hourly precipitation. During a storm on January 24\xe2\x80\x9325, 1956, a rain gauge at Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\xe2\x80\x99s former Kilauea Sugar Plantation recorded a record 12&#160;in (300&#160;mm) of precipitation in just 60 minutes. The 12&#160;in (300&#160;mm) value for one hour is an underestimate, since the rain gauge overflowed, which may have resulted in an error by as much as an inch.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[12]</a></sup> An accurate measurement may have exceeded Holt, Missouri\'s world record 60-minute rainfall of 12 inches in 42 minutes on June 22, 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_zone">Time zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Time zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hawaii%E2%80%93Aleutian_Time_Zone" title="Hawaii\xe2\x80\x93Aleutian Time Zone">Hawaii Standard Time</a> is observed on Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i year-round. When most states are on <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">daylight saving time</a>, for example, the time on Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i is three hours behind the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast</a> of the United States and six hours behind the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit href="/wiki/File:Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen,_Hawaii.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen%2C_Hawaii.jpg/220px-Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen%2C_Hawaii.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen%2C_Hawaii.jpg/440px-Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen%2C_Hawaii.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Commercial_area_of_Port_Allen,_Hawaii.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe commercial area in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Port_Allen_Garcia,_Hawaii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Port Allen Garcia, Hawaii (page does not exist)">Port Allen</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tourism_in_Hawaii" title="Tourism in Hawaii">Tourism</a> is Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s largest industry. In 2007, 1,271,000 people visited Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i. The two largest groups were from the continental United States (84% of all visitors) and Japan (3%).<sup id="cite_ref-kauai.gov_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kauai.gov-16">[15]</a></sup> As of 2003, there were a total of approximately 27,000 jobs on Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i, of which the largest sector was accommodation/food services (26%, 6,800 jobs) followed by government (15%) and retail (14.5%), with agriculture accounting for 2.9% (780 jobs) and educational services providing 0.7% (183 jobs).<sup id="cite_ref-KEDP_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEDP-17">[16]</a></sup> In terms of income, the various sectors that constitute the visitors\' industry accounted for one third of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s income.<sup id="cite_ref-KEDP_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEDP-17">[16]</a></sup> Employment is dominated by small businesses, with 87% of all non-farm businesses having fewer than 20 employees.<sup id="cite_ref-KEDP_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEDP-17">[16]</a></sup> As of 2003, Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s unemployment rate was 3.9%, compared to 3.0% for the entire state and 5.7% for the United States as a whole. Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s poverty rate was 10.5%, compared to the contiguous 48 states at 10.7%.<sup id="cite_ref-KEDP_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEDP-17">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As of mid-2004, the median price of a single-family home was $528,000, a 40% increase over 2003. As of 2003, Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s percentage of home ownership, 48%, was significantly lower than the State\'s 64%, and vacation homes were a far larger part of the housing stock than the Statewide percentage (Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i 15%, State 5%).<sup id="cite_ref-KEDP_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEDP-17">[16]</a></sup> The housing prices decreased significantly in 2008. As of Spring 2014 the median price had risen to about $400,000.</p>\n<p>From the 1830s through the mid-20th century, <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_Hawaii" title="Sugar plantations in Hawaii">plantations</a> were Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s most important industry. In 1835 the first sugarcane plantation was founded on Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i, and for the next century the industry would dominate the economy of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[17]</a></sup> Most of that land is now used for ranching.<sup id="cite_ref-kauai.gov_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kauai.gov-16">[15]</a></sup> Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i\'s sole remaining sugar operation, the 118-year-old Gay &amp; Robinson Plantation, plans to convert its operation to cultivating and processing sugarcane ethanol.<sup id="cite_ref-kauai.gov_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kauai.gov-16">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i is home to the U.S. Navy\'s "Barking Sands" <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Missile_Range_Facility" title="Pacific Missile Range Facility">Pacific Missile Range Facility</a>, on the sunny and dry western shore. <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">HF</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Shortwave_radio" title="Shortwave radio">shortwave</a>") radio station <a href="/wiki/WWVH" title="WWVH">WWVH</a>, sister station to <a href="/wiki/WWV_(radio_station)" title="WWV (radio station)">WWV</a> and <a href="/wiki/WWVB" title="WWVB">WWVB</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Collins,_Colorado" title="Fort Collins, Colorado">Fort Collins, Colorado</a>, is located on the west coast of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i about 3 miles (5&#160;km) south of Barking Sands. WWVH, WWV and <a href="/wiki/WWVB" title="WWVB">WWVB</a> are operated by the US <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">National Institute of Standards and Technology</a>, broadcasting standard time and frequency information to the public.</p>\n<p>Land in Kaua\xca\xbbi is very fertile; farmers raise many varieties of fruit and other crops. <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">Guava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a>, sugarcane, <a href="/wiki/Mango" title="Mango">mango</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">banana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Papaya" title="Papaya">papaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avocado" title="Avocado">avocado</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carambola" title="Carambola">star fruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kava" title="Kava">kava</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morinda_citrifolia" title="Morinda citrifolia">noni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pineapple" title="Pineapple">pineapple</a> are all cultivated on the island.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Towns_and_cities">Towns and cities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Towns and cities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The city of <a href="/wiki/Lihue,_Hawaii" title="Lihue, Hawaii">L\xc4\xabhu<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>e</a>, on the island\'s southeast coast, is the seat of <a href="/wiki/Kauai_County,_Hawaii" title="Kauai County, Hawaii">Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i County</a> and the second largest city on the island. Route 50</a> at the junction of Rice Street in L\xc4\xabhu<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>e to the junction of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i Route 560 in Princeville.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ha%CA%BBena_State_Park" title="Ha\xca\xbbena State Park">Ha\xca\xbbena State Park</a>.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Other major highways that link other parts of the Island to the main highways of miles (12.2&#160;km) from the junction of Route 50 in <a href="/wiki/Kekaha,_Hawaii" title="Kekaha, Hawaii">Kekaha</a> to meet with <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_Route_550" title="Hawaii Route 550">Hawaii class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kauai&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The island of Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i has been featured in more than seventy Hollywood movies and television shows, including the musical <i><a href="/wiki/South_Pacific_(1958_film)" title="South Pacific (1958 film)">South Pacific</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Disney\'s</a> 2002 <a href="/wiki/List_of_animated_feature_films" class="mw-redirect" title="List of animated feature films">animated feature film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Lilo_%26_Stitch" title="Lilo &amp; Stitch">Lilo &amp; Stitch</a></i> along with <a href="/wiki/Lilo_%26_Stitch_(franchise)" title="Lilo &amp; Stitch (franchise)">its franchise\'s</a> three sequel films (2003\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Stitch!_The_Movie" title="Stitch! The Movie">Stitch! The Movie</a></i>, 2005\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Lilo_%26_Stitch_2:_Stitch_Has_a_Glitch" title="Lilo &amp; Stitch 2: Stitch Has a Glitch">Lilo &amp; Stitch 2: Stitch Has a Glitch</a></i>, and 2006\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Leroy_%26_Stitch" title="Leroy &amp; Stitch">Leroy &amp; Stitch</a></i>) and first <a href="/wiki/Television_program#series" title="Television program">television series</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Lilo_%26_Stitch:_The_Series" title="Lilo &amp; Stitch: The Series">Lilo &amp; Stitch: The Series</a></i>). Scenes from <i>South Pacific</i> were filmed in the vicinity of Hanalei. Waimea Canyon was used in the filming of the 1993 <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>. Parts of the island were also used for the opening scenes of the <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_(franchise)" title="Indiana Jones (franchise)">Indiana Jones</a></i> film <i><a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">Raiders of the Lost Ark</a></i>. Other movies filmed here include <i><a href="/wiki/Six_Days_Seven_Nights" class="mw-redirect" title="Six Days Seven Nights">Six Days Seven Nights</a></i>, the 1976 version <i><a href="/wiki/King_Kong_(1976_film)" title="King Kong (1976 film)">King Kong</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[20]</a></sup> and John Ford\'s 1963 film <i><a href="/wiki/Donovan%27s_Reef" title="Donovan\'s Reef">Donovan\'s Reef</a></i>. Recent films include <i><a href="/wiki/Tropic_Thunder" title="Tropic Thunder">Tropic Thunder</a></i> and a biopic of <a href="/wiki/Bethany_Hamilton" title="Bethany Hamilton">Bethany Hamilton</a> titled <i><a href="/wiki/Soul_Surfer_(film)" title="Soul Surfer (film)">Soul Surfer</a></i>. A scene in the opening credits of popular TV show M*A*S*H was filmed in Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i (helicopter flying over mountain top). Some scenes from <i><a href="/wiki/Just_Go_with_It" title="Just Go with It">Just Go with It</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_of_the_Jungle_(film)" title="George of the Jungle (film)">George of the Jungle</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pirates_of_the_Caribbean:_On_Stranger_Tides" title="Pirates of the Caribbean: On Stranger Tides">Pirates of the Caribbean: On Stranger Tides</a></i> were also filmed in Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[21]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Perfect_Getaway" title="A Perfect Getaway">A Perfect Getaway</a></i> is set in Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.</p>\n<p>Parts of the 2002 film <i><a href="/wiki/Dragonfly_(2002_film)" title="Dragonfly (2002 film)">Dragonfly</a></i> were filmed there (although the people and the land were presented as South American) and the producers hired extras (at least three with speaking parts) from the ancient Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ian native population, which seeks to preserve its cultural heritage,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[22]</a></sup> including the pre-USA name of these two islands, Atooi or Taua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Major acts of two Elvis Presley films, 1962\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Blue_Hawaii" title="Blue Hawaii">Blue Hawaii</a></i> and 1966\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Paradise,_Hawaiian_Style" title="Paradise, Hawaiian Style">Paradise, Hawaiian Style</a></i>, were filmed at various locations on Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i. Both films boast scenes shot at the famous <a href="/wiki/Coco_Palms_Resort" title="Coco Palms Resort">Coco Palms resort</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iniki" title="Hurricane Iniki">Hurricane Iniki</a> destroyed the Coco Palms and it was subsequently never rebuilt. However, <i>Paradise, Hawaiian Style</i> in particular, showcases the resort at its peak for posterity\'s sake. Coco Palms resort is now in the process of being torn down and rebuilt.</p>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Descendants" title="The Descendants">The Descendants</a></i>, a film by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Payne" title="Alexander Payne">Alexander Payne</a> released in November 2011 and featuring <a href="/wiki/George_Clooney" title="George Clooney">George Clooney</a> as lead actor, has major parts shot in Kaua<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i, where the main character and his cousins own ancestral lands which they are considering selling. The film is based on the 2007 <a href="/wiki/The_Descendants_(novel)" title="The Descendants (novel)">novel</a> by the Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>ian writer <a href="/wiki/Kaui_Hart_Hemmings" title="Kaui Hart Hemmings">Kaui Hart Hemmings</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
