blizzard as a severe <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">Snow</a> <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">storm</a> characterized by strong winds causing <a href="/wiki/Blowing_snow" title="Blowing snow">blowing snow</a> that results in low visibilities. The difference between a blizzard and a snowstorm is the strength of the wind, not the amount of snow. To be a blizzard, a snow storm must have sustained winds or frequent gusts that are greater than or equal to 56&#160;km/h (35&#160;mph) with blowing or drifting snow which reduces <a href="/wiki/Visibility" title="Visibility">visibility</a> to 400&#160;m or 0.25&#160;mi or less and must last for a prolonged period of time\xe2\x80\x94typically three hours or more.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While severe cold and large amounts of <a href="/wiki/Snowdrift" title="Snowdrift">drifting snow</a> may accompany blizzards, they are not required. Blizzards can bring <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteout</a> conditions, and can paralyze regions for days at a time, particularly where snowfall is unusual or rare.</p>\n<p>A severe blizzard has winds over 72&#160;km/h (45&#160;mph), near zero visibility, and temperatures of \xe2\x88\x9212&#160;\xc2\xb0C (10&#160;\xc2\xb0F) or lower.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, blizzards are associated with winds spilling over the edge of the ice plateau at an average velocity of 160&#160;km/h (99&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Ground_blizzard" title="Ground blizzard">Ground blizzard</a> refers to a weather condition where loose snow or ice on the ground is lifted and blown by strong winds. The primary difference between a ground blizzard as opposed to a regular blizzard is that in a ground blizzard no precipitation is produced at the time, but rather all the precipitation is already present in the form of snow or ice at the surface.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg/220px-Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg/330px-Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg/440px-Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Old_farm_at_Overtown_in_deep_snow_in_1963.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDrifted snow near <a href="/wiki/Burrow-with-Burrow" title="Burrow-with-Burrow">Burrow-with-Burrow</a>, Lancashire, England, January 1963</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The Australia Bureau of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">Meteorology</a> describes a blizzard as, "Violent and very cold wind which is laden with snow, some part, at least, of which has been raised from snow covered ground." <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> concludes the term <i>blizzard</i> is likely onomatopoeic, derived from the same sense as <i>blow, blast, blister, and bluster</i>; the first recorded use of it for weather dates to 1829, when it was defined as a "violent blow". It achieved its modern definition by 1859, when it was in use in the western United States. The term became common in the press during the harsh winter of 1880\xe2\x80\x9381.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States storm systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb blizzard, March 2007</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blizzard2_-_NOAA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Blizzard2_-_NOAA.jpg/220px-Blizzard2_-_NOAA.jpg" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blizzard2_-_NOAA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMarch blizzard in North Dakota, 1966.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In the United States, storm systems powerful enough to cause blizzards usually form when the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> dips far to the south, allowing cold, dry polar air from the north to clash with warm, humid air moving up from the south.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> They are most common in the Great Plains, the Great Lakes states, and the northeastern states along the coast, and less common in the Pacific Northwest.</p>\n<p>When cold, moist air from the Pacific Ocean moves eastward to the <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>, and warmer, moist air moves north from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, all that is needed is a movement of cold polar air moving south to form potential blizzard conditions that may extend from the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Panhandle" title="Texas Panhandle">Texas Panhandle</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>.</p>\n<p>Another storm system occurs when a cold core low over the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> area in Canada is displaced southward over southeastern Canada, the Great Lakes, and <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a>. When the rapidly moving cold front collides with warmer air coming north from the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>, strong surface winds, significant cold air <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>, and extensive wintry precipitation occur.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG/220px-Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG/330px-Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG/440px-Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nearwhiteoutinminnesota.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nConditions approaching a blizzard whiteout in <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, on March 1, 2007. Note the unclear horizon near the center.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure systems">Low pressure systems</a> moving out of the Rocky Mountains onto the Great Plains, a broad expanse of flat land, much of it covered in <a href="/wiki/Prairie" title="Prairie">prairie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steppe" title="Steppe">steppe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grassland</a>, can cause thunderstorms and rain to the south and heavy snows and strong winds to the north. With few trees or other obstructions to reduce wind and blowing, this part of the country is particularly vulnerable to blizzards with very low temperatures and <a href="/wiki/Whiteout_(weather)" title="Whiteout (weather)">whiteout</a> conditions. In a true whiteout there is no visible horizon. People can become lost in their own front yards, when the door is only 3&#160;m (10&#160;ft) away, and they would have to feel their way back. Motorists have to stop their cars where they are, as the road is impossible to see.</p>\n<h3><span id="Nor\'easter_blizzards"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nor.27easter_blizzards">Nor\'easter blizzards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nor\'easter blizzards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TotallyFreeImages_com-281074-Standard-preview.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/TotallyFreeImages_com-281074-Standard-preview.jpg/200px-TotallyFreeImages_com-281074-Standard-preview.jpg" width="200" height="306" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/fc/TotallyFreeImages_com-281074-Standard-preview.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="294" data-file-height="450" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TotallyFreeImages_com-281074-Standard-preview.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nIllustration of the Great Blizzard of 1888</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">nor\'easter</a> is a macro-scale <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> along the upper <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>. It gets its name from the direction the wind is coming from. The usage of the term in North America comes from the wind associated with many different types of storms some of which can form in the North Atlantic Ocean and some of which form as far south as the Gulf of Mexico. The term is most often used in the coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>. This type of storm has characteristics similar to a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a>. More specifically it describes a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> whose center of <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a> is just off the East Coast and whose leading winds in the left-forward quadrant rotate onto land from the northeast. High storm waves may sink ships at sea and cause coastal flooding and beach erosion. Notable nor\'easters include The <a href="/wiki/Great_Blizzard_of_1888" title="Great Blizzard of 1888">Great Blizzard of 1888</a>, one of the worst blizzards in U.S. history. It dropped 100\xe2\x80\x93130&#160;cm (40\xe2\x80\x9350&#160;in) of snow and had sustained winds of more than 45 miles per hour (72&#160;km/h) that produced snowdrifts in excess of 50 feet (15&#160;m). Railroads were shut down and people were confined to their houses for up to a week. It killed 400 people, mostly in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_events">Historic events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historic events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1972_Iran_blizzard">1972 Iran blizzard</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1972 Iran blizzard">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/1972_Iran_blizzard" title="1972 Iran blizzard">1972 Iran Blizzard</a>, which caused 4,000 reported deaths, was the deadliest blizzard in recorded history. Dropping as much as 26 feet (7.9&#160;m) of snow, it completely covered 200 villages. After a snowfall lasting nearly a week, an area the size of <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a> was entirely buried in snow.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="The_Snow_Winter_of_1880\xe2\x80\x931881"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Snow_Winter_of_1880.E2.80.931881">The Snow Winter of 1880\xe2\x80\x931881</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Snow Winter of 1880\xe2\x80\x931881">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Train_stuck_in_snow.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Train_stuck_in_snow.jpg/220px-Train_stuck_in_snow.jpg" width="220" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/08/Train_stuck_in_snow.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="291" data-file-height="396" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Train_stuck_in_snow.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA snow blockade in southern Minnesota. On March 29, 1881, snowdrifts in Minnesota were higher than locomotives.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The winter of 1880\xe2\x80\x931881 is widely considered the most severe winter ever known in the United States. Many children\xe2\x80\x94and their parents\xe2\x80\x94learned of "The Snow Winter" through the children\'s book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Long_Winter_(novel)" title="The Long Winter (novel)">The Long Winter</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Laura_Ingalls_Wilder" title="Laura Ingalls Wilder">Laura Ingalls Wilder</a>, in which the author tells of her family\'s efforts to survive. The snow arrived in October 1880 and blizzard followed blizzard throughout the winter and into March 1881, leaving many areas snowbound throughout the entire winter. Accurate details in Wilder\'s novel include the blizzards\' frequency and the deep cold, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_and_North_Western_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago and North Western Railway">Chicago and North Western Railway</a> stopping trains until the spring thaw because the snow made the tracks impassable, the near-starvation of the townspeople, and the courage of her future husband Almanzo and another man, who ventured out on the open prairie in search of a cache of wheat that no one was even sure existed.</p>\n<p>The October blizzard brought snowfalls so deep that two-story homes had snow up to the second floor windows. No one was prepared for the deep snow so early in the season and farmers all over the region were caught before their crops had even been harvested, their grain milled, or with their fuel supplies for the winter in place. By January the train service was almost entirely suspended from the region. Railroads hired scores of men to dig out the tracks but it was a wasted effort: As soon as they had finished shoveling a stretch of line, a new storm arrived, filling up the line and leaving their work useless.</p>\n<p>There were no winter thaws and on February 2, 1881, a second massive blizzard struck that lasted for nine days. In the towns the streets were filled with solid drifts to the tops of the buildings and tunneling was needed to secure passage about town. Homes and barns were completely covered, compelling farmers to tunnel to reach and feed their stock.</p>\n<p>When the snow finally melted in late spring of 1881, huge sections of the plains were flooded. Massive ice jams clogged the <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">Missouri River</a> and when they broke the downstream areas were ravaged. Most of the town of <a href="/wiki/Yankton,_South_Dakota" title="Yankton, South Dakota">Yankton</a>, in what is now South Dakota, was washed away when the river overflowed its banks.<sup Carolina</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/1993_Storm_of_the_Century" title="1993 Storm of the Century">Storm of the Century</a>, also known as the Great Blizzard of 1993, was a large <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclonic</a> storm that formed over the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> on March 12, 1993, and dissipated in the North Atlantic Ocean on March 15. It is unique for its intensity, massive size and wide-reaching effect. At its height, the storm stretched from Canada towards Central America, but its main impact was on the Eastern United States and Cuba. The cyclone moved through the Gulf of Mexico, and then through the Eastern United States before moving into Canada. Areas as far south as central Alabama and Georgia received 15 to 20&#160;cm (6 to 8&#160;in) of snow and areas such as <a href="/wiki/Birmingham,_Alabama" title="Birmingham, Alabama">Birmingham, Alabama</a>, received up to 30&#160;cm (12&#160;in) with isolated reports of 41&#160;cm (16&#160;in). Even the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Panhandle" title="Florida Panhandle">Florida Panhandle</a> reported up to 10&#160;cm (4&#160;in),<sup id="cite_ref-ncdc-event-details_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-event-details-12">[12]</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Wind#Wind_force_scale" title="Wind">hurricane-force</a> wind gusts and record low <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressures</a>. Between Louisiana and Cuba, hurricane-force winds produced high storm surges across northwestern Florida, which along with scattered <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> killed dozens of people. Record cold temperatures were seen across portions of the South and East in the wake of this storm. In the United States, the storm was responsible for the loss of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electric power</a> to over 10&#160;million customers. It is purported to have been directly experienced by nearly 40&#160;percent of the country\'s population at that time. A total of 310 people, including 10 from Cuba, perished during this storm.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_blizzards">List of blizzards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: List of blizzards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blizzard&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Regional_Snowfall_Index_Category_5_winter_storms" title="List of Regional Snowfall Index Category 5 winter storms">List of Regional Snowfall Index Category 5 winter storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_NESIS_storms" class="mw-redirect" title="List of NESIS storms">List of NESIS storms</a></div>\n<dl>\n<dt>1600 to 1799</dt>\n</dl>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Snow_of_1717" title="The Great Snow of 1717">The Great Snow 1717</a> series of four snowstorms between February 27 and March 7, 1717.
