environment">environment</a> or astronomical body\'s <a href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Celestial body atmosphere">atmosphere</a> especially affecting its surface, and strongly implying <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a>. It may be marked by significant disruptions to normal conditions such as strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> (a <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a>), heavy <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> (<a href="/wiki/Snowstorm" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowstorm">snowstorm</a>, rainstorm), heavy <a href="/wiki/Freezing_rain" title="Freezing rain">freezing rain</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ice_storm" title="Ice storm">ice storm</a>), strong winds (<a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>, windstorm), or wind transporting some <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">substance</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> as in a <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzard</a>, sandstorm, etc.</p>\n<p>Storms have the potential to harm lives and property via <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> causing <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a> or road impassibility, <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a>, and vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>; however, systems with significant rainfall and duration help alleviate drought in places they move through. Heavy snowfall can allow special recreational activities to take place which would not be possible otherwise, such as skiing and snowmobiling.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/GreatBlizzardof2006.jpg/220px-GreatBlizzardof2006.jpg" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" Note the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a>-like eye at the center.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Storms are created when a center of <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low pressure</a> develops with a system of <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure area">high pressure</a> surrounding it. This combination of opposing forces can create winds and result in the formation of storm <a href="/wiki/Clouds" class="mw-redirect" title="Clouds">clouds</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a>. Small localized areas of low pressure can form from hot air rising off hot ground, resulting in smaller disturbances such as <a href="/wiki/Dust_devils" class="mw-redirect" title="Dust devils">dust devils</a> and <a href="/wiki/Whirlwinds" class="mw-redirect" title="Whirlwinds">whirlwinds</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Classic_Storm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Classic_Storm.jpg/220px-Classic_Storm.jpg" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Classic_Storm.jpg/330px-Classic_Storm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Classic_Storm.jpg/440px-Classic_Storm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4164" data-file-height="3000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Classic_Storm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nClassic storm of summer, in <a href="/wiki/Sierras_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Sierras de C\xc3\xb3rdoba">Sierras de C\xc3\xb3rdoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>There are many varieties and names for storms:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Ice_storm" title="Ice storm">Ice storm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Ice storms are one of the most dangerous forms of <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">winter storms</a>. When surface temperatures are below freezing, but a thick layer of above-freezing air remains aloft, rain can fall into the freezing layer and freeze upon impact into a glaze of ice. In general, 8 millimetres (0.31&#160;in) of accumulation is all that is required, especially in combination with breezy conditions, to start downing power lines as well as tree limbs.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Ice storms also make unheated road surfaces too slick to drive upon. Ice storms can vary in time range from hours to days and can cripple small towns and large urban centers alike.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">Blizzard</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 There are varying definitions for blizzards, both over time and by location. In general, a blizzard is accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">gale</a>-force winds, heavy snow (accumulating at a rate of at least 5 centimeters (2&#160;in) per hour), and very cold conditions (below approximately -10 degrees Celsius or 14&#160;F). Lately, the temperature criterion has fallen out of the definition across the United States<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Snowstorm" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowstorm">Snowstorm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 A heavy fall of snow accumulating at a rate of more than 5 centimeters (2&#160;in) per hour that lasts several hours. Snow storms, especially ones with a high liquid equivalent and breezy conditions, can down tree limbs, cut off power, and paralyze travel over a large region.</li>\n<li>Coastal Storm&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 large <a href="/wiki/Wind_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind waves">wind waves</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> that strike the coastal zone. Their impacts include <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coastal_flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal flooding">coastal flooding</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Ocean Storm&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Storm conditions out at sea are defined as having sustained winds of 48 knots (55&#160;mph or 90&#160;km/h) or greater.<sup id="cite_ref-OPC_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OPC-6">[6]</a></sup> Usually just referred to as a storm, these systems can sink vessels of all types and sizes.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Firestorm" title="Firestorm">Firestorm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 Firestorms are conflagrations which attain such intensity that they create and sustain their own wind systems. It is most commonly a natural phenomenon, created during some of the largest bushfires, forest fires, and wildfires. The <a href="/wiki/Peshtigo_Fire" title="Peshtigo Fire">Peshtigo Fire</a> is one example of a firestorm. Firestorms can also be deliberate effects of targeted explosives such as occurred as a result of the aerial <a href="/wiki/Bombing_of_Dresden_in_World_War_II" title="Bombing of Dresden in World War II">bombings of Dresden</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapon" title="Nuclear weapon">Nuclear detonations</a> generate firestorms if high winds are not present.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Dust_devil" title="Dust devil">Dust devil</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 a small, localized updraft of rising air.</li>\n<li>Wind storm\xe2\x80\x94 A storm marked by high wind with little or no precipitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Merriam-Webster_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Merriam-Webster-7">[7]</a></sup> Windstorm damage often opens the door for massive amounts of water and debris to cause further damage to a structure.<sup id="cite_ref-Hurricane_and_Windstorm_Coverage_from_Adjusting_Today_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurricane_and_Windstorm_Coverage_from_Adjusting_Today-8">[8]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/European_windstorm" title="European windstorm">European windstorms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derecho" title="Derecho">derechos</a> are two type of windstorms.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-9">[9]</a></sup> High wind is also the cause of <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">sandstorms</a> in dry climates.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">Squall</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 sudden onset of wind increase of at least 16 knots (30&#160;km/h) or greater sustained for at least one minute.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Gale" title="Gale">Gale</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 An <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Extratropical storm">extratropical storm</a> with sustained winds between 34-48 knots (39-55&#160;mph or 63\xe2\x80\x9390&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-OPC_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OPC-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 A thunderstorm is a type of storm that generates <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and the attendant <a href="/wiki/Thunder" title="Thunder">thunder</a>. It is normally accompanied by heavy <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a>. Thunderstorms occur throughout the world, with the highest frequency in <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> regions where there are conditions of high humidity and temperature along with atmospheric instability. These storms occur when high levels of condensation form in a volume of unstable air that generates deep, rapid, upward motion in the atmosphere. The heat energy creates powerful rising air currents that swirl upwards to the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>. Cool descending air currents produce strong downdraughts below the storm. After the storm has spent its energy, the rising currents die away and downdraughts break up the cloud. Individual storm clouds can measure 2\xe2\x80\x9310&#160;km across.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 A tropical cyclone is a storm system with a closed circulation around a centre of <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low pressure</a>, fueled by the heat released when moist air rises and condenses. The name underscores its origin in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclonic">cyclonic</a> nature. Tropical cyclones are distinguished from other cyclonic storms such as <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easter" title="Nor\'easter">nor\'easters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a> by the heat mechanism that fuels them, which makes them "warm core" storm systems.</li>\n</ul>\n<dl>\n<dd>Tropical cyclones form in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> if the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Formation" title="Tropical cyclone">conditions in the area are favorable</a>, and depending on their strength and location, there are various terms by which they are called, such as <i>tropical depression</i>, <i>tropical storm</i>, <i>hurricane</i> and <i>typhoon</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></dd>\n</dl>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">Hailstorm</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 a type of storm that precipitates round chunks of ice. Hailstorms usually occur during regular thunder storms. While most of the hail that precipitates from the clouds is fairly small and virtually harmless, there are occasional occurrences of hail greater than 2&#160;inches (5&#160;cm) in diameter that can cause much damage and injuries.</li>\n</ul>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg/220px-Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg/330px-Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg/440px-Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg 2x" data-file-width="533" data-file-height="333" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Binger_Oklahoma_Tornado.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA tornado in Binger, Oklahoma during the <a href="/wiki/May_1981_tornado_outbreak" title="May 1981 tornado outbreak">1981 outbreak</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a>&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 A tornado is a violent, destructive wind storm occurring on land. Usually its appearance is that of a dark, funnel-shaped cloud. Often tornadoes are preceded by thunderstorms and a <a href="/wiki/Wall_cloud" title="Wall cloud">wall cloud</a>. They are often called the most destructive of storms, and while they form all over the world, the interior of the United States is the most prone area, especially throughout <a href="/wiki/Tornado_Alley" title="Tornado Alley">Tornado Alley</a>.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A strict <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> definition of a terrestrial storm is a wind measuring 10 or higher on the <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">Beaufort scale</a>, meaning a wind speed of 24.5 <a href="/wiki/Metre_per_second" title="Metre per second">m/s</a> (89&#160;km/h, 55&#160;mph) or more; however, popular usage is not so restrictive. Storms can last anywhere from 12 to 200 hours, depending on season and geography. In North America, the east and northeast storms are noted for the most frequent repeatability and duration, especially during the cold period. Big terrestrial storms alter the <a href="/wiki/Oceanographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Oceanographic">oceanographic</a> conditions that in turn may affect food abundance and distribution: strong currents, strong tides, increased siltation, change in water temperatures, overturn in the water column, etc.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_storms">Extraterrestrial storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/220px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif" width="220" height="73" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/330px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif/440px-PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif 2x" data-file-width="1799" data-file-height="600" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PIA02863_-_Jupiter_surface_motion_animation.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> on Jupiter</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Storms do not only occur on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>; other planetary bodies with a sufficient atmosphere (<a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a> in particular) also undergo stormy weather. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a> on <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a> provides a well-known example. Though technically an anticyclone with greater than hurricane wind speeds, it is larger than the Earth and has persisted for at least 340 years, having first been observed by astronomer <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>. <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a> also had its own lesser-known <a href="/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot" title="Great Dark Spot">Great Dark Spot</a>.</p>\n<p>In September 1994 the <a href="/wiki/Hubble_telescope" class="mw-redirect" title="Hubble telescope">Hubble telescope</a> \xe2\x80\x93 using <a href="/wiki/Wide_Field_Planetary_Camera_2" class="mw-redirect" title="Wide Field Planetary Camera 2">Wide Field Planetary Camera 2</a> \xe2\x80\x93 imaged storms on <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a> generated by upwelling of warmer air, similar to a terrestrial thunderhead. The east-west extent of the same-year<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> storm equalled the diameter of Earth. The storm was observed earlier in September 1990 and acquired the name <a href="/wiki/Dragon_Storm_(astronomy)" title="Dragon Storm (astronomy)">Dragon Storm</a>.</p>\n<p>The dust storms of <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> vary in size, but can often cover the entire planet. They tend to occur when Mars comes closest to the Sun, and have been shown to increase the global temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>One particularly large Martian storm was exhaustively studied up close due to coincidental timing. When the first <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">spacecraft</a> to successfully orbit another planet, <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">Mariner 9</a>, arrived and successfully orbited Mars on 14 November 1971, <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary scientists</a> were surprised to find the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_body%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Celestial body\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> was thick with a planet-wide robe of <a href="/wiki/Dust" title="Dust">dust</a>, the largest storm ever observed on Mars. The surface of the planet was totally obscured. Mariner 9\'s computer was reprogrammed from Earth to delay imaging of the surface for a couple of months until the dust settled. However, the surface-obscured images contributed much to the collection of Mars atmospheric and planetary surface science.<sup id="cite_ref-Pyle2012-M9_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pyle2012-M9-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Two <a href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planets</a> are known to have storms: <a href="/wiki/HD_209458_b" title="HD 209458 b">HD 209458 b</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/HD_80606_b" title="HD 80606 b">HD 80606 b</a>. The former\'s storm was discovered on June 23, 2010 and measured at 6,200&#160;km/h, while the latter produces winds of 17,700 kilometers (11,000&#160;mi) per hour across the surface. title="Sunset">sunset</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>.</p>\n<p>Shipwrecks are common with the passage of strong tropical cyclones. Such shipwrecks can change the course of history,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> as well as influence art and literature. A hurricane led to a victory of the Spanish over the French for control of Fort Caroline, and ultimately the Atlantic coast of North America, in 1565.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Strong <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> from any storm type can damage or destroy vehicles, buildings, bridges, and other outside objects, turning loose debris into deadly flying projectiles. In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">major hurricanes</a> comprise just 21% of all landfalling tropical cyclones, but account for 83% of all damage.<sup id="cite_ref-faqd5_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-faqd5-17">[17]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones often knock out power to tens or hundreds of thousands of people, preventing vital communication and hampering rescue efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-Power_failures_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Power_failures-18">[18]</a></sup> Tropical cyclones often destroy key bridges, overpasses, and roads, complicating efforts to transport food, clean water, and medicine to the areas that need it. Furthermore, the damage caused by tropical cyclones to buildings and dwellings can result in economic damage to a region, and to a <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> of the population of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-oxfo_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a>, or the increase in sea level due to the cyclone, is typically the worst effect from landfalling tropical cyclones, historically resulting in 90% of tropical cyclone deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-oxfo_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxfo-19">[19]</a></sup> The relatively quick surge in sea level can move miles/kilometers inland, flooding homes and cutting off escape routes. The storm surges and winds of hurricanes may be destructive to human-made structures, but they also stir up the waters of coastal estuaries, which are typically important fish breeding locales.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Lightning#Cloud-to-ground" title="Lightning">Cloud-to-ground lightning</a> frequently occurs within the phenomena of thunderstorms and have numerous hazards towards landscapes and populations. One of the more significant hazards lightning can pose is the wildfires they are capable of igniting.<sup id="cite_ref-Scott2000_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scott2000-20">[20]</a></sup> Under a regime of low precipitation (LP) thunderstorms, where little precipitation is present, rainfall cannot prevent fires from starting when vegetation is dry as lightning produces a concentrated amount of extreme heat.<sup id="cite_ref-ufl_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ufl-21">[21]</a></sup> Wildfires can devastate vegetation and the biodiversity of an ecosystem. Wildfires that occur close to urban environments can inflict damages upon infrastructures, buildings, crops, and provide risks to explosions, should the flames be exposed to gas pipes. Direct damage caused by lightning strikes occurs on occasion.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In areas with a high frequency for cloud-to-ground lightning, like <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, lightning causes several fatalities per year, most commonly to people working outside.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Precipitation with low <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">potential of hydrogen levels</a> (pH), otherwise known as acid rain, is also a frequent risk produced by lightning. <a href="/wiki/Distilled_water" title="Distilled water">Distilled water</a>, which contains no <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, has a <a href="/wiki/Neutral_solution" class="mw-redirect" title="Neutral solution">neutral</a> <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> of 7. Liquids with a pH less than 7 are acidic, and those with a pH greater than 7 are bases. \xe2\x80\x9cClean\xe2\x80\x9d or unpolluted rain has a slightly acidic pH of about 5.2, because carbon dioxide and water in the air react together to form <a href="/wiki/Carbonic_acid" title="Carbonic acid">carbonic acid</a>, a weak acid (pH 5.6 in distilled water), but unpolluted rain also contains other chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nitric_oxide" title="Nitric oxide">Nitric oxide</a> present during thunderstorm phenomena,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> caused by the splitting of nitrogen molecules, can result in the production of acid rain, if nitric oxide forms compounds with the water molecules in precipitation, thus creating acid rain. Acid rain can damage infrastructures containing calcite or other solid chemical compounds containing carbon. In ecosystems, acid rain can dissolve plant tissues of vegetations and increase acidification process in bodies of water and in <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>, resulting in deaths of marine and terrestrial organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Hail damage to roofs often goes unnoticed until further structural damage is seen, such as leaks or cracks. It is hardest to recognize hail damage on shingled roofs and flat roofs, but all roofs have their own hail damage detection problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Hail_Damage_to_Roofs_from_Adjusting_Today_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hail_Damage_to_Roofs_from_Adjusting_Today-27">[27]</a></sup> Metal roofs are fairly resistant to hail damage, but may accumulate cosmetic damage in the form of dents and damaged coatings.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Hail is also a common nuisance to drivers of automobiles, severely denting the vehicle and cracking or even shattering <a href="/wiki/Windshields" class="mw-redirect" title="Windshields">windshields</a> and <a href="/wiki/Car_glass" title="Car glass">windows</a>. Rarely, massive hailstones have been known to cause <a href="/wiki/Concussion" title="Concussion">concussions</a> or fatal head <a href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical trauma">trauma</a>. Hailstorms have been the cause of costly and deadly events throughout history. One of the earliest recorded incidents occurred around the 9th century in <a href="/wiki/Roopkund" title="Roopkund">Roopkund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttarakhand" title="Uttarakhand">Uttarakhand</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The largest hailstone in terms of diameter and weight ever recorded in the United States fell on July 23, 2010 in <a href="/wiki/Vivian,_South_Dakota" title="Vivian, South Dakota">Vivian, South Dakota</a> in the United States; it measured 8 inches (20&#160;cm) in diameter and 18.62 inches (47.3&#160;cm) in circumference, weighing in at 1.93 pounds (0.88&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-Record_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Record-30">[30]</a></sup> This broke the previous record for diameter set by a hailstone 7&#160;inches diameter and 18.75&#160;inches circumference which fell in <a href="/wiki/Aurora,_Nebraska" title="Aurora, Nebraska">Aurora, Nebraska</a> in the United States on June 22, 2003, as well as the record for weight, set by a hailstone of 1.67 pounds (0.76&#160;kg) that fell in <a href="/wiki/Coffeyville,_Kansas" title="Coffeyville, Kansas">Coffeyville, Kansas</a> in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-Record_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Record-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Various hazards, ranging from hail to lightning can affect outside technology facilities, such as <a href="/wiki/Antennas" class="mw-redirect" title="Antennas">antennas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Satellite_dishes" class="mw-redirect" title="Satellite dishes">satellite dishes</a>, and towers. As a result, companies with outside facilities have begun installing such facilities underground, in order to reduce the risk of damage from storms.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Substantial snowfall can disrupt public infrastructure and services, slowing human activity even in regions that are accustomed to such weather. Air and ground transport may be greatly inhibited or shut down entirely. Populations living in snow-prone areas have developed various ways to travel across the snow, such as <a href="/wiki/Ski" title="Ski">skis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe" title="Snowshoe">snowshoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sled" title="Sled">sleds</a> pulled by horses, dogs, or other animals and later, <a href="/wiki/Snowmobile" title="Snowmobile">snowmobiles</a>. Basic utilities such as <a href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity distribution">electricity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telephone_line" title="Telephone line">telephone lines</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas#Uses" title="Natural gas">gas supply</a> can also fail. In addition, snow can make roads much harder to travel and vehicles attempting to use them can easily become stuck.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The combined effects can lead to a "<a href="/wiki/Weather-related_cancellation" title="Weather-related cancellation">snow day</a>" on which gatherings such as school, work, or church are officially canceled. In areas that normally have very little or no snow, a snow day may occur when there is only light accumulation or even the threat of snowfall, since those areas are unprepared to handle any amount of snow. In some areas, such as some states in the United States, schools are given a yearly quota of snow days (or "calamity days"). Once the quota is exceeded, the snow days must be made up.<sup id="cite_ref-DDN_Larsen_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DDN_Larsen-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WCMH_Willis_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WCMH_Willis-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WKTV_Ferris_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WKTV_Ferris-35">[35]</a></sup> In other states, all snow days must be made up.<sup id="cite_ref-Enquirer_Wolff_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Enquirer_Wolff-36">[36]</a></sup> For example, schools may extend the remaining school days later into the afternoon, shorten <a href="/wiki/Spring_break" title="Spring break">spring break</a>, or delay the start of <a href="/wiki/Summer_vacation" title="Summer vacation">summer vacation</a>.</p>\n<p>Accumulated snow is <a href="/wiki/Snow_removal" title="Snow removal">removed</a> to make travel easier and safer, and to decrease the long-term effect of a heavy snowfall. This process utilizes <a href="/wiki/Shovel" title="Shovel">shovels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowplow" title="Snowplow">snowplows</a>, and is often assisted by sprinkling <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">salt</a> or other chloride-based chemicals, which reduce the melting temperature of snow.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> In some areas with abundant snowfall, such as <a href="/wiki/Yamagata_Prefecture" title="Yamagata Prefecture">Yamagata Prefecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, people harvest snow and store it surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">insulation</a> in ice houses. This allows the snow to be used through the summer for refrigeration and air conditioning, which requires far less electricity than traditional cooling methods.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agriculture">Agriculture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agriculture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Hail can cause serious damage, notably to <a href="/wiki/Automobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile">automobiles</a>, aircraft, skylights, glass-roofed structures, <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">livestock</a>, and most commonly, farmers\' <a href="/wiki/Crops" class="mw-redirect" title="Crops">crops</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nolanhail_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nolanhail-39">[39]</a></sup> Wheat, corn, soybeans, and tobacco are the most sensitive crops to hail damage.<sup id="cite_ref-Oliver_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oliver-40">[40]</a></sup> Hail is one of Canada\'s most expensive hazards.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Snowfall can be beneficial to agriculture by serving as a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_insulation" title="Thermal insulation">thermal insulator</a>, conserving the heat of the Earth and protecting <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">crops</a> from subfreezing weather. Some agricultural areas depend on an accumulation of snow during winter that will melt gradually in spring, providing water for crop growth. If it melts into water and refreezes upon sensitive crops, such as oranges, the resulting ice will protect the fruit from exposure to lower temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Although tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> take an enormous toll in lives and personal property, they may be important factors in the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> regimes of places they affect and bring much-needed precipitation to otherwise dry regions. Hurricanes in the eastern north Pacific often supply moisture to the <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">Southwestern United States</a> and parts of Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-2005_EPac_outlook_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2005_EPac_outlook-43">[43]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> receives over half of its rainfall from typhoons.<sup id="cite_ref-Whipple_54_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whipple_54-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Camille" title="Hurricane Camille">Hurricane Camille</a> averted drought conditions and ended water deficits along much of its path,<sup id="cite_ref-Christopherson_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Christopherson-45">[45]</a></sup> though it also killed 259&#160;people and caused $9.14&#160;billion (2005 <a href="/wiki/USD" class="mw-redirect" title="USD">USD</a>) in damage.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" Merely correcting for the initial gust front can have dire consequences.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Hail is one of the most significant thunderstorm hazards to aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> When hail stones exceed 0.5 inches (13&#160;mm) in diameter, planes can be seriously damaged within seconds.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The hailstones accumulating on the ground can also be hazardous to landing aircraft. Strong wind outflow from thunderstorms causes rapid changes in the three-dimensional wind velocity just above ground level. Initially, this outflow causes a headwind that increases airspeed, which normally causes a pilot to reduce engine power if they are unaware of the wind shear. As the aircraft passes into the region of the downdraft, the localized headwind diminishes, reducing the aircraft\'s airspeed and increasing its sink rate. Then, when the aircraft passes through the other side of the downdraft, the headwind becomes a tailwind, reducing lift generated by the wings, and leaving the aircraft in a low-power, low-speed descent. This can lead to an accident if the aircraft is too low to effect a recovery before ground contact. As the result of the accidents in the 1970s and 1980s, in 1988 the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration" title="Federal Aviation Administration">Federal Aviation Administration</a> mandated that all commercial aircraft have <a href="/wiki/Airborne_wind_shear_detection_and_alert_system" title="Airborne wind shear detection and alert system">on-board wind shear detection systems</a> by 1993. Between 1964 and 1985, wind shear directly caused or contributed to 26 major civil transport aircraft accidents in the U.S. that led to 620 deaths and 200 injuries. Since 1995, the number of major civil aircraft accidents caused by wind shear has dropped to approximately one every ten years, due to the mandated on-board detection as well as the addition of Doppler <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> units on the ground. (<a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recreation">Recreation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recreation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Winter_sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Winter sports">winter sports</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding" title="Snowboarding">snowboarding</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Snowmobiling" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowmobiling">snowmobiling</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ISMA-stat_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ISMA-stat-51">[51]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Snowshoe" title="Snowshoe">snowshoeing</a> depend upon snow. Where snow is scarce but the temperature is low enough, <a href="/wiki/Snow_cannon" class="mw-redirect" title="Snow cannon">snow cannons</a> may be used to produce an adequate amount for such sports.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> Children and adults can play on a <a href="/wiki/Sled" title="Sled">sled</a> or ride in a <a href="/wiki/Sleigh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sleigh">sleigh</a>. Although a person\'s footsteps remain a visible <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lifeline" class="extiw" title="wikt:lifeline">lifeline</a> within a snow-covered landscape, snow cover is considered a general danger to hiking since the snow obscures landmarks and makes the landscape itself appear uniform.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Storm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In mythology and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a>, a giant storm sent by <a href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a> flooded the Earth. <a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Noah</a> and his family and the animals entered <a href="/wiki/Noah%27s_Ark" title="Noah\'s Ark">the Ark</a>, and "the same day were all the fountains of the great deep broken up, and the windows of heaven were opened, and the rain was upon the earth forty days and forty nights." The flood covered even the highest mountains to a depth of more than twenty feet, and all creatures died; only Noah and those with him on the Ark were left alive. In the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> is recorded to have calmed a storm on the <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Galilee" title="Sea of Galilee">Sea of Galilee</a>.</p>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Genesis_flood_myth" class="mw-redirect" title="Genesis flood myth">Genesis flood myth</a></div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Gilgamesh_flood_myth" title="Gilgamesh flood myth">Gilgamesh flood myth</a> is a deluge story in the <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_of_Gilgamesh" title="Epic of Gilgamesh">Epic of Gilgamesh</a></i>.</p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> there were several gods of storms: <a href="/wiki/Briareos" class="mw-redirect" title="Briareos">Briareos</a>, the god of sea storms; Aigaios, a god of the violent sea storms; and <a href="/wiki/Aiolos" class="mw-redirect" title="Aiolos">Aiolos</a>, keeper of storm-winds, squalls and tempests.</p>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Sea_Venture" title="Sea Venture">Sea Venture</a></i> was wrecked near <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> in 1609, which led to the colonization of Bermuda<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> and provided the inspiration for <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare\'s</a> play <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Tempest (play)">The Tempest</a></i>(1611).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> Specifically, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gates_(governor)" title="Thomas Gates (governor)">Sir Thomas Gates</a>, future governor of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, was on his way to England from <a href="/wiki/Jamestown,_Virginia" title="Jamestown, Virginia">Jamestown, Virginia</a>. On <a href="/wiki/Saint_James_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint James Day">Saint James Day</a>, while he was between <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>, a hurricane raged for nearly two days. Though one of the small vessels in the fleet sank to the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Straits" class="mw-redirect" title="Florida Straits">Florida Straits</a>, seven of the remaining vessels reached <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> within several days after the storm. The flagship of the fleet, known as <i>Sea Adventure</i>, disappeared and was presumed lost. A small bit of fortune befell the ship and her crew when they made landfall on Bermuda. The vessel was damaged on a surrounding <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reef</a>, but all aboard survived for nearly a year on the island. The British colonists claimed the island and quickly settled Bermuda. In May 1610, they set forth for Jamestown, this time arriving at their destination.</p>\n<p>The children\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Wizard_of_Oz" title="The Wonderful Wizard of Oz">The Wonderful Wizard of Oz</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/L._Frank_Baum" title="L. Frank Baum">L. Frank Baum</a> and illustrated by <a href="/wiki/W._W._Denslow" class="mw-redirect" title="W. W. Denslow">W. W. Denslow</a>, chronicles the adventures of a young girl named <a href="/wiki/Dorothy_Gale" title="Dorothy Gale">Dorothy Gale</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Oz" title="Land of Oz">Land of Oz</a>, after being swept away from her Kansas farm home by a tornado. The story was originally published by the George M. Hill Company in Chicago on May 17, 1900 and has since been reprinted numerous times, most often under the name <i>The Wizard of Oz</i>, and adapted for use in other media. Thanks in part to the <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1939_film)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1939 film)">1939 MGM movie</a>, it is one of the best-known stories in American popular culture and has been widely translated. Its initial success, and the success of the popular <a href="/wiki/The_Wizard_of_Oz_(1902_musical)" title="The Wizard of Oz (1902 musical)">1902 Broadway musical</a> which Baum adapted from his original story, led to Baum\'s writing thirteen more <a href="/wiki/List_of_Oz_books" title="List of Oz books">Oz books</a>.</p>\n<p>Hollywood director <a href="/wiki/King_Vidor" title="King Vidor">King Vidor</a> (February 8, 1894&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 November 1, 1982) survived the <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Hurricane_of_1900" class="mw-redirect" title="Galveston Hurricane of 1900">Galveston Hurricane of 1900</a> as a boy. Based on that experience, he published a fictionalized account of that cyclone, titled "Southern Storm", for the May 1935 issue of <a href="/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)"><i>Esquire</i> magazine</a>. <a href="/wiki/Erik_Larson_(author)" title="Erik Larson (author)">Erik Larson</a> excerpts a passage from that article in his 2005 book, <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Storm" title="Isaac\'s Storm">Isaac\'s Storm</a></i>:<sup id="cite_ref-Larson.2C_Erik_1999_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larson.2C_Erik_1999-56">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>I remember now that it seemed as if we were in a bowl looking up toward the level of the sea. As we stood there in the sandy street, my mother and I, I wanted to take my mother\'s hand and hurry her away. I felt as if the sea was going to break over the edge of the bowl and come puring down upon us.</i></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Numerous other accounts of the Galveston Hurricane of 1900 have been made in print and in film. Larson cites many of them in <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac%27s_Storm" title="Isaac\'s Storm">Isaac\'s Storm</a></i>, which centrally features that storm, as well as chronicles the creation of the Weather Bureau (which came to known as the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>) and that agency\'s fateful rivalry with the weather service in Cuba, and a number of other major storms, such as those which ravaged <a href="/wiki/Indianola,_Texas" title="Indianola, Texas">Indianola, Texas</a> in 1875 and <a href="/wiki/1886_Indianola_hurricane" title="1886 Indianola hurricane">1886</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Larson.2C_Erik_1999_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larson.2C_Erik_1999-56">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Great_Storm_of_1987" title="Great Storm of 1987">Great Storm of 1987</a> is key in an important scene near the end of <i><a href="/wiki/Possession_(Byatt_novel)" title="Possession (Byatt novel)">Possession: A Romance</a></i>, the bestselling and <a href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Booker Prize">Man Booker Prize</a>-winning novel by <a href="/wiki/A._S._Byatt" title="A. S. Byatt">A. S. Byatt</a>. The Great Storm of 1987 occurred on the night of October 15\xe2\x80\x9316, 1987, when an unusually strong weather system caused winds to hit much of <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">southern England</a> and northern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. It was the worst storm to hit England since the <a href="/wiki/Great_Storm_of_1703" title="Great Storm of 1703">Great Storm of 1703</a><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina_in_fiction" title="Hurricane Katrina in fiction">Hurricane Katrina</a> (2005) has Turner\'s representations of powerful natural forces reinvented the traditional <a href="/wiki/Seascape" title="Seascape">seascape</a> during the first half of the nineteenth century.</p>\n<p>Upon his travels to Holland, he took note of the familiar large rolling waves of the English seashore transforming into the sharper, choppy waves of a Dutch storm. A characteristic example of Turner\xe2\x80\x99s dramatic seascape is <i><a href="/wiki/The_Slave_Ship" title="The Slave Ship">The Slave Ship</a></i> of 1840. Aivazovsky left several thousand turbulent canvases in which he increasingly eliminated human figures and historical background to focus on such essential elements as light, sea, and sky.
