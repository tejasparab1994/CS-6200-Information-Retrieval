atmospheric, <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ground_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Ground water">ground water</a>. A drought can last for months or years, or may be declared after as few as 15 days.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It can have a substantial impact on the <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> of the affected region<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and harm to the local <a href="/wiki/Economy" title="Economy">economy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Annual dry seasons in the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> significantly increase the chances of a drought developing and subsequent bush fires. Periods of heat can significantly worsen drought conditions by hastening evaporation of <a href="/wiki/Water_vapour" class="mw-redirect" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a>.</p>\n<p>Many plant species, such as those in the family Cactaceae (or <a href="/wiki/Cactus" title="Cactus">cacti</a>), have <a href="/wiki/Drought_tolerant" class="mw-redirect" title="Drought tolerant">drought tolerance</a> adaptations like reduced leaf area and waxy cuticles to enhance their ability to tolerate drought. Some others survive dry periods as buried seeds. Semi-permanent drought produces arid biomes such as deserts and grasslands.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Prolonged droughts have caused <a href="/wiki/Mass_migration" title="Mass migration">mass migrations</a> and humanitarian crises. Most arid ecosystems have inherently low productivity. The most prolonged drought ever in the world in <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a> occurred in the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> (400 Years).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" <a href="/wiki/Convective" class="mw-redirect" title="Convective">convective</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratiform</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Orographic_lift" title="Orographic lift">orographic</a> rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Convective processes involve strong vertical motions that can cause the overturning of the atmosphere in that location within an hour and cause heavy precipitation,<sup id="cite_ref-convection_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-convection-8">[8]</a></sup> while stratiform processes involve weaker upward motions and less intense precipitation over a longer duration.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Precipitation can be divided into three categories, based on whether it falls as liquid water, liquid water that freezes on contact with the surface, or ice. Droughts occur mainly in areas where normal levels of rainfall are, in themselves, low. If these factors do not support precipitation volumes sufficient to reach the surface over a sufficient time, the result is a drought. Drought can be triggered by a high level of reflected sunlight and above average prevalence of high <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">pressure systems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> carrying continental, rather than oceanic air masses, and ridges of <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure area">high pressure areas</a> aloft can prevent or restrict the developing of thunderstorm activity or rainfall over one certain region. Once a region is within drought, feedback mechanisms such as local arid air,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> hot conditions which can promote warm core ridging,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and minimal evapotranspiration can worsen drought conditions.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet</a> and dry <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a> emerge due to the movement of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">Monsoon trough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wang_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wang-12">[12]</a></sup> The dry season greatly increases drought occurrence,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and is characterized by its low humidity, with watering holes and rivers drying up. Because of the lack of these watering holes, many grazing animals are forced to migrate due to the lack of water and feed to more fertile spots. Examples of such animals are <a href="/wiki/Zebras" class="mw-redirect" title="Zebras">zebras</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elephants" class="mw-redirect" title="Elephants">elephants</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wildebeest" title="Wildebeest">wildebeest</a>. Because of the lack of water in the plants, bushfires are common.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Since water vapor becomes more energetic with increasing temperature, more water vapor is required to increase relative humidity values to 100% at higher temperatures (or to get the temperature to fall to the dew point).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Periods of warmth quicken the pace of fruit and vegetable production,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> increase evaporation and transpiration from plants,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> and worsen drought conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="El_Ni\xc3\xb1o"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="El_Ni.C3.B1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</span><span title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> during El Ni\xc3\xb1o events. Winters during the El Ni\xc3\xb1o are warmer and drier than average conditions in the Northwest, northern Midwest, and northern Mideast United States, so those regions experience reduced snowfalls. Conditions are also drier than normal from December to February in south-central Africa, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Zambia" title="Zambia">Zambia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">Mozambique</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">Botswana</a>. Direct effects of El Ni\xc3\xb1o resulting in drier conditions occur in parts of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Australia" title="Northern Australia">Northern Australia</a>, increasing <a href="/wiki/Bush_fire" class="mw-redirect" title="Bush fire">bush fires</a>, worsening <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a>, and decreasing air quality dramatically. Drier-than-normal conditions are also in general observed in <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a>, inland <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, inland <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> from June to August. As warm water spreads from the west Pacific and the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> to the east Pacific, it causes extensive drought in the western Pacific. Singapore experienced the driest February in 2014 since records began in 1869, with only 6.3&#160;mm of rain falling in the month and temperatures hitting as high as 35&#160;\xc2\xb0C on 26 February. People use <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> deforestation to clear land for agriculture.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Human activity can directly trigger exacerbating factors such as over farming, excessive <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Galilee_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Galilee-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> adversely impact the ability of the land to capture and hold water.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In arid climates, the main source of erosion is wind.<sup id="cite_ref-Erosion_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Erosion-23">[23]</a></sup> Erosion can be the result of material movement by the wind. The wind can cause small particles to be lifted and therefore moved to another region (deflation). Suspended particles within the wind may impact on solid objects causing erosion by abrasion (ecological succession). Wind erosion generally occurs in areas with little or no vegetation, often in areas where there is insufficient rainfall to support vegetation.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fields_outside_benambra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/220px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg" width="220" height="90" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/330px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Fields_outside_benambra.jpg/440px-Fields_outside_benambra.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2250" data-file-height="924" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fields_outside_benambra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFields outside <a href="/wiki/Benambra,_Victoria" title="Benambra, Victoria">Benambra, Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> suffering from drought conditions.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">Loess</a> is a homogeneous, typically nonstratified, porous, <a href="/wiki/Friable" class="mw-redirect" title="Friable">friable</a>, slightly coherent, often calcareous, fine-grained, <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silty</a>, pale yellow or buff, windblown (<a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">Aeolian</a>) <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> It generally occurs as a widespread blanket deposit that covers areas of hundreds of square kilometers and tens of meters thick. Loess often stands in either steep or vertical faces.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Loess tends to develop into highly rich soils. Under appropriate climatic conditions, areas with loess are among the most agriculturally productive in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Loess deposits are geologically unstable by nature, and will erode very readily. Therefore, windbreaks (such as big trees and bushes) are often planted by farmers to reduce the wind erosion of loess.<sup id="cite_ref-Erosion_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Erosion-23">[23]</a></sup> Wind erosion is much more severe in arid areas and during times of drought. For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Plains" title="Great Plains">Great Plains</a>, it is estimated that soil loss due to wind erosion can be as much as 6100 times greater in drought years than in wet years.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drought&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a></div>\n<p>Activities resulting in global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> are expected to trigger droughts with a <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_and_agriculture" title="Climate change and agriculture">substantial impact on agriculture</a><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> throughout the world, and especially in <a href="/wiki/Developing_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Developing nation">developing nations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Overall, global warming will result in increased world rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Along with drought in some areas, flooding and erosion will increase in others. irrigation and crop rotation. Failure to develop adequate drought mitigation strategies carries a grave human cost in the modern era, exacerbated by <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">ever-increasing</a> population densities. President Roosevelt on April 27, 1935, signed documents creating the Soil Conservation Service (SCS)\xe2\x80\x94now the Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS). Models of the law were sent to each state where they were enacted. These were the first enduring practical programs to curtail future susceptibility to drought, creating agencies that first began to stress soil conservation measures to protect farm lands today. It was not until the 1950s that there was an importance placed on water conservation was put into the existing laws (NRCS 2014).<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/220px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png" width="220" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/330px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png/440px-September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="716" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:September_Smoke_Over_the_Amazon_from_2005-2008.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAerosols over the Amazon each September for four burning seasons (2005 through 2008) during the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> drought. For instance, analysis of water usage in <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">Yemen</a> has revealed that their <a href="/wiki/Water_table" title="Water table">water table</a> (underground water level) is put at grave risk by over-use to fertilize their <a href="/wiki/Khat" title="Khat">Khat</a> crop.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Careful monitoring of moisture levels can also help predict increased risk for wildfires, using such metrics as the <a href="/wiki/Keetch-Byram_Drought_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Keetch-Byram Drought Index">Keetch-Byram Drought Index</a><sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-49">[49]</a></sup> or <a href="/wiki/Palmer_Drought_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Palmer Drought Index">Palmer Drought Index</a>.</li>\n<li>Land use - Carefully planned <a href="/wiki/Crop_rotation" title="Crop rotation">crop rotation</a> can help to minimize <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and allow farmers to plant less water-dependent crops in drier years.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Outdoor_water-use_restriction" title="Outdoor water-use restriction">Outdoor water-use restriction</a> - Regulating the use of sprinklers, hoses or buckets on outdoor plants, filling pools, and other water-intensive home maintenance tasks. <a href="/wiki/Xeriscaping" title="Xeriscaping">Xeriscaping</a> yards can significantly reduce unnecessary water use by residents of towns and cities.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Rainwater_harvesting" title="Rainwater harvesting">Rainwater harvesting</a> - Collection and storage of rainwater from roofs or other suitable catchments.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Recycled_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Recycled water">Recycled water</a> - Former wastewater (sewage) that has been treated and purified for reuse.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Transvasement" class="mw-redirect" title="Transvasement">Transvasement</a> - Building canals or redirecting rivers as massive attempts at <a href="/wiki/Irrigation" title="Irrigation">irrigation</a> in drought-prone areas.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
