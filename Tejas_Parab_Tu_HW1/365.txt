Pielke">Roger A. Pielke</a>, atmospheric scientist (land and sea interactions with atmosphere, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_dynamics" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric dynamics">atmospheric dynamics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>)</div>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Roger A. Pielke Jr.</b> (born November 2, 1968) is an American political scientist and <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> and the director of the Sports Governance Center within the Department of Athletics at the Center for Science and Technology Policy Research at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Boulder" title="University of Colorado Boulder">University of Colorado Boulder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Center_for_Science_and_Technology_Policy_Research_17_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Center_for_Science_and_Technology_Policy_Research_17-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>He previously served in the Environmental Studies Program and was a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_Institute_for_Research_in_Environmental_Sciences" title="Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences">Cooperative Institute for Research in Environmental Sciences</a> (CIRES) where he served as Director of the Center for Science and Technology Policy Research at the University of Colorado Boulder from 2001 to 2007. Pielke was a visiting scholar at Oxford University\'s <a href="/wiki/Sa%C3%AFd_Business_School" title="Sa\xc3\xafd Business School">Sa\xc3\xafd Business School</a> in the 2007-2008 academic year.<sup id="cite_ref-JMI_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JMI-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A prolific writer, his interests include understanding the <a href="/wiki/Politicization_of_science" title="Politicization of science">politicization of science</a>; <a href="/wiki/Decision_making" class="mw-redirect" title="Decision making">decision making</a> under uncertainty; policy education for scientists in areas such as climate change, <a href="/wiki/Disaster_mitigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Disaster mitigation">disaster mitigation</a>, and world trade; and research on the governance of sports organizations, including <a href="/wiki/FIFA" title="FIFA">FIFA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Collegiate_Athletic_Association" title="National Collegiate Athletic Association">NCAA</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Education_and_background"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_A._Pielke_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Pielke earned a B.A. in <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">mathematics</a> (1990), an M.A. in <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">public policy</a> (1992), and a Ph.D. in <a href="/wiki/Political_science" title="Political science">political science</a>, all from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_Boulder" title="University of Colorado Boulder">University of Colorado Boulder</a>. Prior to his positions at CU-Boulder, from 1993 to 2001 he was a staff scientist<sup id="cite_ref-Revkin08_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Revkin08-3">[3]</a></sup> in the Environmental and Societal Impacts Group of the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a>. From 2002 to 2004 Pielke was Director of Graduate Studies for the CU-Boulder Graduate Program in Environmental Studies and in 2001 students selected him for the Outstanding Graduate Advisor Award. Pielke serves on numerous editorial boards and advisory committees, retains many professional affiliations, and sat on the Board of Directors of WeatherData, Inc. from 2001 to 2006. In 2012 he was awarded an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">honorary doctorate</a> by <a href="/wiki/Link%C3%B6ping_University" title="Link\xc3\xb6ping University">Link\xc3\xb6ping University</a><sup id="cite_ref-Linkoping_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linkoping-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Linkoping2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linkoping2-5">[5]</a></sup> and the Public Service Award of the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Society_of_America" title="Geological Society of America">Geological Society of America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GSA-award_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GSA-award-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_writing">Professional writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_A._Pielke_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Pielke\'s early work was on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> program. In 1993 he argued that the shuttle was expensive and risky - that it was "probable" that another orbiter would be lost within 20-35 flights.<sup id="cite_ref-SpacePolicy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpacePolicy-7">[7]</a></sup> Shortly before the loss of Columbia he warned that loss of another shuttle was only a matter of time.<sup id="cite_ref-HC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HC-8">[8]</a></sup> He has also been critical of the space station program.<sup id="cite_ref-SPA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPA-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Pielke has also written extensively on <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_global_warming" title="Politics of global warming">policy</a>. He has written that he accepts the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">IPCC</a> view of the underlying science, stating, "The IPCC has concluded that greenhouse gas emissions resulting from human activity are an important driver of changes in climate. And on this basis alone I am personally convinced that it makes sense to take action to limit greenhouse gas emissions."<sup id="cite_ref-HGRT_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HGRT-10">[10]</a></sup> He also states that, "Any conceivable emissions reductions policies, even if successful, cannot have a perceptible impact on the climate for many decades", and from this he concludes that, "In coming decades the only policies that can effectively be used to manage the immediate effects of climate variability and change will be adaptive."<sup id="cite_ref-NatureTaboo_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatureTaboo-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Adaptation_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Adaptation-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On the issues of <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">hurricanes and climate change</a> he has argued that the trend in increasing damage from hurricanes is primarily due to societal and economic factors (chiefly an increase in wealth density), rather than change in the frequency and intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-Society_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Society-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Pielke has been labeled a "skeptic" and "denier" of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, though Pielke strongly objects to those labels.<sup id="cite_ref-Pielke_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pielke-14">[14]</a></sup> His views on climate change have been widely criticized by scientists, including US presidential science adviser <a href="/wiki/John_Holdren" title="John Holdren">John Holdren</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Pielke rejected Holdren\'s criticisms, saying "the facts are on my side",<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> and several publications defended Pielke.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> In October 2016, in a <a href="/wiki/Podesta_emails" title="Podesta emails">hacked email</a> disclosed by Wikileaks,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Judd_Legum" title="Judd Legum">Judd Legum</a>, the editor of <a href="/wiki/ThinkProgress" title="ThinkProgress">ThinkProgress</a>, a site that is part of the Center for American Progress Action Fund, states that a ThinkProgress blog helped to discredit <a href="/wiki/Roger_Pielke_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Roger Pielke Jr.">Roger Pielke Jr.</a> and get him fired from the <a href="/wiki/FiveThirtyEight" title="FiveThirtyEight">FiveThirtyEight</a> website.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> But <i><a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a></i> reported on this in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Calderone_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calderone-22">[22]</a></sup> FiveThirtyEight published a Pielke article in March 2014<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> that climate scientists <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mann" title="Michael Mann">Michael Mann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Trenberth" class="mw-redirect" title="Kevin Trenberth">Kevin Trenberth</a> criticized as "deeply misleading" and "demonstrably wrong" in interviews with ThinkProgress.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> The scientists said that Pielke sent them threatening e-mails in response to their criticism, calling it "libelous"; Pielke responded that their characterization of his e-mails as "threatening" was "ridiculous". Legum told <i>The Huffington Post</i> that he contacted <a href="/wiki/Nate_Silver" title="Nate Silver">Nate Silver</a>, the editor of FiveThirtyEight, which apologized to the two scientists for Pielke\'s behavior.<sup id="cite_ref-Calderone_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Calderone-22">[22]</a></sup> Other criticisms of Pielke\'s article followed.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> published an article by Dana Nuccitelli called "FiveThirtyEight undermines its brand by misrepresenting climate research",<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/John_Abraham_(professor)" title="John Abraham (professor)">John Abraham</a> wrote that Pielke\'s "conclusions are not taken seriously by myself and other climate scientists."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In April 2015, Pielke joined with a group of scholars in issuing <i>An Ecomodernist Manifesto</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-EcoModernistManifesto_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EcoModernistManifesto-28">[28]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT41415_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT41415-29">[29]</a></sup> The other authors were: John Asafu-Adjaye, Linus Blomqvist, <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Brand" title="Stewart Brand">Stewart Brand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barry_Brook_(scientist)" title="Barry Brook (scientist)">Barry Brook</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ruth_DeFries" title="Ruth DeFries">Ruth DeFries</a>, Erle Ellis, Christopher Foreman, <a href="/wiki/David_Keith_(scientist)" title="David Keith (scientist)">David Keith</a>, Martin Lewis, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Lynas" title="Mark Lynas">Mark Lynas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Nordhaus" title="Ted Nordhaus">Ted Nordhaus</a>, Rachel Pritzker, Joyashree Roy, Mark Sagoff, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Shellenberger" title="Michael Shellenberger">Michael Shellenberger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stone_(director)" title="Robert Stone (director)">Robert Stone</a>, and Peter Teague<sup id="cite_ref-EcoModernismAuthors_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EcoModernismAuthors-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grijalva_Investigation">Grijalva Investigation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_A._Pielke_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grijalva Investigation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On February 24, 2015, ranking Democratic member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Natural_Resources" title="United States House Committee on Natural Resources">United States House Committee on Natural Resources</a> <a href="/wiki/Ra%C3%BAl_Grijalva" title="Ra\xc3\xbal Grijalva">Ra\xc3\xbal Grijalva</a> sent a letter to <a href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado" title="University of Colorado">University of Colorado</a> president <a href="/wiki/Bruce_D._Benson" title="Bruce D. Benson">Bruce D. Benson</a> requesting information on the sources of funding for and communications related to Pielke\'s research and Congressional testimony. The letter was one of seven Grijalva sent to various institutions stemming from concerns about the influence of funding from <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> companies on the work and testimony of <a href="/wiki/Harvard-Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics">Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics</a> researcher <a href="/wiki/Willie_Soon" title="Willie Soon">Willie Soon</a>. Pielke responded that he has no funding from fossil fuel interests and characterized the letter as part of a politically motivated "\'witch-hunt\'".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> In response to criticism that requesting communications was counter to principles of <a href="/wiki/Academic_freedom" title="Academic freedom">academic freedom</a>, Grijalva said he was willing to eliminate that part of the request.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The heads of some mainstream scientific organizations criticized Grijalva\xe2\x80\x99s letters. <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Leinen" title="Margaret Leinen">Margaret Leinen</a>, the president of the <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">American Geophysical Union</a> posted in her AGU blog that in requiring information only of a few scientists, based only on their scientific views, Grivalja\xe2\x80\x99s action was contrary to academic freedom: \xe2\x80\x9cWe view the singling out of any individual or group of scientists by any entity \xe2\x80\x93 governmental, corporate or other \xe2\x80\x93 based solely on their interpretations of scientific research as a threat to that freedom.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The executive director of the <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> wrote in a letter to Grvalja that his action \xe2\x80\x9csends a chilling message to all academic researchers,\xe2\x80\x9d and \xe2\x80\x9cimpinges on the free pursuit of ideas that is central to the concept of academic freedom.\xe2\x80\x9d<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Conservative columnist <a href="/wiki/Rich_Lowry" title="Rich Lowry">Rich Lowry</a>, editor of the <a href="/wiki/National_Review" title="National Review">National Review</a>, noted in an opinion piece that, "It\xe2\x80\x99s not that he [Pielke] doubts climate change, or even doubts that it could be harmful. His offense is merely pointing to data showing that extreme weather events like hurricanes, tornadoes, and droughts haven\xe2\x80\x99t yet been affected by climate change. This is enough to enrage advocates who need immediate disasters as a handy political cudgel."<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Pielke was also named in a letter sent by Senator <a href="/wiki/Edward_Markey" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Markey">Edward Markey</a> (D\xe2\x80\x93MA) to numerous energy industry groups, asking them to disclose the names of scientists they had funded. Regarding <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_Interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Conflict of Interest">Conflict of Interest</a> disclosures, Pielke said that "if you look at our community, the failure to disclose conflicts of interest is fairly endemic."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Roger_A._Pielke_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li>Editor, with Daniel Sarewitz and Radford Byerly Jr., <i>Prediction: Science, Decision Making, and the Future of Nature</i>, Island Press;
