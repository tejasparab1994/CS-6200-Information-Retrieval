There are several elements to tropical cyclone forecasting: track forecasting, intensity forecasting, rainfall forecasting, storm surge, tornado, and seasonal forecasting. While skill is increasing in regard to track forecasting, intensity forecasting skill remains nearly unchanged over the past several years. Seasonal forecasting began in the 1980s in the Atlantic basin and has spread into other basins in the years since.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Short_term">Short term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Short term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The methods through which tropical cyclones are forecast have changed with the passage of time. The first known forecasts in the Western Hemisphere were made by Lt. Col. William Reed of the <a href="/wiki/Corps_of_Royal_Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="Corps of Royal Engineers">Corps of Royal Engineers</a> at Barbados in 1847. Reed mostly utilized <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> measurements as the basis of his forecasts. Benito Vines introduced a forecast and warning system based on cloud cover changes in <a href="/wiki/Havana" title="Havana">Havana</a> during the 1870s. Before the early 1900s, though, most forecasts were done by direct observations at weather stations, which were then relayed to forecast centers via <a href="/wiki/Telegraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegraph">telegraph</a>. It wasn\xe2\x80\x99t until the advent of <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a> in the early twentieth century that observations from ships at sea were available to forecasters. The 1930s saw the usage of <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde" title="Radiosonde">radiosondes</a> in tropical cyclone forecasting. The next decade saw the advent of aircraft-based reconnaissance by the military, starting with the first dedicated flight into a hurricane in 1943, and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a> in 1944. In the 1950s, coastal <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radars</a> began to be used in the United States, and research reconnaissance flights by the precursor of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Oceanographic_and_Meteorological_Laboratory" title="Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratory">Hurricane Research Division</a> began in 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-MWR_NHC_history_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWR_NHC_history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The launch of the first weather satellite, TIROS-I, in 1960, introduced new forecasting techniques that remain important to tropical cyclone forecasting to the present. In the 1970s, buoys were introduced to improve the resolution of surface measurements, which until that point, were not available at all over sea surfaces.<sup id="cite_ref-MWR_NHC_history_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWR_NHC_history-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Long_term">Long term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Long term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In the late 1970s, <a href="/wiki/William_M._Gray" title="William M. Gray">William Gray</a> noticed a trend of low hurricane activity in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic basin</a> during <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> years. He was the first researcher to make a connection between such events and positive results led him to pursue further research. He found numerous factors across the globe influence tropical cyclone activity, such as connecting wet periods over the African <a href="/wiki/Sahel" title="Sahel">Sahel</a> to an increase in <a href="/wiki/Major_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Major hurricane">major hurricane</a> <a href="/wiki/Landfall" title="Landfall">landfalls</a> along the <a href="/wiki/United_States_East_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="United States East Coast">United States East Coast</a>. However, his findings also showed inconsistencies when only looking at a single factor as a primary influence.<sup id="cite_ref-HurricaneWatch_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HurricaneWatch-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Utilizing his findings, Gray developed an objective, statistical forecast for seasonal hurricane activity; he predicted only the number of tropical storms, hurricanes, and major hurricanes, foregoing specifics on tracks and potential landfalls due to the aforementioned inconsistencies.<sup id="cite_ref-HurricaneWatch_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HurricaneWatch-2">[2]</a></sup> Gray issued his first seasonal forecast ahead of the 1984 season, which used the statistical relationships between tropical cyclone activity, the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o%E2%80%93Southern_Oscillation" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o\xe2\x80\x93Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o\xe2\x80\x93Southern Oscillation</a> (ENSO), <a href="/wiki/Quasi-biennial_oscillation" title="Quasi-biennial oscillation">Quasi-biennial oscillation</a> (QBO), and Caribbean basin sea-level pressures.<sup id="cite_ref-1984_forecast_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1984_forecast-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The endeavor proved modestly successful.<sup id="cite_ref-HurricaneWatch_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HurricaneWatch-2">[2]</a></sup> He subsequently issued forecasts ahead of the start of the Atlantic hurricane season in May and before the peak of the season in August.<sup id="cite_ref-TMP_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TMP-5">[5]</a></sup> Students and colleagues joined his forecast team in the following years, including <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Landsea" title="Christopher Landsea">Christopher Landsea</a>, Paul W. Mielke Jr., and Kenneth J. Berry.<sup id="cite_ref-MooneyForecasting_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MooneyForecasting-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Track">Track</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Track">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">Tropical cyclone track forecasting</a></div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_forecast_model" title="Tropical cyclone forecast model">Tropical cyclone forecast model</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png/220px-NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png/330px-NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png/440px-NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1800" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NHC_Atlantic_Forecast_Error_Trends.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTrack errors for the Atlantic Basin, 1970\xe2\x80\x932014</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">large-scale synoptic flow</a> determines 70 to 90 percent of a tropical cyclone\'s motion. The deep-layer mean flow is considered to be the best tool in determining track direction and speed. If storms are significantly sheared, use of a lower-level wind is a better predictor. Knowledge of the beta effect can be used to steer a tropical cyclone, since it leads to a more northwest heading for tropical cyclones in the Northern Hemisphere. It is also best to smooth out short term wobbles of the storm center in order to determine a more accurate trajectory.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Because of the forces that affect tropical cyclone tracks, accurate track predictions depend on determining the position and strength of high- and low-pressure areas, and predicting how those areas will change during the life of a tropical system. Combining forecast models with increased understanding of the forces that act on tropical cyclones, and a wealth of data from Earth-orbiting satellites and other sensors, scientists have increased the accuracy of track forecasts over recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_forecast_verifications_models_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_forecast_verifications_models-8">[8]</a></sup> An accurate track forecast is important, because if the track forecast is incorrect, forecasts for intensity, rainfall, storm surge, and tornado threat will also be incorrect.</p>\n<h3><span for severe <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> (specifically <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a>) tracking and prediction. It refers to the rounded long-term <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">NHC/TPC</a> forecast errors of 100-200-300 <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical miles</a> at 24-48-72&#160;hours, respectively. These numbers were close to the 10-year average for the 1982\xe2\x80\x931991 time frame.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> However, these errors have decreased to near 50-100-150 as NHC forecasters become more accurate. The "danger area" to be avoided is constructed by expanding the forecast path by a radius equal to the respective hundreds of miles plus the forecast wind radii (size of the storm at those hours).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensity">Intensity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Intensity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>\n<p>Forecasters say they are less skillful at predicting the intensity of tropical cyclones than cyclone track.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_forecast_verifications_Atlantic_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_forecast_verifications_Atlantic-11">[11]</a></sup> They attribute the lack of improvement in intensity forecasting to the complexity of tropical systems and an incomplete understanding of factors that affect their development.</p>\n<p>An accurate track forecast is essential to creating accurate intensity forecasts, particularly in an area with large islands such as the western north Pacific and the Caribbean Sea, as proximity to land is an inhibiting factor to developing tropical cyclones. A strong hurricane/typhoon/cyclone can weaken if an outer eye wall forms (typically around 80\xe2\x80\x93160&#160;kilometers (50\xe2\x80\x93100&#160;miles) from the center of the storm), choking off the convection within the inner eye wall. cyclone rainfall forecasting is important, since between 1970\xe2\x80\x932004, inland flooding from tropical cyclones caused a majority of the fatalities from tropical cyclones in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Inland_Flooding_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inland_Flooding-16">[16]</a></sup> While <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a> is common to tropical cyclones near a landmass, there are a few factors which lead to excessive rainfall from tropical cyclones. Slow motion, as was seen during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Danny_(1997)" title="Hurricane Danny (1997)">Hurricane Danny</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a>, can lead to high amounts. The presence of topography near the coast, as is the case across much of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, much of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="R\xc3\xa9union">R\xc3\xa9union</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> acts to magnify amounts due to upslope flow into the mountains. Strong upper level forcing from a trough moving through the Westerlies, as was the case during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Floyd" title="Hurricane Floyd">Hurricane Floyd</a>, can lead to high amounts even from systems moving at an average forward motion. A combination of two of these factors could be especially crippling, as was seen during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Mitch" title="Hurricane Mitch">Hurricane Mitch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Are_You_Ready.3F_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Are_You_Ready.3F-17">[17]</a></sup> Therefore, an accurate track forecast is essential in order to produce an accurate tropical cyclone rainfall forecast.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_methods">Operational methods</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Operational methods">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/220px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/330px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ernesto2006modelspread.png/440px-Ernesto2006modelspread.png 2x" data-file-width="1282" data-file-height="914" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernesto2006modelspread.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nForecast model tracks within ATCF. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">NHC</a> official forecast for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ernesto_(2006)" title="Hurricane Ernesto (2006)">Ernesto (2006)</a> is light blue, while the storm\'s actual track is the white line over <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Automated_Tropical_Cyclone_Forecasting_System" title="Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System">Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_tracking_chart" title="Tropical cyclone tracking chart">Tropical cyclone tracking chart</a></div>\n<p>Historically, tropical cyclone tracking charts were used to include the past track and prepare future forecasts at Regional Specialized Meteorological Centers and Tropical Cyclone Warning Centers. The need for a more modernized method for forecasting tropical cyclones had become apparent to operational weather forecasters by the mid-1980s. At that time the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">United States Department of Defense</a> was using paper maps, acetate, grease pencils, and disparate computer programs to forecast tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF1-19">[19]</a></sup> The Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System (ATCF) software was developed by the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) beginning in 1986,<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF2_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF2-20">[20]</a></sup> and used since 1988. During 1990 the system was adapted by the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) for use at the NHC, <a href="/wiki/National_Centers_for_Environmental_Prediction" title="National Centers for Environmental Prediction">National Centers for Environmental Prediction</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF2_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF2-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHC_2_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_2-21">[21]</a></sup> This provided the NHC with a multitasking software environment which allowed them to improve efficiency and cut the time required to make a forecast by 25% or 1 hour.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_2_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_2-21">[21]</a></sup> ATCF was originally developed for use within <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>, before later being adapted to Unix and Linux.<sup id="cite_ref-ATCF2_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCF2-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Storm_surge">Storm surge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Storm surge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">Storm surge</a></div>\n<p>The main storm surge forecast model in the Atlantic basin is <b>SLOSH</b>, which stands for <b>S</b>ea, <b>L</b>ake, <b>O</b>verland, <b>S</b>urge from <b>H</b>urricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It uses the size of a storm, its intensity, its forward motion, and the topography of the coastal plain to estimate the depth of a storm surge at any individual grid point across the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. An accurate forecast track is required in order to produce accurate storm surge forecasts. However, if the landfall point is uncertain, a maximum envelope of water (MEOW) map can be generated based on the direction of approach. If the forecast track itself is also uncertain, a maximum of maximums (MoM) map can be generated which will show the worst possible scenario for a hurricane of a specific strength.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tornado">Tornado</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tornado">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The location of most tropical cyclone-related tornadoes is their northeast quadrant in the Northern Hemisphere and southeast quadrant in the Southern Hemisphere.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Like most of the other forecasts for tropical cyclone effects, an accurate track forecast is required in order to produce an accurate tornado threat forecast.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Seasonal_forecast">Seasonal forecast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_forecasting&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Seasonal forecast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation" title="Atlantic multidecadal oscillation">Atlantic multidecadal oscillation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone#Long-term_activity_trends" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone \xc2\xa7&#160;Long-term_activity_trends</a></div>\n<p>By looking at annual variations in various climate parameters, forecasters can make predictions about the overall number and intensity of tropical cyclones that will occur in a given season. For example, when constructing its seasonal outlooks, the <a href="/wiki/Climate_Prediction_Center" title="Climate Prediction Center">Climate Prediction Center</a> in the United States considers the effects of the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o-Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o-Southern Oscillation</a>, 25\xe2\x80\x9340 year tropical cycle, <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> over the oceans, and ocean surface temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
