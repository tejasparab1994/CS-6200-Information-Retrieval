a <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> condition that occurs when a tropical cyclone intensifies dramatically in a short period of time. The United States National Hurricane Center defines rapid intensification as an increase in the maximum sustained winds of a tropical cyclone of at least 30 knots (35&#160;mph; 55&#160;km/h) in a 24-hour period.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_glossary_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_glossary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="External">External</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rapid_intensification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: External">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In order for rapid deepening to occur, several conditions must be in place. <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">Water temperatures</a> must be extremely warm (near or above 30&#160;\xc2\xb0C, 86&#160;\xc2\xb0F), and water of this temperature must be sufficiently deep such that waves do not churn deeper cooler waters up to the surface. <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">Wind shear</a> must be low; when wind shear is high, the <a href="/wiki/Convection#Atmospheric_convection" title="Convection">convection</a> and circulation in the cyclone will be disrupted.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal">Internal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rapid_intensification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Usually, an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> in the upper layers of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> above the storm must be present as well\xe2\x80\x94for extremely low surface pressures to develop, air must be rising very rapidly in the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall</a> of the storm, and an upper-level anticyclone helps channel this air away from the cyclone efficiently.<sup id="cite_ref-Energetics_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Energetics-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag needs further explanation. (September 2017)">further explanation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Hot_towers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot towers">Hot towers</a> have been implicated in tropical cyclone rapid intensification, though diagnostically has seen varied skill across basins.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_nomenclature_and_definitions">Previous nomenclature and definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rapid_intensification&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Previous nomenclature and definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The United States National Hurricane Center previously defined <i><b>rapid deepening</b></i> of a tropical cyclone, when the minimum central pressure decreased by 42 millibars (1.240&#160;inHg) over a 24-hour period.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC.2FTPC_glossary_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC.2FTPC_glossary-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2017)">when?</span></a></i>]</sup> However it is now defined as an increase in the maximum sustained winds of a tropical cyclone of at least 30 knots (35&#160;mph; 55&#160;km/h) in a 24-hour period.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_glossary_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_glossary-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_instances">Notable It weakened significantly over the island chain, but it soon hit open water again and rapidly intensified back into a category 5 hurricane just hours before making a second landfall at category 5 strength in Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On September 10, 1997, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Linda_(1997)" title="Hurricane Linda (1997)">Hurricane Linda</a> was just a tropical storm. The next day, Linda became a minimal hurricane. By 6AM on September 12, however, Linda\'s wind speeds increased to a record 185&#160;mph, the pressure dropping at an average rate of 3.38 millibars per hour.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charley" title="Hurricane Charley">Hurricane Charley</a> was approaching the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> as a category two storm on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a> of hurricane strength. When just off the coast, however, its sustained winds rapidly increased from 110 to 150&#160;mph (along with a pressure drop from 965 to 941&#160;mbar) in only three hours. Charley caused unprecedented destruction in the <a href="/wiki/Punta_Gorda,_Florida" title="Punta Gorda, Florida">Punta Gorda</a> area, and inflicted major damage across the state of Florida.<sup id="cite_ref-CharleyTCR_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CharleyTCR-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg/150px-HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg" width="150" height="193" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg/225px-HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg/300px-HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5600" data-file-height="7200" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HurricaneWilma20Oct2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane Wilma at record intensity</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>During 2005, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Hurricane Wilma</a> sustained winds rapidly intensified, from 60 knots (70&#160;mph; 110&#160;km/h) to 150 knots (175&#160;mph; 280&#160;km/h) within 24 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilma_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilma-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result, Wilma intensified from being a tropical storm to a category 5 hurricane on the Saffir-Simpson hurricane scale.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilma_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilma-10">[10]</a></sup> Over the same time span the pressure dropped from 982 millibars (29.00&#160;inHg) to 892 millibars (26.34&#160;inHg).<sup id="cite_ref-Wilma_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilma-10">[10]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rita" title="Hurricane Rita">Hurricane Rita</a> both rapidly intensified, Katrina from a Category 2 to a 5, and Rita from a 2 to a 5. Katrina rapidly weakened as a result, dropping one category every 6 hours. Rita kept Cat. 5 intensity for 24 hours, before slowly weakening to a Category 3 and dropping to a Cat. 1 just after landfall.</p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/2006_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2006 Pacific typhoon season">2006</a>, the minimum central pressure of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Chebi_(2006)" title="Typhoon Chebi (2006)">Typhoon Chebi</a> in the Western Pacific dropped 75 mbar in 24 hours, including a 60 mbar pressure drop in 6 hours, as it intensified from a tropical storm to a category four equivalent typhoon in one advisory.<sup id="cite_ref-nii_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nii-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/2007_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2007 Atlantic hurricane season">2007</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Felix" title="Hurricane Felix">Hurricane Felix</a> rapidly intensified from a tropical depression to a Category 5 hurricane in 51 hours with a wind speed rise of 85knots/98&#160;mph in just 24 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>2008\'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ike" title="Hurricane Ike">Hurricane Ike</a> with the hurricane undergoing a 24 millibar pressure drop in 3 hours.</p>\n<p>In 2010, there were multiple occurrences. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Igor" title="Hurricane Igor">Igor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Julia" title="Hurricane Julia">Julia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Earl_(2010)" title="Hurricane Earl (2010)">Earl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Danielle_(2010)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Danielle (2010)">Danielle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karl_(2010)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Karl (2010)">Karl</a> are prime examples. Each intensified from a minimal hurricane into a Category 4 (Category 3 for Karl).</p>\n<p>In 2013, Typhoon <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Usagi_(2013)" title="Typhoon Usagi (2013)">Usagi</a> underwent a 55 mb drop as it had intensified from a category 1 to a category 4 super typhoon in just 24 hours. Typhoon <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Lekima_(2013)" title="Typhoon Lekima (2013)">Lekima</a> rapidly intensified from a category 1 to category 5 in 24 hours, and most notably <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a> deepened from 35 mb within 12 hours, and, 965 mb to 895 mb within a day.</p>\n<p>In 2014, Cyclones <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gillian_(2014)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclone Gillian (2014)">Gillian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Hellen" title="Cyclone Hellen">Hellen</a> underwent rapid intensification, going from tropical storms to Category 5 cyclones in less than 48 hrs barely a week apart.</p>\n<p>A few instances have been recorded in the <a href="/wiki/North_Indian_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="North Indian Ocean">North Indian Ocean</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/1999_Odisha_Cyclone" class="mw-redirect" title="1999 Odisha Cyclone">1999 Odisha Cyclone</a>, the strongest storm on record in the basin, undergoing a 28 millibar pressure drop in 6 hours and <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Giri" title="Cyclone Giri">Cyclone Giri</a> undergoing 24 millibar drop in 6 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In May 2014, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Amanda" title="Hurricane Amanda">Hurricane Amanda</a> rapidly intensified from a tropical storm to a Category&#160;4 in 24&#160;hours. Over the span of 30&#160;hours, its central pressure decreased by 62&#160;mbar (hPa; 1.83&#160;inHg) while its winds increased by 95&#160;mph (153&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In August 2014, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Genevieve_(2014)" title="Hurricane Genevieve (2014)">Hurricane Genevieve</a> underwent explosive intensification while crossing the international date line from a tropical storm to a Category 5 typhoon in 30 hours with a wind speed rise of 90 knots. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Marie_(2014)" title="Hurricane Marie (2014)">Hurricane Marie</a> rapidly deepened from Category 1 at 2:00 PM PDT on August 23 to Category 5 with sustained winds of 160&#160;mph (gusting to 195&#160;mph) at 2:00 PM PDT on August 24, the pressure dropping from 988mbar to 918mbar (an average of 2.916 millibars per hour) during that same period.</p>\n<p>In September 2014, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Odile_(2014)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Odile (2014)">Hurricane Odile</a> rapidly intensified from a Category 1 into a Category 4, with pressure dropping to 918 mbar. <a href="/wiki/2014_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Simon" title="2014 Pacific hurricane season">Hurricane Simon</a> intensified from a Tropical Storm into a Category 4 hurricane</p>\n<p>In October 2014, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Vongfong_(2014)" title="Typhoon Vongfong (2014)">Typhoon Vongfong</a> deepened 30 mbar in 6 hours and 65 mbar in just a day.</p>\n<p>In November 2014, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Nuri_(2014)" title="Typhoon Nuri (2014)">Typhoon Nuri</a> explosively intensified from a tropical storm to a category 5 super typhoon in 18 hours. Its pressure had dropped from 975 mbar to 910 mbar.</p>\n<p>In December 2014, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Hagupit_(2014)" title="Typhoon Hagupit (2014)">Typhoon Hagupit</a> explosively intensified from a category 3 typhoon to a category 5 super typhoon for 12 hours, with 10-minute maximum sustained winds at 215&#160;km/h (130&#160;mph) and the central pressure at 905 <a href="/wiki/HPa" class="mw-redirect" title="HPa">hPa</a> (26.72 <a href="/wiki/InHg" class="mw-redirect" title="InHg">inHg</a>).</p>\n<p>In February 2015, Tropical <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Marcia" title="Cyclone Marcia">Cyclone Marcia</a> developed in the Coral Sea of the Queensland coast in Australia. It was named a category 1 (Australian scale) cyclone by the Bureau of Meteorology on 18 February, and underwent a period of very rapid intensification on 19 February, reaching category 4 status. The following morning, it was upgraded to category 5 status, before crossing the coast north of Yeppoon, Queensland. Meanwhile, in the northwest Pacific, <a href="/wiki/2015_Pacific_typhoon_season#Typhoon_Higos" title="2015 Pacific typhoon season">Typhoon Higos</a> rapidly deepened 45 <a href="/wiki/Millibars" class="mw-redirect" title="Millibars">millibars</a> in 30 hours.</p>\n<p>In late March 2015, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Maysak_(2015)" title="Typhoon Maysak (2015)">Typhoon Maysak</a> rapidly intensified from a Category 2 typhoon to a Category 5 super typhoon in a day and became the strongest storm before April within the basin. In early May, <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Noul_(2015)" title="Typhoon Noul (2015)">Typhoon Noul</a> also rapidly intensified from a Category 2 typhoon to a Category 5 super typhoon within a similar timeframe with a 45 millibar drop. <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Dolphin_(2015)" title="Typhoon Dolphin (2015)">Typhoon Dolphin</a> rapidly intensified from a Category 2 to a Category 5 within 24 hours.</p>\n<p>In October 2015, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Patricia" title="Hurricane Patricia">Hurricane Patricia</a> underwent explosive intensification. The storm reached hurricane status just before 00:00 on October 22, and reached Category 4 status 18 hours later. Patricia further intensified to reach Category 5 status the next day, and peaked with winds of 215&#160;mph by 12:00 UTC on October 23. Patricia\'s winds increased a record 120&#160;mph. Despite this, in the five hours until landfall, an eyewall replacement cycle resulted in traumatic weakening, winds decreased 65&#160;mph before landfall, however, with winds of 150&#160;mph, Patricia still holds the record for the strongest Mexican landfall on the Pacific side.</p>\n<p>In September 2017, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Maria" title="Hurricane Maria">Hurricane Maria</a> explosively intensified from a category 1 with a pressure of 982 mbar (hPa; 29.00 inHg) to a category 5 with a pressure of 925&#160;mbar (hPa; 28.23 inHg) in under a 24-hour period.</p>\n<h2><span
