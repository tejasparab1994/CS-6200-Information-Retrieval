example the degree to which it is hot or cold, wet or dry, calm or stormy, clear or cloudy.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Most weather phenomena occur in the lowest level of the atmosphere, the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-trop_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trop-3">[3]</a></sup> just below the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Weather refers to day-to-day temperature and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> activity, whereas <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">climate</a> is the term for the averaging of atmospheric conditions over longer periods of time.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When used without qualification, "weather" is generally understood to mean the weather of <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>.</p>\n<p>Weather is driven by air pressure, temperature and moisture differences between one place and another. These differences can occur due to the sun\'s angle at any particular spot, which varies with <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>. The strong temperature contrast between polar and tropical air gives rise to the largest scale <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulations</a>: the Hadley Cell, the Ferrel Cell, the Polar Cell, and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>. Weather systems in the <a href="/wiki/Mid-latitudes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-latitudes">mid-latitudes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, are caused by instabilities of the jet stream flow. Because the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">axis</a> is tilted relative to its orbital plane, <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> is incident at different angles at different times of the year. On Earth\'s surface, temperatures usually range \xc2\xb140&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9240&#160;\xc2\xb0F to 100&#160;\xc2\xb0F) annually. Over thousands of years, changes in Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">orbit</a> can affect the amount and distribution of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> received by the Earth, thus influencing long-term climate and global <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>.</p>\n<p>Surface temperature differences in turn cause pressure differences. Higher altitudes are cooler than lower altitudes as most atmospheric heating is due to contact with the Earth\'s surface while radiative losses to space are mostly constant. Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the state of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> for a future time and a given location. The Earth\'s weather system is a <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic system</a>; as a result, small changes to one part of the system can grow to have large effects on the system as a whole. Human attempts to <a href="/wiki/Weather_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Weather control">control the weather</a> have occurred throughout history, and there is evidence that human activities such as agriculture and industry have modified weather patterns.</p>\n<p>Studying how the weather works on other planets has been helpful in understanding how weather works on Earth. A famous landmark in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a></i>, is an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> storm known to have existed for at least 300 years. However, weather is not limited to planetary bodies. A star\'s <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> is constantly being lost to space, creating what is essentially a very thin atmosphere throughout the Solar System. The movement of mass ejected from the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> is known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" id="Causes">Causes</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stormclouds.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Stormclouds.jpg/250px-Stormclouds.jpg" width="250" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Stormclouds.jpg/375px-Stormclouds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Stormclouds.jpg/500px-Stormclouds.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="2000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stormclouds.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">Cumulus mediocris</a> cloud surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratocumulus">stratocumulus</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, the common <a href="/wiki/List_of_meteorological_phenomena" title="List of meteorological phenomena">weather phenomena</a> include wind, <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a>, rain, snow, <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dust_storm" title="Dust storm">dust storms</a>. Less common events include <a href="/wiki/Natural_disaster" title="Natural disaster">natural disasters</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typhoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoons">typhoons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winter_storm" title="Winter storm">ice storms</a>. Almost all familiar weather phenomena occur in the troposphere (the lower part of the atmosphere).<sup id="cite_ref-trop_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-trop-3">[3]</a></sup> Weather does occur in the stratosphere and can affect weather lower down in the troposphere, but the exact mechanisms are poorly understood.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Weather occurs primarily due to air pressure, temperature and <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a> differences between one place to another. These differences can occur due to the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a> angle at any particular spot, which varies by latitude from the tropics. In other words, the farther from the tropics one lies, the lower the sun angle is, which causes those locations to be cooler due the spread of the <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> over a greater surface.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The strong temperature contrast between <a href="/wiki/Polar_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar region">polar</a> and tropical air gives rise to the large scale <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">atmospheric circulation</a> cells and the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Stimac_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stimac-7">[7]</a></sup> Weather <a href="/wiki/Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Systems">systems</a> in the mid-latitudes, such as <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>, are caused by instabilities of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> flow (see <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinity</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> Weather systems in the tropics, such as <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a> or organized <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> systems, are caused by different processes.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png/300px-16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png" width="300" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png/450px-16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png/600px-16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1080" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:16-008-NASA-2015RecordWarmGlobalYearSince1880-20160120.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<center>2015 \xe2\x80\x93 <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">Warmest Global Year</a> on Record (since 1880) \xe2\x80\x93 Colors indicate temperature anomalies (<a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>/<a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>; 20 January 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-20160120_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-20160120-9">[9]</a></sup></center>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Because the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth\'s rotation">axis</a> is tilted relative to its orbital plane, <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a> is incident at different angles at different times of the year. In June the Northern Hemisphere is tilted towards the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">sun</a>, so at any given Northern Hemisphere latitude sunlight falls more directly on that spot than in December (see <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">Effect of sun angle on climate</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> This effect causes seasons. Over thousands to hundreds of thousands of years, changes in Earth\'s orbital parameters affect the amount and distribution of <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> received by the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> and influence long-term climate. (See <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">Milankovitch cycles</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The uneven solar heating (the formation of zones of temperature and moisture gradients, or <a href="/wiki/Frontogenesis" title="Frontogenesis">frontogenesis</a>) can also be due to the weather itself in the form of cloudiness and precipitation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Higher altitudes are typically cooler than lower altitudes, which the result of higher surface temperature and radiational heating, which produces the <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic</a> <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">lapse rate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> In some situations, the temperature actually increases with height. This phenomenon is known as an <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> and can cause mountaintops to be warmer than the valleys below. Inversions can lead to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a> and often act as a <a href="/wiki/Capping_inversion" title="Capping inversion">cap</a> that <a href="/wiki/Convective_inhibition" title="Convective inhibition">suppresses</a> thunderstorm development. On local scales, temperature differences can occur because different surfaces (such as oceans, forests, <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">ice</a> sheets, or man-made objects) have differing physical characteristics such as <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">reflectivity</a>, roughness, or moisture content.</p>\n<p>Surface temperature differences in turn cause pressure differences. A hot surface warms the air above it causing it to expand and lower the density and the resulting surface <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">air pressure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The resulting horizontal <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient_(atmospheric)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pressure gradient (atmospheric)">pressure gradient</a> moves the air from higher to lower pressure regions, creating a wind, and the Earth\'s rotation then causes deflection of this air flow due to the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The simple systems thus formed can then display <a href="/wiki/Emergence" title="Emergence">emergent behaviour</a> to produce more <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">complex systems</a> and thus other weather phenomena. Large scale examples include the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a> while a smaller scale example would be <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">coastal breezes</a>.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic system</a>. As a result, small changes to one part of the system can accumulate and magnify to cause large effects on the system as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> This atmospheric instability makes weather forecasting less predictable than tides or eclipses.<sup id="cite_ref-Lorenz.2C_1969_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lorenz.2C_1969-18">[18]</a></sup> Although it is difficult to accurately predict weather more than a few days in advance, <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">weather forecasters</a> are continually working to extend this limit through <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological</a> research and refining current methodologies in weather prediction. However, it is theoretically impossible to make useful day-to-day <a href="/wiki/Predictions" class="mw-redirect" title="Predictions">predictions</a> more than about two weeks ahead, imposing an upper limit to <a href="/wiki/Potential" title="Potential">potential</a> for improved prediction skill.<sup id="cite_ref-Chaos_in_the_Atmosphere_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chaos_in_the_Atmosphere-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shaping_the_planet_Earth">Shaping the planet Earth</span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">Weathering</a></div>\n<p>Weather is one of the fundamental processes that shape the Earth. The process of weathering breaks down the rocks and soils into smaller fragments and then into their constituent substances.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> During rains precipitation, the water droplets absorb and dissolve carbon dioxide from the surrounding air. This causes the rainwater to be slightly acidic, which aids the erosive properties of water. The released sediment and chemicals are then free to take part in <a href="/wiki/Chemical" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical">chemical</a> reactions that can affect the surface further (such as <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>), and sodium and chloride ions <a href="/wiki/Sea_salt" title="Sea salt">(salt)</a> deposited in the seas/oceans. The sediment may reform in time and by geological forces into other rocks and soils. In this way, weather plays a major role in <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> of the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_wind_and_pressure_systems_and_related_weather">Major wind and pressure systems and related weather</span></h2>\n<table class="wikitable">\n<tr>\n<th>Region</th>\n<th>Name</th>\n<th>Pressure</th>\n<th>Surface Winds</th>\n<th>Weather</th>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Equator (0\xc2\xb0)</td>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Doldrums" title="Doldrums">Doldrums</a> (ITCZ) (equatorial low)</td>\n<td>Low</td>\n<td>Light, variable winds</td>\n<td>Cloudiness, abundant precipitation in all seasons; breeding ground for hurricanes. There are socially and scientifically constructed understandings of what weather is, what makes it change, the effect it has on humans in different situations, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Therefore, weather is something people often communicate about.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_populations">Effects on populations</span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg/220px-Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg/330px-Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg/440px-Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2878" data-file-height="1850" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Navy-FloodedNewOrleans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nNew Orleans, Louisiana, after being struck by Hurricane Katrina. Aside from <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climatic changes</a> that have caused the gradual drift of populations (for example the <a href="/wiki/Desertification" title="Desertification">desertification</a> of the Middle East, and the formation of <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge" title="Land bridge">land bridges</a> during <a href="/wiki/Glacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Glacial">glacial</a> periods), <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">extreme weather</a> events have caused smaller scale population movements and intruded directly in historical events. One such event is the saving of Japan from invasion by the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongol</a> fleet of <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">Kublai Khan</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Kamikaze_(typhoon)" title="Kamikaze (typhoon)">Kamikaze</a> winds in 1281.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> French claims to Florida came to an end in 1565 when a hurricane destroyed the French fleet, allowing Spain to conquer <a href="/wiki/Fort_Caroline" title="Fort Caroline">Fort Caroline</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> More recently, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> redistributed over one million people from the central <a href="/wiki/Gulf_coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf coast">Gulf coast</a> elsewhere across the United States, becoming the largest <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> in the history of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a> caused crop failures and <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">famines</a> in Europe. The 1690s saw the worst famine in France since the Middle Ages. Finland suffered a severe famine in 1696\xe2\x80\x931697, during which about one-third of the Finnish population died.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a and north Atlantic Ocean as on 9 June 2008</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Weather forecasting is the application of science and technology to predict the state of the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> for a future time and a given location. Human beings have attempted to predict the weather informally for millennia, and formally since at least the nineteenth century.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Weather forecasts are made by collecting <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_data" class="mw-redirect" title="Quantitative data">quantitative data</a> about the current state of the atmosphere and using <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">scientific understanding of atmospheric processes</a> to project how the atmosphere will evolve.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Once an all-human endeavor based mainly upon changes in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a>, current weather conditions, and sky condition,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">forecast models</a> are now used to determine future conditions. On the other hand, human input is still required to pick the best possible <a href="/wiki/Forecast_model" class="mw-disambig" title="Forecast model">forecast model</a> to base the forecast upon, which involve many disciplines such as pattern recognition skills, <a href="/wiki/Teleconnection" title="Teleconnection">teleconnections</a>, knowledge of model performance, and knowledge of model biases.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chaos_theory" title="Chaos theory">chaotic</a> nature of the atmosphere, the massive computational power required to solve the equations that describe the atmosphere, error involved in measuring the initial conditions, and an incomplete understanding of atmospheric processes mean that forecasts become less accurate as the difference in current time and the time for which the forecast is being made (the <i>range</i> of the forecast) increases. The use of ensembles and model consensus helps to narrow the error and pick the most likely outcome.<sup id="cite_ref-Klaus_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klaus-31">[31]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TBK_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TBK-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>There are a variety of end users to weather forecasts. Weather warnings are important forecasts because they are used to protect life and property.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Forecasts based on temperature and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> are important to agriculture,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> and therefore to commodity traders within stock markets. Temperature forecasts are used by utility companies to estimate demand over coming days.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In some areas, people use weather forecasts to determine what to wear on a given day. Since outdoor activities are severely curtailed by heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Wind_chill" title="Wind chill">wind chill</a>, forecasts can be used to plan activities around these events, and to plan ahead to survive through them.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modification">Modification</span></h2>\n<p>The aspiration to <a href="/wiki/Weather_modification" title="Weather modification">control the weather</a> is evident throughout human history: from ancient rituals intended to bring rain for crops to the U.S. Military <a href="/wiki/Operation_Popeye" title="Operation Popeye">Operation Popeye</a>, an attempt to disrupt <a href="/wiki/Military_supply_chain_management" title="Military supply chain management">supply lines</a> by lengthening the North Vietnamese <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a>. The most successful attempts at influencing weather involve <a href="/wiki/Cloud_seeding" title="Cloud seeding">cloud seeding</a>; they include the <a href="/wiki/Fog" title="Fog">fog</a>- and low <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratus</a> dispersion techniques employed by major airports, techniques used to increase <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">winter precipitation</a> over mountains, and techniques to suppress <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AMS_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-43">[43]</a></sup> A recent example of weather control was China\'s preparation for the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympic Games</a>. China shot 1,104 rain dispersal rockets from 21 sites in the city of <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> in an effort to keep rain away from the opening ceremony of the games on 8 August 2008. Guo Hu, head of the Beijing Municipal Meteorological Bureau (BMB), confirmed the success of the operation with 100 millimeters falling in <a href="/wiki/Baoding" title="Baoding">Baoding</a> City of <a href="/wiki/Hebei_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebei Province">Hebei Province</a>, to the southwest and Beijing\'s <a href="/wiki/Fangshan_District" title="Fangshan District">Fangshan District</a> recording a rainfall of 25 millimeters.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Whereas there is inconclusive evidence for these techniques\' efficacy, there is extensive evidence that human activity such as agriculture and industry results in inadvertent weather modification:<sup id="cite_ref-AMS_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMS-43">[43]</a></sup></p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">Acid rain</a>, caused by industrial <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">emission</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, adversely affects <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">freshwater lakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">vegetation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Building" title="Building">structures</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">Anthropogenic</a> pollutants reduce <a href="/wiki/Air_quality_index" title="Air quality index">air quality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Visibility" title="Visibility">visibility</a>.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">Climate change</a> caused by human activities that emit <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a> into the air is expected to affect the frequency of <a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">extreme weather</a> events such as drought, extreme temperatures, <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flooding</a>, high winds, and <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">severe storms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a>, generated by large metropolitan areas have been shown to minutely affect nearby weather, even at distances as far as 1,600 kilometres (990&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<p>The effects of inadvertent weather modification may pose serious threats to many aspects of civilization, including <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystems</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>, food and fiber production, <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a>, and human health.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Microscale_meteorology">Microscale meteorology</span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">Microscale meteorology</a> is the study of short-lived <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmospheric</a> phenomena smaller than <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a>, about 1&#160;km or less. These two branches of <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> are sometimes grouped together as "mesoscale and microscale meteorology" (MMM) and together study all phenomena smaller than <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a>; that is they study features generally too small to be depicted on a <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">weather map</a>. These include small and generally fleeting cloud "puffs" and other small cloud features.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremes_on_Earth">Extremes on Earth</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bratislava_sun.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bratislava_sun.jpg/220px-Bratislava_sun.jpg" width="220" height="59" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bratislava_sun.jpg/330px-Bratislava_sun.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Bratislava_sun.jpg/440px-Bratislava_sun.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="1340" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bratislava_sun.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nEarly morning sunshine over <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a>, Slovakia. February 2008.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bratislava_snowy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bratislava_snowy.jpg/220px-Bratislava_snowy.jpg" width="220" height="57" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bratislava_snowy.jpg/330px-Bratislava_snowy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bratislava_snowy.jpg/440px-Bratislava_snowy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="1292" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bratislava_snowy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe same area, just three hours later, after light snowfall</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Extremes_on_Earth" title="Extremes on Earth">Extremes on Earth</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_weather_records" title="List of weather records">List of weather records</a></div>\n<p>On Earth, temperatures usually range \xc2\xb140&#160;\xc2\xb0C (100&#160;\xc2\xb0F to \xe2\x88\x9240&#160;\xc2\xb0F) annually. The range of climates and latitudes across the planet can offer extremes of temperature outside this range. The coldest air temperature ever recorded on Earth is \xe2\x88\x9289.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x92128.6&#160;\xc2\xb0F), at <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a> on 21 July 1983. The hottest air temperature ever recorded was 57.7&#160;\xc2\xb0C (135.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F) at <a href="/wiki/%27Aziziya" class="mw-redirect" title="\'Aziziya">\'Aziziya</a>, Libya, on 13 September 1922,<sup id="cite_ref-NCDC_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCDC-49">[49]</a></sup> but <a href="/wiki/%27Aziziya#Geography_and_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="\'Aziziya">that reading is queried</a>. The highest recorded average annual temperature was 34.4&#160;\xc2\xb0C (93.9&#160;\xc2\xb0F) at <a href="/wiki/Dallol,_Ethiopia" title="Dallol, Ethiopia">Dallol</a>, Ethiopia.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The coldest recorded average annual temperature was \xe2\x88\x9255.1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9267.2&#160;\xc2\xb0F) at <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">Vostok Station</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The coldest average annual temperature in a permanently inhabited location is at <a href="/wiki/Eureka,_Nunavut" title="Eureka, Nunavut">Eureka, Nunavut</a>, in Canada, where the annual average temperature is \xe2\x88\x9219.7&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x923.5&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_within_the_Solar_System">Extraterrestrial within the Solar System</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/220px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg" width="220" height="208" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/330px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg/440px-Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="895" data-file-height="848" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Great_Red_Spot_From_Voyager_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nJupiter\'s Great Red Spot in February 1979, photographed by the unmanned <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">Voyager 1</a> NASA space probe.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Studying how the weather works on other planets has been seen as helpful in understanding how it works on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> Weather on other planets follows many of the same physical principles as weather on Earth, but occurs on different scales and in atmospheres having different chemical composition. The <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini\xe2\x80\x93Huygens">Cassini\xe2\x80\x93Huygens</a> mission to <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a> discovered clouds formed from methane or ethane which deposit rain composed of liquid <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">organic compounds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> Earth\'s atmosphere includes six latitudinal circulation zones, three in each hemisphere.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup> In contrast, Jupiter\'s banded appearance shows many such zones,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Titan has a single jet stream near the 50th parallel north latitude,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a> has a single jet near the equator.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>\n<p>One of the most famous landmarks in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Great_Red_Spot" title="Great Red Spot">Great Red Spot</a></i>, is an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> storm known to have existed for at least 300 years.<sup id="cite_ref-HaydPlan_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HaydPlan-59">[59]</a></sup> On other <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">gas giants</a>, the lack of a surface allows the wind to reach enormous speeds: gusts of up to 600&#160;metres per second (about 2,100&#160;km/h or 1,300&#160;mph) have been measured on the planet <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Suomi1991_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suomi1991-60">[60]</a></sup> This has created a puzzle for <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">planetary scientists</a>. The weather is ultimately created by solar energy and the amount of energy received by Neptune is only about <span class="nowrap">\xe2\x80\x8a</span><span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>\xe2\x81\x84<sub>900</sub></span> of that received by Earth, yet the intensity of weather phenomena on Neptune is far greater than on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> The strongest planetary winds discovered so far are on the <a href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" class="mw-redirect" title="Extrasolar planet">extrasolar planet</a> <a href="/wiki/HD_189733_b" title="HD 189733 b">HD 189733 b</a>, which is thought to have easterly winds moving at more than 9,600 kilometres per hour (6,000&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-Knutson_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knutson-62">[62]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_weather">Space weather</span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Polarlicht.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Polarlicht.jpg/220px-Polarlicht.jpg" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Polarlicht.jpg/330px-Polarlicht.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Polarlicht.jpg/440px-Polarlicht.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3008" data-file-height="1960" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Polarlicht.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aurora (astronomy)">Aurora Borealis</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">Space weather</a></div>\n<p>Weather is not limited to planetary bodies. Like all stars, the sun\'s <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">corona</a> is constantly being lost to space, creating what is essentially a very thin <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> throughout the <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a>. The movement of mass ejected from the Sun is known as the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a>. Inconsistencies in this wind and larger events on the surface of the star, such as <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">coronal mass ejections</a>, form a system that has features analogous to conventional weather systems (such as pressure and wind) and is generally known as <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">space weather</a>. Coronal mass ejections have been tracked as far out in the <a href="/wiki/Solar_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar system">solar system</a> as <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> The activity of this system can affect planetary <a href="/wiki/Atmospheres" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheres">atmospheres</a> and occasionally surfaces. The interaction of the <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">solar wind</a> with the terrestrial atmosphere can produce spectacular <a href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aurora (astronomy)">aurorae</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and can play havoc with electrically sensitive systems such as <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electricity grids</a> and radio signals.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">Weather station</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Outline_of_meteorology" title="Outline of meteorology">Outline of meteorology</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
