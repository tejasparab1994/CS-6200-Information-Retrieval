cyclones and climate change</b> concerns how <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> have changed (in number, intensity, track or otherwise), and are expected to further change, under <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>. The topic receives considerable attention from <a href="/wiki/Climate_scientists" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate scientists">climate scientists</a> who study the connections between storms and climate, and notably since 2005 makes news during active storm seasons.</p>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricane</a> is a tropical cyclone that occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoon</a> occurs in the northwestern Pacific Ocean, and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> occurs in the south Pacific or <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HCT_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div increases in rainfall rates within 100 km of the storm centre of up to 20%.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A tropical cyclone\'s rainfall is primarily controlled by its actual environmental <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a> (SST) - relative to the tropical mean SST, called the relative sea surface temperature. The relative SST has also been identified to more precise explain variations in Atlantic hurricane frequency, than absolute SST and tropical <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a>. The relative SST is defined as the SST in the TC environment minus the tropical (30\xc2\xb0S-30\xc2\xb0N) mean SST. Rainfall will expand outwards as the relative SST increases, associated with an expansion of a storm wind field. The largest tropical cyclones are observed in the western <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="North Pacific">North Pacific</a> tropics, where the largest values of relative SST and mid-tropospheric <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">relative humidity</a> are located. Flooding by TCs with global warming is projected primarily by increases in rainfall rate, rather than area extent.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Based on satellite imagery, the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> is the primary technique used to estimate globally the tropical cyclone intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Today measurement for a named system is also done with the ACE index (<a href="/wiki/Accumulated_Cyclone_Energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Accumulated Cyclone Energy">Accumulated Cyclone Energy</a>), a wind energy index - the sum of the squares of the estimated six-hour maximum sustained wind speed in knots, to assess the storm duration and intensity. The Potential Intensity (PI) of tropical cyclones can be computed from observed data, primarily derived from vertical profiles of temperature, humidity and sea surface temperatures (SSTs). The convective available potential energy (CAPE), was computed from radiosonde stations in parts of the tropics from 1958 to 1997, but is considered to be of poor quality. The Power Dissipation Index (PDI) represents the total power dissipation for the North Atlantic and western North Pacific, and is strongly correlated with tropical SSTs.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Various <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_scales" title="Tropical cyclone scales">tropical cyclone scales</a> exist to classify a system.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_record">Historical record</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical record">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Since the satellite era, which began around 1970, trends are considered to be robust enough in regards to the connection of storms and sea surface temperatures. Agreement exists that there were active storm periods in the more distant past, but the sea surface temperature related Power Dissipation Index was not as high.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paleotempestology" title="Paleotempestology">Paleotempestology</a> is the science of past&#160;tropical cyclone&#160;activity by means of geological proxies (flood sediment), or historical documentary records, such as shipwrecks or tree ring anomalies.</p>\n<h3><span id="El_Ni\xc3\xb1o"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="El_Ni.C3.B1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: El Ni\xc3\xb1o">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o">El Ni\xc3\xb1o</a> (ENSO) shifts the region (warmer water, up and down welling at different locations, due to winds) in the Pacific and Atlantic where more storms form, resulting in nearly constant ACE values in any one basin. The El Ni\xc3\xb1o event typically decreases hurricane formation in the Atlantic, and far western Pacific and Australian regions, but instead increases the odds in the central North and South Pacific and particular in the western North Pacific typhoon region.<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_perception">Public perception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Public perception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The tremendous destruction caused by recent <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>, such as Hurricanes Katrina, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Wilma" title="Hurricane Wilma">Wilma</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Sandy</a> caused a substantial upsurge in interest in the subject of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">climate change</a> and hurricanes by news media and the wider public, and concerns that global <a href="/wiki/Climatic_change" class="mw-redirect" title="Climatic change">climatic change</a> <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">may have played a significant role</a> in those events. Global analyses show that the amount of water vapor in the atmosphere has in fact increased due to human-caused warming. This extra moisture is available to storm systems, resulting in heavier rainfalls.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A review by Walsh et al 2015 found good understanding of the links between climate and tropical cyclones on various timescales, with significant observed trends in the Atlantic for frequencies and intensities in the past few decades. However, these trends are still heavily studied. The authors note that the theory for maximum tropical cyclone intensity is considered well established, but with cyclone genesis still elusive. Most climate models predict future decreases in global tropical cyclones, project more intense of the strongest storms, with increased rainfall rates. Additional <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> will likely increase storm surge threats.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A 2017 study looked at compounding effects from floods, storm surge, and terrestrial flooding (rivers), and projects an increase <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming" title="Effects of global warming">due to global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Studies on increased rainfall rates, are largely based on the <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius\xe2\x80\x93Clapeyron relation">Clausius\xe2\x80\x93Clapeyron relation</a>, which yields \xe2\x89\x887% increase in water vapor in the atmosphere per 1\xc2\xb0C warming, and the duration of storms.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> Storm tracks indicate a global poleward expansion of the latitude at which the maximum intensity of tropical cyclones occur.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Some model based research predicts a significant increase (+90%) in the frequency of very intense (category 4 and 5) hurricanes, with average climate change projections.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Climate_Assessment" title="National Climate Assessment">National Climate Assessment</a> from 2014 states:</p>\n<blockquote class="templatequote">\n<p>The recent increases in activity are linked, in part, to higher sea surface temperatures in the region that Atlantic hurricanes form in and move through. The relationship between climate change and the frequency of hurricanes (or tropical cyclones) is still unclear, and is the subject of continued research.<sup id="cite_ref-Miles_Aug_29_2017_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miles_Aug_29_2017-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NOAA1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA1-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>P.J. Webster and others published in 2005 an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">Science</a></i> examining the "changes in tropical cyclone number, duration, and intensity" over the past 35&#160;years, the period when satellite data has been available. Their main finding was although the number of cyclones decreased throughout the planet excluding the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, there was a great increase in the number and proportion of very strong cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster_et_al._2005_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster_et_al._2005-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to 2006 studies by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>, "the strongest hurricanes in the present climate may be upstaged by even more intense hurricanes over the next century as the Earth\'s climate is warmed by increasing levels of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere".<sup id="cite_ref-GFDL_warming_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GFDL_warming-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The 2007 <a href="/wiki/IPCC" class="mw-redirect" title="IPCC">IPCC</a> report noted many observed changes in the climate, including atmospheric composition, global average temperatures, ocean conditions, and others. The report concluded the observed increase in tropical cyclone intensity is larger than climate models predict. In addition, the report considered that it is likely that storm intensity will continue to increase through the 21st century, and declared it more likely than not that there has been some human contribution to the increases in tropical cyclone intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-ipcc_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ipcc-34">[34]</a></sup> The report also noted, "While attention has often been focussed simply on the frequency or number of storms, the intensity, size and duration likely matter more."<sup id="cite_ref-IPCC2007_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPCC2007-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Studies published since 2008, by <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a> from MIT, indicate that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> is likely to increase the intensity but decrease the frequency of hurricane and cyclone activity.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> In an article in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i>, Kerry Emanuel stated that potential hurricane destructiveness, a measure combining hurricane strength, duration, and frequency, "is highly correlated with tropical sea surface temperature, reflecting well-documented climate signals, including <a href="/wiki/Climate_oscillation" title="Climate oscillation">multidecadal oscillations</a> in the North Atlantic and North Pacific, and global warming". Emanuel predicted "a substantial increase in hurricane-related losses in the twenty-first century".<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_Emanuel_2005_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature_Emanuel_2005-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Research reported in the September 3, 2008 issue of <i>Nature</i> found that the strongest tropical cyclones are getting stronger, in particular over the North Atlantic and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. Wind speeds for the strongest tropical storms increased from an average of 225&#160;km/h (140&#160;mph) in 1981 to 251&#160;km/h (156&#160;mph) in 2006, while the ocean temperature, averaged globally over all the regions where tropical cyclones form, increased from 28.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (82.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F) to 28.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (83.3&#160;\xc2\xb0F) during this period.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricanes">Hurricanes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hurricanes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific hurricane season">Pacific hurricane season</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_multidecadal_oscillation" title="Atlantic multidecadal oscillation">Atlantic multidecadal oscillation</a></div>\n<p>A 2011 study linked increased activity of intense hurricanes in the North Atlantic with a northward shift and amplification of convective activities from the <a href="/wiki/African_easterly_jet" title="African easterly jet">African easterly waves</a> (AEWs).<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> A 2014 study investigated the response of AEWs to high emissions scenarios, and found increases in regional temperature gradients, convergence and uplift along the Intertropical Front of Africa, resulting in strengthening of the African easterly waves, affecting the climate over West Africa and the larger Atlantic basin.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Typhoons">Typhoons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclones_and_climate_change&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Typhoons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pacific_typhoon_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific typhoon season">Pacific typhoon season</a></div>\n<p>Research based on records from Japan and Hawaii indicate that <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a> in the north-west Pacific intensified by 12\xe2\x80\x9315% on average since 1977. The observed strongest typhoons doubled, or tripled in some regions, the intensity of particular landfalling systems is most pronounced. This uptick in storm intensity affects coastal populations in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, and has been attributed to warming ocean waters. The authors noted that it is not yet clear to what extent global warming caused the increased water temperatures, but observations are consistent with what the IPCC projects for warming of sea surface temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
