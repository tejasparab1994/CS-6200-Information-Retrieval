title="Textron">Textron</a></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>AAI Aerosonde</b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">unmanned aerial vehicle</a> (UAV) designed to collect weather data, including <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> measurements over oceans and remote areas. The Aerosonde was developed by <a href="/wiki/Insitu" title="Insitu">Insitu</a>, and is now manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Aerosonde_Ltd" title="Aerosonde Ltd">Aerosonde Ltd</a>, which is a strategic business of <a href="/wiki/AAI_Corporation" title="AAI Corporation">AAI Corporation</a>. The Aerosonde is powered by a modified <a href="/w/index.php?title=Enya_R120&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Enya R120 (page does not exist)">Enya R120</a> <a href="/wiki/Model_aircraft" title="Model aircraft">model aircraft</a> engine, and carries on board a small computer, <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorological instruments</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> receiver for navigation. It is also used by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">United States Armed Forces</a> for <a href="/wiki/Intelligence,_surveillance_and_reconnaissance" class="mw-redirect" title="Intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance">intelligence, surveillance and reconnaissance</a> (ISR).</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AAI_Aerosonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Design and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On August 21, 1998, a Phase 1 Aerosonde nicknamed "<a href="/wiki/Laima" title="Laima">Laima</a>", after the ancient <a href="/wiki/Latvian_mythology" title="Latvian mythology">Latvian deity</a> of good fortune, completed a 2,031 mile (3,270&#160;km) flight across the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. This was the first crossing of the Atlantic Ocean by a <a href="/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicle" title="Unmanned aerial vehicle">UAV</a>; at the time, it was also the smallest aircraft ever to cross the Atlantic<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> (the smallest aircraft record was subsequently broken by the <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_Butts_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Spirit of Butts Farm">Spirit of Butts Farm</a> UAV). Launched from a roof rack of a moving car due to its lack of undercarriage, <i>Laima</i> flew from <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> to Benbecula, an island off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 26&#160;hours 45&#160;minutes in stormy weather, using approximately 1.5 U.S. gallons (1.25 imperial gallons or 5.7 litres) of gasoline (petrol). Other than for take-off and landing, the flight was autonomous, without external control, at an altitude of 5,500&#160;ft (1,680&#160;meters). Aerosondes have also been the first unmanned aircraft to penetrate tropical cyclones, with an initial mission in 2001<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> followed by eye penetrations in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_history">Operational history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=AAI_Aerosonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operational history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>On 5 March 2012, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Special_Operations_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Special Operations Command">U.S. Special Operations Command</a> (SOCOM) awarded AAI a contract to provide the Aerosonde-G for their Mid-Endurance UAS II program. The catapult-launched air vehicle has a takeoff weight 39 or 55&#160;lb (18 or 25&#160;kg) depending on engine type, with endurance of over 10 hours and an electro-optic/infrared and laser-pointer payload.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The Aerosonde has been employed by SOCOM and U.S. <a href="/wiki/Naval_Air_Systems_Command" title="Naval Air Systems Command">Naval Air Systems Command</a> (NAVAIR) under the designation <b>MQ-19</b> under service provision contracts. A typical system comprises four air vehicles and two ground control stations that are accommodated in tents or tailored to fit in most vehicles. The system can also include remote video terminals for individual users to uplink new navigation waypoints and sensor commands to, and receive sensor imagery and video from, the vehicle from a ruggedized <a href="/wiki/Table_computer" title="Table computer">tablet</a> device. Originally, the Aerosonde suffered from engine-reliability issues, but Textron says it has rectified those issues.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>By November 2015, <a href="/wiki/Textron_Systems" class="mw-redirect" title="Textron Systems">Textron Systems</a> was performing Aerosonde operations in "eight or nine" countries for its users, including the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>, and SOCOM, as well as for commercial users consisting of a customer in the oil and gas industry. Instead of buying hardware, customers pay for "sensor hours," and the company decides how many aircraft are produced to meet requirements. 4,000 fee-for-service hours were being performed monthly, and the Aerosonde had exceeded 110,000 flight hours in service.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
