class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CirrusField-color.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA sky filled with many types of cirrus clouds accompanied by cirrocumulus upper centre and upper right</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Cirrus</b> (<a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">cloud classification</a> symbol: <b>Ci</b>) is a genus of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">atmospheric cloud</a> generally characterized by thin, wispy strands, giving the type its name from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i>cirrus</i>, meaning a ringlet or curling lock of hair.<sup id="cite_ref-richmond_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-richmond-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-2">[2]</a></sup> This cloud can form at any <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">altitude</a> between 16,500&#160;ft (5.0&#160;km) and 45,000&#160;ft (14&#160;km) above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. The strands of cloud sometimes appear in tufts of a distinctive form referred to by the common name of "mares\' tails".<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On planet Earth, cirrus generally appears white or light gray in color. It forms when <a href="/wiki/Water_vapor" title="Water vapor">water vapor</a> undergoes <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(phase_transition)" title="Deposition (phase transition)">deposition</a> at altitudes above 5,500&#160;m (18,000&#160;ft) in temperate regions and above 6,400&#160;m (21,000&#160;ft) in tropical regions. It also forms from the outflow of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Anvil_cloud" class="mw-redirect" title="Anvil cloud">anvils</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus cloud</a>. Since cirrus clouds arrive in advance of the <a href="/wiki/Frontal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Frontal system">frontal system</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>, it indicates that weather conditions may soon deteriorate. While it indicates the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> (rain), cirrus clouds only produce <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">fall streaks</a> (falling ice crystals that evaporate before landing on the ground).</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">Jet stream</a>-powered cirrus can grow long enough to stretch across continents while remaining only a few kilometers deep.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-974_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-974-4">[4]</a></sup> When visible light interacts with the ice crystals in cirrus cloud, it produces <a href="/wiki/Optical_phenomena" title="Optical phenomena">optical phenomena</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Sun_dog" title="Sun dog">sun dogs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a>. Cirrus is known to raise the temperature of the air beneath the main cloud layer by an average of 10&#160;\xc2\xb0C (18&#160;\xc2\xb0F). When the individual filaments become so extensive that they are virtually indistinguishable from one another, they form a sheet of high cloud called <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus</a>. <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">Convection</a> at high altitudes can produce another high-based genus called <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus_cloud" title="Cirrocumulus cloud">cirrocumulus</a>, a pattern of small cloud tufts that contain droplets of <a href="/wiki/Supercooled" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercooled">supercooled</a> water.</p>\n<p>Cirrus clouds form on other <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planets</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">Jupiter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">Saturn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">Neptune</a>. They have even been seen on <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>, one of Saturn\'s moons. Some of these extraterrestrial cirrus clouds are composed of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> or <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane ice</a> rather than water ice. The term <i>cirrus</i> is also used for certain <a href="/wiki/Interstellar_cloud" title="Interstellar cloud">interstellar clouds</a> composed of sub-<a href="/wiki/Micrometer_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Micrometer (unit)">micrometer</a>-sized dust grains.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Long, thin, straight cirrus against a blue sky on the left merging to cirrocumulus on the right" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg/220px-Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg/440px-Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_fibratus_and_Cirrocumulus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCirrus clouds merging to cirrocumulus clouds</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus cloud ranges in thickness from 100&#160;m (330&#160;ft) to 8,000&#160;m (26,000&#160;ft), with an average thickness of 1,500&#160;m (4,900&#160;ft). There are, on average, 30&#160;<a href="/wiki/Ice_crystals" title="Ice crystals">ice crystals</a> per <a href="/wiki/Liter" class="mw-redirect" title="Liter">liter</a> (96&#160;ice crystals per <a href="/wiki/Gallon" title="Gallon">gallon</a>), but this ranges from one ice crystal per 10,000 liters (3.7&#160;ice crystals per 10,000 gallons) to 10,000 ice crystals per liter (37,000 ice crystals per gallon), a difference of eight <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders of magnitude">orders of magnitude</a>. The length of each of these ice crystals is usually 0.25&#160;millimeters long,<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-977_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-977-5">[5]</a></sup> but they range from as short as 0.01&#160;millimeters or as long as several millimeters.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup> The ice crystals in <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a> are much smaller than those in naturally occurring cirrus cloud, as they are around 0.001&#160;millimeters to 0.1&#160;millimeters in length.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-2-7">[7]</a></sup> Cirrus can vary in temperature from \xe2\x88\x9220&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">\xc2\xb0C</a> (\xe2\x88\x924&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">\xc2\xb0F</a>) to \xe2\x88\x9230&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9222&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The ice crystals in cirrus clouds have different shapes in addition to different sizes. Some shapes include solid columns, hollow columns, plates, rosettes, and conglomerations of the various other types. The shape of the ice crystals is determined by the air temperature, atmospheric pressure, and ice <a href="/wiki/Supersaturation" title="Supersaturation">supersaturation</a>. Cirrus in temperate regions typically have the shapes segregated by type: the columns and plates tend to be at the top of the cloud, whereas the rosettes and conglomerations tend to be near the base.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup> In the northern <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> region, cirrus tend to be composed of only the columns, plates, and conglomerations, and these crystals tend to be at least four times larger than the minimum size. In <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, cirrus are usually composed of only the columns, and these columns are much longer than normal.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A picture of contorted cirrus cloud shining red in the sunset. Fall streaks (like long thin streamers) descend from the clouds." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg/220px-Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg/330px-Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg/440px-Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="1280" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_o_zachodzie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFall streaks in a cirrus cloud</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Scientists have studied the characteristics of cirrus using several different methods. One, <a href="/wiki/Light_Detection_and_Ranging" class="mw-redirect" title="Light Detection and Ranging">Light Detection and Ranging</a> (LiDAR), gives highly accurate information on the cloud\'s altitude, length, and width. Balloon-carried <a href="/wiki/Hygrometer" title="Hygrometer">hygrometers</a> give information on the humidity of the cirrus cloud but are not accurate enough to measure the depth of the cloud. Radar units give information on the altitudes and thicknesses of cirrus clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-971_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-971-8">[8]</a></sup> Another data source is satellite measurements from the <a href="/wiki/Stratospheric_Aerosol_and_Gas_Experiment" title="Stratospheric Aerosol and Gas Experiment">Stratospheric Aerosol and Gas Experiment</a> (SAGE) program. These satellites measure where <a href="/wiki/Infrared_radiation" class="mw-redirect" title="Infrared radiation">infrared radiation</a> is absorbed in the atmosphere, and if it is absorbed at cirrus altitudes, then it is assumed that there are cirrus clouds in that location.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-972_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-972-9">[9]</a></sup> The United States <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a>\'s (NASA) <a href="/wiki/Moderate-Resolution_Imaging_Spectroradiometer" class="mw-redirect" title="Moderate-Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer">MODerate resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer</a> (MODIS) also gives information on the cirrus cloud cover by measuring reflected infrared radiation of various specific frequencies during the day. During the night, it determines cirrus cover by detecting the Earth\'s infrared emissions. The cloud reflects this radiation back to the ground, thus enabling satellites to see the "shadow" it casts into space.<sup id="cite_ref-cirrus-detection_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cirrus-detection-10">[10]</a></sup> Visual observations from aircraft or the ground provide additional information about cirrus clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-972_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-972-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Cirrus fibratus clouds pictured against the sky" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg/220px-Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg/330px-Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg/440px-Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Long_Cirrus_fibratus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<i>Cirrus fibratus</i> clouds</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Based upon data taken from the United States using these methods, cirrus cloud cover was found to vary <a href="/wiki/Diurnal_cycle" title="Diurnal cycle">diurnally</a> and seasonally. The researchers found that in the summer, at noon, the cover is the lowest, with an average of 23% of the United States\' land area covered by cirrus. Around midnight, the cloud cover increases to around 28%. In winter, the cirrus cloud cover did not vary appreciably from day to night. These percentages include clear days and nights, as well as days and nights with other cloud types, as lack of cirrus cloud cover. When these clouds are present, the typical coverage ranges from 30% to 50%.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-974_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-974-4">[4]</a></sup> Based on satellite data, cirrus covers an average of 20% to 25% of the Earth\'s surface. In the tropical regions, this cloud covers around 70% of the region\'s surface area.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Cirrus clouds often produce hair-like filaments\xe2\x80\x94similar to the <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> produced in liquid\xe2\x80\x93water clouds\xe2\x80\x94called <a href="/wiki/Cavum_(cloud)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cavum (cloud)">fall streaks</a>, and they are made of heavier ice crystals that fall from the cloud. The sizes and shapes of fall streaks are determined by the <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Illinois-University_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Illinois-University-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_clouds2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Hooked cirrus clouds showing the cirrus uncinus subform." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cirrus_clouds2.jpg/220px-Cirrus_clouds2.jpg" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cirrus_clouds2.jpg/330px-Cirrus_clouds2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Cirrus_clouds2.jpg/440px-Cirrus_clouds2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="713" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_clouds2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe <i>cirrus uncinus</i> subform of cirrus clouds</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus comes in four distinct species; Cirrus <i>castellanus</i>, <i>fibratus</i>, <i>spissatus</i>, and <i>uncinus</i>; which are each divided into four varieties: <i>intortus</i>, <i>vertebratus</i>, <i>radiatus</i>, and <i>duplicatus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> <i>Cirrus castellanus</i> is a species that has cumuliform tops caused by high-altitude convection rising up from the main cloud body. <i>Cirrus fibratus</i> looks striated and is the most common cirrus species. <i>Cirrus uncinus</i> clouds are hooked and are the form that is usually called <i>mare\'s tails</i>. Of the varieties, <i>Cirrus intortus</i> has an extremely contorted shape, and <i>cirrus radiatus</i> has large, radial bands of cirrus clouds that stretch across the sky. <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability" title="Kelvin\xe2\x80\x93Helmholtz instability">Kelvin\xe2\x80\x93Helmholtz waves</a> are a form of cirrus intortus that has been twisted into loops by vertical wind shear.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-446_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-446-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cirrus clouds are formed when water vapor undergoes deposition at high altitudes where the <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> ranges from 600&#160;<a href="/wiki/Millibars" class="mw-redirect" title="Millibars">mbar</a> at 4,000&#160;m (13,000&#160;ft) above sea level to 200&#160;mbar at 12,000&#160;m (39,000&#160;ft) above sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-D.26R-973_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D.26R-973-14">[14]</a></sup> These conditions commonly occur at the leading edge of a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-447_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-447-15">[15]</a></sup> Because humidity is low at such high altitudes, this genus-type tends to be very thin.<sup id="cite_ref-usatoday_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usatoday-3">[3]</a></sup>Cirrus clouds are composed of ice crystals that originate from the freezing of super cooled water droplets in regions where air temperature is lower than -20\xc2\xb0C or -30\xc2\xb0C.Cirrus usually occur in fair weather.They are formed when it is high enough to be cold and freeze the water drops into ice. They sometimes may be caused by turbulence and wind shear, or by upper-tropospheric convection. Sometimes they are like blown out ice-crystals spreading from the top of a dying cumulonimbus.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cyclones">Cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A picture showing the vast shield of cirrus clouds accompanying Hurricane Isabel in 2003." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg/220px-Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg/330px-Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg/440px-Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7800" data-file-height="6000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Isabel_10_sept_2003_1640Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA vast shield of cirrus clouds accompanying the west side of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isabel" title="Hurricane Isabel">Hurricane Isabel</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus forms from tropical cyclones, and is commonly seen fanning out from the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewalls</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a>. A large shield of cirrus and <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">cirrostratus</a> typically accompanies the high altitude <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> of hurricanes or <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-cirrus-detection_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cirrus-detection-10">[10]</a></sup> and these can make the underlying <a href="/wiki/Rain_band" class="mw-redirect" title="Rain band">rain bands</a>\xe2\x80\x94and sometimes even the eye\xe2\x80\x94difficult to detect in satellite photographs.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thunderstorms">Thunderstorms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Thunderstorms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG" class="image"><img alt="A picture showing the cirrus clouds lancing out from the anvil of the thunderstorm. Picture taken just before the lower mass of the cumulonimbus cloud went over the photographer." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG/220px-Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG/330px-Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG/440px-Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirren_von_Cumulonimbus-Amboss_und_Cu%26Sc.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCirrus in an anvil cloud</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorms</a> can form dense cirrus at their tops. As the cumulonimbus cloud in a thunderstorm grows vertically, the liquid water droplets freeze when the air temperature reaches the <a href="/wiki/Freezing_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Freezing point">freezing point</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-lydolph-122_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lydolph-122-17">[17]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Anvil_cloud" class="mw-redirect" title="Anvil cloud">anvil cloud</a> takes its shape because the <a href="/wiki/Temperature_inversion" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperature inversion">temperature inversion</a> at the tropopause prevents the warm, moist air forming the thunderstorm from rising any higher, thus creating the flat top.<sup id="cite_ref-G.26N-212_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26N-212-18">[18]</a></sup> In the tropics, these thunderstorms occasionally produce copious amounts of cirrus from their anvils.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> High-altitude winds commonly push this dense mat out into an anvil shape that stretches <a href="/wiki/Downwind" class="mw-redirect" title="Downwind">downwind</a> as much as several kilometers.<sup id="cite_ref-G.26N-212_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26N-212-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Individual cirrus cloud formations can be the remnants of anvil clouds formed by thunderstorms. In the dissipating stage of a cumulonimbus cloud, when the normal column rising up to the anvil has evaporated or dissipated, the mat of cirrus in the anvil is all that is left.<sup id="cite_ref-G.26N-213_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-G.26N-213-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contrails">Contrails</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contrails">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">Contrails</a> are a manmade type of cirrus cloud formed when water vapor from the exhaust of a jet engine condenses on particles, which come from either the surrounding air or the exhaust itself, and freezes, leaving behind a visible trail. The exhaust can also trigger the formation of cirrus by providing <a href="/wiki/Ice_nuclei" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice nuclei">ice nuclei</a> when there is an insufficient naturally-occurring supply in the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-2-7">[7]</a></sup> One of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_aviation" title="Environmental impact of aviation">environmental impacts of aviation</a> is that persistent contrails can form into large mats of cirrus,<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-cirrus_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-cirrus-21">[21]</a></sup> and increased air traffic has been implicated as one possible cause of the increasing frequency and amount of cirrus in Earth\'s atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-cirrus_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-cirrus-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Minnis-1671_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Minnis-1671-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subforms">Subforms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subforms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Cirrus Fibratus</b> clouds appear as thin and fibrous looking. Noticeably, they are the most common type of cirrus cloud. They might indicate windy weather, since they are mostly formed by <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> on higher altitudes.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Uncinus</b> clouds appear as thin and fibrous like, except that they always have a hook or curl on the tip.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Spissatus</b> clouds are the highest clouds of the main cloud genera. They may form in the higher <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> or even at the lower <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. They are dense and opaque, not allowing the light of the sun or moon to pass through. They are more common on the anvils of <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumulonimbus">cumulonimbus</a> clouds.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Floccus</b> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, which means "lock of wool" or cirrus with ragged bases. They are not to be confused with cirrocumulus floccus since they are larger than <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirrocumulus">cirrocumulus</a> and mostly are isolated. A precipitation-based supplementary feature <a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a> is mostly visible, which makes it easier to distinguish the difference between cirrocumulus floccus and cirrus floccus.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Castellanus</b> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, which means "castle" or round turrets. They indicate that <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">atmospheric instability</a> is occurring on the higher altitudes of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. They appear as tall clouds that originate from a flat base.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opacity-based_Varieties">Opacity-based Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Opacity-based Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Cirrus clouds are translucent and so have no opacity-based varieties; only one species is opaque, which is cirrus spissatus.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern-based_Varieties">Pattern-based Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pattern-based Varieties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><b>Cirrus Intortus</b> clouds is derived from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> which means "twisted", or "wound". These clouds appear twisted, or simply, intorted.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Vertebratus</b> clouds, resembling the appearance of bones as hinted by the word "vertebratus". They look more like fish bones, with fibrous lines that originate from one which resembles the appearance of fish bones.</p>\n<p><b>Cirrus Radiatus</b> clouds appear as parallel lines that seem to originate from one point and can stretch for hundreds of miles, and they move parallel to the wind shear.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_forecasting">Use in forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use in forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Weather_forecasting" title="Weather forecasting">Weather forecasting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">Tropical cyclone track forecasting</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Highcloudsymbols.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif/300px-Highcloudsymbols.gif" width="300" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif/450px-Highcloudsymbols.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b4/Highcloudsymbols.gif 2x" data-file-width="454" data-file-height="430" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Highcloudsymbols.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHigh cloud weather map symbols.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Random, isolated cirrus do not have any particular significance.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-447_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-447-15">[15]</a></sup> A large number of cirrus clouds can be a sign of an approaching <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal system</a> or upper air disturbance. This signals a change in weather in the near future, which usually becomes stormier.<sup id="cite_ref-WeatherBattan_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WeatherBattan-23">[23]</a></sup> If the cloud is a <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_castellanus_cloud" title="Cirrus castellanus cloud">cirrus castellanus</a>, there might be instability at the high altitude level.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-447_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-447-15">[15]</a></sup> When the clouds deepen and spread, especially when they are of the <i>cirrus radiatus</i> variety or <i>cirrus fibratus</i> species, this usually indicates an approaching weather front. If it is a warm front, the cirrus clouds spread out into cirrostratus, which then thicken and lower into <a href="/wiki/Altocumulus_cloud" title="Altocumulus cloud">altocumulus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Altostratus_cloud" title="Altostratus cloud">altostratus</a>. The next set of clouds are the rain-bearing <a href="/wiki/Nimbostratus_cloud" title="Nimbostratus cloud">nimbostratus clouds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-audubon-447_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-447-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-whiteman-84_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whiteman-84-24">[24]</a></sup> When cirrus clouds precede a <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a> or <a href="/wiki/Multicellular_thunderstorm" title="Multicellular thunderstorm">multicellular thunderstorm</a>, it is because they are blown off the anvil, and the next to arrive are the cumulonimbus clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-whiteman-84_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whiteman-84-24">[24]</a></sup> Kelvin-Helmholtz waves indicate extreme wind shear at high levels.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-447_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-447-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Within the tropics, 36&#160;hours prior to the center passage of a tropical cyclone, a veil of white cirrus clouds approaches from the direction of the cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> In the mid to late 19th century, forecasters used these cirrus veils to predict the arrival of hurricanes. In the early 1870s the president of Bel\xc3\xa9n College in <a href="/wiki/Havana,_Cuba" class="mw-redirect" title="Havana, Cuba">Havana, Cuba</a>, Father <a href="/wiki/Benito_Vines" title="Benito Vines">Benito Vi\xc3\xb1es</a>, developed the first hurricane forecasting system, and he mainly used the motion of these clouds in formulating his predictions.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> He would observe the clouds hourly from 4:00 am to 10:00 pm. After accumulating enough information, Vi\xc3\xb1es began accurately predicting the paths of hurricanes, and he eventually summarized his observations in his book, <i>Apuntes Relativos a los Huracanes de las Antilles</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_climate">Effects on climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Effects on climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cirrus clouds cover up to 25% of the Earth (up to 70% in the tropics<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup>) and have a net heating effect.<sup id="cite_ref-Nucleation_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nucleation-29">[29]</a></sup> When they are thin and translucent, the clouds efficiently absorb outgoing <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> radiation while only marginally reflecting the incoming sunlight.<sup id="cite_ref-stephens-1742_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stephens-1742-30">[30]</a></sup> When cirrus clouds are 100&#160;m (330&#160;ft) thick, they reflect only around 9% of the incoming sunlight, but they prevent almost 50% of the outgoing infrared radiation from escaping, thus raising the temperature of the atmosphere beneath the clouds by an average of 10&#160;\xc2\xb0C (18&#160;\xc2\xb0F)<sup id="cite_ref-liou-1191_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-liou-1191-31">[31]</a></sup>\xe2\x80\x94a process known as the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-earthobs-global-warming_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-earthobs-global-warming-32">[32]</a></sup> Averaged worldwide, cloud formation results in a temperature loss of 5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (9&#160;\xc2\xb0F) at the earth\'s surface, mainly the result of <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">stratocumulus clouds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cloud-heating_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-heating-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_Clounds.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Fine type of Cirrus Clouds" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cirrus_Clounds.jpg/220px-Cirrus_Clounds.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cirrus_Clounds.jpg/330px-Cirrus_Clounds.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Cirrus_Clounds.jpg/440px-Cirrus_Clounds.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3664" data-file-height="2748" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_Clounds.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<i>Cirrus fibratus</i> clouds</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>As a result of their warming effects when relatively thin, cirrus clouds have been implicated as a potential partial cause of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stephens-1742_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stephens-1742-30">[30]</a></sup> Scientists have speculated that global warming could cause high thin cloud cover to increase, thereby increasing temperatures and humidity. This, in turn, would increase the cirrus cloud cover, effectively creating a <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> circuit. A prediction of this hypothesis is that the cirrus would move higher as the temperatures rose, increasing the volume of air underneath the clouds and the amount of infrared radiation reflected back down to earth.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-2_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-2-7">[7]</a></sup> In addition, the hypothesis suggests that the increase in temperature would tend to increase the size of the ice crystals in the cirrus cloud, possibly causing the reflection of <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">solar radiation</a> and the reflection of the Earth\'s infrared radiation to balance out.<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-2_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-2-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cloud-heating_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-heating-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A similar hypothesis put forth by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lindzen" title="Richard Lindzen">Richard Lindzen</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Iris_hypothesis" title="Iris hypothesis">iris hypothesis</a> in which an increase in tropical <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperatures" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea surface temperatures">sea surface temperatures</a> results in less cirrus clouds and thus more infrared radiation emitted to space.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Optical_phenomena">Optical phenomena</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Optical phenomena">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A circumhorizontal arc projected onto a sheet of striated cirrus clouds seen through a hole in lower-level cumulus clouds." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg/220px-CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg/330px-CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg/440px-CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CircumhorizonArcIdaho.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA <a href="/wiki/Circumhorizontal_arc" title="Circumhorizontal arc">circumhorizontal arc</a> over Idaho, June 2006</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus clouds, like <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirrostratus">cirrostratus clouds</a>, can produce several optical effects, such as <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halos</a> around the sun and moon. Halos are caused by interaction of the light with hexagonal ice crystals present in the clouds, which, depending on their shape and orientation, can result in a wide variety of white and colored rings, arcs and spots in the sky. Common <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">halo</a> varieties are the <a href="/wiki/22%C2%B0_halo" title="22\xc2\xb0 halo">22\xc2\xb0 halo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sun_dogs" class="mw-redirect" title="Sun dogs">sun dogs</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Circumzenithal_arc" title="Circumzenithal arc">circumzenithal arc</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Circumhorizontal_arc" title="Circumhorizontal arc">circumhorizontal arc</a> (also known as fire rainbows).<sup id="cite_ref-McGraw-1_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGraw-1-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-natlgeo-rainbow_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natlgeo-rainbow-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ucsb_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucsb-36">[36]</a></sup> Halos produced by cirrus clouds tend to be more pronounced and colorful than those caused by <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirrostratus">cirrostratus</a>.</p>\n<p>More rarely, cirrus clouds are capable of producing <a href="/wiki/Glory_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Glory (optical phenomenon)">glories</a>, more commonly associated with liquid water-based clouds such as <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratus</a>. A glory is a set of concentric, faintly-colored glowing rings that appear around the shadow of the observer, and are best observed from a high viewpoint or from a plane.<sup id="cite_ref-glory-weather.gov_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glory-weather.gov-37">[37]</a></sup> Cirrus clouds only form glories when the constituent ice crystals are <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/aspherical" class="extiw" title="wikt:aspherical">aspherical</a>, and researchers suggest that the ice crystals must be between 0.009&#160;millimeters and 0.015&#160;millimeters in length.<sup id="cite_ref-glory-1433_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glory-1433-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_other_clouds">Relation to other clouds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Relation to other clouds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">List of cloud types</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wolkenstockwerke.png" class="image"><img alt="A diagram showing clouds at various heights" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/220px-Wolkenstockwerke.png" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/330px-Wolkenstockwerke.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Wolkenstockwerke.png/440px-Wolkenstockwerke.png 2x" data-file-width="912" data-file-height="600" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wolkenstockwerke.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe heights of various cloud genera including high, middle, low, and vertical</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus clouds are one of three different genera of high-\xc3\xa9tage (high-level) clouds. High-\xc3\xa9tage clouds form at 5,000&#160;m (16,500&#160;ft) and above in temperate regions. The other two genera, <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus_cloud" title="Cirrocumulus cloud">cirrocumulus</a> and cirrostratus, are also high clouds.</p>\n<p>In the intermediate range, from 2,000&#160;m (6,500&#160;ft) to 7,000&#160;m (23,000&#160;ft) in temperate regions, are the mid-\xc3\xa9tage clouds. They comprise two or three genera depending on the system of height classification being used: <a href="/wiki/Altostratus_cloud" title="Altostratus cloud">altostratus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Altocumulus_cloud" title="Altocumulus cloud">altocumulus</a>, and, according to <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">WMO</a> classification, <a href="/wiki/Nimbostratus_cloud" title="Nimbostratus cloud">nimbostratus</a>. These clouds are formed from ice crystals, supercooled water droplets, or liquid water droplets.<sup id="cite_ref-nws-jetstream_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nws-jetstream-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Low-\xc3\xa9tage clouds form at less than 2,000&#160;m (6,500&#160;ft). The two genera that are strictly low-\xc3\xa9tage are <a href="/wiki/Stratus_cloud" title="Stratus cloud">stratus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stratocumulus_cloud" title="Stratocumulus cloud">stratocumulus</a>. These clouds are composed of water droplets, except during winter when they are formed of <a href="/wiki/Supercooled_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercooled water">supercooled waterdroplets</a> or ice crystals if the temperature at cloud level is below freezing. Two additional genera usually form in the low altitude range, but may be based at higher levels under conditions of very low humidity. They comprise the genera <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus</a>, which along with <a href="/wiki/Nimbostratus_cloud" title="Nimbostratus cloud">nimbostratus</a>, are often classified separately as clouds of vertical development, especially when their tops are high enough to be composed of super-cooled water droplets or ice crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-Plymouth_State_Meteorology_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plymouth_State_Meteorology-40">[40]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The altitudes of high-\xc3\xa9tage clouds like cirrus vary considerably with latitude. In the polar regions, they are at their lowest, with a minimum altitude of only 3,000&#160;m (10,000&#160;ft) to a maximum of 7,600&#160;m (25,000&#160;ft). In tropical regions, they are at their highest, ranging in altitude from about 6,100&#160;m (20,000&#160;ft) to around 18,000&#160;m (60,000&#160;ft). In temperate regions, they range in altitude from 5,000&#160;m (16,500&#160;ft) to 14,000&#160;m (45,000&#160;ft)\xe2\x80\x94a variation in contrast to low-\xc3\xa9tage clouds, which do not appreciably change altitude with latitude.<sup id="cite_ref-nws-jetstream_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nws-jetstream-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_high_cloud_genera">Summary of high cloud genera</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Summary of high cloud genera">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A picture of a solar halo shown as the fun sets" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg/220px-Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg/330px-Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg/440px-Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1360" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sunset_Solar_Halo_at_Keys_View_of_Joshua_Tree_National_Park.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA solar halo</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>There are three main genera in the family of high clouds: cirrus, cirrocumulus, and cirrostratus.<sup id="cite_ref-H.26H-340_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.26H-340-41">[41]</a></sup> Cirrostratus clouds commonly produce halos because they are composed almost entirely of ice crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-glossary-cirriform_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glossary-cirriform-42">[42]</a></sup> Cirrocumulus and cirrostratus are sometimes informally referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Cirriform_cloud" class="mw-redirect" title="Cirriform cloud">cirriform clouds</a>" because of their frequent association with cirrus. They are given the prefix "cirro-", but this refers more to their altitude range than their physical structure. Cirrocumulus in its pure form is actually a high cumuliform genus, and cirrostratus is stratiform, like altostratus and lower based sheet clouds.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cirrocumulus">Cirrocumulus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cirrocumulus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A large field of cirrocumulus clouds in a blue sky, beginning to merge near the upper left." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/220px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/330px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg/440px-Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1500" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrocumulus_in_Hong_Kong.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA large field of cirrocumulus clouds</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cirrocumulus_cloud" title="Cirrocumulus cloud">Cirrocumulus cloud</a></div>\n<p>Cirrocumulus clouds form in sheets or patches<sup id="cite_ref-YDN-364_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YDN-364-43">[43]</a></sup> and do not cast shadows. They commonly appear in regular, rippling patterns<sup id="cite_ref-H.26H-340_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.26H-340-41">[41]</a></sup> or in rows of clouds with clear areas between.<sup id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-2">[2]</a></sup> Cirrocumulus are, like other members of the cumuliform category, formed via <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> processes.<sup id="cite_ref-parungo-251_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-251-44">[44]</a></sup> Significant growth of these patches indicates high-altitude instability and can signal the approach of poorer weather.<sup id="cite_ref-common-clouds_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-audubon-448_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-448-46">[46]</a></sup> The ice crystals in the bottoms of cirrocumulus clouds tend to be in the form of hexagonal cylinders. They are not solid, but instead tend to have stepped funnels coming in from the ends. Towards the top of the cloud, these crystals have a tendency to clump together.<sup id="cite_ref-parungo-252_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-252-47">[47]</a></sup> These clouds do not last long, and they tend to change into cirrus because as the water vapor continues to deposit on the ice crystals, they eventually begin to fall, destroying the upward convection. The cloud then dissipates into cirrus.<sup id="cite_ref-parungo-254_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-254-48">[48]</a></sup> Cirrocumulus clouds come in four species: <i>stratiformis</i>, <i>lenticularis</i>, <i>castellanus</i>, and <i>floccus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-common-clouds_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-45">[45]</a></sup> They are <a href="/wiki/Iridescence" title="Iridescence">iridescent</a> when the constituent supercooled water droplets are all about the same size.<sup id="cite_ref-audubon-448_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-audubon-448-46">[46]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cirrostratus">Cirrostratus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cirrostratus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Close_Cirrostratus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Milky-white cirrostratus clouds cause the sky to appear lighter and have a milky tint." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Close_Cirrostratus.jpg/220px-Close_Cirrostratus.jpg" width="220" height="160" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Close_Cirrostratus.jpg/330px-Close_Cirrostratus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Close_Cirrostratus.jpg/440px-Close_Cirrostratus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2046" data-file-height="1488" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Close_Cirrostratus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA cirrostratus cloud</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cirrostratus_cloud" title="Cirrostratus cloud">Cirrostratus cloud</a></div>\n<p>Cirrostratus clouds can appear as a milky sheen in the sky<sup id="cite_ref-common-clouds_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-45">[45]</a></sup> or as a striated sheet.<sup id="cite_ref-H.26H-340_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H.26H-340-41">[41]</a></sup> They are sometimes similar to altostratus and are distinguishable from the latter because the sun or moon is always clearly visible through transparent cirrostratus, in contrast to altostratus which tends to be opaque or translucent.<sup id="cite_ref-Day-56_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Day-56-49">[49]</a></sup> Cirrostratus come in two species, <i>fibratus</i> and <i>nebulosus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-common-clouds_45-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-common-clouds-45">[45]</a></sup> The ice crystals in these clouds vary depending upon the height in the cloud. Towards the bottom, at temperatures of around \xe2\x88\x9235&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9231&#160;\xc2\xb0F) to \xe2\x88\x9245&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9249&#160;\xc2\xb0F), the crystals tend to be long, solid, hexagonal columns. Towards the top of the cloud, at temperatures of around \xe2\x88\x9247&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9253&#160;\xc2\xb0F) to \xe2\x88\x9252&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9262&#160;\xc2\xb0F), the predominant crystal types are thick, hexagonal plates and short, solid, hexagonal columns.<sup id="cite_ref-parungo-254_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-254-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-parungo-256_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-parungo-256-50">[50]</a></sup> These clouds commonly produce halos, and sometimes the halo is the only indication that such clouds are present.<sup id="cite_ref-ahrens-120_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ahrens-120-51">[51]</a></sup> They are formed by warm, moist air being lifted slowly to a very high altitude.<sup id="cite_ref-hamilton-24_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hamilton-24-52">[52]</a></sup> When a warm front approaches, cirrostratus clouds become thicker and descend forming altostratus clouds,<sup id="cite_ref-cloud-classification_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cloud-classification-2">[2]</a></sup> and rain usually begins 12 to 24 hours later.<sup id="cite_ref-ahrens-120_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ahrens-120-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial">Extraterrestrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cirrus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A composite black-and-white photograph showing cirrus clouds over the surface of Mars." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg/500px-Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg" width="500" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg/750px-Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg/1000px-Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1907" data-file-height="607" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cirrus_clouds_on_mars.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCirrus clouds on Mars</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cirrus clouds have been observed on several other planets. On 18 September 2008, the Martian Lander <i><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Lander" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix Lander">Phoenix</a></i> took a <a href="/wiki/Time-lapse" class="mw-redirect" title="Time-lapse">time-lapse</a> photograph of a group of cirrus clouds moving across the Martian sky using LiDAR.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA-photo_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA-photo-53">[53]</a></sup> Near the end of its mission, the Phoenix Lander detected more thin clouds close to the north pole of Mars. Over the course of several days, they thickened, lowered, and eventually began snowing. The total precipitation was only a few thousandths of a millimeter. James Whiteway from <a href="/wiki/York_University" title="York University">York University</a> concluded that "precipitation is a component of the [Martian] <a href="/wiki/Hydrologic_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrologic cycle">hydrologic cycle</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-MSNBC-Mars-snow_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MSNBC-Mars-snow-54">[54]</a></sup> These clouds formed during the Martian night in two layers, one around 4,000&#160;m (13,000&#160;ft) above ground and the other at surface level. They lasted through early morning before being burned away by the sun. The crystals in these clouds were formed at a temperature of \xe2\x88\x9265&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9285&#160;\xc2\xb0F), and they were shaped roughly like ellipsoids 0.127&#160;millimeters long and 0.042&#160;millimeters wide.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On Jupiter, cirrus clouds are composed of <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>. When Jupiter\'s <a href="/wiki/South_Equatorial_Belt" class="mw-redirect" title="South Equatorial Belt">South Equatorial Belt</a> disappeared, one hypothesis put forward by Glenn Orten was that a large quantity of ammonia cirrus clouds had formed above it, hiding it from view.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> NASA\'s <a href="/wiki/Cassini_probe" class="mw-redirect" title="Cassini probe">Cassini probe</a> detected these clouds on Saturn<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> and thin water-ice cirrus on Saturn\'s moon <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">Titan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Cirrus clouds composed of <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> ice exist on Uranus.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> On Neptune, thin wispy clouds which could possibly be cirrus have been detected over the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dark_Spot" title="Great Dark Spot">Great Dark Spot</a>. As on Uranus, these are probably methane crystals.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Infrared_cirrus" title="Infrared cirrus">Interstellar cirrus clouds</a> are composed of tiny dust grains smaller than a <a href="/wiki/Micrometer_(unit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Micrometer (unit)">micrometer</a> and are therefore not true clouds of this genus which are composed of ice crystals or other frozen liquids.<sup id="cite_ref-bluebook_c1_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bluebook_c1-61">[61]</a></sup> They range from a few <a href="/wiki/Light_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Light year">light years</a> to dozens of light years across. While they are not technically cirrus clouds, the dust clouds are referred to as "cirrus" because of their similarity to the clouds on Earth.
