images</a></small></span></li>\n</ul>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Hurricane Isabel</b> was the costliest, deadliest, and strongest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. The ninth named storm, fifth hurricane, and second major hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">season</a>, Isabel <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">formed near the Cape Verde Islands</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> on September 6 in the tropical <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>. It moved northwestward, and within an environment of light wind shear and warm waters it steadily strengthened to reach peak winds of 165&#160;mph (265&#160;km/h) on September 11. After fluctuating in intensity for four days, Isabel gradually weakened and made landfall on the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> with winds of 105&#160;mph (165&#160;km/h) on September 18. It quickly weakened over land and became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical</a> over western <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a> the next day.</p>\n<p>In North Carolina, the storm surge from Isabel washed out a portion of <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">Hatteras Island</a> to form what was unofficially known as <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Inlet" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabel Inlet">Isabel Inlet</a>. Damage was greatest along the Outer Banks, where thousands of homes were damaged or even destroyed. The worst of the effects of Isabel occurred in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> area and along the shores of rivers as far west and north as <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_Virginia" title="Richmond, Virginia">Richmond</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a>. Virginia reported the most deaths and damage from the hurricane. About 64% of the damage and 68% of the deaths occurred in North Carolina and Virginia. Electric service was disrupted in areas of Virginia for several days, some more rural areas were without electricity for weeks, and local flooding caused thousands of dollars in damage.</p>\n<p>Moderate to severe damage extended up the Atlantic coastline and as far inland as <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. Roughly six million people were left without electric service in the eastern United States from the strong winds of Isabel. Rainfall from the storm extended from <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> to <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">Maine</a>, and westward to <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>. Throughout the path of Isabel, damage totalled about <span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7009537000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>$5.37&#160;billion (2003&#160;USD, <span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7009699129703300000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>$6.99&#160;billion 2016&#160;USD).<sup id="cite_ref-costliest_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-costliest-1">[1]</a></sup> 16&#160;deaths in seven U.S. states were directly related to the hurricane, with 35&#160;deaths in six states and one Canadian province indirectly related to the hurricane.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" href="#cite_note-sept4two-5">[5]</a></sup> The system gradually became better organized, and <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak classifications</a> began early on September 5. Based on the development of a closed surface circulation, it is estimated the system developed into Tropical Depression Thirteen early on September 6. Hours later, it intensified into Tropical Storm Isabel,<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> though operationally the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> did not begin issuing advisories until 13&#160;hours after it first developed.<sup id="cite_ref-disco1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco1-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Located within an area of light <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and warm waters, Isabel gradually organized as curved bands developed around a circular area of deep convection near the center.<sup id="cite_ref-disco2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco2-7">[7]</a></sup> It steadily strengthened as it moved to the west-northwest, and Isabel strengthened to a hurricane on September 7 subsequent to the development of a large, yet ragged <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> located near the deepest convection.<sup id="cite_ref-disco6_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco6-8">[8]</a></sup> The eye, overall convective pattern, and outflow steadily improved in organization,<sup id="cite_ref-isabel7_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isabel7-9">[9]</a></sup> and deep convection quickly surrounded the 40-mile (60&#160;km)-wide eye.<sup id="cite_ref-disco8_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco8-10">[10]</a></sup> Isabel intensified on September 8 to reach major hurricane status while located 1,300&#160;miles (2,100&#160;km) east-northeast of <a href="/wiki/Barbuda" title="Barbuda">Barbuda</a>. On September 9, Isabel reached a primary peak intensity of 135&#160;mph (215&#160;km/h) for around 24&#160;hours, a minimal Category&#160;4 hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-2" Station</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Early on September 10, the <a href="/wiki/Eyewall" class="mw-redirect" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> became less defined, the convection near the eye became eroded, and northeasterly outflow became slightly restricted.<sup id="cite_ref-disco16_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco16-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result, Isabel weakened slightly to a Category&#160;3 hurricane. The hurricane turned more to the west due to the influence of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">Bermuda-Azores High</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> Later on September 10, Isabel restrengthened to a Category&#160;4 hurricane after convection deepened near the increasingly organizing eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-disco19_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco19-12">[12]</a></sup> The hurricane continued to intensify, and Isabel reached its peak intensity of 165&#160;mph (270&#160;km/h) on September 11, a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Category_5_Atlantic_hurricanes" title="List of Category 5 Atlantic hurricanes">Category 5 hurricane</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> Due to an <a href="/wiki/Eyewall_replacement_cycle" title="Eyewall replacement cycle">eyewall replacement cycle</a>, Isabel weakened slightly, though it retained Category&#160;5 status for 24&#160;hours.<sup id="cite_ref-disco26_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco26-13">[13]</a></sup> As Isabel underwent another eyewall replacement cycle, outflow degraded in appearance and convection around the eye weakened,<sup id="cite_ref-isabel28_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isabel28-14">[14]</a></sup> and early on September 13 Isabel weakened to a strong Category&#160;4 hurricane. A weakness in the ridge to its north allowed the hurricane to turn to the west-northwest.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> After completing the replacement cycle, the hurricane\'s large 40&#160;mile (65&#160;km) wide eye became better defined,<sup id="cite_ref-disco30_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco30-15">[15]</a></sup> and late on the 13th Isabel re-attained Category&#160;5 status.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters" title="NOAA Hurricane Hunters">NOAA Hurricane Hunter</a> Reconnaissance Aircraft flying into the hurricane launched a <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsonde</a> which measured an instantaneous wind speed of 233&#160;mph (375&#160;km/h), the strongest instantaneous wind speed recorded in an <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic tropical cyclone">Atlantic hurricane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iswav_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iswav-16">[16]</a></sup> Cloud tops warmed again shortly thereafter,<sup id="cite_ref-32lawrence_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32lawrence-17">[17]</a></sup> and Isabel weakened to a strong Category&#160;4 hurricane early on September 14. Later that day, it re-organized, and for the third time it attained Category&#160;5 status while located 400&#160;miles (650&#160;km) north of <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">San Juan, Puerto Rico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Cloud tops around the center warmed again early on September 15, and Isabel weakened to a Category 4 hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> Later that day, the inner core of deep convection began to deteriorate while the eye decayed in appearance. As a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> to its northwest build southeastward, it resulted in Isabel decelerating as it turned to the north-northwest.<sup id="cite_ref-disco38_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco38-18">[18]</a></sup> Increasing vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> contributed in weakening the hurricane further, and Isabel weakened to a Category&#160;2 hurricane on September 16 while located 645&#160;miles (1035&#160;km) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Hatteras" title="Cape Hatteras">Cape Hatteras</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> Convection remained minimal, though outflow retained excellent organization,<sup id="cite_ref-disco48_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disco48-19">[19]</a></sup> and Isabel remained a Category&#160;2 hurricane for two&#160;days, until it made landfall between <a href="/wiki/Cape_Lookout_(North_Carolina)" title="Cape Lookout (North Carolina)">Cape Lookout</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ocracoke_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocracoke Island">Ocracoke Island</a> on September 18, with winds of 105&#160;mph (165&#160;km/h). It weakened after it made landfall, though due to its fast forward motion, Isabel remained a hurricane until it reached western <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, early on September 19. After passing through <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a> as a tropical storm, Isabel became <a href="/wiki/Extratropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Extratropical">extratropical</a> over <a href="/wiki/Western_Pennsylvania" title="Western Pennsylvania">Western Pennsylvania</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>. The system crossed <a href="/wiki/Lake_Erie" title="Lake Erie">Lake Erie</a> into <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>. Early on September 20, the extratropial remnant of Isabel was absorbed by a larger extratropical storm, over the <a href="/wiki/Cochrane_District,_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="Cochrane District, Ontario">Cochrane District</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Isabel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurricane_Isabel_18_sept_2003_1555Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane Isabel making landfall in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> on September 18</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Two days before Isabel made landfall, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane watch</a> from <a href="/wiki/Little_River,_South_Carolina" title="Little River, South Carolina">Little River, South Carolina</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chincoteague,_Virginia" title="Chincoteague, Virginia">Chincoteague, Virginia</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">Pamlico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albemarle_Sound" title="Albemarle Sound">Albemarle Sounds</a> and the lower <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>. The NHC also issued a tropical storm watch south of Little River, South Carolina to the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Santee_River" title="Santee River">Santee River</a>, as well as from Chincoteague, Virginia northward to <a href="/wiki/Little_Egg_Inlet" title="Little Egg Inlet">Little Egg Inlet</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>. Hurricane and tropical storm warnings were gradually issued for portions of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a>. By the time Isabel made landfall, a tropical storm warning existed from Chincoteague, Virginia to <a href="/wiki/Fire_Island,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire Island, New York">Fire Island, New York</a> and from <a href="/wiki/Cape_Fear_(headland)" title="Cape Fear (headland)">Cape Fear, North Carolina</a> to the mouth of the Santee River in South Carolina, and a hurricane warning existed from Chincoteague, Virginia to Cape Fear. Landfall forecasts were very accurate; from three days prior, the average track forecast error for its landfall was only 36&#160;miles (58&#160;km), and for 48&#160;hours in advance the average track error was 18&#160;miles (29&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Officials declared mandatory evacuations for 24&#160;counties in <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, though in general not many left. According to a survey conducted by the United States Department of Commerce, evacuation rates were estimated as follows; 45% in the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Banks" title="Outer Banks">Outer Banks</a>, 23% in the area around the <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">Pamlico Sound</a>, 23% in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, and about 15% in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-assess_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assess-20">[20]</a></sup> The threat of Isabel resulted in the evacuation of hundreds of thousands of residents, primarily in North Carolina and Virginia, and included more than 12,000&#160;residents staying in emergency shelters.<sup id="cite_ref-scotsman_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotsman-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>19 major airports along the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast of the United States</a> were closed, with more than 1,500&#160;flights canceled. The <a href="/wiki/Washington_Metro" title="Washington Metro">Washington Metro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metrobus_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Metrobus (Washington, D.C.)">Metrobus</a> system closed prior to the arrival of the storm, and <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">Amtrak</a> canceled nearly all trains south of the nation\'s capital. Schools and businesses throughout its path closed prior to Isabel\'s arrival to allow time to prepare; hardware and home improvement stores reported brisk business of plywood, flashlights, batteries, and portable generators, as residents prepared for the storm\'s potential impact. The federal government was closed excluding emergency staff members.<sup id="cite_ref-scotsman_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotsman-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> ordered the removal of 40&#160;ships and submarines and dozens of aircraft from naval sites near <a href="/wiki/Norfolk,_Virginia" title="Norfolk, Virginia">Norfolk, Virginia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnnn_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnnn-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A contingency plan was established at the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns" title="Tomb of the Unknowns">Tomb of the Unknowns</a> at <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> that, should the winds exceed 120MPH, the guards could take positions in the trophy room (above the Tomb Plaza and providing continual sight of the Tomb) but the plan was never implemented. However, it spawned an urban legend that the Third Infantry sent orders to seek shelter, orders that were deliberately disobeyed.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On September 18, the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hurricane_Centre" title="Canadian Hurricane Centre">Canadian Hurricane Centre</a> issued heavy rainfall and wind warnings for portions of southern Ontario. A gale warning was also issued for <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">Lake Ontario</a>, eastern Lake Erie, the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Bay" title="Georgian Bay">Georgian Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chc918_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc918-24">[24]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chc919_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc919-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chc919a_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc919a-26">[26]</a></sup> A news report on September 14 warned conditions could be similar to the disaster caused by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hurricane Hazel</a> 49&#160;years prior, resulting in widespread media coverage on the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-chc03_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc03-27">[27]</a></sup> Researchers on a <a href="/wiki/Convair_240" class="mw-redirect" title="Convair 240">Convair 580</a> flight studied the structure of Isabel transitioning into an extratropical storm, after two similar studies for <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Michael_(2000)" title="Hurricane Michael (2000)">Hurricane Michael</a> in 2000 and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karen_(2001)" title="Hurricane Karen (2001)">Tropical Storm Karen</a> in 2001. While flying in a thunderstorm, ice build forced the plane to descend.<sup id="cite_ref-summary_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-summary-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span extended from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and westward to <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>. The winds, combined with previous rainfall which moistened the soil, downed many trees and power lines across its path, leaving about 6&#160;million electricity customers without power at some point. Parts of coastal <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Roads" title="Hampton Roads">Hampton Roads</a> and Northeast <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> areas, were without <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> for almost a month. Coastal areas suffered from waves and its powerful storm surge, with areas in eastern North Carolina and southeast <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> reporting severe damage from both winds and the storm surge. Throughout its path, Isabel resulted in $3.6&#160;billion in damage (2003&#160;USD, $4.69&#160;billion 2017&#160;USD) and 51&#160;deaths, of which 16 were directly related to the storm\'s effects.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The governors of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> declared states of emergency.<sup id="cite_ref-scotsman_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotsman-21">[21]</a></sup> Isabel was the first major hurricane to threaten the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-Atlantic States">Mid-Atlantic States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Upper_South" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper South">Upper South</a> since <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Floyd" title="Hurricane Floyd">Hurricane Floyd</a> in September 1999. Isabel\'s greatest effect was due to <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">flood</a> damage, the worst in some areas of <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> since 1972\'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Agnes" title="Hurricane Agnes">Hurricane Agnes</a>. More than 60&#160;million people were affected to some degree\xe2\x80\x94a similar number to Floyd but more than any other hurricane in recent memory.<sup id="cite_ref-ncdcisfl_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncdcisfl-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_and_Southeast_United_States">Caribbean and Southeast United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Isabel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Caribbean and Southeast United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/220px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" width="220" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/330px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif/440px-Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif 2x" data-file-width="992" data-file-height="878" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Isabel_2003_rainfall.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nRainfall totals from Hurricane Isabel</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Powerful surf affected the northern coastlines of the islands in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>. Strong swells also lashed the <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>. During most hurricanes, the location of the Bahamas prevents powerful swells of Atlantic hurricanes from striking southeast <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>. However, the combination of the location, forward speed, and strength of Isabel produced strong swells through the <a href="/wiki/Tongue_of_the_Ocean" title="Tongue of the Ocean">Providence Channel</a> onto a narrow 10&#160;mile (16&#160;km) stretch of the southeastern Florida coastline; wave heights peaked at 14&#160;feet (4.3&#160;m) at <a href="/wiki/Delray_Beach,_Florida" title="Delray Beach, Florida">Delray Beach</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iswav_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iswav-16">[16]</a></sup> The swells capsized a watercraft and injured its two&#160;passengers at <a href="/wiki/Boynton_Beach,_Florida" title="Boynton Beach, Florida">Boynton Beach</a>, and a swimmer required assistance to be rescued near <a href="/wiki/Juno_Beach,_Florida" title="Juno Beach, Florida">Juno Beach</a>. Minor beach erosion was reported in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach_County,_Florida" title="Palm Beach County, Florida">Palm Beach County</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-flncdc_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flncdc-31">[31]</a></sup> In the northern portion of the state, waves reached up to 15&#160;feet (4.5&#160;m) in height at <a href="/wiki/Flagler_Beach,_Florida" title="Flagler Beach, Florida">Flagler Beach</a>, causing the Flagler Beach Pier to be closed due to damaged boards from the waves.<sup id="cite_ref-flncdc2_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flncdc2-32">[32]</a></sup> Rip currents from Isabel killed a surfer at an unguarded beach in <a href="/wiki/Nassau_County,_Florida" title="Nassau County, Florida">Nassau County</a>, with an additional six people requiring rescue from the currents.<sup id="cite_ref-flncdc3_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flncdc3-33">[33]</a></sup> The beaches were later closed during the worst of the rough surf.<sup id="cite_ref-flncdc4_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flncdc4-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In northeastern <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, the outer <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> produced moderate winds reaching 45&#160;mph (72&#160;km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Myrtle_Beach,_South_Carolina" title="Myrtle Beach, South Carolina">Myrtle Beach</a>. Rainfall was light, peaking at 1.34&#160;inches (34&#160;mm) in <a href="/wiki/Loris,_South_Carolina" title="Loris, South Carolina">Loris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wilnws_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wilnws-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Carolina">North Carolina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Isabel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North Carolina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_North_Carolina" title="Effects of Hurricane Isabel in North Carolina">Effects of Hurricane Isabel in North Carolina</a></div>\n<p>Isabel produced moderate to heavy damage across eastern North Carolina, totaling $450&#160;million (2003&#160;<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>, $586&#160;million 2017&#160;USD).<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> Damage was heaviest in <a href="/wiki/Dare_County,_North_Carolina" title="Dare County, North Carolina">Dare County</a>, where storm surge flooding and strong winds damaged thousands of houses.<sup id="cite_ref-ncncdc_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncncdc-36">[36]</a></sup> The storm surge produced a 2,000&#160;foot (600&#160;m) wide inlet on <a href="/wiki/Hatteras_Island" title="Hatteras Island">Hatteras Island</a>, unofficially known as Isabel Inlet, isolating <a href="/wiki/Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Hatteras, North Carolina">Hatteras</a> by road for two months.<sup id="cite_ref-outban_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-outban-37">[37]</a></sup> Strong winds downed hundreds of trees of across the state, leaving up to 700,000&#160;residents without power. Most areas with power outages had power restored within a few days.<sup id="cite_ref-ncncdc_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncncdc-36">[36]</a></sup> The hurricane directly killed one person and indirectly killed two in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-isab_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isab-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" The surge caused significant damage to homes along river ways,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-39">[39]</a></sup> especially along the middle reaches of the James River basin.<sup id="cite_ref-rain_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rain-40">[40]</a></sup> The strong storm surge surpassed the floodgate to the <a href="/wiki/Midtown_Tunnel_(Virginia)" title="Midtown Tunnel (Virginia)">Midtown Tunnel</a> while workers attempted to close the gate; about 44&#160;million US gallons (170,000&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) of water flooded the tunnel entirely in just 40&#160;minutes, with the workers barely able to escape.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-41">[41]</a></sup> The damage to the electrical grid and flooding kept <a href="/wiki/Old_Dominion_University" title="Old Dominion University">Old Dominion University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_State_University" title="Norfolk State University">Norfolk State University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Commonwealth_University" title="Virginia Commonwealth University">Virginia Commonwealth University</a> and many of the region\'s other major educational institutions closed for almost a week. Further inland, heavy rainfall was reported, peaking at 20.2&#160;inches (513&#160;mm) in <a href="/wiki/Sherando,_Virginia" title="Sherando, Virginia">Sherando, Virginia</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-rain_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rain-40">[40]</a></sup> causing damage and severe <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Shore_of_Maryland" title="Eastern Shore of Maryland">Eastern Maryland</a>, hundreds of buildings were damaged or destroyed by the storm surge and related tidal flooding. The most severe flooding occurred in the southern portions of Dorchester and Somerset counties and on Kent Island in Queen Anne\'s County. Thousands of houses were affected in Central Maryland, with severe storm surge flooding reported in <a href="/wiki/Baltimore,_Maryland" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltimore, Maryland">Baltimore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Annapolis,_Maryland" title="Annapolis, Maryland">Annapolis</a>. Washington, D.C. sustained moderate damage, primarily from the winds. Throughout Maryland and Washington, damage totaled about $945&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $1.23&#160;billion 2017&#160;USD), with only one direct fatality due to flooding.<sup id="cite_ref-esncdc_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esncdc-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-midncdc_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-midncdc-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wmdncdc_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmdncdc-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dcncdc_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dcncdc-46">[46]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The effects of the hurricane in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> were compounded by flooding caused by the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Henri_(2003)" title="Tropical Storm Henri (2003)">Tropical Storm Henri</a> days before.<sup id="cite_ref-drc_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-drc-47">[47]</a></sup> Moderate winds of up to 62&#160;mph (100&#160;km/h) in <a href="/wiki/Lewes,_Delaware" title="Lewes, Delaware">Lewes</a><sup id="cite_ref-phil_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-phil-48">[48]</a></sup> downed numerous trees, tree limbs, and power lines across the state,<sup id="cite_ref-delncdc_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delncdc-49">[49]</a></sup> leaving at least 15,300 without power.<sup id="cite_ref-isabel1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-isabel1-50">[50]</a></sup> Numerous low-lying areas were flooded due to high surf, strong storm surge, or run-off from flooding further inland.<sup id="cite_ref-delncdc_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-delncdc-49">[49]</a></sup> The passage of Hurricane Isabel resulted in $40&#160;million in damage (2003&#160;USD, $52.1&#160;million 2017&#160;USD) and no casualties in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" 2&#160;indirect deaths in <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> One person suffered from carbon monoxide poisoning, believed to be caused due to improperly ventilated generators in an area affected by the power outages.<sup id="cite_ref-mono_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mono-53">[53]</a></sup> Moderate winds left about 1.4&#160;million customers without power across the state as a result of trees falling into power lines, with dozens of houses and cars damaged by the trees.<sup id="cite_ref-penncdc_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-penncdc-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cenpendcd_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cenpendcd-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nwscen_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nwscen-56">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Damage in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> totaled $90&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $117&#160;million 2017&#160;USD),<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> with Vermont reporting about $100,000 in damage (2003&#160;USD, $130,000 2017&#160;USD).<sup id="cite_ref-verncdc1_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verncdc1-57">[57]</a></sup><sup Two people died in the region as a result of the hurricane, both due to the rough surf from Isabel.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Isabel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Elsewhere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia" title="West Virginia">West Virginia</a>, the storm produced moderate rainfall across the state that peaked at 6.88&#160;in (175&#160;mm) near <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Grove,_West_Virginia" title="Sugar Grove, West Virginia">Sugar Grove</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mid_Atl_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mid_Atl-63">[63]</a></sup> The rainfall resulted in mudslides and flash flooding, covering several roads and washing away two bridges. The <a href="/wiki/South_Branch_Potomac_River" class="mw-redirect" title="South Branch Potomac River">South Branch Potomac River</a> crested at 24.7&#160;feet (7.5&#160;m), 9.3&#160;feet (2.8&#160;m) above flood state near <a href="/wiki/Springfield,_West_Virginia" title="Springfield, West Virginia">Springfield</a>. The flooding broke a levee at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Field,_West_Virginia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael Field, West Virginia (page does not exist)">Michael Field</a>, and in Mineral County one&#160;school and 14&#160;basements were flooded. In <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_County,_West_Virginia" title="Jefferson County, West Virginia">Jefferson County</a>, two people required rescue after a car drove into floodwaters.<sup id="cite_ref-ncdc_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-64">[64]</a></sup> Although sustained winds were weak in the state, wind gusts reached 46&#160;mph (74&#160;km/h) at <a href="/wiki/Martinsburg,_West_Virginia" title="Martinsburg, West Virginia">Martinsburg</a>. With the wet grounds, the wind gusts toppled thousands of trees, which fell onto homes, roads, and power lines.<sup id="cite_ref-ncdc_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-64">[64]</a></sup> About 1.4&#160;million residents across the state were left without power.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-65">[65]</a></sup> Damage in the state totaled $20&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $26&#160;million 2017&#160;USD). No deaths were reported,<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup> and three were injured from the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-ncdc_64-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncdc-64">[64]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Isabel dropped light to moderate precipitation across the eastern half of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, with isolated locations reporting over 3&#160;in (75&#160;mm).<sup id="cite_ref-rain_40-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rain-40">[40]</a></sup> Moisture from Isabel dropped light rainfall across eastern <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> and peaked at 1.55&#160;inches (39&#160;mm) at <a href="/wiki/Mount_Clemens,_Michigan" title="Mount Clemens, Michigan">Mount Clemens</a>. Additionally, <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> estimated rainfall approached 2.5&#160;inches (64&#160;mm) in <a href="/wiki/St._Clair_County,_Michigan" title="St. Clair County, Michigan">St. Clair County</a>. No damage was reported from Isabel in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-Michigan_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Michigan-66">[66]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Swells from Isabel produced moderate surf along the Atlantic coast of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, particularly in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Maine" title="Gulf of Maine">Gulf of Maine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chc918_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc918-24">[24]</a></sup> Isabel also produced rough surf in Lake Ontario, with waves reaching 4&#160;m (13&#160;ft) along the western portion. At <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton</a>, the waves surpassed seawalls and produced spray onto coastal streets.<sup id="cite_ref-summary_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-summary-28">[28]</a></sup> Rainfall peaked at 59&#160;mm (2.3&#160;in), which caused minor flooding and led to one traffic fatality. About 27,000&#160;people lost power, mostly near Toronto.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup> The strong pressure gradient between Isabel and a <a href="/wiki/High_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="High pressure area">high pressure system</a> over eastern Canada produced strong easterly winds across lakes Ontario and Erie.<sup id="cite_ref-summary_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-summary-28">[28]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Buoy" title="Buoy">buoy</a> in Lake Ontario reported a peak gust of 78&#160;km/h (49&#160;mph),<sup id="cite_ref-chc920_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chc920-68">[68]</a></sup> and gusts reached as strong as 81&#160;km/h (51&#160;mph) at <a href="/wiki/Port_Colborne,_Ontario" class="mw-redirect" title="Port Colborne, Ontario">Port Colborne, Ontario</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_2-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_retired_Atlantic_hurricane_names" title="List of retired Atlantic hurricane names">List of retired Atlantic hurricane names</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg/330px-Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg 1.5x, data-file-height="2048" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatteras_Island_damage_by_Hurricane_Isabel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDamage to the Barrier Islands after Hurricane Isabel (USGS)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>By about a week after the passage of the hurricane, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> declared disaster areas for 36&#160;<a href="/wiki/List_of_North_Carolina_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="List of North Carolina counties">North Carolina counties</a>, 77&#160;<a href="/wiki/List_of_Virginia_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Virginia counties">counties and independent cities</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>, the entire state of <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a>, all three counties in <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a> and six <a href="/wiki/List_of_West_Virginia_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="List of West Virginia counties">West Virginia counties</a>. The disaster declaration allocated the use of federal funds for rebuilding and providing aid in the aftermath of hurricane Isabel.<sup id="cite_ref-assess_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-assess-20">[20]</a></sup> By about four months after the passage of the hurricane, disaster aid totaled about $516&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $672&#160;million 2017&#160;USD), primarily in North Carolina and Virginia. Over 166,000 residents applied for individual assistance, with about $117&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $152&#160;million 2017&#160;USD) approved for residents to assist with temporary housing and home repairs. About 50,000&#160;business owners applied for Small Business Administration loans, with about $178&#160;million (2003&#160;USD, $232&#160;million 2017&#160;USD) approved for the assistance loans. About 40,000&#160;people visited local disaster recovery centers, designed to provide additional information regarding the aftermath of the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-ncfema_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncfema-69">[69]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vafema_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vafema-70">[70]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mdfema_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mdfema-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-defema_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-defema-72">[72]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pafema_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pafema-73">[73]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>, hundreds of residents were stranded in <a href="/wiki/Hatteras,_North_Carolina" title="Hatteras, North Carolina">Hatteras</a> following the formation of Isabel Inlet.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-74">[74]</a></sup> People who were not residents were not allowed to be on the Outer Banks for two weeks after the hurricane due to damaged road conditions. When visitors were allowed to return, many ventured to see the new inlet, despite a 1-mile (1.6-km) walk from the nearest road.<sup id="cite_ref-outban_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-outban-37">[37]</a></sup> Initially, long term solutions to the Isabel Inlet such as building a bridge or a ferry system were considered, though they were ultimately canceled in favor of pumping sand and filling the inlet. Coastal geologists were opposed to the solution, stating the evolution of the Outer Banks is dependent on inlets from hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-natgeo_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-75">[75]</a></sup> Dredging operations began on October 17, about a month after the hurricane struck. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">United States Geological Survey</a> used sand from the ferry channel to the southwest of Hatteras Island, a choice made to minimize the impact to submerged aquatic vegetation and due to the channel being filled somewhat during the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-fem3_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fem3-76">[76]</a></sup> On November 22, about two months after the hurricane struck, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Highway_12" title="North Carolina Highway 12">North Carolina Highway 12</a> and Hatteras Island were reopened to public access. On the same day, the ferry between Hatteras and Ocracoke was reopened.<sup id="cite_ref-outban_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-outban-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In West Virginia, the power outages were restored within a week.<sup id="cite_ref-iser3_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iser3-77">[77]</a></sup> Power workers throughout Canada assisted the severely affected power companies from <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_Maryland_and_Washington,_D.C." title="Effects of Hurricane Isabel in Maryland and Washington, D.C.">Maryland</a> to <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_Hurricane_Isabel_in_North_Carolina" title="Effects of Hurricane Isabel in North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-fact_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fact-78">[78]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hydro-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Hydro-Qu\xc3\xa9bec">Hydro-Qu\xc3\xa9bec</a> sent 25&#160;teams to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> area to assist in power outages.<sup id="cite_ref-cbcis_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbcis-79">[79]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Isabel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Because of widespread property damage and extensive death tolls the name <i>Isabel</i> was retired after the 2003 season, and will not be used for future Atlantic hurricanes. It was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ida_(2009)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Ida (2009)"><i>Ida</i></a> for the naming list for the <a href="/wiki/2009_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2009 Atlantic hurricane season">2009 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wmo1_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo1-80">[80]</a></sup> The names <i>Ina</i> and <i>Ivy</i> were also suggested as possible replacement names.<sup id="cite_ref-wmo2_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo2-81">[81]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span
