For the album by Biosphere, see <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde_(album)" title="Dropsonde (album)">Dropsonde (album)</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg/220px-Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg/330px-Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/06/Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="280" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_delivery_system.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDropsonde delivery system on a NOAA P-3 Hurricane Hunter.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>dropsonde</b> is an expendable <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">weather</a> reconnaissance device created by the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR), designed to be dropped from an <a href="/wiki/Aircraft" title="Aircraft">aircraft</a> at altitude over water to measure (and therefore track) storm conditions as the device falls to the surface. The sonde contains a <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">GPS</a> receiver, along with <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">pressure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> (PTH) <a href="/wiki/Sensor" title="Sensor">sensors</a> to capture <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> profiles and <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamic</a> <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">data</a>. It typically relays these data to a computer in the aircraft by radio transmission.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dropsonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/280px-Dropsonde.png" width="280" height="340" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/420px-Dropsonde.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dropsonde.png/560px-Dropsonde.png 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="777" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA diagram of an NCAR GPS Dropsonde</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Since the early 1970s<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_hunters" title="Hurricane hunters">hurricane hunters</a> have employed dropsondes while flying over the ocean to obtain meteorological data on the structure of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricanes</a> deemed to be of possible concern to land locations in the northern Atlantic and northeastern Pacific oceans. Dropsonde instruments are typically the only way to measurement the wind and pressure near the sea surface within the core of such cyclones, allowing meteorologists to reliably establish the storm\'s intensity and size. The data obtained is typically fed into supercomputers for <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a>, enabling forecasters to better track and predict what will happen to the hurricane. During a typical hurricane season, the <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a> Hurricane Hunters deploys 1000 to 1500 sondes on training and storm missions.</p>\n<p>Aircraft reconnaissance missions are also sometimes requested to investigate the broader atmospheric structure over the ocean when cyclones may pose a significant threat to the United States. These interests include not only potential hurricanes, but also possible snow events (like <a href="/wiki/Nor%27easters" class="mw-redirect" title="Nor\'easters">nor\'easters</a>) or significant <a href="/wiki/Tornado_outbreak" title="Tornado outbreak">tornado outbreaks</a>. The dropsondes are used to supplement the large gaps over oceans within the global network of <a href="/wiki/Radiosonde#Routine_radiosonde_launches" title="Radiosonde">daily radiosonde launches</a>. Typically satellite data provides an estimate of conditions in such areas, but the increased precision of sondes can improve forecasts, particularly of the storm path.</p>\n<p>Dropsondes may also be employed during meteorological research projects.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Device_and_Launch_Details">Device and Launch Details</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dropsonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Device and Launch Details">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The sonde is a lightweight system designed to be operated by one person and is launched through a chute installed in the measuring aircraft. The device\'s descent is slowed and stabilized by a small square-cone <a href="/wiki/Parachute#Round" title="Parachute">parachute</a>, allowing for more readings to be taken before it reaches the ocean surface. The parachute is designed to immediately deploy after release so as to reduce or eliminate any <a href="/wiki/Pendulum" title="Pendulum">pendulum</a> effect, and the device typically drops for three to five minutes. The sonde has a casing of stiff cardboard to protect electronics and form a more stable aerodynamic profile.</p>\n<p>To obtain data in a tropical cyclone, an aircraft (in the US, operated either by by <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">NOAA</a> or the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Air Force">U.S. Air Force</a>) flies into the system. A series of dropsondes are typically released as the plane passes through the storm, typically launched with greatest frequency near the center of the storm, including into the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eyewall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> eye (center), if one exists. Most drops are performed at a flight level of around 10,000 feet (approx. 3,000 meters).</p>\n<p>The dropsonde sends back coded data, which includes:</p>\n<ol>\n<li>The date and time of the drop. Time is always in <a href="/wiki/UTC" class="mw-redirect" title="UTC">UTC</a>.</li>\n<li>Location of the drop, indicated by the <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marsden_square" title="Marsden square">Marsden square</a>.</li>\n<li>The height, temperature, <a href="/wiki/Dewpoint_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Dewpoint depression">dewpoint depression</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind_direction" title="Wind direction">wind direction</a> recorded at any standard isobaric surfaces encountered as the dropsonde descends, which are from the set of: 1000, 925, 850, 700, 500, 400, 300, 250 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)#Hectopascal_and_millibar_units" title="Pascal (unit)">hectopascals</a> (hPa), and at the sea surface.</li>\n<li>The temperature and dewpoint depression at all other atmospheric pressure deemed significant due to important changes or values in the atmospheric conditions found</li>\n<li>Air pressure, temperature, dewpoint depression, wind speed and wind direction of the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>.</li>\n</ol>\n<p>Also included in the report is information on the aircraft, the mission, the dropsonde itself, and other remarks.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Driftsondes">Driftsondes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dropsonde&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Driftsondes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/220px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg" width="220" height="241" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/330px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg/440px-Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1127" data-file-height="1233" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dropsonde_U.S._Air_Force.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDropsonde (source <a href="/wiki/US_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="US Air Force">US Air Force</a>)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A driftsonde is a high altitude, durable <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">weather balloon</a> holding a transmitter and a bank (35 in the first models) of miniature dropsonde capsules which can then be dropped at automatic intervals or remotely. The water-bottle-sized transmitters in the dropsondes have enough power to send information to the balloon during their parachute-controlled fall. The balloon carries a larger transmitter powerful enough to relay readings to a satellite. The single-use sensor packages cost US$300 to $400 each.<sup id="cite_ref-SciDay_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SciDay-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After being introduced in April 2007, around a thousand a year are expected to be used to track winds in hurricane breeding grounds off of West Africa, which are outside the operating region of Hurricane Hunter planes.<sup id="cite_ref-SciDay_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SciDay-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
