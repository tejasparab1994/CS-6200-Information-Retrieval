types">Precipitation cloud</a>?</th>\n<td>Yes, often intense</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Cumulonimbus</b>, from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> cumulus ("heap") and nimbus ("rainstorm", "storm cloud"), is a dense towering vertical <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a><sup id="cite_ref-Cumulonimbus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cumulonimbus-1">[1]</a></sup> associated with <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">atmospheric instability</a>, forming from water vapor carried by powerful upward air currents. If observed during a storm, these clouds may be referred to as thunderheads. Cumulonimbus can form alone, in clusters, or along <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">cold front squall lines</a>. These clouds are capable of producing <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a> and other dangerous severe weather, such as <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. Cumulonimbus progress from overdeveloped <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_congestus_cloud" title="Cumulus congestus cloud">cumulus congestus clouds</a> and may further develop as part of a <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a>. Cumulonimbus is abbreviated <b>Cb</b>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulonimbus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Towering cumulonimbus clouds are typically accompanied by smaller <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumulus</a> clouds. The cumulonimbus base may extend several miles across and occupy low to middle altitudes- formed at altitude from approximately 200 to 4,000&#160;m (700 to 10,000&#160;ft). Peaks typically reach to as much as 40,000&#160;ft (12,000&#160;m), with extreme instances as high as 70,000&#160;ft (21,000&#160;m) or more.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Well-developed cumulonimbus clouds are characterized by a flat, <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a>-like top (anvil dome), caused by wind shear or <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology)" title="Inversion (meteorology)">inversion</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>. The shelf of the anvil may precede the main cloud\'s vertical component for many miles, and be accompanied by <a href="/wiki/Lightning" title="Lightning">lightning</a>. Occasionally, rising air parcels surpass the equilibrium level (due to momentum) and form an <a href="/wiki/Overshooting_top" title="Overshooting top">overshooting top</a> culminating at the <a href="/wiki/Maximum_parcel_level" title="Maximum parcel level">maximum parcel level</a>. When vertically developed, this largest of all clouds usually extends through all three cloud regions. Even the smallest cumulonimbus cloud dwarfs its neighbors in comparison.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulonimbus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">Severe weather</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">Thunderstorm</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">Tornado</a></div>\n<p>Cumulonimbus storm cells can produce torrential <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> nature (often in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Rain_shaft" class="mw-redirect" title="Rain shaft">rain shaft</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Straight-line_winds" class="mw-redirect" title="Straight-line winds">straight-line winds</a>. Most storm cells die after about 20&#160;minutes, when the <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> causes more downdraft than updraft, causing the energy to dissipate. If there is enough <a href="/wiki/Solar_energy" title="Solar energy">solar energy</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth\'s atmosphere">atmosphere</a>, however (on a hot summer\'s day, for example), the moisture from one storm cell can <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporate</a> rapidly\xe2\x80\x94resulting in a new cell forming just a few miles from the former one. This can cause thunderstorms to last for several hours. Cumulonimbus clouds can also bring dangerous winter storms (called "<a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzards</a>") which bring lightning, thunder, and torrential snow. However, cumulonimbus clouds are most common in tropical regions.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_cycle_or_stages">Life cycle or stages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulonimbus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life cycle or stages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In general, cumulonimbus require moisture, an unstable air mass, and a lifting force (heat) in order to form. Cumulonimbus typically go through three stages: the <b>developing stage</b>, the <b>mature stage</b> (where the main cloud may reach <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a> status in favorable conditions), and the <b>dissipation stage</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Extreme_Weather_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Extreme_Weather-10">[10]</a></sup> The average thunderstorm has a 24&#160;km (15&#160;mi) diameter. Depending on the conditions present in the atmosphere, these three stages take an average of 30 minutes to go through.<sup id="cite_ref-tsbasics_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tsbasics-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_formation.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/500px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg" width="500" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/750px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Thunderstorm_formation.jpg/1000px-Thunderstorm_formation.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1087" data-file-height="554" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thunderstorm_formation.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nStages of a cumulonimbus cloud\'s life.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg/220px-Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg/330px-Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg/440px-Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1067" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anvil_cumulus_feb_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTowering vertical cumulonimbus capillatus with anvil-shaped incus supplementary feature. High layer of cirrus spissatus near top of image.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cloud_types">Cloud types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cumulonimbus_cloud&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cloud types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cloud_types" title="List of cloud types">List of cloud types</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">Clouds</a> form when the <a href="/wiki/Dewpoint" class="mw-redirect" title="Dewpoint">dewpoint</a> of <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> is reached in the presence of <a href="/wiki/Condensation_nuclei" class="mw-redirect" title="Condensation nuclei">condensation nuclei</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>. The atmosphere is a dynamic system, and the local conditions of <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">turbulence</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">uplift</a> and other parameters give rise to many types of clouds. Various types of cloud occur frequently enough to have been categorized. Furthermore, some atmospheric processes can make the clouds organize in distinct patterns such as <a href="/wiki/Wave_cloud" title="Wave cloud">wave clouds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Actinoform_cloud" title="Actinoform cloud">actinoform clouds</a>. These are large-scale structures and are not always readily identifiable from a single point of view.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
