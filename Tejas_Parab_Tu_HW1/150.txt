Fetch is used in <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> and its effects are usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Sea_state" title="Sea state">sea state</a> and when it reaches shore it is the main factor that creates <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surge</a> which leads to coastal <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a>. It also plays a large part in <a href="/wiki/Longshore_drift" title="Longshore drift">longshore drift</a> as well.</p>\n<p>Fetch length, along with the wind speed (wind strength), determines the size (<a href="/wiki/Sea_state" title="Sea state">sea state</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean surface wave">waves</a> produced. The wind direction is considered constant. The longer the fetch and the faster the wind speed, the more wind energy is imparted to the water surface and the larger the resulting sea state will be.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
