These include the north <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the eastern and western parts of the northern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, the southwestern Pacific, the southwestern and southeastern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Oceans</a>, and the northern Indian Ocean (<a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>). The western Pacific is the most active and the north Indian the least active. An average of 86&#160;tropical cyclones of tropical storm intensity form annually worldwide, with 47 reaching hurricane/typhoon strength, and 20 becoming intense tropical cyclones (at least of <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> intensity).<sup id="cite_ref-Landsea_variability_table_1-0" class="reference"><a class="mw-headline" id="North_Atlantic_Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North Atlantic Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlantic_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Atlantic_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/200px-Atlantic_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" width="200" height="124" class="thumbimage" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Atlantic_hurricane_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTracks of all tropical cyclones in the northern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> between 1980 and 2005</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>This region includes the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. Tropical cyclone formation here varies widely from year to year, ranging from one to over twenty-five per year.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_E10_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_E10-6">[6]</a></sup> Most Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical storms</a> and hurricanes form between June 1 and November 30. The United States <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> (NHC) monitors the basin and issues reports, watches and warnings about <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> weather systems for the Atlantic Basin as one of the <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centres</a> for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> as defined by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-Atl_avg_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-Atl_avg-7">[7]</a></sup> On average, 11 named storms (of tropical storm or higher strength) occur each season, with an average of 6 becoming hurricanes and 2 becoming major hurricanes. The <a href="/wiki/Climatology" title="Climatology">climatological</a> peak of activity is around September 10 each season.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_Atl_peak_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_Atl_peak-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The United States Atlantic coast and Gulf Coast, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Caribbean Islands">Caribbean Islands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> are frequently affected by storms in this basin. Venezuela, the 4 provinces of <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>, and Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Macaronesia" title="Macaronesia">Macaronesian islands</a> also are occasionally affected. Many of the more intense Atlantic storms are <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde-type_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Cape Verde-type hurricane">Cape Verde-type hurricanes</a>, which form off the west coast of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> near the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">Cape Verde</a> islands. Occasionally, a hurricane that evolves into an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> can reach western <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Gordon_(2006)" title="Hurricane Gordon (2006)">Hurricane Gordon</a>, which spread high winds across Spain and the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> in September 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-GordonTCR_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GordonTCR-9">[9]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">Hurricane Vince</a>, which made landfall on the southwestern coast of Spain as a tropical depression in October 2005, is the only known system to impact mainland Europe as a tropical cyclone in the NHC study period commencing in 1851<sup id="cite_ref-VinceTCR_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VinceTCR-10">[10]</a></sup> (it is believed a hurricane made <a href="/wiki/1842_Atlantic_hurricane_season#Late_October" title="1842 Atlantic hurricane season">landfall in Spain in 1842</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_Pacific">Eastern Pacific</span><span The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_hurricane_seasons" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Pacific hurricane seasons">hurricane season</a> runs between May 15 and November 30 each year, and encompasses the vast majority of tropical cyclone activity in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-EPac_avg_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-EPac_avg-12">[12]</a></sup> In the 1971\xe2\x80\x932005 period, there were an average of 15\xe2\x80\x9316 tropical storms, 9 <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a>, and 4\xe2\x80\x935 major hurricanes (storms of <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3 intensity</a> or greater) annually in the basin.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-EPac_avg_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-EPac_avg-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Storms that form here often affect western <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and less commonly the <a href="/wiki/Continental_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Continental United States">Continental United States</a> (in particular <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>), or northern <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. No hurricane included in the modern database has made landfall in California; however, historical records from 1858 speak of a <a href="/wiki/1858_San_Diego_Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="1858 San Diego Hurricane">storm</a> that brought <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> winds over 75&#160;mph (65&#160;kn; 121&#160;km/h) (marginal hurricane force), though it is not known if the storm actually made landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1858_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego_1858-13">[13]</a></sup> Tropical storms in <a href="/wiki/1939_Long_Beach_Tropical_Storm" class="mw-redirect" title="1939 Long Beach Tropical Storm">1939</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Kathleen_(1976)" title="Hurricane Kathleen (1976)">1976</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Nora_(1997)" title="Hurricane Nora (1997)">1997</a> brought gale-force winds to California.<sup id="cite_ref-San_Diego_1858_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-San_Diego_1858-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/Area_of_responsibility" title="Area of responsibility">area of responsibility</a> (AOR) begins at the boundary with the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>\' AOR (at 140&#160;<a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">\xc2\xb0W</a>), and ends at the International Date Line, where the Northwestern Pacific begins.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_F1_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_F1-14">[14]</a></sup> The hurricane season in the North Central Pacific runs annually from June 1 to November 30;<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_Climatology_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Climatology-15">[15]</a></sup> The Central Pacific Hurricane Center monitors the storms that develop or move into the defined area of responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_F1_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_F1-14">[14]</a></sup> The CPHC previously tasked with monitoring tropical activity in the basin was originally known as the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Hurricane_Warning_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint Hurricane Warning Center">Joint Hurricane Warning Center</a>; today it is called the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>.</p>\n<p>Central Pacific hurricanes are rare and on average 4 to 5 storms form or move in this area annually.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_Climatology_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Climatology-15">[15]</a></sup> As there are no large contiguous landmasses in the basin, direct hits and <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfalls</a> are rare; however, they occur occasionally, as with <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Iniki" title="Hurricane Iniki">Hurricane Iniki</a> in 1992, which made landfall on <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_Iniki_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_Iniki-16">[16]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ioke" title="Hurricane Ioke">Hurricane Ioke</a> in 2006, which made a direct hit on <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Atoll" title="Johnston Atoll">Johnston Atoll</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ioke_Atoll_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ioke_Atoll-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northwestern_Pacific_Ocean">Northwestern Pacific Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Northwestern Pacific Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/200px-Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" width="200" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/300px-Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/400px-Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4700" data-file-height="3600" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pacific_typhoon_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTracks of all tropical cyclones in the northwestern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> between 1980 and 2005. The vertical line to the right is the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">Typhoon</a></div>\n<p>The Northwest Pacific Ocean is the most active basin on the planet, accounting for one-third of all tropical cyclone activity. Annually, an average of 25.7 tropical cyclones in the basin acquire <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> strength or greater; also, an average of 16 <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a> occurred each year during the 1968\xe2\x80\x931989 period.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_E10_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_E10-6">[6]</a></sup> The basin occupies all the territory north of the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> and west of the International Date Line, including the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_F1_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_F1-14">[14]</a></sup> The basin sees activity year-round; however, tropical activity is at its minimum in February and March.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_G1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_G1-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tropical storms in this region often affect <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, plus numerous <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">Oceanian</a> islands such as <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Marianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Marianas">Northern Marianas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">Palau</a>. Sometimes, tropical storms in this region affect <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>. The coast of <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People\'s Republic of China">China</a> sees the most landfalling tropical cyclones worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Weyman_and_Anderson-Berry_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weyman_and_Anderson-Berry-19">[19]</a></sup> The Philippines <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a> receives an average of 6\xe2\x80\x937 tropical cyclone landfalls per year.<sup id="cite_ref-Shoemaker_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shoemaker-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Indian_Ocean">North Indian Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: North Indian Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/200px-North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/300px-North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg/400px-North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3300" data-file-height="2200" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_tracks_1980-2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTracks of all tropical cyclones in the northern <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> between 1980 and 2005</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>This basin is divided into two areas: the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, with the Bay of Bengal dominating (5 to 6 times more activity). Still, this basin is the most inactive worldwide, with only 4 to 6 storms per year.</p>\n<p>This basin\xe2\x80\x99s season has a double peak: one in April and May, before the onset of <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">the monsoon</a>, and another in October and November, just after.<sup id="cite_ref-2003_ATCR_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2003_ATCR-21">[21]</a></sup> This double peak occurs because powerful <a href="/wiki/Vertical_wind_shear" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertical wind shear">vertical wind shear</a> in between the surface monsoonal low and upper tropospheric high during the monsoon season tears apart incipient cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> High shear explains why no cyclones can form in the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a>, which possesses the necessary depth, vorticity and surface temperatures year-round. Rarely do tropical cyclones that form elsewhere in this basin affect the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a> or <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>; however, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Gonu" title="Cyclone Gonu">Cyclone Gonu</a> caused heavy damage in Oman on the peninsula in 2007.</p>\n<p>Although the North Indian Ocean is a relatively inactive basin, extremely high population densities in the <a href="/wiki/Ganges_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ganges River">Ganges</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ayeyarwady_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayeyarwady River">Ayeyarwady</a> Deltas mean that the deadliest tropical cyclones in the world have formed here, including the <a href="/wiki/1970_Bhola_cyclone" title="1970 Bhola cyclone">1970 Bhola cyclone</a>, which killed 500,000 people. Nations affected include <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">Myanmar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean tropical cyclone">Mediterranean tropical cyclone</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg/220px-Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg" width="220" height="228" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg/330px-Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f0/Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg/440px-Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="458" data-file-height="475" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_hurricane_Medicanes_tropical_cyclone_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nImage of January 1995 system</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>On rare occasions, tropical-like systems, that can reach the intensity of hurricanes, occur over the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Such a phenomenon is called a <a href="/wiki/Medicane" class="mw-redirect" title="Medicane">Medicane</a> (mediterranean-hurricane). Although the geographical dimensions of tropical <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> Sea are clearly different, the precursor mechanisms of these perturbations, based on the air-sea thermodynamic imbalance, are similar.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-23">[23]</a></sup> Their origins are typically non-tropical, and develop over open waters under strong, initially cold-core <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, similar to subtropical cyclones or anomalous tropical cyclones in the Atlantic Basin, like <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Karl_(1980)" title="Hurricane Karl (1980)">Karl (1980)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">Vince (2005)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Grace_(2009)" title="Tropical Storm Grace (2009)">Grace (2009)</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Chris_(2012)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Chris (2012)">Chris (2012)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Sea surface temperatures in late-August and early-September are quite high over the basin (24/28&#160;\xc2\xb0C or 75/82&#160;\xc2\xb0F), though research indicates water temperatures of 20&#160;\xc2\xb0C (68&#160;\xc2\xb0F) are normally required for development.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Meteorological literature document that such systems occurred in September 1947, September 1969, January 1982, September 1983, January 1995, October 1996, September 2006, November 2011 and November 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-01M_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-01M-27">[27]</a></sup> The 1995 system developed a well-defined <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, and a ship recorded 85&#160;mph (140&#160;km/h) winds, along with an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 975 <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>. Although it had the structure of a tropical cyclone, it occurred over 61&#160;\xc2\xb0F (16&#160;\xc2\xb0C) water temperatures, suggesting it could have been a <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar low</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2b_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2b-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Southern Hemisphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> tropical cyclones generally form on a regular basis between the African coast and the middle of the South Pacific. Tropical and Subtropical Cyclones have also been noted occurring in the Southern Atlantic Ocean at times. For various reasons including where tropical cyclones form, there are several different ways to split the area between the American and African coasts. For instance the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> define three different basins for the tracking and warning of tropical cyclones. These are the South-West Indian Ocean between the African Coast and 90\xc2\xb0E, the Australian region between 90\xc2\xb0E and 160\xc2\xb0E and the South Pacific between 160\xc2\xb0E and 120\xc2\xb0W. The United States <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> also monitors the whole region, but splits it at 135\xc2\xb0E into the South Pacific and the Southern Indian Ocean.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South-West_Indian_Ocean">South-West Indian Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span South-West Indian Ocean is located within the Southern Hemisphere between the Africa\'s east coast and 90\xc2\xb0E and is primarily monitored by the Meteo France\'s La Reunion RSMC, while the Mauritian, Australian Indonesian, and Malagasy weather services also monitor parts of it.<sup id="cite_ref-WMO_RSMC_La_Reunion_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_RSMC_La_Reunion-29">[29]</a></sup> Until the start of the 1985\xe2\x80\x9386 tropical cyclone season the basin only extended to 80\xc2\xb0E, with the 10 degrees between 80 and 90E considered to be a part of the Australian region.<sup id="cite_ref-SWIO_bound_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWIO_bound-30">[30]</a></sup> On average about 9 cyclones per develop into tropical storms, while 5 of those go on to become tropical cyclones that are equivalent to a hurricane or a typhoon.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> The tropical cyclones that form in this area can affect some of the various Indian Ocean island nations and or various countries along Africa\'s east coast.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span Since then, its western boundary has been 90E.<sup id="cite_ref-SWIO_bound_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWIO_bound-30">[30]</a></sup> Tropical activity in this region affects <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. According to the Australian Bureau of Meteorology, the most frequently hit portion of Australia is between <a href="/wiki/Exmouth,_Western_Australia" title="Exmouth, Western Australia">Exmouth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Broome,_Western_Australia" title="Broome, Western Australia">Broome</a> in <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BoM_Climatology_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BoM_Climatology-32">[32]</a></sup> The basin sees an average of about seven cyclones each year, although more can form or come in from other basins, such as the South Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_E10_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_E10-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> The tropical cyclone <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Vance" title="Cyclone Vance">Cyclone Vance</a> in 1999 produced the highest recorded speed winds in an Australian town or city at around 267&#160;km/h (166&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Pacific">South Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span the southwestern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> between 1980 and 2005</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The South Pacific Ocean basin runs between 160\xc2\xb0E and 120\xc2\xb0W, with tropical cyclones developing in it officially monitored by the <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Fiji Meteorological Service</a> and New Zealand\'s <a href="/wiki/MetService" title="MetService">MetService</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP_2-36">[36]</a></sup> Tropical Cyclones that develop within this basin generally affect countries to the west of the dateline, though during El Ni\xc3\xb1os cyclones have been known to develop to the east of the dateline near French Polynesia. On average the basin sees nine tropical cyclones annually with about half of them becoming severe tropical cyclones.</p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Atlantic_Ocean">South Atlantic Ocean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: South Atlantic Ocean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic tropical cyclone</a></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg/220px-Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg/330px-Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg/440px-Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="5200" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Catarina_27_mar_2004_1630Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</a> is currently the only known tropical cyclone in the South Atlantic Ocean to have actually reached hurricane intensity.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Cyclones rarely form in other tropical ocean areas, which are not formally considered tropical cyclone basins. Tropical depressions and tropical storms occur occasionally in the <a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="South Atlantic">South Atlantic</a>, and the only full-blown tropical cyclones on record were 2004\'s <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</a>, which made landfall in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and 2010\'s Tropical Storm Anita, which formed off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a>. The South Atlantic Ocean is not officially classified as a tropical cyclone basin by the World Meteorological Organization and does not have a designated <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">regional specialized meteorological center (RSMC)</a>. However, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy</a> Hydrographic Center has started in 2011 to assign names to tropical and subtropical systems in this basin, when they have sustained wind speeds of at least 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph).</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_tropical_cyclones" title="List of tropical cyclones">List of tropical cyclones</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_cyclone_basins&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
