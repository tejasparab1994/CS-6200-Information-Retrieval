srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Sturmfront_auf_Doppler-Radar-Schirm.jpg/525px-Sturmfront_auf_Doppler-Radar-Schirm.jpg 1.5x, class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sturmfront_auf_Doppler-Radar-Schirm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nBand of thunderstorms seen on a <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> display</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>rainband</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> structure associated with an area of rainfall which is significantly elongated. Rainbands can be <a href="/wiki/Stratiform" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratiform">stratiform</a> or <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">convective</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and are generated by differences in temperature. When noted on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> imagery, this precipitation elongation is referred to as banded structure.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Rainbands within tropical cyclones are curved in orientation. Tropical cyclone rainbands contain showers and thunderstorms that, together with the eyewall and the eye, constitute a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane or tropical storm</a>. The extent of rainbands around a tropical cyclone can help determine the cyclone\'s intensity.</p>\n<p>Rainbands spawned near and ahead of <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a> can be <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> which are able to produce <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>. Rainbands associated with cold fronts can be warped by mountain barriers perpendicular to the front\'s orientation due to the formation of a low-level <a href="/wiki/Barrier_jet" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrier jet">barrier jet</a>. Bands of thunderstorms can form with <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_breeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Land breeze">land breeze</a> boundaries, if enough moisture is present. If sea breeze rainbands become active enough just ahead of a cold front, they can mask the location of the cold front itself. Banding within the comma head precipitation pattern of an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> can yield significant amounts of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>. Behind extratropical cyclones, rainbands can form downwind of relative warm bodies of water such as the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a>. If the atmosphere is cold enough, these rainbands can yield heavy snow.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Feb242007_blizzard.gif" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Feb242007_blizzard.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA February 24, 2007 radar image of a large extratropical cyclonic storm system at its peak over the central United States. Note the band of thunderstorms along its trailing cold front.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">Extratropical cyclone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake_effect_snow" class="mw-redirect" title="Lake effect snow">Lake effect snow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">Squall line</a></div>\n<p>Rainbands in advance of warm <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded fronts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm fronts</a> are associated with weak upward motion,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and tend to be wide and stratiform in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> In an atmosphere with rich low level moisture and vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> narrow, convective rainbands known as <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall lines</a> generally in the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>\'s warm sector, ahead of strong cold fronts associated with extratropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Wider rain bands can occur behind cold fronts, which tend to have more stratiform, and less convective, precipitation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Within the cold sector north to northwest of a cyclone center, in colder cyclones, <a href="/wiki/Microscale_meteorology" title="Microscale meteorology">small scale</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a>, bands of heavy snow can occur within a cyclone\'s comma head precipitation pattern with a width of 32 kilometres (20&#160;mi) to 80 kilometres (50&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> These bands in the comma head are associated with areas of frontogensis, or zones of strengthening temperature contrast.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Southwest of extratropical cyclones, curved flow bringing cold air across the relatively warm <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> can lead to narrow lake effect snow bands which bring significant localized snowfall.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_cyclones">Tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg/350px-Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg" width="350" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg/525px-Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg/700px-Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2160" data-file-height="1440" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Isidore091902-p3sunset.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nPhotograph of rainbands in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Isidore" title="Hurricane Isidore">Hurricane Isidore</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></div>\n<p>Rainbands exist in the periphery of tropical cyclones, which point towards the cyclone\'s center of <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low pressure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Rainbands within tropical cyclones require ample moisture and a low level pool of cooler air.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Bands located 80 kilometres (50&#160;mi) to 150 kilometres (93&#160;mi) from a cyclone\'s center migrate outward.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> They are capable of producing heavy rains and <a href="/wiki/Squall" title="Squall">squalls</a> of wind, as well as tornadoes,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> particularly in the storm\'s right-front quadrant.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Some rainbands move closer to the center, forming a secondary, or outer, <a href="/wiki/Eyewall" class="mw-redirect" title="Eyewall">eyewall</a> within intense hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Spiral rainbands are such a basic structure to a tropical cyclone that in most <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>, use of the satellite-based <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> is the primary method used to determine a tropical cyclone\'s <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">maximum sustained winds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODT_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODT-17">[17]</a></sup> Within this method, the extent of spiral banding and difference in temperature between the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and eyewall is used to assign a maximum sustained wind and a central pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">Central pressure</a> values for their centers of <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure">low pressure</a> derived from this technique are approximate.</p>\n<p>Different programs have been studying these rainbands, including <a href="/wiki/The_Hurricane_Rainband_and_Intensity_Change_Experiment" title="The Hurricane Rainband and Intensity Change Experiment">RAINEX</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forced_by_geography">Forced by geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rainband&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forced by geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Convective rainbands can form parallel to terrain on its <a href="/wiki/Windward" class="mw-redirect" title="Windward">windward</a> side, due to <a href="/wiki/Lee_wave" title="Lee wave">lee waves</a> triggered by hills just upstream of the cloud\'s formation.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Their spacing is normally 5 kilometres (3.1&#160;mi) to 10 kilometres (6.2&#160;mi) apart.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> When bands of precipitation near frontal zones approach steep topography, a low-level barrier <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> forms parallel to and just prior to the mountain ridge, which slows down the frontal rainband just prior to the mountain barrier.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> If enough moisture is present, <a href="/wiki/Sea_breeze" title="Sea breeze">sea breeze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_breeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Land breeze">land breeze</a> fronts can form convective rainbands. Sea breeze front <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> lines can become strong enough to mask the location of an approaching cold front by evening.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The edge of <a href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" class="mw-redirect" title="Ocean currents">ocean currents</a> can lead to the development of thunderstorm bands due to heat differential at this interface.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Downwind of islands, bands of showers and thunderstorms can develop due to low level wind convergence downwind of the island edges.
