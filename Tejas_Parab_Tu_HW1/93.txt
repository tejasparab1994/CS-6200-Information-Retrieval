href="/w/index.php?title=Saturated_fluid&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Saturated fluid">Saturated fluid</a>)</span></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">\n\t\t\t\t\tJump to:\t\t\t\t\t<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, \t\t\t\t\t<a href="#p-search">search</a>\n\t\t\t\t</div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineering" title="Chemical engineering">chemical engineering</a>, the <b>vapor\xe2\x80\x93liquid equilibrium</b> (VLE) describes the distribution of a <a href="/wiki/Chemical_species" title="Chemical species">chemical species</a> between the gas phase and a liquid phase.</p>\n<p>The concentration of a vapor in contact with its liquid, especially at equilibrium, is often expressed in terms of <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a>, which will be a <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">partial pressure</a> (a part of the total gas pressure) if any other gas(es) are present with the vapor. The equilibrium vapor pressure of a liquid is in general strongly dependent on temperature. At vapor\xe2\x80\x93liquid equilibrium, a liquid with individual components in certain concentrations will have an equilibrium vapor in which the concentrations or partial pressures of the vapor components have certain values depending on all of the liquid component concentrations and the temperature. The converse is also true: if a vapor with components at certain concentrations or partial pressures is in vapor\xe2\x80\x93liquid equilibrium with its liquid, then the component concentrations in the liquid will be determined dependent on the vapor concentrations and on the temperature. The equilibrium concentration of each component in the liquid phase is often different from its concentration (or vapor pressure) in the vapor phase, but there is a relationship. The VLE concentration data can be determined experimentally, or computed or approximated with the help of theories such as <a href="/wiki/Raoult%27s_law" title="Raoult\'s law">Raoult\'s law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dalton%27s_law" title="Dalton\'s law">Dalton\'s law</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Henry%27s_law" title="Henry\'s law">Henry\'s law</a>.</p>\n<p>Such vapor\xe2\x80\x93liquid equilibrium information is useful in designing <a href="/wiki/Fractionating_column" title="Fractionating column">columns</a> for <a href="/wiki/Distillation" title="Distillation">distillation</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Fractional_distillation" title="Fractional distillation">fractional distillation</a>, which is a particular specialty of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineer" title="Chemical engineer">chemical engineers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kister_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kister-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Perry_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perry-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SeaderHenley_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SeaderHenley-3">[3]</a></sup> Distillation is a process used to separate or partially separate components in a mixture by boiling (vaporization) followed by <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">condensation</a>. Distillation takes advantage of differences in concentrations of components in the liquid and vapor phases.</p>\n<p>In mixtures containing two or more components, the concentrations of each component are often expressed as <a href="/wiki/Mole_fraction" title="Mole fraction">mole fractions</a>. The mole fraction of a given component of a mixture in a particular phase (either the vapor or the liquid phase) is the number of <a href="/wiki/Mole_(unit)" title="Mole (unit)">moles</a> of that component in that <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">phase</a> divided by the total number of moles of all components in that phase.</p>\n<p>Binary mixtures are those having two components. Three-component mixtures are called ternary mixtures. There can be VLE data for mixtures with even more components, but such data is often hard to show graphically. VLE data is a function of the total pressure, such as 1&#160;<a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atm</a> or whatever pressure the process is conducted at.</p>\n<p>When a temperature is reached such that the sum of the equilibrium vapor pressures of the liquid components becomes equal to the total pressure of the system (it is otherwise smaller), then vapor bubbles generated from the liquid begin to displace the gas that was maintaining the overall pressure, and the mixture is said to <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boil</a>. This temperature is called the <i>boiling point</i> of the liquid mixture at the given pressure. (It is assumed that the total pressure is held steady by adjusting the total volume of the system to accommodate the specific volume changes that accompany boiling.) The boiling point at an overall pressure of 1&#160;atm is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Normal_boiling_point" class="mw-redirect" title="Normal boiling point">normal boiling point</a></i> of the liquid mixture.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Binary mixture VLE data at a certain overall pressure, such as 1&#160;atm, showing mole fraction vapor and liquid concentrations when boiling at various temperatures can be shown as a two-dimensional <a href="/wiki/Graphics" title="Graphics">graph</a> called a <b>boiling-point diagram</b>. The mole fraction of component&#160;1 in the mixture can be represented by the symbol <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span>. The mole fraction of component&#160;2, represented by <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>2</sub></span>, is related to <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span> in a binary mixture as follows:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>x</i><sub>2</sub> = 1</span></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>In multi-component mixtures in general with n components, this becomes:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>x</i><sub>2</sub> + ... + <i>x</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> = 1</span></dd>\n</dl>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:393px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg/391px-Binary_Boiling_Point_Diagram_new.svg.png" width="391" height="378" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="391" data-file-height="378" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">Boiling-point diagram</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The preceding equilibrium equations are typically applied for each phase (liquid or vapor) individually, but the result can be plotted in a single diagram. In a binary boiling-point diagram, temperature&#160;(<span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T\xe2\x80\x89</span>) is graphed vs. <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span>. At any given temperature where both phases are present, vapor with a certain mole fraction is in equilibrium with liquid with a certain mole fraction. The two mole fractions often differ. These vapor and liquid mole fractions are represented by two points on the same horizontal isotherm (constant <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">T\xe2\x80\x89</span>) line. When an entire range of temperatures vs. vapor and liquid mole fractions is graphed, two (usually curved) lines result. The lower one, representing the mole fraction of the boiling liquid at various temperatures, is called the <i><a href="/wiki/Bubble_point" title="Bubble point">bubble point</a> curve</i>. The upper one, representing the mole fraction of the vapor at various temperatures, is called the <i>dew point curve</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kister_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kister-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>These two curves necessarily meet where the mixture becomes purely one component, namely where <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 0</span> (and <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>2</sub> = 1</span>, pure component&#160;2) or <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 1</span> (and <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>2</sub> = 0</span>, pure component&#160;1). The temperatures at those two points correspond to the boiling points of each of the two pure components.</p>\n<p>For certain pairs of substances, the two curves also coincide at some point strictly between <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 0</span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 1</span>. When they meet, they meet tangently; the dew-point temperature always lies above the boiling-point temperature for a given composition when they are not equal. The meeting point is called an <b><a href="/wiki/Azeotrope" title="Azeotrope">azeotrope</a></b> for that particular pair of substances. It is characterized by an azeotrope temperature and an azeotropic composition, often expressed as a mole fraction. There can be <b>maximum-boiling azeotropes</b>, where the azeotrope temperature is at a maximum in the boiling curves, or <b>minimum-boiling azeotropes</b>, where the azeotrope temperature is at a minimum in the boiling curves.</p>\n<p>If one wants to represent a VLE data for a three-component mixture as a boiling point "diagram", a <a href="/wiki/Graph_of_a_function_of_two_variables" class="mw-redirect" title="Graph of a function of two variables">three-dimensional graph</a> can be used. Two of the dimensions would be used to represent the composition mole fractions, and the third dimension would be the temperature. Using two dimensions, the composition can be represented as an equilateral triangle in which each corner represents one of the pure components. The edges of the triangle represent a mixture of the two components at each end of the edge. Any point inside the triangle represents the composition of a mixture of all three components. The mole fraction of each component would correspond to where a point lies along a line starting at that component\'s corner and perpendicular to the opposite edge. The <a href="/wiki/Bubble_point" title="Bubble point">bubble point</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> data would become curved surfaces inside a triangular prism, which connect the three boiling points on the vertical temperature "axes". Each face of this triangular prism would represent a two-dimensional boiling-point diagram for the corresponding binary mixture. Due to their three-dimensional complexity, such boiling-point diagrams are rarely seen. Alternatively, the three-dimensional curved surfaces can be represented on a two-dimensional graph by the use of curved isotherm lines at graduated intervals, similar to iso-altitude lines on a map. Two sets of such isotherm lines are needed on such a two-dimensional graph: one set for the bubble point surface and another set for the dew point surface.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" Such a diagram would graph liquid mole fraction on a horizontal axis and vapor mole fraction on a vertical axis. In such VLE diagrams, liquid mole fractions for components 1 and 2 can be represented as <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>2</sub></span> respectively, and vapor mole fractions of the corresponding components are commonly represented as <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i><sub>1</sub></span> and <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i><sub>2</sub></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-Perry_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Perry-2">[2]</a></sup> Similarly for binary mixtures in these VLE diagrams:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>x</i><sub>2</sub> = 1</span> and&#160; <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>y</i><sub>2</sub> = 1</span></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Such VLE diagrams are square with a diagonal line running from the (<span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 0, <i>y</i><sub>1</sub> = 0</span>) corner to the (<span class="texhtml"><i>x</i><sub>1</sub> = 1, <i>y</i><sub>1</sub> = 1</span>) corner for reference.</p>\n<p>These types of VLE diagrams are used in the <a href="/wiki/McCabe%E2%80%93Thiele_method" title="McCabe\xe2\x80\x93Thiele method">McCabe\xe2\x80\x93Thiele method</a> to determine the number of equilibrium stages (or <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_plate" title="Theoretical plate">theoretical plates</a>) needed to distill a given composition binary feed mixture into one distillate fraction and one bottoms fraction. Corrections can also be made to take into account the incomplete efficiency of each tray in a distillation column when compared to a theoretical plate.</p>\n<h2><span id="Raoult\'s_law"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Raoult.27s_law">Raoult\'s law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Raoult\'s law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Raoult%27s_law" title="Raoult\'s law">Raoult\'s law</a></div>\n<p>At boiling and higher temperatures the sum of the individual component partial pressures becomes equal to the overall pressure, which can symbolized as <i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>.</p>\n<p>Under such conditions, <a href="/wiki/Dalton%27s_law" title="Dalton\'s law">Dalton\'s law</a> would be in effect as follows:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>P</i><sub>tot</sub> = <i>P</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>P</i><sub>2</sub> + ...</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Then for each component in the vapor phase:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>y</i><sub>1</sub> = <i>P</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>,&#160;&#160; <i>y</i><sub>2</sub> = <i>P</i><sub>2</sub>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>, ... etc.</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>where <i>P</i><sub>1</sub> = partial pressure of component 1, <i>P</i><sub>2</sub> = partial pressure of component 2, etc.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Raoult%27s_law" title="Raoult\'s law">Raoult\'s law</a> is approximately valid for mixtures of components between which there is very little interaction other than the effect of dilution by the other components. Examples of such mixtures includes mixtures of <a href="/wiki/Alkane" title="Alkane">alkanes</a>, which are non-<a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">polar</a>, relatively inert <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">compounds</a> in many ways, so there is little attraction or repulsion between the molecules. Raoult\'s law states that for components 1, 2, etc. in a mixture:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>P</i><sub>1</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub>,&#160;&#160; <i>P</i><sub>2</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub>, ... etc.</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>where <i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub>, P<sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub>, etc. are the vapor pressures of components 1, 2, etc. when they are pure, and <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>, <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>, etc. are mole fractions of the corresponding component in the liquid.</p>\n<p>Recall from the first section that vapor pressures of liquids are very dependent on temperature. Thus the <i>P</i><sup>o</sup> pure vapor pressures for each component are a function of temperature (<i>T</i>): For example, commonly for a pure liquid component, the <a href="/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation" title="Clausius\xe2\x80\x93Clapeyron relation">Clausius\xe2\x80\x93Clapeyron relation</a> may be used to approximate how the vapor pressure varies as a function of temperature. This makes each of the partial pressures dependent on temperature also regardless of whether Raoult\'s law applies or not. When Raoult\'s law is valid these expressions become:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>P</i><sub>1</sub><i>T</i> = <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub><i>T</i>,&#160;&#160; <i>P</i><sub>2</sub><i>T</i> = <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub><i>T</i>, ... etc.</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>At boiling temperatures if Raoult\'s law applies, the total pressure becomes:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>P</i><sub>tot</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub><i>T</i> + <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub><i>T</i> + ... etc.</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>At a given <i>P</i><sub>tot</sub> such as 1&#160;atm and a given liquid composition, <i>T</i> can be solved for to give the liquid mixture\'s boiling point or bubble point, although the solution for <i>T</i> may not be mathematically analytical (i.e., may require a numerical solution or approximation). For a binary mixture at a given <i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>, the bubble point&#160;<i>T</i> can become a function of <i>x</i><sub>1</sub> (or <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>) and this function can be shown on a two-dimensional graph like a binary boiling point diagram.</p>\n<p>At boiling temperatures if Raoult\'s law applies, a number of the preceding equations in this section can be combined to give the following expressions for vapor mole fractions as a function of liquid mole fractions and temperature:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>y</i><sub>1</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub><i>T</i>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>,&#160;&#160; <i>y</i><sub>2</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>2</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub><i>T</i>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>, ... etc.</dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Once the bubble point <i>T\'</i>s as a function of liquid composition in terms of mole fractions have been determined, these values can be inserted into the above equations to obtain corresponding vapor compositions in terms of mole fractions. When this is finished over a complete range of liquid mole fractions and their corresponding temperatures, one effectively obtains a temperature&#160; <i>T</i> function of vapor composition mole fractions. This function effectively acts as the dew point <i>T</i> function of vapor composition.</p>\n<p>In the case of a binary mixture, <i>x</i><sub>2</sub> = 1 \xe2\x88\x92 <i>x</i><sub>1</sub> and the above equations can be expressed as:</p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>y</i><sub>1</sub> = <i>x</i><sub>1</sub>\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>1</sub><i>T</i>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub>, and</dd>\n<dd><i>y</i><sub>2</sub> = (1 \xe2\x80\x89\xe2\x88\x92 \xe2\x80\x89<i>x</i><sub>1</sub>)\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sup>\xe2\x80\x89o</sup><sub>2</sub><i>T</i>\xe2\x80\x89/\xe2\x80\x89<i>P</i><sub>tot</sub></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>For many kinds of mixtures, particularly where there is interaction between components beyond simply the effects of dilution, Raoult\'s law does not work well for determining the shapes of the curves in the boiling point or VLE diagrams. Even in such mixtures, there are usually still differences in the vapor and liquid equilibrium concentrations at most points, and distillation is often still useful for separating components at least partially. For such mixtures, empirical data is typically used in determining such boiling point and VLE diagrams. <a href="/wiki/Chemical_engineer" title="Chemical engineer">Chemical engineers</a> have done a significant amount of research trying to develop equations for correlating and/or predicting VLE data for various kinds of mixtures which do not obey Raoult\'s law well.</p>\n<h2><span
