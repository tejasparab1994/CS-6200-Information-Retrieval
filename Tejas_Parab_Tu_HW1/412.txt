class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">\n\t\t\t\t\tJump to:\t\t\t\t\t<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, \t\t\t\t\t<a href="#p-search">search</a>\n\t\t\t\t</div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>hypercane</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Hypothetical" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypothetical">hypothetical</a> class of extreme <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that could form if ocean temperatures reached 50&#160;\xc2\xb0C (122&#160;\xc2\xb0F), which is 15&#160;\xc2\xb0C (27&#160;\xc2\xb0F) warmer than the warmest ocean temperature ever recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Such an increase could be caused by a large <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">comet</a> impact, a large <a href="/wiki/Supervolcano" title="Supervolcano">supervolcanic</a> eruption, or extensive <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> There is some speculation that a series of hypercanes resulting from an <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact</a> by a large <a href="/wiki/Chicxulub_impactor" title="Chicxulub impactor">asteroid or comet</a> contributed to the <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous\xe2\x80\x93Paleogene extinction event">demise</a> of the non-avian <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a>. The hypothesis was created by <a href="/wiki/Kerry_Emanuel" title="Kerry Emanuel">Kerry Emanuel</a> of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">MIT</a>, who also coined the term.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MIT_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-extinction_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-extinction-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Description"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Description</span></a></li>\n<li class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoonsizes.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Typhoonsizes.jpg/220px-Typhoonsizes.jpg" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4e/Typhoonsizes.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="288" data-file-height="180" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoonsizes.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe relative sizes of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Tracy" title="Cyclone Tracy">Cyclone Tracy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In order to form a hypercane, according to Emanuel\'s hypothetical model, the ocean temperature would have to be at least 49&#160;\xc2\xb0C (120&#160;\xc2\xb0F). A critical difference between a hypercane and present-day hurricanes, is that a hypercane would extend into the upper <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, whereas present-day hurricanes extend into only the lower stratosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Hypercanes would have wind speeds of over 800&#160;km/h (500&#160;mph), and would also have a central pressure of less than 70 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kilopascals</a> (21&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) (700 millibars), giving them an enormous lifespan.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-4">[4]</a></sup> For comparison, the largest and most intense storm on record was 1979\'s <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a>, with a wind speed of 305 kilometres per hour (190&#160;mph) and central pressure of 87 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">kilopascals</a> (26&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) (870 millibars). Such a storm would be eight times more powerful than the strongest storms yet recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The extreme conditions needed to create a hypercane could conceivably produce a system up to the size of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, creating <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">storm surges</a> of 18&#160;m (59&#160;ft) and an eye nearly 300&#160;km (190&#160;mi) across.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The waters could remain hot enough for weeks, allowing more hypercanes to be formed. A hypercane\'s clouds would reach 30&#160;km (19&#160;mi) into the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>. Such an intense storm would also damage the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MIT_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MIT-4">[4]</a></sup> Water molecules in the stratosphere would react with <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> to accelerate decay into O<sub>2</sub> and reduce absorption of <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanism">Mechanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypercane&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mechanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A hurricane functions as a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">Carnot heat engine</a> powered by the temperature difference between the sea and the uppermost layer of the troposphere. As air is drawn in towards the eye it acquires <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> from evaporating sea-water, which is then released as <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> during the rise inside the eyewall and radiated away at the top of the storm system. The energy input is balanced by energy dissipation in a turbulent <a href="/wiki/Boundary_layer" title="Boundary layer">boundary layer</a> close to the surface, which leads to an energy balance equilibrium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>However, in Emanuel\'s model, if the temperature difference between the sea and the top of the troposphere is too large, there is no solution to the equilibrium equation. As more air is drawn in, the released heat reduces the central pressure further. The lower the pressure the more heat is drawn in, leading to a runaway process. The actual limit to hypercane intensity depends on other energy dissipation factors that are uncertain: whether inflow ceases to be <a href="/wiki/Isothermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Isothermal">isothermal</a>, whether <a href="/wiki/Shock_waves" class="mw-redirect" title="Shock waves">shock waves</a> would form in the outflow around the eye, or whether turbulent breakdown of the vortex happens.<sup id="cite_ref-extinction_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-extinction-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
