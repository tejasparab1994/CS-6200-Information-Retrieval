class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">fluid dynamics</a>, <b>wave setup</b> is the increase in <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">mean</a> water level due to the presence of breaking <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">waves</a>. Similarly, <b>wave setdown</b> is a wave-induced decrease of the mean water level before the waves break (during the shoaling process). For short, the whole phenomenon is often denoted as wave setup, including both increase and decrease of mean elevation. This setup is primarily present in and near the coastal <a href="/wiki/Surf_zone" title="Surf zone">surf zone</a>. Besides a spatial variation in the (mean) wave setup, also a variation in time may be present \xe2\x80\x93 known as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Surf_beat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Surf beat (page does not exist)">surf beat</a> \xe2\x80\x93 causing <a href="/wiki/Infragravity_wave" title="Infragravity wave">infragravity wave</a> <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">radiation</a>.</p>\n<p>Wave setup can be mathematically modeled by considering the variation in <a href="/wiki/Radiation_stress" title="Radiation stress">radiation stress</a> (<a href="#CITEREFLonguet-HigginsStewart1962">Longuet-Higgins &amp; Stewart 1962</a>). Radiation stress is the <a href="/wiki/Tensor" title="Tensor">tensor</a> of excess horizontal-<a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">momentum</a> <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">fluxes</a> due to the presence of the waves.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_and_near_the_coastal_surf_zone">In and near the coastal surf zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_setup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In and near the coastal surf zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As a progressive wave approaches shore and the water depth decreases, the wave height increases due to <a href="/wiki/Wave_shoaling" title="Wave shoaling">wave shoaling</a>. As a result, there is additional wave-induced flux of horizontal momentum. The horizontal momentum equations of the mean flow requires this additional wave-induced flux to be balanced: this causes a decrease in the mean water level before the waves break, called a "setdown".</p>\n<p>After the waves <a href="/wiki/Breaking_wave" title="Breaking wave">break</a>, the wave energy flux is no longer constant, but decreasing due to energy dissipation. The radiation stress therefore decreases after the break point, causing a free surface level increase to balance: wave setup. Both of the above descriptions are specifically for beaches with mild bed slope.</p>\n<p>Wave setup is particularly of concern during storm events, when the effects of big waves generated by wind from the storm are able to increase the mean sea level (by wave setup), enhancing the risks of damage to coastal infrastructure.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wave_setup&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><cite id="CITEREFLonguet-HigginsStewart1962" class="citation"><a href="/wiki/Michael_S._Longuet-Higgins" title="Michael S.
