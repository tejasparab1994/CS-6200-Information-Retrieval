For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Typhoon (disambiguation)">Typhoon (disambiguation)</a>.</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other areas in which tropical cyclones occur, see <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Tropical cyclone basins</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg/220px-Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg" width="220" height="275" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg/330px-Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg/440px-Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7200" data-file-height="9000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_saomai_060807.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThree different tropical cyclones spinning over the western Pacific Ocean on August 7, 2006 (Maria, Bopha, Saomai.). The cyclone on the lower right has intensified into a typhoon.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>typhoon</b> is a mature <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that develops between <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">180\xc2\xb0</a> and <a href="/wiki/100th_meridian_east" title="100th meridian east">100\xc2\xb0E</a>. This region is referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins#Northwestern_Pacific_Ocean" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Northwestern Pacific Basin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and is the most active tropical cyclone basin on <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, accounting for almost one-third of the world\'s annual tropical cyclones. For organizational purposes, the northern Pacific Ocean is divided into three regions: the eastern (North America to <a href="/wiki/140th_meridian_west" title="140th meridian west">140\xc2\xb0W</a>), central (140\xc2\xb0 to 180\xc2\xb0W), and western (180\xc2\xb0 to 100\xc2\xb0E). The <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> (RSMC) for tropical cyclone forecasts is in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, with other tropical cyclone warning centers for the northwest Pacific in <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>. While the RSMC names each system, the main name list itself is coordinated among 18 countries that have territories threatened by typhoons each year. Only the Philippines use their own naming list for systems approaching the country<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.</p>\n<p>A typhoon differs from a cyclone or hurricane only on the basis of location.<sup id="cite_ref-HCT_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-2">[2]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricane</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Storm" title="Storm">storm</a> that occurs in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>, a typhoon occurs in the northwestern Pacific Ocean, and a <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a> occurs in the south Pacific or <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HCT_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HCT-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Within the northwestern Pacific there are no official typhoon seasons as tropical cyclones form throughout the year. Like any tropical cyclone, there are six main requirements for typhoon formation and development: sufficiently warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures</a>, atmospheric instability, high <a href="/wiki/Relative_humidity" title="Relative humidity">humidity</a> in the lower to middle levels of the troposphere, enough <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis force</a> to develop a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low pressure center</a>, a pre-existing low level focus or disturbance, and low vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. While the majority of storms form between June and November, a few storms do occur between December and May (although tropical cyclone formation is at a minimum during that time). On average, the northwestern Pacific features the most numerous and intense tropical cyclones globally. Like other basins, they are steered by the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> towards the west or northwest, with some systems recurving near and east of <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>. The Philippines receive the brunt of the landfalls, with <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and Japan being impacted slightly less. Some of the deadliest typhoons in history have struck China. Southern China has the longest record of typhoon impacts for the region, with a thousand-year sample via documents within their archives. <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> has received the wettest known typhoon on record for the northwest Pacific <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclone basin">tropical cyclone basins</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_usage">Etymology and usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The term <i>typhoon</i> is the regional name in the northwest Pacific for a severe (or mature) <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> whereas <i>hurricane</i> is the regional term in the northeast Pacific and northern Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Elsewhere this is called a <i>tropical cyclone</i>, <i>severe tropical cyclone</i>, or <i>severe cyclonic storm</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-oed_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup> cites <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> <i>\xe1\xb9\xad\xc5\xabf\xc4\x81n</i> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> <i>tai fung</i> giving rise to several early forms in English. The earliest forms -- "touffon", later "tufan", "tuffon", and others -- derive from Urdu <i>\xe1\xb9\xad\xc5\xabf\xc4\x81n</i>, with citations as early as 1588. From 1699 appears "tuffoon", later "tiffoon", derived from Chinese with spelling influenced by the older Urdu-derived forms. The modern spelling "typhoon" dates to 1820, preceded by "tay-fun" in 1771 and "ty-foong", all derived from the Chinese <i>tai fung</i>.</p>\n<p>The Urdu source word \xd8\xaa\xd9\x88\xd9\x81\xd8\xa7\xd9\x86 <i>\xe1\xb9\xad\xc5\xabf\xc4\x81n</i> ("violent storm"; cognate to <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> \xe0\xa4\xa4\xe0\xa5\x82\xe0\xa4\xab\xe0\xa4\xbc\xe0\xa4\xbe\xe0\xa4\xa8 (<i>t\xc5\xabf\xc4\x81n</i>))<sup id="cite_ref-online_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> comes via Persian from <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> \xd8\xb7\xd9\x88\xd9\x81\xd8\xa7\xd9\x86 (<i>\xe1\xb9\xad\xc5\xabf\xc4\x81n</i>), which may derive from the verb <i>t\xc5\xabf\xc4\xabdan</i> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" xml:lang="fa">\xd8\xaa\xd9\x88\xd9\x81\xdb\x8c\xd8\xaf\xd9\x86/\xd8\xb7\xd9\x88\xd9\x81\xdb\x8c\xd8\xaf\xd9\x86</span>\xe2\x80\x8e\xe2\x80\x8e, "to roar, to blow furiously")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> or Arabic <i>\xe1\xb9\xad\xc4\x81fa</i>, to turn round.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Chinese source is the word <i>tai fung</i><sup id="cite_ref-online_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%B0%E9%A3%8E" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe5\x8f\xb0\xe9\xa3\x8e">\xe5\x8f\xb0\xe9\xa3\x8e</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>: <span lang="zh-Hant" xml:lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A2%B1%E9%A2%A8" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe9\xa2\xb1\xe9\xa2\xa8">\xe9\xa2\xb1\xe9\xa2\xa8</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>: <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">t\xc3\xa1if\xc4\x93ng</span></i>), cited as a common dialect form of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin</a> <i>d\xc3\xa0</i> "big" and <i>f\xc4\x93ng</i> "wind".<sup id="cite_ref-oed_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup> In Mandarin the word for the windstorm is <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7%E9%A3%8E" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe5\xa4\xa7\xe9\xa3\x8e">\xe5\xa4\xa7\xe9\xa3\x8e</a> (<i>d\xc3\xa0f\xc4\x93ng</i>, "big wind") and in <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7%E9%A2%A8" class="extiw" title="wikt:\xe5\xa4\xa7\xe9\xa2\xa8">\xe5\xa4\xa7\xe9\xa2\xa8</a> (<i>daai<sup>6</sup> fung<sup>1</sup></i>, "big wind"). The modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> word, \xe5\x8f\xb0\xe9\xa2\xa8 (\xe3\x81\x9f\xe3\x81\x84\xe3\x81\xb5\xe3\x81\x86, <i>taifuu</i>), is also derived from Chinese. The first character is normally used to mean "pedestal" or "stand", but is actually a simplification of the older <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> \xe9\xa2\xb1, which means "typhoon"; thus the word originally meant "typhoon wind".</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Ancient Greek</a> \xce\xa4\xcf\x85\xcf\x86\xe1\xbf\xb6\xce\xbd (<i>Tuph\xc3\xb4n</i>, <a href="/wiki/Typhon" title="Typhon">Typhon</a>) is not unrelated and has secondarily contaminated the word.<sup id="cite_ref-online_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-online-7">[7]</a></sup> The Persian and Chinese terms may have been originated by the Greek word in the first place.<sup id="cite_ref-oed_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oed-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intensity_classifications">Intensity classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> uses and is the term used for a tropical system that has wind speeds not exceeding 33 knots (38&#160;mph; 61&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> A tropical depression is upgraded to a <i>tropical storm</i> should its <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained wind speeds</a> exceed 34 knots (39&#160;mph; 63&#160;km/h). Tropical storms also receive official names from RSMC Tokyo.<sup id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> Should the storm intensify further and reach sustained wind speeds of 48 knots (55&#160;mph; 89&#160;km/h) then it will be classified as a <i>severe tropical storm</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup> Once the system\'s maximum sustained winds reach wind speeds of 64 knots (74&#160;mph; 119&#160;km/h), the JMA will designate the tropical cyclone as a <i>typhoon</i>\xe2\x80\x94the highest category on its scale.<sup id="cite_ref-TCOM_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCOM-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>From 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Observatory" title="Hong Kong Observatory">Hong Kong Observatory</a> started to further divide typhoons into three different classifications: <i>typhoon</i>, <i>severe typhoon</i> and <i>super typhoon</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HKO_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">[10]</a></sup> A <i>typhoon</i> has wind speed of 64-79 knots (73-91&#160;mph; 118\xe2\x80\x93149&#160;km/h), a severe typhoon has winds of at least 80 knots (92&#160;mph; 150&#160;km/h), and a super typhoon has winds of at least 100 knots (120&#160;mph; 190&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-HKO_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HKO-10">[10]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>\' <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) unofficially classifies typhoons with wind speeds of at least 130&#160;knots (67&#160;m/s; 150&#160;mph; 241&#160;km/h)\xe2\x80\x94the equivalent of a strong Category&#160;4 storm in the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson scale</a>\xe2\x80\x94as <i>super typhoons</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> However, the maximum sustained wind speed measurements that the JTWC uses are based on a 1-minute averaging period, akin to the U.S.\' <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a>. As a result, the JTWC\'s wind reports are higher than JMA\'s measurements, as the latter are based on a 10-minute averaging interval.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_FAQ_fcstdiff_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_FAQ_fcstdiff-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genesis">Genesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Depth26Cisotherm.gif" class="image"><img alt="" class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Depth26Cisotherm.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDepth of 26&#160;\xc2\xb0C <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">isotherm</a> on October 1, 2006</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></div>\n<p>There are six main requirements for tropical cyclogenesis: sufficiently warm sea surface temperatures, atmospheric instability, high <a href="/wiki/Humidity" title="Humidity">humidity</a> in the lower to middle levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, enough <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> to develop a low pressure center, a pre-existing low level focus or disturbance, and low vertical wind shear. While these conditions are necessary for tropical cyclone formation, <a href="/wiki/Necessary_but_not_sufficient" class="mw-redirect" title="Necessary but not sufficient">they do not guarantee</a> that a tropical cyclone will form. Normally, an ocean temperature of 26.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C (79.7&#160;\xc2\xb0F) spanning through a depth of at least 50 metres (160&#160;ft) is considered the minimum to maintain the special <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> that is the tropical cyclone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These warm waters are needed to maintain the <a href="/wiki/Warm_core" class="mw-redirect" title="Warm core">warm core</a> that fuels tropical systems. A minimum distance of 500&#160;km (300&#160;mi) from the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> is normally needed for tropical cyclogenesis.<sup id="cite_ref-A15_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A15-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Whether it be a depression in the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ) or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a>, a broad <a href="/wiki/Surface_weather_analysis" title="Surface weather analysis">surface front</a>, or an <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundary</a>, a low level feature with sufficient <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> and convergence is required to begin tropical cyclogenesis. About 85 to 90&#160;percent of Pacific typhoons form within the monsoon trough.<sup id="cite_ref-KingTUTT_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KingTUTT-14">[14]</a></sup> Even with perfect upper level conditions and the required atmospheric instability, the lack of a surface focus will prevent the development of organized convection and a surface low. Vertical wind shear of less than 10&#160;m/s (20&#160;kn, 33&#160;ft/s) between the ocean surface and the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a> is required for tropical cyclone development.<sup id="cite_ref-A15_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A15-13">[13]</a></sup> Typically with Pacific typhoons, there are two <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jets</a>: one to the north ahead of an upper trough in the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">Westerlies</a>, and a second towards the equator.<sup id="cite_ref-KingTUTT_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KingTUTT-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In general, westerly wind increases associated with the <a href="/wiki/Madden%E2%80%93Julian_oscillation" title="Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation">Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation</a> lead to increased tropical cyclogenesis in all <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>. As the oscillation propagates from west to east, it leads to an eastward march in tropical cyclogenesis with time during that hemisphere\'s summer season.<sup id="cite_ref-AMS2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMS2-15">[15]</a></sup> On average, twice per year twin tropical cyclones will form in the western Pacific Ocean, near the <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_north" title="5th parallel north">5th parallel north</a> and the <a href="/wiki/5th_parallel_south" title="5th parallel south">5th parallel south</a>, along the same meridian, or line of longitude.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> There is an inverse relationship between tropical cyclone activity in the western Pacific basin and the north Atlantic basin, however. When one basin is active, the other is normally quiet, and vice versa. The main reason for this appears to be the phase of the Madden\xe2\x80\x93Julian oscillation, or MJO, which is normally in opposite modes between the two basins at any given time.<sup id="cite_ref-Mad-Jul_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mad-Jul-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency">Frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<table class="wikitable" style="width:300px; text-align:center; margin:0 1em; float:right;">\n<caption>Storm Frequency<br />\nTropical storms and Typhoons by month,<br />\nfor the period 1959\xe2\x80\x932015 (Northwest Pacific)</caption>\n<tr>\n<th>Month</th>\n<th>Count</th>\n<th>Average</th>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Jan</td>\n<td>28</td>\n<td>0.5</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Feb</td>\n<td>14</td>\n<td>0.2</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Mar</td>\n<td>26</td>\n<td>0.5</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Apr</td>\n<td>37</td>\n<td>0.6</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>May</td>\n<td>66</td>\n<td>1.2</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Jun</td>\n<td>100</td>\n<td>1.8</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Jul</td>\n<td>221</td>\n<td>3.9</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Aug</td>\n<td>310</td>\n<td>5.4</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Sep</td>\n<td>280</td>\n<td>4.9</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Oct</td>\n<td>228</td>\n<td>4.0</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Nov</td>\n<td>139</td>\n<td>2.4</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Dec</td>\n<td>69</td>\n<td>1.2</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td>Annual</td>\n<td>1518</td>\n<td>26.6</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td colspan="3" style="text-align:center;">Source: <b>JTWC</b><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>Nearly one-third of the world\'s tropical cyclones form within the western Pacific. This makes this basin the most active on Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-enso_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enso-19">[19]</a></sup> Pacific typhoons have formed year round, with peak months from August to October. The peak months correspond to that of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane seasons</a>. Along with a high storm frequency, this basin also features the most globally <a href="/wiki/List_of_notable_tropical_cyclones#Major_records" class="mw-redirect" title="List of notable tropical cyclones">intense storms</a> on record. One of the most recent busy seasons was <a href="/wiki/2013_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2013 Pacific typhoon season">2013</a>. Tropical cyclones form in any month of the year across the northwest Pacific Ocean, and concentrate around June and November in the northern Indian Ocean. The area just northeast of the Philippines is the most active place on Earth for tropical cyclones to exist. Across the Philippines themselves, activity reaches a minimum in February, before increasing steadily through June, and spiking from July through October, with September being the most active month for tropical cyclones across the <a href="/wiki/Archipelago" title="Archipelago">archipelago</a>. Activity falls off significantly in November, although <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a>, the strongest Philippine typhoon on record, was a November typhoon.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The most frequently impacted areas of the Philippines by tropical cyclones are northern and central <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> and eastern <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> A ten-year average of satellite determined precipitation showed that at least 30&#160;percent of the annual rainfall in the northern Philippines could be traced to tropical cyclones, while the southern islands receive less than 10&#160;percent of their annual rainfall from tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The genesis and intensity of typhoons are also modulated by slow variation of the sea surface temperature and circulation features following a near-10-year frequency.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a Areas west of Japan and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a> tend to experience many fewer September\xe2\x80\x93November tropical cyclone impacts during El Ni\xc3\xb1o and neutral years. During El Ni\xc3\xb1o years, the break in the subtropical ridge tends to lie near <a href="/wiki/130th_meridian_east" title="130th meridian east">130\xc2\xb0E</a>, which would favor the Japanese archipelago.<sup id="cite_ref-China_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China-25">[25]</a></sup> During La Ni\xc3\xb1a years, the formation of tropical cyclones, and the subtropical ridge position, shift westward across the western Pacific Ocean, which increases the landfall threat to <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and greater intensity to <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-China_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China-25">[25]</a></sup> Those that form near the <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">Marshall Islands</a> find their way to <a href="/wiki/Jeju_Island" title="Jeju Island">Jeju Island</a>, Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Typhoon paths follow three general directions.<sup id="cite_ref-enso_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-enso-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<ul>\n<li>Straight track (or straight runner). entries from 17 Southeast and East Asian nations and the United States who have territories directly affected by typhoons. The submitted names are arranged into five lists, and each list is cycled with each year. Unlike tropical cyclones in other parts of the world, typhoons are not named after people. href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> when 39&#160;storms of tropical storm strength formed. Only 15 seasons had 30 or more storms developing since reliable records began. The least activity seen in the northwest Pacific Ocean was during the <a href="/wiki/2010_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2010 Pacific typhoon season">2010 Pacific typhoon season</a>, when only 14&#160;tropical storms and seven typhoons formed. In the Philippines, the most active season, since 1945, for tropical cyclone strikes was <a href="/wiki/1993_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1993 Pacific typhoon season">1993</a> when nineteen tropical cyclones moved through the country.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> There was only one tropical cyclone that moved through the Philippines in <a href="/wiki/1958_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="1958 Pacific typhoon season">1958</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/2004_Pacific_typhoon_season" title="2004 Pacific typhoon season">2004 Pacific typhoon season</a> was the busiest for Okinawa since 1957.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Within <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> in southern China, during the past thousand years, the most active decades for typhoon strikes were the 1660s and 1670s.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The highest reliably-estimated <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_winds" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximum sustained winds">maximum sustained winds</a> on record for a typhoon were those of <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan_(2013)" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoon Haiyan (2013)">Typhoon Haiyan</a> at 195 miles per hour (314&#160;km/h) shortly before its landfall in the central <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> on Nov. 8, 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> The most intense storm based on minimum pressure was <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Tip" title="Typhoon Tip">Typhoon Tip</a> in the northwestern Pacific Ocean in 1979, which reached a minimum pressure of 870 hectopascals (26&#160;inHg) and maximum sustained wind speeds of 165&#160;knots (85&#160;m/s, 190&#160;mph, 310&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-37">[37]</a></sup> The deadliest typhoon of the 20th century was <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Nina_(1975)" title="Typhoon Nina (1975)">Typhoon Nina</a>, which killed nearly 100,000 in China in 1975 due to a flood that caused 12 reservoirs to fail.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> After <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Morakot" title="Typhoon Morakot">Typhoon Morakot</a> landed in Taiwan at midnight on August 8, 2009, almost the entire southern region of Taiwan (<a href="/wiki/Chiayi_County" title="Chiayi County">Chiayi County</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chiayi_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiayi City">Chiayi City</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tainan_County" title="Tainan County">Tainan County</a>/<a href="/wiki/Tainan_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Tainan City">Tainan City</a> (now merged as Tainan), <a href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_County" title="Kaohsiung County">Kaohsiung County</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kaohsiung_City" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaohsiung City">Kaohsiung City</a> (now merged as Kaohsiung), and <a href="/wiki/Pingtung_County" title="Pingtung County">Pingtung County</a>) and parts of <a href="/wiki/Taitung_County" title="Taitung County">Taitung County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nantou_County" title="Nantou County">Nantou County</a> were flooded by record-breaking heavy rain. The rainfall in <a href="/wiki/Pingtung_County" title="Pingtung County">Pingtung County</a> reached 2,327 millimeters (91.6&#160;in),<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> breaking all rainfall records of any single place in Taiwan induced by a single typhoon,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> and making the cyclone the wettest known typhoon.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
