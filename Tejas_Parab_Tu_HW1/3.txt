(NASA)</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div style="clear:left;"></div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_objectives">Mission objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Expedition_7&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The seventh crew of the International Space Station lifted off in <b><a href="/wiki/Soyuz_TMA-2" title="Soyuz TMA-2">Soyuz TMA-2</a></b> from the Russian Space Agency\'s Baikonur Cosmodrome in Kazakhstan on 25 April 2003, at 05:56:20 UTC. The Soyuz docked on 28 April 2003 and took over command of the ISS. The <i><a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a></i> fleet had been grounded due to the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_disaster" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia disaster"><i>Columbia</i> disaster</a>, so the crew size was reduced to two, as opposed to the three that could be carried by the shuttle. The Expedition Seven crew\xe2\x80\x94along with <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">European Space Agency</a> Astronaut <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Duque" title="Pedro Duque">Pedro Duque</a>\xe2\x80\x94landed back on Earth on 27 October 2003 at Kazakhstan at 02:41:20 UTC, after undocking from the International Space Station in their Soyuz spacecraft at 23:17 UTC.</p>\n<p>Due to the reduced crew size, the scientific work had to be scaled down as well. Only 15 different experiments were conducted during the mission. Malenchenko and Lu were also tasked with periodic maintenance work on the station, as well as spacewalk training (although no spacewalks were planned). Supplies were delivered by <i><a href="/wiki/Progress_M1-10" title="Progress M1-10">Progress M1-10</a></i> in June and <i><a href="/wiki/Progress_M-48" title="Progress M-48">Progress M-48</a></i> in August.</p>\n<p>From Houston, ISS Spacecraft Communicator Mike Fossum informed Expedition 7 Commander Yuri Malenchenko and Science Officer Edward Lu on 15 October 2003 of the successful launch of the <a href="/wiki/Long_March_rocket" class="mw-redirect" title="Long March rocket">Long March rocket</a> carrying the <a href="/wiki/Shenzhou_5" title="Shenzhou 5">Shenzhou 5</a> spacecraft and Chinese <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%A1ng_L%C3%ACw%C4%9Bi" class="mw-redirect" title="Y\xc3\xa1ng L\xc3\xacw\xc4\x9bi">Y\xc3\xa1ng L\xc3\xacw\xc4\x9bi</a>. "It\'s really some exciting news to share. The world\'s spacefaring nations have been joined by a new member tonight: China."</p>\n<p>"First off, we want to congratulate them," Lu replied. "The more people that go into space, the better off we all are. This is a great achievement and good for everyone in the long run." In Chinese, he later added, "Welcome to space. Have a safe journey."</p>\n<p>"I would also like to say I love to have somebody else in space instead of me and Ed," said Malenchenko. "I also know this is great for thousands and thousands of people from China. I congratulate all of them."</p>\n<p>Malenchenko and Lu were previously crewmates on the <a href="/wiki/STS-106" title="STS-106">STS-106</a> shuttle mission and did a spacewalk together during that flight.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
