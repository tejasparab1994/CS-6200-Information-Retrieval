close-knit group of friends beginning in high school and continuing in college that ran from 1998 to 2003. The series stars <a href="/wiki/James_Van_Der_Beek" title="James Van Der Beek">James Van Der Beek</a> as <a href="/wiki/Dawson_Leery" title="Dawson Leery">Dawson Leery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katie_Holmes" title="Katie Holmes">Katie Holmes</a> as his best friend and <a href="/wiki/Romantic_interest" class="mw-redirect" title="Romantic interest">love interest</a> <a href="/wiki/Joey_Potter" title="Joey Potter">Joey Potter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joshua_Jackson" title="Joshua Jackson">Joshua Jackson</a> as their fellow best friend <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pacey_Witter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Pacey Witter (page does not exist)">Pacey Witter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michelle_Williams_(actress)" title="Michelle Williams (actress)">Michelle Williams</a> as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jen_Lindley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jen Lindley (page does not exist)">Jen Lindley</a>, a New York City transplant to the fictional town of Capeside, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, where the series was set. The show was created by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Williamson_(screenwriter)" title="Kevin Williamson (screenwriter)">Kevin Williamson</a> and debuted on <a href="/wiki/The_WB" title="The WB">The WB</a> on January 20, 1998. It was produced by Columbia TriStar Television (renamed <a href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Television" title="Sony Pictures Television">Sony Pictures Television</a> before the sixth and final season) and was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>.</p>\n<p>Part of a new craze for teen-themed movies and television shows in America in the late 1990s, it catapulted its leads to stardom and became a defining show for The WB.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">\'s</span> "New TV Classics" list in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> The series ended on May 14, 2003.<sup Williamson\'s script was initially turned down by Fox, but the WB picked it up for its new Tuesday night lineup.</p>\n<p>Williamson said, "I pitched it as <i><a href="/wiki/Some_Kind_of_Wonderful_(film)" title="Some Kind of Wonderful (film)">Some Kind of Wonderful</a></i>, meets <i><a href="/wiki/Pump_Up_the_Volume_(film)" title="Pump Up the Volume (film)">Pump Up the Volume</a></i>, meets <i><a href="/wiki/James_at_15" title="James at 15">James at 15</a></i>, meets <i><a href="/wiki/My_So-Called_Life" title="My So-Called Life">My So-Called Life</a></i>, meets <i><a href="/wiki/Little_House_on_the_Prairie_(TV_series)" title="Little House on the Prairie (TV series)">Little House on the Prairie</a></i>".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The show\'s lead character, <a href="/wiki/Dawson_Leery" title="Dawson Leery">Dawson Leery</a>, was based on Williamson himself: obsessed with movies and platonically sharing his bed with the girl down the creek.</p>\n<p>The entire first season, thirteen episodes, was filmed before the first episode even aired.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After the end of the second season, Williamson left to focus on <i><a href="/wiki/Wasteland_(TV_series)" title="Wasteland (TV The company, however, sold its interest in the show three months before the premiere when printed stories surfaced about the racy dialogue and risqu\xc3\xa9 plot lines.<sup id="cite_ref-P.26G_end_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P.26G_end-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filming_locations">Filming locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Filming locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Dawson\'s Creek was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>, at <a href="/wiki/EUE/Screen_Gems" title="EUE/Screen Gems">EUE/Screen Gems</a> studios and on location around Wilmington, Southport and Wrightsville Beach, North Carolina. College scenes in the fifth and sixth seasons were shot at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>, <a href="/wiki/Durham,_North_Carolina" title="Durham, North Carolina">Durham, North Carolina</a>, and additional shooting was done in <a href="/wiki/Raleigh,_North_Carolina" title="Raleigh, North Carolina">Raleigh, North Carolina</a>. In 1999, some scenes were shot on the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Richmond" title="University of Richmond">University of Richmond</a> campus. The fourth-season episode "Eastern Standard Time" also did location shooting in New York City, including at <a href="/wiki/Grand_Central_Terminal" title="Grand Central Terminal">Grand Central Terminal</a>. The Ice House restaurant, owned by the Potter family and featured in the show, is actually Dockside Restaurant in Wilmington, NC. (The location at the end of the show, when Ice House was owned by Pacey, actually switched to Elijah\'s, also in Wilmington, NC.)</p>\n<p>The Wilmington area benefited greatly from the show. While a number of films, commercials and music videos had been shot at the studios, the show was the first to occupy numerous <a href="/wiki/Soundstage" class="mw-redirect" title="Soundstage">soundstages</a> for many years. <i><a href="/wiki/One_Tree_Hill_(TV_series)" title="One Tree Hill (TV series)">One Tree Hill</a></i> later occupied some of those same soundstages for several years and used some of the same locations in Wilmington.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In addition to business brought into the community by the project, it attracted attention to the city as a filming location and boosted tourism.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The visitors\' bureau distributed a special guide to filming locations used in the show.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup> When the program was cancelled in 2003, the news was reported on the front-page of Wilmington\'s daily newspaper, the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.starnewsonline.com">Wilmington StarNews</a>.</p>\n<h4><span id="Dawson\'s_Creek_and_home_(6424_Head_Road)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dawson.27s_Creek_and_home_.286424_Head_Road.29">Dawson\'s Creek and home (6424 Head Road)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dawson\'s Creek and home (6424 Head Road)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Sunset shots of Dawson standing on his dock among the marsh grass were filmed along Hewlett\'s Creek on Pine Grove Drive between Masonboro Loop Road and Holly Tree Drive in Masonboro, North Carolina.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-legacy2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy2-11">[11]</a></sup> The private residences used as homes for Dawson, Jen, and Joey are all located along the shores of Hewlett\'s Creek.</p>\n<p>Some of the scenes shown during the opening credits and miscellaneous scenery shots throughout the episodes were filmed in <a href="/wiki/Martha%27s_Vineyard" title="Martha\'s Vineyard">Martha\'s Vineyard</a>, an island off the coast of Massachusetts. One of which is a pan of Oak Bluffs Harbor and another includes a shot of Circuit Avenue also in Oak Bluffs, MA.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Capeside">Capeside</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Capeside">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Capeside is a fictional town in Massachusetts where <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i> takes place. It is located on Cape Cod, possibly somewhere mid-Cape between Falmouth and Yarmouth, as an early episode includes these real towns in a "hurricane day" announcement. Incorporated in 1815, the town has a population of 35,000 and is located between the cities of <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>. Capeside exteriors were shot in and around <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_North_Carolina" title="Wilmington, North Carolina">Wilmington, North Carolina</a>. Its bays and coastlines are similar to those found along the coast of Massachusetts.</p>\n<p>Capeside High School is the high school in Capeside, Massachusetts attended by several characters during the first four seasons of the show. Exteriors were filmed at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Wilmington" title="University of North Carolina at Wilmington">University of North Carolina at Wilmington</a>.</p>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Dawson_Creek,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Dawson Creek, British Columbia">Dawson Creek</a> actually exists in the Canadian province of <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>. It is named for the river of the same name that runs through it. Another exists in <a href="/wiki/Oriental,_NC" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental, NC">Oriental, NC</a>, which flows into the <a href="/wiki/Neuse_River" title="Neuse River">Neuse River</a>. This served as the inspiration for the show\'s name. There is also a Dawson\'s Creek that runs through <a href="/wiki/Baton_Rouge,_Louisiana" title="Baton Rouge, Louisiana">Baton Rouge, Louisiana</a>.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Restaurants_and_bars_in_the_show">Restaurants and bars in the show</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Restaurants and bars in the show">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Interiors for The Icehouse were filmed at The Icehouse bar in downtown Wilmington several blocks from less picturesque water so exteriors were filmed at the Dockside Restaurant at 1308 Airlie Road in Wrightsville Beach, NC. Nearby constructions at the real IceHouse forced producers to eliminate the bar from the storyline by burning it down.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Hell\'s Kitchen bar featured in the show was a natural food store at 118 Princess Street in Wilmington which was purchased by producers, dressed as a seedy college bar and used for production during the show\'s last season. When production completed, the building was purchased by a local restaurateur, along with much of the set and decorations, and converted it into a real restaurant and bar. It retains the name as well.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Leery\'s Fresh Fish, exteriors were filmed at Water Street Restaurant at 5 South Water Street in Wilmington.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Worthington_University">Worthington University</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Worthington University">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Worthington University is a fictional university from <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i>. Joey (played by <a href="/wiki/Katie_Holmes" title="Katie Holmes">Katie Holmes</a>) and Audrey (played by <a href="/wiki/Busy_Philipps" title="Busy Philipps">Busy Philipps</a>), characters from the series, attended this school. It is supposed to be located in <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a> and to have been founded in 1787 by Josiah Worthington. It is sometimes said to be an "<a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> college".</p>\n<p>Producers had not planned for the show to extend beyond the characters\' high school years. The architectural uniformity of <a href="/wiki/UNC_Wilmington" class="mw-redirect" title="UNC Wilmington">UNC Wilmington</a> prevented it from being used for Worthington University exteriors. The scenes at Worthington were filmed over two hours away at <a href="/wiki/Duke_University" title="Duke University">Duke University</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Duke_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duke-12">[12]</a></sup> and a number of its students served as extras.<sup id="cite_ref-Duke_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duke-12">[12]</a></sup> Some filming was also done on <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Street_(Chapel_Hill)" title="Franklin Street (Chapel Hill)">Franklin Street</a> adjacent to nearby <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Carolina_at_Chapel_Hill" title="University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill">University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p><i>Dawson\'s Creek</i> generated a high amount of publicity before its debut, with several television critics and consumer watchdog groups expressing concerns about its anticipated "racy" plots and dialogue. The controversy drove one of the original production companies away from the project.<sup id="cite_ref-P.26G_end_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-P.26G_end-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-controversy_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-controversy-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>John Kiesewetter, television columnist for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cincinnati_Enquirer" title="The Cincinnati Enquirer">The Cincinnati Enquirer</a></i> wrote, "As much as I want to love the show\xe2\x80\x94the cool kids, charming <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> setting, and stunning cinematography\xe2\x80\x94I can\'t get past the consuming preoccupation with sex, sex, sex."<sup id="cite_ref-controversy_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-controversy-13">[13]</a></sup> In his defense, Williamson denied this was his intention, stating that "I never set out to make something provocative and racy".<sup id="cite_ref-controversy_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-controversy-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Syndicated columnist <a href="/wiki/John_Leo" title="John Leo">John Leo</a> said the show should be called "When Parents Cringe," and went on to write "The first episode contains a good deal of chatter about breasts, genitalia, <a href="/wiki/Masturbation" title="Masturbation">masturbation</a>, and penis size. Then the title and credits come on and the story begins." <a href="/wiki/Tom_Shales" title="Tom Shales">Tom Shales</a>, of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> commented that creator Kevin Williamson was "the most overrated wunderkind in Hollywood" and "what he\'s brilliant at is pandering."</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Parents_Television_Council" title="Parents Television Council">Parents Television Council</a> proclaimed the show as the single worst program of the 1997\xe2\x80\x9398 and 1998-99 seasons by being "the crudest of the network shows aimed at kids," complaining about "an almost obsessive focus on pre-marital sexual activity", references to pornography and condoms, and the show\'s acceptance of homosexuality.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Council also cited it as the fourth worst show in 2000\xe2\x80\x932001.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Former <a href="/wiki/UPN" title="UPN">UPN</a> President <a href="/wiki/Lucie_Salhany" title="Lucie Salhany">Lucie Salhany</a> criticized WB for airing <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i> which features "adolescent characters in adult situations" in an early timeslot while the network is supposed to be "the family network".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> However, on the opposite end of the ideological spectrum, the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a> offered an endorsement, deeming it one of the least sexually exploitative shows on the air.</p>\n<p>Despite the controversy surrounding the sexual-oriented topics, the show was a major critical success. Before its premiere, <i><a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="San Francisco Chronicle">San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> explained the buzz around the show was due to its creator Kevin Williamson who wrote the screenplays for <i>Scream</i> and <i>Scream 2</i> and that the show might be "one of the year\'s tangier hits". He also found <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i> scenically "downright luxuriant" and liked that it "doesn\'t have the rushed feel of so many teen shows. The edginess is in the situations, not the pacing."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote that it was "an addictive drama with considerable heart...the teenage equivalent of a <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a> movie\xe2\x80\x94a kind of \'Deconstructing Puberty\'".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Atlanta_Journal-Constitution" title="The Atlanta Journal-Constitution">The Atlanta Journal-Constitution</a></i> called it "a teen\'s dream". <i>The <a href="/wiki/Dayton_Daily_News" title="Dayton Daily News">Dayton Daily News</a></i> listed Capeside as a television town they\'d most like to live in. <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seattle_Times" title="The Seattle Times">The Seattle Times</a></i> declared it the best show of the 1997\xe2\x80\x931998 season and said it "belongs to the small-pantheon <i><a href="/wiki/My_So-Called_Life" title="My So-Called Life">My So-Called Life</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/James_at_15" title="James at 15">James at 15</a></i> and to a lesser extent, <i><a href="/wiki/Party_of_Five" title="Party of Five">Party of Five</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Doogie_Howser,_M.D." title="Doogie Howser, M.D.">Doogie Howser, M.D.</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_accolades">Awards and accolades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Awards and among the general television population, <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i> did very well with the younger demographic it targeted and became a defining show for the WB Network. The pilot episode was watched by 6.8 million viewers and had a 4.8 rating which was the network\'s highest rating at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The first season\'s highest ranked episode was the finale, which was fifty-ninth, while the second highest rated was the second episode (probably scoring so well partially because the other major networks carried President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Clinton</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/State_of_the_Union" title="State of the Union">State of the Union</a> address in the midst of the <a href="/wiki/Lewinsky_scandal" title="Lewinsky scandal">Lewinsky scandal</a> rather than their regular programming).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The finale itself was watched by 7.8 million U.S. viewers, which was its largest audience ever.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off">Spin-off</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dawson%27s_Creek&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Spin-off">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The show had, in the words of television experts <a href="/wiki/Tim_Brooks_(television_historian)" title="Tim Brooks (television historian)">Tim Brooks</a> and Earle Marsh, a "semi-spinoff" \xe2\x80\x93 <i><a href="/wiki/Young_Americans_(TV_series)" title="Young Americans (TV series)">Young Americans</a></i>. The protagonist of <i>Young Americans</i>, Will Krudski (<a href="/wiki/Rodney_Scott" title="Rodney Scott">Rodney Scott</a>), was introduced in three episodes at the end of the show\'s third season, as a former classmate of Dawson, Joey, and Pacey, who had moved away some years before and had returned for a visit. He was never referred to before or seen again. <i>Young Americans</i> was made by the same company as <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i>, <a href="/wiki/Columbia_TriStar_Television" title="Columbia TriStar Television">Columbia TriStar Television</a>, and appeared in <i>Dawson\'s Creek\'s</i> timeslot when it went on hiatus during the summer of 2000. The show had 8 episodes. The reason the show is considered a semi-spinoff instead of a true spinoff is that Will was not originally created for <i>Dawson\'s Creek</i>. He was added to <i>Dawson\'s</i> solely to set up and promote the series <i>Young Americans</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Simon_%26_Schuster" title="Simon &amp; Schuster">Simon &amp; Schuster</a> published a series of fifteen mass-market paperback novelizations of the series.<sup id="cite_ref-legacy_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-legacy-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Amanda_Show" title="The Amanda first soundtrack album of the teen drama, <i>Songs from Dawson\'s Creek</i>, was released. It features <a href="/wiki/Sophie_B._Hawkins" title="Sophie B. Hawkins">Sophie B. Hawkins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Simpson" title="Jessica Simpson">Jessica Simpson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luciana_Caporaso#Bands" title="Luciana Caporaso">Shooter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heather_Nova" title="Heather Nova">Heather Nova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adam_Cohen_(musician)" title="Adam Cohen (musician)">Adam Cohen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sixpence_None_the_Richer" title="Sixpence None the Richer">Sixpence None the Richer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paula_Cole" title="Paula Cole">Paula Cole</a>, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The album was a commercial success in the United States and scattered two hits in the charts, "<a href="/wiki/Kiss_Me_(Sixpence_None_the_Richer_song)" title="Kiss Me (Sixpence None the Richer song)">Kiss Me</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Don%27t_Want_to_Wait" title="I Don\'t Want to Wait">I Don\'t Want to Wait</a>".
