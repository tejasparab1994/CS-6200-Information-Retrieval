style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jet_stream.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/87/Jet_stream.jpg/220px-Jet_stream.jpg" width="220" height="220" 2x" data-file-width="543" data-file-height="543" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jet_stream.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">Jet streams</a> (shown in pink) are well-known examples of thermal wind. They arise from the horizontal temperature gradients between the warm <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a> and the colder <a href="/wiki/Polar_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Polar region">polar regions</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>Thermal wind</b> is wind that varies with height due to a balance between the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pressure-gradient_force" title="Pressure-gradient force">pressure-gradient</a> forces in the atmosphere. It is the primary physical mechanism for the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> and plays an important role in other large-scale atmospheric phenomena. The thermal wind ensures the jet stream is typically strongest in the upper half of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, which is the atmospheric layer extending from the surface of the planet up to a height of 12 km to 15 km.</p>\n<p>Mathematically, the thermal wind relation defines a vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>--a variation in wind speed or direction with height. The wind shear in this case is a function of a horizontal temperature gradient, which is a variation in temperature over some horizontal distance. Also called <b>baroclinic flow</b>, the thermal wind varies with height in proportion to the horizontal temperature gradient. The thermal wind relation results from <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_balance" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrostatic balance">hydrostatic balance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_balance" class="mw-redirect" title="Geostrophic balance">geostrophic balance</a> in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Temperature_gradient" title="Temperature gradient">temperature gradient</a> along constant pressure surfaces, or <a href="/wiki/Contour_line#Barometric_pressure" title="Contour line">isobars</a>.</p>\n<p>The term <i>thermal wind</i> is often considered a misnomer, since it really describes the change in wind with height, rather the wind itself. However, one can view the thermal wind as a <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a> that varies with height, so that the term <i>wind</i> seems appropriate. In the early years of meteorology, when data was scarce, the wind field could be estimated using the thermal wind relation and knowledge of a surface wind speed and direction as well as thermodynamic soundings aloft.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> In this way, the thermal wind relation acts to define the wind itself, rather than just its shear. Many authors retain the <i>thermal wind</i> moniker, even though it describes a wind gradient, sometimes offering a clarification to that effect.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_intuition">Physical intuition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical intuition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The thermal wind is the change in the amplitude or sign of the <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a> due to a horizontal temperature gradient. The <a href="/wiki/Geostrophic_wind" title="Geostrophic wind">geostrophic wind</a> is an idealized wind that results from a balance of forces along a horizontal dimension. Whenever the Earth\'s rotation plays a dominant role in fluid dynamics, as in the mid-latitudes, a balance between the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pressure-gradient_force" title="Pressure-gradient force">pressure-gradient force</a> develops. Intuitively, a horizontal difference in pressure pushes air across that difference in a similar way that the horizontal difference in the height of a hill causes objects to roll downhill. However, the Coriolis force intervenes and nudges the air towards the right (in the northern hemisphere). This is illustrated in panel (a) of the figure below. The balance that develops between these two forces results in a flow that parallels the horizontal pressure difference, or pressure gradient.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">[1]</a></sup> In addition, when forces acting in the vertical dimension are dominated by the vertical <a href="/wiki/Pressure-gradient_force" title="Pressure-gradient force">pressure-gradient force</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_force" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravitational force">gravitational force</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_balance" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrostatic balance">hydrostatic balance</a> occurs.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:431px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermal_wind,_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg/429px-Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg" width="429" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg/644px-Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg/858px-Thermal_wind%2C_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="792" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thermal_wind,_Baroclinic_%26_Barotropic_Atmospheres.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe geostrophic wind on different isobaric levels in a barotropic atmosphere (a) and in a baroclinic atmosphere (b). The blue portion of the surface denotes a cold region while the orange portion denotes a warm region. This temperature structure is restricted to the surface in (a) but extends through the depth of the fluid in (b). The dotted lines enclose isobaric surfaces which remain at constant slope with increasing height in (a) and increase in slope with height in (b). Pink arrows illustrate the direction and amplitude of the horizontal wind. Only in the baroclinic atmosphere (b) do these vary with height. Such variation illustrates the thermal wind.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Barotropic_fluid" title="Barotropic fluid">barotropic</a> atmosphere, where density is a function only of pressure, a horizontal pressure gradient will drive a geostrophic wind that is constant with height. However, if a horizontal temperature gradient exists along isobars, the isobars will be thicker. This causes the slope of the isobars to increase with height. This is illustrated in panel (b) of the figure to the left. Because isobars are steeper at higher elevations, the associated pressure gradient force is stronger there. However, the Coriolis force is the same, so the resulting geostrophic wind at higher elevations must be greater in the direction of the pressure force.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Baroclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroclinic">baroclinic</a> atmosphere, where density is a function of both pressure and temperature, such horizontal temperature gradients can exist. The difference in horizontal wind speed with height that results is a vertical wind shear, traditionally called the thermal wind.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_2-1" class="reference"><a In (b), warm advection is occurring, so the geostrophic wind rotates clockwise with height.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>If a component of the geostrophic wind is parallel to the temperature gradient, the thermal wind will cause the geostrophic wind to rotate with height. If geostrophic wind blows from cold air to warm air (cold <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>) the geostrophic wind will turn <a href="/wiki/Counterclockwise" class="mw-redirect" title="Counterclockwise">counterclockwise</a> with height (for the northern hemisphere), a <a href="/wiki/Phenomenon" title="Phenomenon">phenomenon</a> known as wind backing. Otherwise, if geostrophic wind blows from warm air to cold air (warm advection) the wind will turn <a href="/wiki/Clockwise" title="Clockwise">clockwise</a> with height, also known as wind veering.</p>\n<p>Wind backing and veering allow an estimation of the horizontal temperature gradient with data from an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sounding" title="Atmospheric sounding">atmospheric sounding</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frontogenesis">Frontogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Frontogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As in the case of advection turning, when there is a cross-<a href="/wiki/Isothermal" class="mw-redirect" title="Isothermal">isothermal</a> component of the geostrophic wind, a sharpening of the temperature gradient results. Thermal wind causes a deformation field and <a href="/wiki/Frontogenesis" title="Frontogenesis">frontogenesis</a> may occur.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jet_stream">Jet stream</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Jet stream">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A horizontal temperature gradient exists while moving <a href="/wiki/North" title="North">North</a>-<a href="/wiki/South" title="South">South</a> along a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> because curvature of the Earth allows for more <a href="/wiki/Insolation" class="mw-redirect" title="Insolation">solar heating</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> than at the poles. This creates a <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerly</a> geostrophic wind pattern to form in the mid-latitudes. Because thermal wind causes an increase in wind <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a> with height, the westerly pattern increases in intensity up until the <a href="/wiki/Tropopause" title="Tropopause">tropopause</a>, creating a strong wind current known as the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemispheres</a> exhibit similar jet stream patterns in the mid-latitudes.</p>\n<p>The strongest part of jet streams should be in proximity where temperature gradients are the largest. Due to land masses in the northern hemisphere, largest temperature contrasts are observed on the east coast of North America (boundary between Canadian cold air mass and the Gulf Stream/warmer Atlantic) and Eurasia (boundary between the boreal winter monsoon/Siberian cold air mass and the warm Pacific). Therefore, the strongest boreal winter jet streams are observed over east coast of North America and Eurasia. Since stronger vertical shear promotes <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic instability</a>, the most rapid development of <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Extratropical cyclones">extratropical cyclones</a> (so called <a href="/wiki/Bomb_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bomb (meteorology)">bombs</a>) is also observed along the east coast of North America and Eurasia.</p>\n<p>The lack of land masses in the Southern Hemisphere leads to a more constant jet with longitude (i.e. a more zonally symmetric jet).</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_wind&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-:0-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-:0_1-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-:0_1-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation book">Cushman-Roisin, Benoit (1994). <i>Introduction to Geophysical Fluid Dynamics</i>.
