class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">Heat wave</a>.</div>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a>, a <b>heat burst</b> is a rare atmospheric phenomenon characterized by gusty winds and a rapid increase in <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> and decrease in <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> (moisture). Heat bursts typically occur during night-time and are associated with decaying <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" class="mw-redirect" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Although this phenomenon is not fully understood, it is theorized that the event is caused when rain evaporates (<a href="/wiki/Virga" title="Virga">virga</a>) into a parcel of cold dry air high in the atmosphere making the air denser than its surroundings.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The parcel descends rapidly, warming due to compression, overshoots its <a href="/wiki/Equilibrium_level" title="Equilibrium level">equilibrium level</a> and reaches the surface, similar to a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Recorded temperatures during heat bursts have reached well above 38&#160;<a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">\xc2\xb0C</a> (100&#160;<a href="/wiki/Fahrenheit" title="Fahrenheit">\xc2\xb0F</a>), sometimes rising by 11&#160;\xc2\xb0C (20&#160;\xc2\xb0F) or more within only a few minutes. More extreme events have also been documented, where temperatures have been reported to exceed 120&#160;\xc2\xb0F (49&#160;\xc2\xb0C). However, such extreme events have never been officially verified. Heat bursts are also characterized by extremely dry air and are sometimes associated with very strong, even damaging, <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Characteristics"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Characteristics</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Causes"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Causes</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#Forecasting"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">Forecasting</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Some_documented_cases"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Some documented cases</span></a>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-2 tocsection-5"><a href="#Extreme_cases"><span class="tocnumber">4.1</span> <span class="toctext">Extreme cases</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-8"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In general, heat bursts occur during the late spring and summer seasons. During the late spring and summer seasons, thunderstorms tend to generate day heating and lose their main energy during the evening hours.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Due to a potential temperature increase, heat bursts normally occur at night. In rare cases, heat bursts have been recorded to occur during the daytime as well. Heat bursts have lasted for times spanning from a couple of minutes to several hours. The rare phenomenon is usually accompanied by strong gusty winds, extreme temperature changes, and an extreme decrease in humidity. They occur near the end of a weakening thunderstorm cluster. Dry air and a low-level inversion are also present during the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">Causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As the thunderstorm starts to dissipate, the layer of clouds start to rise. After the layer of clouds rise, a rain-cooled layer remains. The cluster shoots a burst of unsaturated air down towards the ground. In doing so, the system loses all of its up-draft related fuel.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The raindrops begin to evaporate into dry air, which emphasizes the effects of the heat bursts. As the unsaturated air descends, the air pressure increases. The descending air parcel warms at the dry adiabatic lapse rate of approximately 10\xc2\xb0 Celsius per 1000 meters (5.5\xc2\xb0 Fahrenheit per 1000 feet) of descent. The warm air from the cluster replaces the cool air on the ground. The effect is similar to someone blowing down on a puddle of water. On 4 March 1990, the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> in <a href="/wiki/Goodland,_Kansas" title="Goodland, Kansas">Goodland, Kansas</a> detected a system that had weakening, light rain showers and snow showers. It was followed by gusty winds and a temperature increase. A heat burst was being observed. The detection proved that heat bursts can occur in both summer months and winter months. The occurrence also proved that a weakening thunderstorm was not needed in the development of heat bursts.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Microburstcrosssection.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Microburstcrosssection.JPG/220px-Microburstcrosssection.JPG" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Microburstcrosssection.JPG/330px-Microburstcrosssection.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Microburstcrosssection.JPG/440px-Microburstcrosssection.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2155" data-file-height="975" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Microburstcrosssection.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHeat bursts are a variety of downburst.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forecasting">Forecasting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forecasting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The first step of forecasting and preparing for heat bursts is recognizing the events that come before heat bursts occur. Rain from a high <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> cloud falls below cloud level and evaporates, cooling the air. Air parcels that are cooler than the surrounding environment fall. And lastly, temperature conversion mixed with a downdraft momentum continue downward until the air reaches the ground. The air parcels then become warmer than their environment. McPherson, Lane, Crawford, and McPherson Jr. researched the heat burst system at the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_Mesonet" title="Oklahoma Mesonet">Oklahoma Mesonet</a>, which is owned by both the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oklahoma" title="University of Oklahoma">University of Oklahoma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oklahoma State University">Oklahoma State University</a>. The purpose of their research was to discover any technological benefits and challenges in detecting heat bursts, document the time of day and year that heat bursts mostly occur, and to research the topography of where heat bursts mostly occur in Oklahoma. Scientists and meteorologists use archived data to manually study data that detected 390 potential heat burst days during a fifteen-year period. In studying the archived data, they observed that 58% of the potential days had dry-line passages, frontal passages or a temperature change. The temperature change was due to increase in solar radiation in the hours of the morning or a daytime precipitation weather system. By studying the archived data, the scientists\' have the ability to determine the beginning, peak and end of heat burst conditions. The peak of heat burst conditions is the maximum observed temperature. The beginning of the heat burst occurrence is the time when the air temperature began to increase without decreasing until after the heat burst. The end of the heat burst is when the system ceased to affect the temperature and dew point of the area. In addition to researching the life cycle and characteristics of heat bursts, a group of scientists concluded that the topography of Oklahoma coincided with the change in atmospheric moisture between northwest and southeast Oklahoma. An increase in convection normally occurs over the <b><a href="/wiki/High_Plains_(United_States)" title="High Plains (United States)">United States High Plains</a></b> during the late spring and summer. They also concluded that a higher increase in convection develops if a mid-tropospheric lifting mechanism interacts with an elevated moist layer.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_documented_cases">Some documented cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heat_burst&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Some documented cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hobart,_Oklahoma" title="Hobart, Oklahoma">Hobart, Oklahoma</a>, 6\xe2\x80\x937 July 2016: The temperature rose from 80.6&#160;\xc2\xb0F (27.0&#160;\xc2\xb0C) just before 11:00 pm CDT, July 6 to 105.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F (41.0&#160;\xc2\xb0C) at 12:15 am CDT, July 7.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, 30 July 2014: Between 10:00 pm, July 29, and 12:00 am, the <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">dew point</a> fell from 12&#160;\xc2\xb0C (54&#160;\xc2\xb0F) to 0&#160;\xc2\xb0C (32&#160;\xc2\xb0F), with southwest wind gusts of 85 kilometres per hour (53&#160;mph) at the airport.
