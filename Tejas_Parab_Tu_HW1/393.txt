class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alice_par_John_Tenniel_30.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAn illustration from <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" playing a <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantastical</a> game of <a href="/wiki/Croquet" title="Croquet">croquet</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Fiction</b> is the classification for any <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">story</a> or <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">setting</a> that is derived from <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">imagination</a>\xe2\x80\x94in other words, not based strictly on history or fact.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-C._Hugh_Holman_1990.2C_p._212_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C._Hugh_Holman_1990.2C_p._212-3">[3]</a></sup> Fiction can be expressed in a variety of formats, including <a href="/wiki/Writing" title="Writing">writings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">live performances</a>, <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video games</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing games</a>, though the term originally and most commonly refers to the narrative forms of <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literature</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">literary fiction</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novellas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">plays</a>. Fiction is occasionally used in its narrowest sense to mean simply any "literary narrative".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A work of fiction is an act of creative imagination, so its total faithfulness to the real-world is not typically assumed by its audience.<sup id="cite_ref-litfiction_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-litfiction-6">[6]</a></sup> Therefore, fiction is not commonly expected to present only <a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">characters</a> who are actual people or descriptions that are factually accurate. Instead, the context of fiction, not adhering precisely to the real world, is generally understood as being more open to <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">interpretation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Characters and events within a fictional work may even be set in their own context entirely separate from the known universe: an independent <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">fictional universe</a>. Fiction is regarded as the traditional opposite of <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fiction</a>, whose creators assume responsibility for presenting only the historical and factual truth; however, the distinction between fiction and non-fiction can be unclear, for example, in <a href="/wiki/Postmodern_literature" title="Postmodern literature">postmodern literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 class="toclevel-1 tocsection-9"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">9</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formats">Formats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_genres" class="mw-redirect" title="List of fictional genres">List of fictional genres</a></div>\n<p>Traditionally, fiction includes <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short stories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fables" class="mw-redirect" title="Fables">fables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">legends</a>, <a href="/wiki/Myths" class="mw-redirect" title="Myths">myths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Fairy tales">fairy tales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epic_poetry" title="Epic poetry">epic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narrative_poetry" title="Narrative poetry">narrative poetry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">plays</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Operas" class="mw-redirect" title="Operas">operas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">musicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puppet_play" class="mw-redirect" title="Puppet play">puppet plays</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Concert_dance" title="Concert dance">various kinds of theatrical dances</a>). However, fiction may also encompass <a href="/wiki/Comic_book" title="Comic book">comic books</a>, and many <a href="/wiki/Animated_cartoon" title="Animated cartoon">animated cartoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stop_motion" title="Stop motion">stop motions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fictional_film" title="Fictional film">films</a>, <a href="/wiki/Video_games" class="mw-redirect" title="Video games">video games</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio_drama" title="Radio drama">radio programs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Television_program" title="Television program">television programs</a> (<a href="/wiki/Television_comedy" title="Television comedy">comedies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dramatic_programming" class="mw-redirect" title="Dramatic programming">dramas</a>), etc.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> has had a major impact on the creation and distribution of fiction, calling into question the feasibility of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> as a means to ensure <a href="/wiki/Royalties" class="mw-redirect" title="Royalties">royalties</a> are paid to copyright holders.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Also, <a href="/wiki/Digital_libraries" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital libraries">digital libraries</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Project_Gutenberg" title="Project Gutenberg">Project Gutenberg</a> make <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a> texts more readily available. The combination of inexpensive home computers, the Internet and the creativity of its users has also led to new forms of fiction, such as interactive <a href="/wiki/Computer_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer game">computer games</a> or computer-generated comics. Countless forums for <a href="/wiki/Fan_fiction" title="Fan fiction">fan fiction</a> can be found online, where loyal followers of specific <a href="/wiki/Fictional_realm" class="mw-redirect" title="Fictional realm">fictional realms</a> create and distribute derivative stories. The Internet is also used for the development of <a href="/wiki/Blog_fiction" title="Blog fiction">blog fiction</a>, where a story is delivered through a <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a> either as flash fiction or serial <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blog</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Collaborative_fiction" title="Collaborative fiction">collaborative fiction</a>, where a story is written sequentially by different authors, or the entire text can be revised by anyone using a <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a>.</p>\n<p>Types of <a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">literary fiction</a> in <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">prose</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">Short story</a>: A work of at least 2,000 words but under 7,500 words. The boundary between a long short story and a novella is vague.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">Novella</a>: A work of at least 17,500 words but under 50,000 words. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i> (1899) is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">novella</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">Novel</a>: A work of 50,000 words or more.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_fiction">Genre fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Genre fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Genre_fiction" title="Genre fiction">Genre fiction</a></div>\n<p>Fiction is commonly broken down into a variety of <a href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_genres" class="mw-redirect" title="List of fictional genres">genres</a>: subsets of fiction, each differentiated by a particular unifying <a href="/wiki/Tone_(literature)" title="Tone (literature)">tone or style</a>, <a href="/wiki/Literary_technique#literary_techniques" class="mw-redirect" title="Literary technique">narrative technique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Content_(media)" title="Content (media)">media content</a>, or popularly defined criterion. <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">Science fiction</a>, for example, predicts or supposes technologies that are not realities at the time of the work\'s creation. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">From the Earth to the Moon</a></i> was published in 1865 and only in 1969 did astronaut <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a> first land on the moon.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Historical_fiction" title="Historical fiction">Historical fiction</a> places imaginary characters into real historical events. In the early historical novel <i><a href="/wiki/Waverley_(novel)" title="Waverley (novel)">Waverley</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Walter_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Walter Scott">Sir Walter Scott</a>\'s fictional character Edward Waverley meets a figure from history, <a href="/wiki/Bonnie_Prince_Charlie" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonnie Prince Charlie">Bonnie Prince Charlie</a>, and takes part in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Prestonpans" title="Battle of Prestonpans">Battle of Prestonpans</a>. Some works of fiction are slightly or greatly re-imagined based on some originally true story, or a reconstructed biography.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Role_of_Narrative_Fiction_and_Semi-Fiction_in_Organizational_Studies_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Role_of_Narrative_Fiction_and_Semi-Fiction_in_Organizational_Studies-13">[13]</a></sup> Often, even when the author claims the fictional story is basically true, there may be artificial additions and subtractions from the true story to make it more interesting. One such example would be Tim O\'Brien\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Things_They_Carried" title="The Things They Carried">The Things They Carried</a></i>, a series of historical fiction <a href="/wiki/Short_stories" class="mw-redirect" title="Short stories">short stories</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.</p>\n<p>Fictional works that explicitly involve supernatural, magical, or scientifically impossible elements are often classified under the genre of <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">fantasy</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice\'s Adventures in Wonderland">Alice In Wonderland</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> series, and <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>. Creators of fantasy sometimes introduce entire imaginary creatures or beings such as dragons and fairies.<sup id="cite_ref-C._Hugh_Holman_1990.2C_p._212_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C._Hugh_Holman_1990.2C_p._212-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_fiction">Literary fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literary fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">Literature</a></div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Literary_fiction" title="Literary fiction">Literary fiction</a> is defined as fictional works that are deemed to be of <a href="/wiki/Literary_merit" title="Literary merit">literary merit</a>, as distinguished from most commercial, or <a href="/wiki/Genre_fiction" title="Genre fiction">"genre" fiction</a>. The distinction can be <a href="/wiki/Western_canon#Debate" title="Western canon">controversial</a> among critics and scholars.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Neal_Stephenson" title="Neal Stephenson">Neal Stephenson</a> has suggested that while any definition will be simplistic there is today a general cultural difference between literary and genre fiction. On the one hand literary authors nowadays are frequently supported by patronage, with employment at a university or a similar institution, and with the continuation of such positions determined not by book sales but by critical acclaim by other established literary authors and critics. On the other hand, he suggests, genre fiction writers tend to support themselves by book sales.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> However, in an interview, <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a> lamented that "the category of \'literary fiction\' has sprung up recently to torment people like me who just set out to write books, and if anybody wanted to read them, terrific, the more the merrier. ... I\'m a genre writer of a sort. I write literary fiction, which is like spy fiction or chick lit".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGrossman2006_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrossman2006-15">[15]</a></sup> Likewise, on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Charlie_Rose_Show" class="mw-redirect" title="The Charlie Rose Show">The Charlie Rose Show</a></i>, he argued that this term, when applied to his work, greatly limited him and his expectations of what might come of his writing, so he does not really like it. He suggested that all his works are literary, simply because "they are written in words".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Literary fiction often involves <a href="/wiki/Social_commentary" title="Social commentary">social commentary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Political_criticism" title="Political criticism">political criticism</a>, or reflection on the <a href="/wiki/Human_condition" title="Human condition">human condition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180-17">[17]</a></sup> In general it focuses on "introspective, in-depth character studies" of "interesting, complex and developed" characters.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009180-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes20097_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes20097-18">[18]</a></sup> This contrasts with genre fiction where plot is the central concern.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009181-182_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009181-182-19">[19]</a></sup> Usually in literary fiction the focus is on the "inner story" of the characters who drive the plot, with detailed motivations to elicit "emotional involvement" in the reader.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes200726_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes200726-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColes20098_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColes20098-21">[21]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Style_(fiction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Style (fiction)">style</a> of literary fiction is often described as "elegantly written, lyrical, and ... layered".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009179_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009179-22">[22]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tone_(literature)" title="Tone (literature)">tone</a> of literary fiction can be darker than genre fiction,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182-23">[23]</a></sup> while the pacing of literary fiction may be slower than popular fiction.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaricks2009182-23">[23]</a></sup> As <a href="/wiki/Terrence_Rafferty" title="Terrence Rafferty">Terrence Rafferty</a> notes, "literary fiction, by its nature, allows itself to dawdle, to linger on stray beauties even at the risk of losing its way".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERafferty2011_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERafferty2011-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Realism">Realism<span id="Reality_and_fiction"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">Literary realism</a></div>\n<p>Realistic fiction typically involves a story whose basic <a href="/wiki/Setting_(narrative)" title="Setting (narrative)">setting</a> (time and location in the world) is real and whose events could feasibly happen in a real-world setting; non-realistic fiction involves a story where the opposite is the case, often being set in an entirely <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/alternate_universe" class="extiw" title="wikt:alternate universe">imaginary universe</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Alternative_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Alternative history">alternative history</a> of the world other than that currently understood as true, or some other non-existent location or time-period, sometimes even presenting <a href="/wiki/Impossible_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Impossible technology">impossible technology</a> or a defiance of the currently understood laws of nature. However, all types of fiction arguably invite their audience to explore real ideas, issues, or possibilities in an otherwise imaginary setting, or using what is understood about reality to mentally construct something similar to reality, though still distinct from it.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[note 1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[note 2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In terms of the traditional separation between fiction and <a href="/wiki/Non-fiction" title="Non-fiction">non-fiction</a>, the lines are now commonly understood as blurred, showing more overlap than mutual exclusion. Even fiction usually has elements of, or grounding in, truth. The distinction between the two may be best defined from the perspective of the audience, according to whom a work is regarded as non-fiction if its people, places, and events are all historically or factually real, while a work is regarded as fiction if it deviates from reality in any of those areas. The distinction between fiction and non-fiction is further obscured by an understanding, on the one hand, that the truth can be presented through imaginary channels and constructions, while, on the other hand, imagination can just as well bring about significant conclusions about truth and reality.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Literary critic <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(critic)" title="James Wood (critic)">James Wood</a>, argues that "fiction is both artifice and <a href="/wiki/Verisimilitude_(narrative)" class="mw-redirect" title="Verisimilitude (narrative)">verisimilitude</a>", meaning that it requires both creative invention as well as some acceptable degree of believability,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[25]</a></sup> a notion often encapsulated in poet <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Taylor_Coleridge" title="Samuel Taylor Coleridge">Samuel Taylor Coleridge</a>\'s term: <a href="/wiki/Willing_suspension_of_disbelief" class="mw-redirect" title="Willing suspension of disbelief">willing suspension of disbelief</a>. Also, infinite fictional possibilities themselves signal the impossibility of fully knowing reality, provocatively demonstrating that there is no criterion to measure constructs of reality.<sup id="cite_ref-subversive_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-subversive-28">[26]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main list: <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_fiction" title="Outline of fiction">Outline of fiction</a></div>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Character_(arts)" title="Character (arts)">Character (arts)</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Fiction_writing" title="Fiction writing">Fiction writing</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">Pseudohistory</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">As philosopher Stacie Friend explains, "in reading we take works of fiction, like works of non-fiction, to be about the real world\xe2\x80\x94even if they invite us to imagine the world to be different from how it actually is.
