The process shown in the upper-right is what is happening in the cloud and the process of condensation to the introduced chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Cloud seeding</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Weather_modification" title="Weather modification">weather modification</a>, a way of changing the amount or type of <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> that falls from <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">clouds</a>, by dispersing substances into the <a href="/wiki/Air" class="mw-redirect" title="Air">air</a> that serve as <a href="/wiki/Cloud_condensation_nuclei" title="Cloud condensation nuclei">cloud condensation</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ice_nucleus" title="Ice nucleus">ice nuclei</a>, which alter the microphysical processes within the cloud. The usual intent is to increase precipitation (rain or snow), but hail and fog suppression are also widely practiced in airports.</p>\n<p>Cloud seeding also occurs due to <a href="/wiki/Ice_nucleus" title="Ice nucleus">ice nucleators</a> in nature, most of which are bacterial in origin.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18309078_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18309078-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methodology">Methodology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Methodology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most common <a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemicals</a> used for cloud seeding include <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Potassium_iodide" title="Potassium iodide">potassium iodide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> (solid <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>). <a href="/wiki/Liquid_propane" class="mw-redirect" title="Liquid propane">Liquid propane</a>, which expands into a gas, has also been used. This can produce ice crystals at higher temperatures than silver iodide. After promising research, the use of <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopy" title="Hygroscopy">hygroscopic</a> materials, such as table salt, is becoming more popular.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> When Cloud seeding, increased snowfall takes place when temperatures within the clouds are between 19 and \xe2\x88\x924&#160;\xc2\xb0F (\xe2\x88\x927 and \xe2\x88\x9220&#160;\xc2\xb0C).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Introduction of a substance such as silver iodide, which has a <a href="/wiki/Crystalline" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystalline">crystalline</a> structure similar to that of ice, will induce <a href="/wiki/Freezing" title="Freezing">freezing</a> <a href="/wiki/Nucleation" title="Nucleation">nucleation</a>.</p>\n<p>In mid-altitude clouds, the usual seeding strategy has been based on the fact that the equilibrium <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> is lower over ice than over water. The formation of ice particles in supercooled clouds allows those particles to grow at the expense of liquid droplets. If sufficient growth takes place, the particles become heavy enough to fall as precipitation from clouds that otherwise would produce no precipitation. This process is known as "static" seeding.</p>\n<p>Seeding of warm-season or tropical (convective) clouds seeks to exploit the <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> released by freezing. This strategy of "dynamic" seeding assumes that the additional latent heat adds buoyancy, strengthens updrafts, ensures more low-level convergence, and ultimately causes rapid growth of properly selected clouds.</p>\n<p>Cloud seeding chemicals may be dispersed by aircraft or by dispersion devices located on the ground (generators or canisters fired from <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_gun" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-aircraft gun">anti-aircraft guns</a> or <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a>). For release by aircraft, silver iodide <a href="/wiki/Flare" title="Flare">flares</a> are ignited and dispersed as an aircraft flies through the inflow of a cloud. When released by devices on the ground, the fine particles are carried downwind and upward by <a href="/wiki/Air_current" title="Air current">air currents</a> after release.</p>\n<p>An electronic mechanism was tested in 2010, when <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">infrared</a> <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> pulses were directed to the air above <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">Berlin</a> by researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Geneva" title="University of Geneva">University of Geneva</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ns_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ns-5">[5]</a></sup> The experimenters posited that the pulses would encourage atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a> to form particles that would then act as seeds.<sup id="cite_ref-ns_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ns-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effectiveness">Effectiveness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effectiveness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Cloud seeding has never been statistically proven to work. An ecologist at Stanford University says, "I think you can squeeze out a little more snow or rain in some places under some conditions, but that\'s quite different from a program claiming to reliably increase precipitation."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Claims are made that new technology and research has produced reliable results that make cloud seeding a dependable and affordable water supply practice for many regions.<sup id="cite_ref-sacbee_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sacbee-7">[7]</a></sup> But while practiced widely around the world, the effectiveness of cloud seeding is still a matter of academic debate.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> In 2003 the <a href="/wiki/National_Research_Council_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Research Council (United States)">US National Research Council</a> (NRC) released a report stating, "...science is unable to say with assurance which, if any, seeding techniques produce positive effects. In the 55 years following the first cloud-seeding demonstrations, substantial progress has been made in understanding the natural processes that account for our daily weather. Yet scientifically acceptable proof for significant seeding effects has not been achieved,...<sup id="cite_ref-NRC-2003_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRC-2003-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:13</sup></p>\n<p>Referring to 1903, 1915, 1919, 1944, and 1947 weather modification experiments, the Australian Federation of Meteorology discounted "rain making". By the 1950s, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">CSIRO</a> Division of Radiophysics switched to investigating the physics of clouds and had hoped by 1957 to better understand these processes. By the 1960s, the dreams of weather making had faded only to be re-ignited post-corporatisation of the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains_Scheme" title="Snowy Mountains Scheme">Snowy Mountains Scheme</a> in order to achieve "above target" water. This would provide enhanced energy generation and profits to the public agencies that are the principal owners. Recently in 2017, this process had been used by the UAE for it had been forecasted that it won\'t rain for the whole year in the UAE</p>\n<p>Cloud seeding has been shown to be effective in altering cloud structure and size and in converting supercooled liquid water to ice particles. The amount of precipitation due to seeding is difficult to quantify. There is statistical evidence for seasonal precipitation increases of about 10 percent with winter seeding.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Clouds were seeded during the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics" title="2008 Summer Olympics">2008 Summer Olympics</a> in Beijing using rockets, to coax rain showers out of clouds before they reached the Olympic city so that there would be no rain during the <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics_opening_ceremony" title="2008 Summer Olympics opening ceremony">opening</a> and <a href="/wiki/2008_Summer_Olympics_closing_ceremony" title="2008 Summer Olympics closing ceremony">closing</a> ceremonies,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> although others dispute their claims of success.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A 2010 <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv_University" title="Tel Aviv University">Tel Aviv University</a> study claimed that the common practice of cloud seeding to improve rainfall, with materials such as <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a> and frozen carbon dioxide, seems to have little if any impact on the amount of precipitation.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> A 2011 study suggested that airplanes may produce ice particles by freezing cloud droplets that cool as they flow around the tips of propellers, over wings or over jet aircraft, and thereby unintentionally seed clouds. This could have potentially serious consequences for particular <a href="/wiki/Hail_stone" class="mw-redirect" title="Hail stone">hail stone</a> formation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_environment_and_health">Impact on environment and health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on environment and health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>With an <a href="/wiki/NFPA_704" title="NFPA 704">NFPA 704</a> health hazard rating of 2, <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a> can cause temporary incapacitation or possible residual injury to humans and mammals with intense or chronic exposure. However, there have been several detailed ecological studies that showed negligible environmental and health impacts.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> The toxicity of silver and silver compounds (from silver iodide) was shown to be of low order in some studies. These findings likely result from the minute amounts of silver generated by cloud seeding, which are about one percent of industry emissions into the atmosphere in many parts of the world, or individual exposure from tooth fillings.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Accumulations in the soil, vegetation, and surface runoff have not been large enough to measure above natural background.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> A 1995 environmental assessment in the Sierra Nevada of California<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> and a 2004 independent panel of experts in Australia confirmed these earlier findings.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>"In 1978, an estimated 2,740 <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a> of silver were released into the US environment. This led the US Health Services and EPA to conduct studies regarding the potential for environmental and human health hazards related to silver. These agencies and other state agencies applied the Clean Water Act of 1977 and 1987 to establish regulations on this type of pollution."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Cloud seeding over <a href="/wiki/Kosciuszko_National_Park" title="Kosciuszko National Park">Kosciuszko National Park</a>\xe2\x80\x94a biosphere reserve\xe2\x80\x94is problematic in that several rapid changes of environmental legislation were made to enable the trial. Environmentalists are concerned about the uptake of elemental silver in a highly sensitive environment affecting the pygmy possum among other species as well as recent high level algal blooms in once pristine glacial lakes. Research 50 years ago and analysis by the former Snowy Mountains Authority led to the cessation of the cloud seeding program in the 1950s with non-definitive results. Formerly, cloud seeding was rejected in Australia on environmental grounds because of concerns about the protected species, the pygmy possum.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> Since silver iodide and not elemental silver is the cloud seeding material, the claims of negative environmental impact are disputed by peer-reviewed research as summarized by the international Weather Modification Association.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_cloud_seeding">History of cloud seeding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of cloud seeding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg/220px-Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg/330px-Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg/440px-Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2605" data-file-height="1954" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cessna_210_Hagelflieger_Detail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Cessna_210" title="Cessna 210">Cessna 210</a> with cloud seeding equipment</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Vincent_Schaefer" title="Vincent Schaefer">Vincent Schaefer</a> (1906\xe2\x80\x931993) discovered the principle of cloud seeding in July 1946 through a series of serendipitous events. Following ideas generated between him and Nobel laureate <a href="/wiki/Irving_Langmuir" title="Irving Langmuir">Irving Langmuir</a> while climbing Mt Washington in New Hampshire, Schaefer, Langmuir\'s research associate, created a way of experimenting with super-cooled clouds using a deep freeze unit of potential agents to stimulate ice crystal growth, i.e., table salt, talcum powder, soils, dust, and various chemical agents with minor effect. Then one hot and humid July 14, 1946, he wanted to try a few experiments at General Electric\'s <a href="/wiki/Schenectady,_New_York" title="Schenectady, New York">Schenectady</a> Research Lab.</p>\n<p>He was dismayed to find that the deep freezer was not cold enough to produce a "cloud" using breath air. He decided to move the process along by adding a chunk of <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> just to lower the temperature of his experimental chamber. To his astonishment, as soon as he breathed into the deep freezer, he noted a bluish haze, followed by an eye-popping display of millions of microscopic ice crystals, reflecting the strong light rays from the lamp illuminating a cross-section of the chamber. He instantly realized that he had discovered a way to change super-cooled water into ice crystals. The experiment was easily replicated, and he explored the temperature gradient to establish the \xe2\x88\x9240&#160;\xc2\xb0C limit for liquid water.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Within the month, Schaefer\'s colleague, the noted atmospheric scientist Dr. <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Vonnegut" title="Bernard Vonnegut">Bernard Vonnegut</a> (brother of novelist <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut" title="Kurt Vonnegut">Kurt Vonnegut</a>) was credited with discovering another method for "seeding" super-cooled cloud water. Vonnegut accomplished his discovery at the desk, looking up information in a basic chemistry text and then tinkering with silver and iodide chemicals to produce <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a>. Together with Professor Henry Chessin, SUNY Albany, a crystallographer, he co-authored a publication in <i>Science</i><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> and received a patent in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> Both methods were adopted for use in cloud seeding during 1946 while working for the General Electric Corporation in the state of New York.</p>\n<p>Schaefer\'s method altered a cloud\'s heat budget, Vonnegut\'s altered formative crystal structure, an ingenious property related to a good match in <a href="/wiki/Lattice_constant" title="Lattice constant">lattice constant</a> between the two types of <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">crystal</a>. (The <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">crystallography</a> of ice later played a role in Kurt Vonnegut\'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Cat%27s_Cradle" title="Cat\'s Cradle">Cat\'s Cradle</a></i>). The first attempt to modify natural clouds in the field through "cloud seeding" began during a flight that began in upstate <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> on 13 November 1946. Schaefer was able to cause snow to fall near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Greylock" title="Mount Greylock">Mount Greylock</a> in western Massachusetts, after he dumped six pounds of <a href="/wiki/Dry_ice" title="Dry ice">dry ice</a> into the target cloud from a plane after a 60-mile easterly chase from the <a href="/wiki/Schenectady_County_Airport" title="Schenectady County Airport">Schenectady County Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Dry ice and silver iodide agents are effective in changing the physical chemistry of super-cooled clouds, thus useful in augmentation of winter snowfall over mountains and under certain conditions, in lightning and <a href="/wiki/Hail" title="Hail">hail</a> suppression. While not a new technique, <a href="/wiki/Hygroscopic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hygroscopic">hygroscopic</a> seeding for enhancement of rainfall in warm clouds is enjoying a revival, based on some positive indications from research in South Africa, Mexico, and elsewhere. The hygroscopic material most commonly used is table salt. It is postulated that hygroscopic seeding causes the droplet size spectrum in clouds to become more maritime (bigger drops) and less continental, stimulating rainfall through coalescence. From March 1967 until July 1972, the US military\'s <a href="/wiki/Operation_Popeye" title="Operation Popeye">Operation Popeye</a> cloud-seeded silver iodide to extend the monsoon season over North Vietnam, specifically the Ho Chi Minh Trail. The operation resulted in the targeted areas seeing an extension of the monsoon period an average of 30 to 45 days.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The 54th Weather Reconnaissance Squadron carried out the operation to "make mud, not war".<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>One private organization that offered, during the 1970s, to conduct weather modification (cloud seeding from the ground using silver iodide flares) was Irving P. Krick and Associates of <a href="/wiki/Palm_Springs,_California" title="Palm Springs, California">Palm Springs, California</a>. They were contracted by <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_State_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oklahoma State University">Oklahoma State University</a> in 1972 to conduct a seeding project to increase warm cloud rainfall in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lake_Carl_Blackwell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lake Carl Blackwell (page does not exist)">Lake Carl Blackwell</a> <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">watershed</a>. That lake was, at that time (1972\xe2\x80\x9373), the primary water supply for <a href="/wiki/Stillwater,_Oklahoma" title="Stillwater, Oklahoma">Stillwater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and was dangerously low. The project did not operate for a long enough time to show statistically any change from natural <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/variation" class="extiw" title="wikt:variation">variations</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>An attempt by the United States military to modify <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_basin" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic basin">Atlantic basin</a> using cloud seeding in the 1960s was called <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a>. Only a few hurricanes were tested with cloud seeding because of the strict rules set by the scientists of the project. It was unclear whether the project was successful. Hurricanes appeared to change slightly in structure, but only temporarily. The fear that cloud seeding could potentially change the course or power of hurricanes and negatively affect people in the storm\'s path stopped the project.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Two federal agencies have supported various weather modification research projects, which began in the early-1960s: The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Reclamation" title="United States Bureau of Reclamation">United States Bureau of Reclamation</a> (Reclamation; Department of the Interior) and the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA; Department of Commerce). Reclamation sponsored several cloud seeding research projects under the umbrella of Project Skywater from 1964 to 1988, and NOAA conducted the Atmospheric Modification Program from 1979 to 1993. The sponsored projects were carried out in several states and two countries (Thailand and Morocco), studying both winter and summer cloud seeding. From 1962 to 1988 Reclamation developed cloud seeding applied research to augment water supplies in the western US. The research focused on winter orographic seeding to enhance snowfall in the Rocky Mountains and Sierra Nevada, and precipitation in coast ranges of southern California. In California Reclamation partnered with the California Department of Water Resources (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.water.ca.gov/">CDWR</a>) to sponsor the Serra Cooperative Pilot Project (SCPP), based in Auburn CA, to conduct seeding experiments in the central Sierra. The University of Nevada and <a href="/wiki/Desert_Research_Institute" title="Desert Research Institute">Desert Research Institute</a> provided cloud physics, physical chemistry, and other field support. The High Plains Cooperative Pilot Project (HIPLEX), focused on convective cloud seeding to increase rainfall during the growing season in Montana, Kansas, and Texas from 1974 to 1979. In 1979, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>, and other member-states led by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Spain" title="Government of Spain">Government of Spain</a> conducted a Precipitation Enhancement Project (PEP) in Spain,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> with inconclusive results due probably to location selection issues.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Reclamation sponsored research at several universities including Colorado State University, Universities of Wyoming, Washington, UCLA, Utah, Chicago, NYU, Montana, Colorado and research teams at Stanford, Meteorology Research Inc., and Penn State University, and South Dakota School of Mines and Technology, North Dakota, Texas A&amp;M, Texas Tech, and Oklahoma. Cooperative efforts with state water resources agencies in California, Colorado, Montana, Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas, and Arizona assured that the applied research met state water management needs. The High Plains Cooperative Pilot Project also engaged in partnerships with NASA, Environment Canada, and the National Center for Atmospheric Research (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://ncar.ucar.edu/">NCAR</a>). More recently, in cooperation with six western states, Reclamation sponsored a small cooperative research program called the Weather Damage Modification Program,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> from 2002\xe2\x80\x932006.</p>\n<p>In the United States, funding for research has declined in the last two decades. However, the Bureau of Reclamation sponsored a six-state research program from 2002\xe2\x80\x932006, called the "Weather Damage Modification Program".<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A 2003 study by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">United States National Academy of Sciences</a> urges a national research program to clear up remaining questions about weather modification\'s efficacy and practice.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Scientific_and_Industrial_Research_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation">Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation</a> (CSIRO) conducted major trials between 1947 and the early-1960s:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>1947&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 1952: CSIRO scientists dropped dry ice into the tops of clouds. The method worked reliably with clouds that were very cold, producing rain that would not have otherwise fallen.</li>\n<li>1953&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 1956: CSIRO carried out similar trials in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> and other states. Experiments used both ground-based and airborne <a href="/wiki/Silver_iodide" title="Silver iodide">silver iodide</a> generators.</li>\n<li>Late-1950s and early-1960s: Cloud seeding in the <a href="/wiki/Snowy_Mountains" title="Snowy Mountains">Snowy Mountains</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Cape_York_Peninsula" title="Cape York Peninsula">Cape York Peninsula</a> in Queensland, in the <a href="/wiki/New_England_(Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="New England (Australia)">New England</a> District of <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, and in the <a href="/wiki/Warragamba" class="mw-redirect" title="Warragamba">Warragamba</a> catchment area west of <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Only the trial conducted in the Snowy Mountains produced statistically significant rainfall increases over the entire experiment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>An Austrian study<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> to use silver iodide seeding for hail prevention ran during 1981\xe2\x80\x932000, and the technique is still actively deployed there.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The largest cloud seeding system is in the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People\'s Republic of China">People\'s Republic of China</a>. They believe that it increases the amount of rain over several increasingly <a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> regions, including its capital city, <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, by firing silver iodide <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rockets</a> into the sky where rain is desired. There is even political strife caused by neighboring regions that accuse each other of "stealing rain" using cloud seeding. About 24 countries currently practice weather modification operationally.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> China used cloud seeding in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a> just before the 2008 Olympic Games in order to have a dry Olympic season.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> In February 2009, China also blasted iodide sticks over Beijing to artificially induce snowfall after four months of drought, and blasted iodide sticks over other areas of northern China to increase snowfall. The snowfall in Beijing lasted for approximately three days and led to the closure of 12 main roads around Beijing.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup> At the end of October 2009 Beijing claimed it had its earliest snowfall since 1987 due to cloud seeding.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, cloud seeding operations were conducted during the years 1983, 1984\xe2\x80\x9387,1993-94 by Tamil Nadu Govt due to severe drought.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> In the years 2003 and 2004 Karnataka government initiated cloud seeding. Cloud seeding operations were also conducted in the same year through US-based Weather Modification Inc. in the state of <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> In 2008, there were plans for 12 districts of state of Andhra Pradesh.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a>, cloud seeding was used to minimize flood risk in anticipation of heavy floods in 2013, according to the Agency for the Assessment and Application of Technology.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Israel has been enhancing rain in convective clouds since the 1950s. The practice involves emitting silver iodide from airplanes and ground stations. The seeding takes place only in the northern parts of Israel. <sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kuwait">Kuwait</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kuwait">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>To counter drought and a growing population in a desert region, <a href="/wiki/Kuwait" title="Kuwait">Kuwait</a> is embarking on its own cloud seeding program, with the local Environment Public Authority conducting a study to gauge its viability locally.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">Southeast Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Southeast Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, open burning <a href="/wiki/Haze" title="Haze">haze</a> pollutes the regional environment. Cloud-seeding has been used to improve the air quality by encouraging rainfall.</p>\n<p>On 20 June 2013, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> said it will begin cloud-seeding operations following reports from <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> that smog caused by forest and bush fires in <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a> have disrupted daily activities in the neighboring countries.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> On 25 June 2013, hailstones were reported to have fallen over some parts of Singapore.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> Despite <a href="/wiki/National_Environment_Agency" title="National Environment Agency">NEA</a> denials, some believe that the hailstones are the result of cloud seeding in Indonesia.</p>\n<p>In 2015 cloud seeding was done daily in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> since the <a href="/wiki/2015_Southeast_Asian_haze" title="2015 Southeast Asian haze">haze</a> began in early-August.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Thailand started a rain-making project in the late-1950s, known today as the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Rainmaking_Project" title="Royal Rainmaking Project">Royal Rainmaking Project</a>. Its first efforts scattered sea salt in the air to catch the humidity and dry ice to condense the humidity to form clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The project took about ten years of experiments and refinement. The first field operations began in 1969 above <a href="/wiki/Khao_Yai_National_Park" title="Khao Yai National Park">Khao Yai National Park</a>. Since then the Thai government claims that rainmaking has been successfully applied throughout Thailand and neighboring countries.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup> On 12 October 2005 the <a href="/wiki/European_Patent_Office" title="European Patent Office">European Patent Office</a> granted to King <a href="/wiki/Bhumibol_Adulyadej" title="Bhumibol Adulyadej">Bhumibol Adulyadej</a> the patent EP 1 491 088 <i>Weather modification by royal rainmaking technology</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Arab_Emirates">United Arab Emirates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United Arab Emirates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The UAE is one of the first countries in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> region to use cloud seeding technology. It adopted the latest technologies available on a global level, using sophisticated weather radar to monitor the atmosphere of the country around the clock.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a>, cloud seeding is being conducted by the weather authorities to create artificial rain.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> The project, which began in July 2010 and cost US$11 million, has been successful in creating rain storms in the Dubai and Abu Dhabi deserts.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Arab_Emirates" title="United Arab Emirates">United Arab Emirates</a> has an arid climate with less than 100mm per year of <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rainfall</a>, a high evaporation rate of surface water and a low <a href="/wiki/Groundwater_recharge" title="Groundwater recharge">groundwater recharge</a> rate. Although rainfall in the UAE has been fluctuating over the last few decades in winter season, most of that occurs in the December to March period. During the summer months, the prevailing Indian <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">Monsoon</a> <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a> effect leads to a build-up of clouds especially along the mountainous terrain in the eastern UAE.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The UAE cloud-seeding Program was initiated in the late 1990s. By early 2001 the Program was being conducted in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research" title="National Center for Atmospheric Research">National Center for Atmospheric Research</a> (NCAR) in Colorado, USA, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_the_Witwatersrand" title="University of the Witwatersrand">Witwatersrand University</a> in South Africa and the US Space Agency, <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 2005, the UAE launched the UAE Prize for Excellence in Advancing the Science and Practice of Weather Modification in collaboration with the World Meteorological Organization <sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup>(WMO). This prize was thereafter reshaped into the International Research Program for Rain Enhancement Science.</p>\n<p>It subsequently became <a href="/wiki/The_UAE_Research_Program_for_Rain_Enhancement_Science" title="The UAE Research Program for Rain Enhancement Science">the UAE Research Program for Rain Enhancement Science</a> in January 2015. The Program for Rain Enhancement Science is an initiative of the United Arab Emirates Ministry of Presidential Affairs. It is overseen by the UAE National Center of Meteorology &amp; Seismology <sup id="cite_ref-:7_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-59">[59]</a></sup> (NCMS) based in <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi" title="Abu Dhabi">Abu Dhabi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-60">[60]</a></sup> Among its key goals are advancing the science, technology and implementation of rain enhancement and encouraging additional investments in research funding and research partnerships to advance the field, increasing rainfall and ensuring water security globally.</p>\n<p>The UAE now has more 75 networked automatic <a href="/wiki/Weather_station" title="Weather station">weather stations</a> distributed across the UAE, 7 air quality stations, a sophisticated <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> network of five stationary and one mobile radars, and six <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_King_Air" title="Beechcraft King Air">Beechcraft King Air</a> C90 aircraft for cloud seeding operations. Natural salts such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium_chloride" title="Potassium chloride">potassium chloride</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sodium_chloride" title="Sodium chloride">sodium chloride</a> are used in these operations.<sup id="cite_ref-:9_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:9-61">[61]</a></sup> At present, the UAE mostly seed with salt particles in the eastern mountains on the border to <a href="/wiki/Oman" title="Oman">Oman</a> to raise levels in aquifers and reservoirs.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Forecasters and scientists have estimated that cloud seeding operations can enhance rainfall by as much as 30 to 35 per cent in a clear atmosphere, and by up to 10 to 15 per cent in a turbid atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A total of 187 missions were sent to seed clouds in the UAE in 2015, with each aircraft taking about three hours to target five to six clouds at a cost of $3,000 per operation<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span cloud seeding is used to increase precipitation in areas experiencing <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">drought</a>, to reduce the size of <a href="/wiki/Hailstones" class="mw-redirect" Eleven western states and one Canadian province (Alberta) have ongoing weather modification operational programs <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.nawmc.org">[3]</a>. In January 2006, an $8.8 million cloud seeding project began in <a href="/wiki/Wyoming" title="Wyoming">Wyoming</a> to examine the effects of cloud seeding on snowfall over Wyoming\'s <a href="/wiki/Medicine_Bow_Mountains" title="Medicine Bow Mountains">Medicine Bow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Madre_Range_(Wyoming)" title="Sierra Madre Range (Wyoming)">Sierra Madre</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wind_River_Range" title="Wind River Range">Wind River</a> mountain ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A number of commercial companies offer weather modification services centered on cloud seeding.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. This utilized both aircraft and ground-based generators that pumped silver iodide into the atmosphere in an attempt to reduce the threat of hail damage. Ralph Langeman, <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Garrison" title="Lynn Garrison">Lynn Garrison</a>, and Stan McLeod, all ex-members of the RCAF\'s 403 Squadron, attending the University of Alberta, spent their summers flying hail suppression. that of the Spanish program in studies conducted by the Spanish Agricultural Ministry.<sup id="cite_ref-francespain_69-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-francespain-69">[69]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> created a specifically designed version of the <a href="/wiki/Antonov_An-30" title="Antonov An-30">Antonov An-30</a> aerial survey aircraft, the An-30M Sky Cleaner, with eight containers of solid <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> in the cargo area plus external pods containing meteorological cartridges that could be fired into clouds.<sup id="cite_ref-gunston95_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gunston95-70">[70]</a></sup> Soviet military pilots seeded clouds over the <a href="/wiki/Belorussian_SSR" class="mw-redirect" title="Belorussian SSR">Belorussian SSR</a> after the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a> to remove radioactive particles from clouds heading toward <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> Currently, <a href="/wiki/An-26" class="mw-redirect" title="An-26">An-26</a> is also used for cloud seeding.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> At the July 2006 <a href="/wiki/G8" class="mw-redirect" title="G8">G8</a> Summit in <a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>, President Putin commented that air force jets had been deployed to seed incoming clouds so they rained over Finland. Another society for cloud seeding operates in the district of <a href="/wiki/Villingen-Schwenningen" title="Villingen-Schwenningen">Villingen-Schwenningen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovenia">Slovenia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cloud_seeding&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Slovenia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>In Slovenia oldest aeroclub: <a href="/wiki/Letalski_center_Maribor" title="Letalski center Maribor">Letalski center Maribor</a> carries air defense against hail. The summit met with the intention of outlining a proposal for a 5-year trial, focusing on Northern NSW.</p>\n<p>The various implications of such a widespread trial were discussed, drawing on the combined knowledge of several worldwide experts, including representatives from the Tasmanian Hydro Cloud Seeding Project however does not make reference to former cloud seeding experiments by the then-Snowy Mountains Authority, which rejected weather modification.
