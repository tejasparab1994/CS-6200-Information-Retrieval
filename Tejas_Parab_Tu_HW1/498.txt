src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Mediterranean_Cyclone_16_jan_1995_1004Z.jpg/250px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_16_jan_1995_1004Z.jpg" width="250" height="250" class="thumbimage" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_16_jan_1995_1004Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSatellite imagery of a well-documented Mediterranean tropical-like cyclone on 16 January 1995</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Mediterranean tropical-like cyclones</b>, sometimes referred to as <b>Mediterranean hurricanes</b> or <b>medicanes</b>, are rare meteorological phenomena observed in the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a>. Due to the dry nature of the Mediterranean region, formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> is infrequent, with only 99&#160;recorded tropical-like storms between 1948 and 2011. No agency, however, is officially responsible for monitoring the formation and development of medicanes. <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a> typically occurs within two separate regions of the sea. The first region, encompassing areas of the western Mediterranean, is more conducive for development than the other, the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a> to the east. The rough mountainous geography of the region raises additional difficulties despite being favorable for the development of severe weather and convective activity in general, and only with abnormal meteorological circumstances can medicanes form. Numerous studies have been conducted on the impact of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> on Mediterranean tropical cyclone formation, generally concluding that fewer yet more intense storms would form.</p>\n<p>The development of tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean Sea can usually only occur under somewhat unusual circumstances. Low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmospheric</a> instability induced by incursions of cold air are often required. A majority of medicanes are also accompanied by upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">troughs</a>, providing energy required for intensifying <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>\xe2\x80\x94thunderstorms\xe2\x80\x94and heavy <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> properties of the Mediterranean region, with high temperature gradients, also provides necessary instability for the formation of tropical cyclones. Another factor, rising cool air, provides necessary moisture as well. Warm <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> are mostly unnecessary, however, as most medicanes\' energy is derived from warmer air temperatures. When these favorable circumstances coincide, the genesis of warm-core Mediterranean tropical cyclones, often from within existing cut-off <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core lows</a>, is possible in a conducive environment for formation.</p>\n<p>Several notable and damaging medicanes are known to have occurred. In September 1969, a north African Mediterranean tropical cyclone produced flooding that killed nearly 600&#160;individuals, left 250,000&#160;homeless, and crippled local economies. A medicane in September&#160;1996 that developed in the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> region spawned six tornadoes and inundated parts of the islands. Several medicanes have also been subject to extensive study, such as those of January&#160;1982, January&#160;1995, September&#160;2006, November&#160;2011, and November&#160;2014. The January&#160;1995 storm is one of the best-studied Mediterranean tropical cyclones, with its close resemblance to tropical cyclones elsewhere and availability of observations. The medicane of September&#160;2006, meanwhile, is well-studied due to availability of existing observations and data. In November&#160;2011, the NOAA\'s <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> monitored a medicane, named Rolf by the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin (FU Berlin)</a>, though it ceased doing so the following month. In 2015, the NOAA resumed issuing advisories for tropical systems in the Mediterranean region.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg" width="200" height="179" class="thumbimage" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_1202Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nVisible satellite imagery of a medicane above the Balearic Islands on 7 October 1996</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> are not considered to be formally classified tropical cyclones and their region of formation is not officially monitored by any agency,<sup id="cite_ref-TCFAQ_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCFAQ-1">[1]</a></sup> and though the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> released information related to <a href="#November_2011_.28Rolf.2F01M.29">a medicane in November&#160;2011</a> while it was active, it ceased doing so on 16&#160;December 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup> The Mediterranean Sea, however, lies within the <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greek</a> area of responsibility,<sup id="cite_ref-AORs_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AORs-3">[3]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9t%C3%A9o-France" title="M\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9o-France">M\xc3\xa9t\xc3\xa9o-France</a> National serves as a "preparation service" for the areas as well.<sup id="cite_ref-AORs3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AORs3-4">[4]</a></sup> A majority of Mediterranean tropical cyclones form over two separate regions. The first, more conducive for development than the other, encompasses an area of the western Mediterranean bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a>, southern France, and the shorelines of the islands of <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a>. The second identified region of development, in the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a> between <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> and Greece and stretching south to <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>, is less favorable for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">tropical cyclogenesis</a>. An additional two regions, in the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic</a> seas, produce fewer medicanes, while activity is minimal in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levantine</a> region. The geographical distribution of Mediterranean tropical cyclones is markedly different than that of other <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a>, with the formation of regular cyclones centering on the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas</a> mountain ranges, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Genoa" title="Gulf of Genoa">Gulf of Genoa</a>, and the island of <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> Although meteorological factors are most advantageous in the Adriatic and Aegean seas, the closed nature of the region\'s geography, bordered by land, allows little time for further evolution.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-18_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-18-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The geography of mountain ranges bordering the Mediterranean are conducive for severe weather and thunderstorms, with the sloped nature of mountainous regions permitting the development of convective activity.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> Although the geography of the Mediterranean region, as well as its dry air, typically prevent the formation of tropical cyclones, when certain meteorological circumstances arise, difficulties influenced by the region\'s geography are overcome.<sup id="cite_ref-Emanuel2005-220_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel2005-220-8">[8]</a></sup> The occurrence of tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean Sea is generally extremely rare, with an average of 1.57&#160;forming annually and merely 99&#160;recorded occurrences of tropical-like storms discovered between 1948 and 2011 in a modern study, with no definitive trend in activity in that period.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-6_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-6-9">[9]</a></sup> Few medicanes form during the summer season, though activity typically rises in autumn, peaks in January, and gradually decreases from February to May.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> In the western Mediterranean region of development, approximately 0.75&#160;such systems form each year, compared to 0.32&#160;in the Ionian Sea region.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-8_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-8-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Studies have evaluated that <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> can result in higher observed intensities of tropical cyclones as a result of deviations in the <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">surface energy flux</a> and atmospheric composition, which both heavily influence the development of medicanes as well. In tropical and subtropical areas, <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> rose 0.2&#160;\xc2\xb0C (0.36&#160;\xc2\xb0F) within a 50-year period, and in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">Western Pacific</a> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>, the potential destructiveness and energy of storms nearly doubled within the same duration, evidencing a clear correlation between global warming and tropical cyclone intensities.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-9_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-9-11">[11]</a></sup> Within a similarly recent 20-year period,<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-10_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-10-12">[12]</a></sup> SSTs in the Mediterranean Sea increased by 0.6 to 1&#160;\xc2\xb0C (1.1 to 1.8&#160;\xc2\xb0F),<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-9_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-9-11">[11]</a></sup> though no observable increase in medicane activity has been noted, as of yet.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-6_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-6-9">[9]</a></sup> In 2006, a computer-driven <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_model" title="Atmospheric model">atmospheric model</a> evaluated the future frequency of Mediterranean cyclones between 2071 and 2100, projecting a decrease in autumn, winter, and spring cyclonic activity coinciding with a dramatic increase in formation near Cyprus, with both scenarios attributed to elevated temperatures as a result of global warming.<sup id="cite_ref-Anagnostopoulou2006-13_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Anagnostopoulou2006-13-13">[13]</a></sup> Other studies, however, have been inconclusive, forecasting both increases and decreases in duration, number, and intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-Gaertner2007-4_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaertner2007-4-14">[14]</a></sup> Three independent studies, using different methodologies and data, evaluated that while medicane activity would likely decline with a rate depending on the climate scenario considered, a higher percentage of those that formed would be of greater strength.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2014_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2014-15">[15]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RomeroEmanuel2013_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RomeroEmanuel2013-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Walsh2014_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Walsh2014-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_and_characteristics">Development and characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development and characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg" width="200" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3260" data-file-height="2476" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_October_27_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA Mediterranean tropical cyclone south of Italy on 27 October 2005</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Factors required for the formation of medicanes are somewhat different than that normally expected of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>; known to emerge over regions with <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> below 26&#160;\xc2\xb0C (79&#160;\xc2\xb0F), Mediterranean tropical cyclones often require incursions of colder air to induce atmospheric instability.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-7_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-7-5">[5]</a></sup> A majority of medicanes develop above regions of the Mediterranean with SSTs of 15 to 26&#160;\xc2\xb0C (59 to 79&#160;\xc2\xb0F), with the upper bound only found in the southernmost reaches of the sea. Despite the low sea surface temperatures, the instability incited by cold atmospheric air within a <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> zone\xe2\x80\x94regions with high differences in temperature and pressure\xe2\x80\x94permits the formation of medicanes, in contrast to tropical areas lacking high baroclinity, where raised SSTs are needed.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-8_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-8-18">[18]</a></sup> While significant deviations in air temperature have been noted around the time of Mediterranean tropical cyclones\' formation, few anomalies in sea surface temperature coincide with their development, indicating that the formation of medicanes is primarily controlled by higher air temperatures, not by anomalous SSTs.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-14_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-14-19">[19]</a></sup> Similar to tropical cyclones, minimal <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>\xe2\x80\x94difference in wind speed and direction over a region\xe2\x80\x94as well as abundant moisture and vorticity encourages the genesis of tropical cyclone-like systems in the Mediterranean Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-Cavicchia2013-15_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cavicchia2013-15-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Due to the confined character of the Mediterranean and the limited capability of <a href="/wiki/Heat_flux" title="Heat flux">heat fluxes</a>\xe2\x80\x94in the case of medicanes, air\xe2\x80\x94sea heat transfer\xe2\x80\x94tropical cyclones with a diameter larger than 300&#160;km (190&#160;mi) cannot exist within the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-3_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-3-21">[21]</a></sup> Despite being a relatively baroclinic area with high temperature gradients, the primary energy source utilized by Mediterranean tropical cyclones is derived from underlying heat sources generated by the presence of convection\xe2\x80\x94thunderstorm activity\xe2\x80\x94in a humid environment, similar to tropical cyclones elsewhere outside the Mediterranean Sea.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-5_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-5-22">[22]</a></sup> In comparison to other <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">tropical cyclone basins</a>, the Mediterranean Sea generally presents a difficult environment for development; although the potential energy necessary for development is not abnormally large, its atmosphere is characterized by its lack of moisture, impeding potential formation. The full development of a medicane often necessitates the formation of a large-scale baroclinic disturbance transitioning late in its life cycle into a tropical cyclone-like system, nearly always under the influence of a deep, cut-off, <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core low</a> within the middle-to-upper <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, frequently resulting from abnormalities in a wide-spreading <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby wave</a>\xe2\x80\x94massive <a href="/wiki/Meanders" class="mw-redirect" title="Meanders">meanders</a> of upper-atmospheric winds.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-6_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-6-23">[23]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg" width="200" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4092" data-file-height="3816" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_January_28_2010.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA weak and disorganized Mediterranean tropical cyclone on 28 January 2010</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The development of medicanes often results from the vertical shift of air in the troposphere as well, resulting in a decrease in its temperature coinciding with an increase in relative humidity, creating an environment more conducive for tropical cyclone formation. This, in turn, leads to in an increase in potential energy, producing heat-induced air-sea instability. Moist air prevents the occurrence of convective <a href="/wiki/Vertical_draft" title="Vertical draft">downdrafts</a>\xe2\x80\x94the vertically downward movement of air\xe2\x80\x94which often hinder the inception of tropical cyclones,<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-6_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-6-23">[23]</a></sup> and in such a scenario, wind shear remains minimal; overall, cold-core cut-off lows serve well for the later formation of compact <a href="/wiki/Flux" title="Flux">surface flux</a>-influenced warm-core lows such as medicanes. The regular genesis of cold-core upper-level lows and the infrequency of Mediterranean tropical cyclones, however, indicate that additional unusual circumstances are involved the emergence of the latter. Elevated sea surface temperatures, contrasting with cold atmospheric air, encourage atmospheric instability, especially within the troposphere.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-8_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-8-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In general, most medicanes maintain a radius of 70 to 200&#160;km (40 to 120&#160;mi), last between 12&#160;hours and 5&#160;days, travel between 700 to 3,000&#160;km (430 to 1,860&#160;mi), develop an eye for less than 72&#160;hours, and feature wind speeds of up to 144&#160;km/h (89&#160;mph);<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2273_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2273-24">[24]</a></sup> in addition, a majority are characterized on <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">satellite imagery</a> as asymmetric systems with a distinct round <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> encircled by <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-3_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-3-21">[21]</a></sup> Weak rotation, similar to that in most tropical cyclones, is usually noted in a medicane\'s early stages, increasing with intensity;<sup id="cite_ref-Fita2007-43_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-43-25">[25]</a></sup> medicanes, however, often have less time to intensify, remaining weaker than most <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic hurricane">North Atlantic hurricanes</a> and only persisting for the duration of a few days.<sup id="cite_ref-Fita2007-53_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-53-26">[26]</a></sup> The maximum achievable intensity of medicanes is equivalent to the lowest classification on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>, Category&#160;1. While the entire lifetime of a cyclone may encompass several days, most will only retain tropical characteristics for less than 24&#160;hours.<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2402_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2402-27">[27]</a></sup> Circumstances sometimes permit the formation of smaller-scale medicanes, although the required conditions differ even from those needed by other medicanes. The development of abnormally small tropical cyclones in the Mediterranean usually requires upper-level atmospheric cyclones inducing cyclogenesis in the lower atmosphere, leading to the formation of warm-core lows, encouraged by favorable moisture, heat, and other environmental circumstances.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Mediterranean cyclones have been compared to <a href="/wiki/Polar_low" title="Polar low">polar lows</a>\xe2\x80\x94cyclonic storms which typically develop in the far regions of the Northern and Southern Hemispheres\xe2\x80\x94for their similarly small size and heat-related instability; however, while medicanes nearly always feature warm-core lows, polar lows are primarily cold-core. The prolonged life of medicanes and similarity to polar lows is caused primarily by origins as synoptic-scale surface lows and heat-related instability.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> Heavy precipitation and convection within a developing Mediterranean tropical cyclone are usually incited by the approach of an upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>\xe2\x80\x94an elongated area of low air pressures\xe2\x80\x94bringing downstream cold air, encircling an existing low-pressure system. After this occurs, however, a considerable reduction in rainfall rates occurs despite further organization,<sup id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2211_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2211-29">[29]</a></sup> coinciding with a decrease in previously high lightning activity as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2404_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2404-30">[30]</a></sup> Although troughs will often accompany medicanes along their track, separation eventually occurs, usually in the later part of a Mediterranean tropical cyclone\'s life cycle.<sup id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2211_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2211-29">[29]</a></sup> At the same time, moist air, saturated and cooled while rising into the atmosphere, then encounters the medicane and permits further development and evolution into a tropical cyclone. Many of these characteristics are also evident in polar lows.<sup id="cite_ref-Emanuel2005-217_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emanuel2005-217-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_storms_and_impacts">Notable storms and impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Notable storms and impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_1969">September 1969</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: September 1969">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png" width="200" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/db/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png 1.5x" data-file-width="241" data-file-height="225" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_23_September_1969_0909Z.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nESSA-8 visible satellite imagery of the September 1969 cyclone</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>An unusually severe Mediterranean tropical cyclone developed on 23&#160;September 1969 southeast of <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a>, producing severe flooding.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> Steep pressure and temperature gradients above the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> were evident on 19&#160;September, a result of cool sea air attempting to penetrate inland; south of the mountains, a lee depression\xe2\x80\x94a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> in a mountainous region\xe2\x80\x94developed. Under the influence of mountainous terrain, the low initially meandered northeastward. Following the entry of cool sea air, however, it recurved to the southeast before transitioning into a Saharan depression associated with a distinct cold front by 22&#160;September. Along the front\'s path, desert air moved northward while cold air drifted in the opposite direction, and in northern Libya, warm arid air clashed with the cooler <a href="/wiki/Levant_(wind)" title="Levant (wind)">levant</a> of the Mediterranean. The organization of the disturbance improved slightly further before emerging into the Mediterranean Sea on 23&#160;September, upon which the system experienced immediate <a href="/wiki/Cyclogenesis" title="Cyclogenesis">cyclogenesis</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-393_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-393-33">[33]</a></sup> rapidly intensifying while southeast of Malta as a cold-core cut-off low,<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-396_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-396-34">[34]</a></sup> and acquiring tropical characteristics.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> In western Africa, meanwhile, several disturbances converged toward <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">Mauritania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a>, while the medicane recurved southwestward back toward the coast, losing its closed circulation and later dissipating.<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-396_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-396-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The cyclone produced severe flooding throughout regions of northern Africa. Malta received upward of 123&#160;mm (4.8&#160;in) of rainfall on 23&#160;September, <a href="/wiki/Sfax" title="Sfax">Sfax</a> measured 45&#160;mm (1.8&#160;in) on 24&#160;September, <a href="/wiki/Tizi_Ouzou" title="Tizi Ouzou">Tizi Ouzou</a> collected 55&#160;mm (2.2&#160;in) on 25&#160;September, <a href="/wiki/Gafsa" title="Gafsa">Gafsa</a> received 79&#160;mm (3.1&#160;in) and <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> measured 46&#160;mm (1.8&#160;in) on 26&#160;September, Cap Bengut collected 43&#160;mm (1.7&#160;in) on 27&#160;September, and <a href="/wiki/Biskra" title="Biskra">Biskra</a> received 122&#160;mm (4.8&#160;in) on 28&#160;September.<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-392_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-392-35">[35]</a></sup> In Malta, a 20000-ton tanker struck a reef and split in two, while in Gafsa, Tunisia, the cyclone flooded <a href="/wiki/Phosphate" title="Phosphate">phosphate</a> mines, leaving over 25,000&#160;miners unemployed and costing the government over \xc2\xa32&#160;million per week. Thousands of camels and snakes, drowned by flood waters, were swept out to sea, and massive <a href="/wiki/Roman_bridge" title="Roman bridge">Roman bridges</a>, which withstood all floods since the <a href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">fall of the Roman Empire</a>, collapsed. In all, the floods in Tunisia and Algeria killed almost 600&#160;individuals, left 250,000&#160;homeless, and severely damaged regional economies.<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-390_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-390-36">[36]</a></sup> Due to communication problems, however, flood relief funds and television appeals were not set up until nearly a month later.<sup id="cite_ref-Winstanley1970-392_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Winstanley1970-392-35">[35]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January_1982">January 1982</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: January 1982">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png" width="200" height="231" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png/300px-Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png/400px-Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png 2x" data-file-width="1303" data-file-height="1502" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_storm_26_January_1982_1236Z.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nNOAA-7 visible satellite imagery of a Mediterranean tropical storm at 1236 UTC on 26 January 1982</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The unusual Mediterranean tropical storm of January&#160;1982 was first detected in waters north of <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libya</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> The storm likely reached the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a> by 23&#160;January 1982, reinforced by an elongated, slowly-drifting trough above the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>. Eventually, a closed circulation center developed by 1310&#160;<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-333_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-333-37">[37]</a></sup> over parts of the Mediterranean with <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> of approximately 16&#160;\xc2\xb0C (61&#160;\xc2\xb0F) and air temperature of 12&#160;\xc2\xb0C (54&#160;\xc2\xb0F).<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup> A hook-shaped cloud developed within the system shortly thereafter, rotating as it elongated into a 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi)-long comma-shaped apparatus. After looping around Sicily, it drifted eastward between the island and <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>, recurving on its track again,<sup id="cite_ref-Reed2001-187_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed2001-187-39">[39]</a></sup> exhibiting clearly curved spiral banding before shrinking slightly.<sup id="cite_ref-Reed2001-189_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed2001-189-40">[40]</a></sup> The cyclone reached its peak intensity at 1800&#160;UTC on the following day, maintaining an <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 992&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.30&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>), and was succeeded by a period of gradual weakening, with the system\'s pressure eventually rising to 1009&#160;mbar (29.80&#160;inHg). The system slightly reintensified, however, for a six-hour period on 26&#160;January. Ship reports indicated winds of 93&#160;km/h (57.7&#160;mph or 50.2 kt) were present in the cyclone at the time, tropical storm-force winds on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-333_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-333-37">[37]</a></sup> likely near the eyewall of the cyclone, which features the highest winds in a tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Global Weather Center\'s Cyclone Weather Center of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">United States Air Force (USAF)</a> initiated "Mediterranean Cyclone Advisories" on the cyclone at six-hour intervals starting at 1800&#160;UTC on 27&#160;January, until 0600&#160;UTC on the following day.<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-337_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-337-41">[41]</a></sup> Convection was most intense in the eastern sector of the cyclone as it drifted east-northeastward. On infrared satellite imagery, the <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> itself was 58.5&#160;km (36.4&#160;mi) in diameter,<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-334_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-334-38">[38]</a></sup> contracting to just 28 kilometres (17&#160;mi) one day prior to making landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-ErnstMatson1983-337_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ErnstMatson1983-337-41">[41]</a></sup> The cyclone passed by Malta, Italy, and Greece before dissipating several days later in the extreme eastern Mediterranean. Observations related to the cyclone, however, were inadequate, and although the system maintained numerous tropical characteristics, it is possible it was merely a compact but powerful <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> exhibiting a clear eye, spiral banding, towering <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbi</a>, and high surface winds along the eyewall.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="January_1995">January 1995</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: January 1995">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="580" data-file-height="580" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_15_jan_1995_1015Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe 1995 Medicane at 1015 UTC on 15&#160;January, shortly after tropical cyclogenesis</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Among numerous documented medicanes, the cyclone of January&#160;1995 is generally considered to be the best-documented instance. Emerging off of the <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">Libyan</a> coast into the central <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> toward the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian</a> shoreline of <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> on 13&#160;January as a compact <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure area</a>, the precursor low maintained winds reaching up to 108&#160;km/h (67&#160;mph) as it traversed the Ionian Sea,<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280-42">[42]</a></sup> while the German research ship <i>Meteor</i> noted winds of 135&#160;km/h (84&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup> Upon the low\'s approach near Greece, it began to envelop an area of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_convection" title="Atmospheric convection">atmospheric convection</a>; meanwhile, in the middle <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended from Russia to the Mediterranean, bringing with it extremely cold temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> Two low-pressure areas were present along the path of the trough, with one situated above Ukraine and the other above the central Mediterranean, likely associated with a low-level cyclone over western Greece. Upon weakening and dissipation on 14&#160;January, a second low, the system which would evolve into the Mediterranean tropical cyclone, developed in its place on 15&#160;January.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At the time of formation, high clouds indicated the presence of intense convection,<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-263_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-263-43">[43]</a></sup> and the cyclone featured an axisymmetric cloud structure, with a distinct, cloud-free <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">rainbands</a> spiraling around the disturbance as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-264_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-264-45">[45]</a></sup> Soon thereafter, the parent low separated from the medicane entirely and continued eastward,<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> meandering toward the <a href="/wiki/Aegean_Sea" title="Aegean Sea">Aegean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2280-42">[42]</a></sup> Initially remaining stationary between Greece and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a> with a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of 1002&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.59&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>), the newly formed system began to drift southwest-to-south in the following days, influenced by northeasterly flow incited by the initial low, now far to the east, and a <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure area</a> above central and eastern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> The system\'s atmospheric pressure increased throughout 15&#160;January due to the fact it was embedded within a large-scale environment, with its rising pressure due to the general prevalence of higher air pressures throughout the region, and was not a sign of deintensification.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-264_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-264-45">[45]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Initial wind speeds within the young medicane were generally low, with sustained winds of merely 28 to 46&#160;km/h (17 to 29&#160;mph), with the highest recorded value associated with the disturbance being 63&#160;km/h (39&#160;mph) at 0000&#160;UTC on 16&#160;January, slightly below the threshold for tropical storm on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>. Its structure now consisted of a distinct eye encircled by counterclockwise-rotating <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbi</a> with <a href="/wiki/Cloud_top" title="Cloud top">cloud top</a> temperatures colder than \xe2\x88\x9250&#160;\xc2\xb0C (\xe2\x88\x9258&#160;\xc2\xb0F), evidencing deep convection and a regular feature observed in most tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-265_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-265-46">[46]</a></sup> At 1200 UTC on 16 January, a ship recorded winds blowing east-southeast of about 50 knots (93&#160;km/h) south-southwest about 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) north-northeast of the cyclone\'s center.<sup id="cite_ref-BlierMa1997_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BlierMa1997-47">[47]</a></sup> Intense convection continued to follow the entire path of the system as it traversed the Mediterranean, and the cyclone made <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in northern Libya at approximately 1800&#160;<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on 17&#160;January, rapidly weakening after coming ashore.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis1999-628_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis1999-628-44">[44]</a></sup> As it moved inland, a minimum atmospheric pressure of 1012&#160;mbar (29.89&#160;inHg) was recorded, accompanied by wind speeds of 93&#160;km/h (58&#160;mph) as it slowed down after passing through the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Sidra" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf of Sidra">Gulf of Sidra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-266_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-266-48">[48]</a></sup> Although the system retained its strong convection for several more hours, the cyclone\'s cloud tops began to warm, evidencing lower clouds, before losing tropical characteristics entirely on 17&#160;January.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-267_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-267-49">[49]</a></sup> Offshore ship reports recorded that the medicane produced intense winds, copious rainfall, and abnormally warm temperatures.<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281-50">[50]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_and_October_1996">September and October 1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: September and October 1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg" width="200" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="540" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_Cyclone_07_oct_1996_0603Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe last major Mediterranean tropical cyclone of 1996 while west of Italy on 7&#160;October</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Three notable medicanes developed in 1996. The first, in mid-September&#160;1996, was a typical Mediterranean tropical cyclone that developed in the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> region.<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> At the time of the cyclone\'s formation, a powerful Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Warm_front" title="Warm front">warm front</a> associated with a large-scale low, producing northeasterly winds over the Iberian peninsula, extended eastward into the Mediterranean, while abundant moisture gathered in the lower troposphere over the Balearic channel.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1473_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1473-52">[52]</a></sup> On the morning of 12&#160;September, a disturbance developed off of <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">Valencia, Spain</a>, dropping heavy rainfall on the coast even without coming ashore. An eye developed shortly thereafter as the system rapidly traversed across <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">Majorca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> in its eastward trek. It made <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> upon the coast of southern Italy on the evening of 13&#160;September with a minimum atmospheric pressure of 990&#160;mbar (29.24&#160;inHg), dissipating shortly after coming ashore,<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2283_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2283-53">[53]</a></sup> with a diameter of about 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At Valencia and other regions of eastern Spain, the storm generated heavy precipitation, while six tornadoes touched down over the Balearic Islands. While approaching the coast of the Balearic Islands, the warm-core low induced a pressure drop of 11&#160;mbar (0.32&#160;inHg) at <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Majorca" title="Palma, Majorca">Palma, Majorca</a> in advance of the tropical cyclone\'s landfall. Medicanes as small as the one that formed in September&#160;1996 are atypical, and often require circumstances different even from those required for regular Mediterranean tropical cyclone formation.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1469_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1469-28">[28]</a></sup> Warm low-level <a href="/wiki/Advection" title="Advection">advection</a>\xe2\x80\x93transfer of heat through air or sea\xe2\x80\x93caused by a large-scale low over the western Mediterranean was a primary factor in the rise of strong convection.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1470_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1470-7">[7]</a></sup> The presence of a mid- to upper-level cut-off <a href="/wiki/Cold-core_low" title="Cold-core low">cold-core low</a>, a method of formation typical to medicanes, was also key to the development of intense thunderstorms within the cyclone. In addition, interaction between a northeastward-drifting <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, the medicane, and the large-scale also permitted the formation of tornadoes within thunderstorms generated by the cyclone after making landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-Homar2003-1471_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Homar2003-1471-54">[54]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The second of the three recorded Mediterranean tropical cyclones in 1996 formed between Sicily and <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> on 4&#160;October, making landfall on both Sicily and southern Italy. The medicane generated major flooding in Sicily; meanwhile, in <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, wind gusts of up to 108&#160;km/h (67&#160;mph) were reported in addition to severe inundation.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup> The third major Mediterranean tropical cyclone of that year formed north of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a> and strengthened while sweeping between the Balearic Islands and Sardinia, with an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature prominent on satellite. The eye was distorted and disappeared after transiting over southern Sardinia throughout the evening of 8&#160;October, with the system weakening as a whole. On the morning of October&#160;9, a smaller eye emerged as the system passed over the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian Sea</a>, gradually strengthening, with reports 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi) from the storm\'s center reporting winds of 90&#160;km/h (56&#160;mph). Extreme damage was reported in the <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_Islands" title="Aeolian Islands">Aeolian Islands</a> after the tropical cyclone passed north of Sicily, though the system dissipated while turning southward over Calabria. Overall, the lowest estimated atmospheric pressure in the third medicane was 998&#160;mbar (29.47&#160;inHg).<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2284_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2284-55">[55]</a></sup> Both October systems featured distinctive spiral bands, intense convection, high sustained winds, and abundant precipitation.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="September_2006">September 2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: September 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg/200px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg" width="200" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg/300px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg/400px-Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4389" data-file-height="3825" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mediterranean_tropical_cyclone_September_26_2006.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe Mediterranean tropical cyclone of 26 September 2006</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A short-lived medicane developed near the end of September&#160;2006 along the coast of Italy. The origins of the medicane can be traced to the alpine <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Mountains" title="Atlas Mountains">Atlas mountain range</a> on the evening of 25&#160;September,<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> likely forming as a normal lee cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2203_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2203-56">[56]</a></sup> At 0600&#160;<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on 26&#160;September, <a href="/wiki/European_Centre_for_Medium-Range_Weather_Forecasts" title="European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts">European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF)</a> model analyses indicated the existence of two <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low-pressure areas</a> along the shoreline of Italy, one on the west coast, sweeping eastward across the <a href="/wiki/Tyrrhenian_Sea" title="Tyrrhenian Sea">Tyrrhenian Sea</a>, while the other, slightly more intense, low was located over the <a href="/wiki/Ionian_Sea" title="Ionian Sea">Ionian Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> As the latter low approached the <a href="/wiki/Strait_of_Sicily" title="Strait of Sicily">Strait of Sicily</a>, it met an eastward-moving convection-producing <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold front</a>, resulting in significant intensification, while the system simultaneously reduced in size.<sup id="cite_ref-Claud2010-2203_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Claud2010-2203-56">[56]</a></sup> It then achieved a minimum <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">atmospheric pressure</a> of approximately 986&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.12&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>) after transiting north-northeastward across the 40&#160;km (25&#160;mi)-wide <a href="/wiki/Salento" title="Salento">Salentine peninsula</a> in the course of roughly 30&#160;minutes at 0915&#160;UTC the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Wind gusts surpassing 144&#160;km/h (89&#160;mph) were recorded as it passed over Salento due to a steep <a href="/wiki/Pressure_gradient" title="Pressure gradient">pressure gradient</a> associated with it, confirmed by regional <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">radar observations</a> denoting the presence of a clear <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> The high winds inflicted moderate damages throughout the peninsula, though specific damage is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2282-51">[51]</a></sup> Around 1000&#160;UTC,<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> both radar and satellite recorded the system\'s entry into the <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Sea" title="Adriatic Sea">Adriatic Sea</a> and its gradual northwestward curve back toward the Italian coast. By 1700&#160;UTC, the cyclone made <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">Apulia</a> while maintaining its intensity, with a minimum atmospheric pressure at 988&#160;mbar (29.18&#160;inHg). The cyclone weakened while drifting further inland over the Italian mainland, eventually dissipating as it curved west-southwestward. A later study in 2008 evaluated that the cyclone possessed numerous characteristics seen in tropical cyclones elsewhere, with a spiral appearance, eye-like apparatus, rapid atmospheric pressure decreases in advance of landfall, and intense sustained winds, concentrated near the storm\'s eyewall;<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4375_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4375-58">[58]</a></sup> the apparent eye-like structure in the cyclone, however, was ill-defined.<sup id="cite_ref-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CavicchiavonStorch-2012-2281-50">[50]</a></sup> Since then, the medicane has been the subject of significant study as a result of the availability of scientific observations and reports related to the cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-Moscatello2008-4374_57-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moscatello2008-4374-57">[57]</a></sup> In particular, the sensitivity of this cyclone to sea-surface temperatures,<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2011_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2011-59">[59]</a></sup> initial conditions, the model,<sup id="cite_ref-Davolio2009_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davolio2009-60">[60]</a></sup> and the parameterization schemes used in the simulations were analyzed.<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2015_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2015-61">[61]</a></sup> The relevance of different instability indices for the diagnosis and the prediction of these events were also studied.<sup id="cite_ref-Conte2010_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conte2010-62">[62]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="November_2011_(Rolf/01M)"></span><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Tropical-like_cyclone_01-M_Rolf.2011312.terra.250m.jpg/200px-Tropical-like_cyclone_01-M_Rolf.2011312.terra.250m.jpg" width="200" height="175" data-file-height="6020" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical-like_cyclone_01-M_Rolf.2011312.terra.250m.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTropical Storm Rolf at peak intensity on 8 November 2011</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>In November&#160;2011, the first officially designated Mediterranean tropical cyclone by the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)</a> formed, christened as 01M by the <a href="/wiki/Satellite_Analysis_Branch" title="Satellite Analysis Branch">Satellite Analysis Branch</a> and given the name Rolf by the <a href="/wiki/Free_University_of_Berlin" title="Free University of Berlin">Free University of Berlin (FU Berlin)</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> despite the fact that no agency is officially responsible for monitoring tropical cyclone activity in the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-TCFAQ_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCFAQ-1">[1]</a></sup> On 4&#160;November 2011, a frontal system associated with another low-pressure area monitored by FU Berlin, designated Quinn, spawned a second low-pressure system inland near <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, which was subsequently named Rolf by the university. An upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> on the European mainland stalled as it approached the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> before approaching and interacting with the low known as Rolf. Heavy rainfall consequently fell over regions of southern France and northwestern Italy, resulting in widespread <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a> and flooding. On 5&#160;November, Rolf slowed while stationed above the <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">Massif Central</a>, maintaining a pressure of 1000&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a> (29.53&#160;<a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary front</a>, stationed between <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, approached Rolf the same day, with the cold front later encountering and become associated with Rolf, which would continue for several days.<sup id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On 6&#160;November, the cyclone drifted toward the Mediterranean from the southern shoreline of France, shrinking to a diameter of merely 150&#160;km (93&#160;mi) across. Slightly weakening, Rolf neared the <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">Balearic Islands</a> on 7&#160;November, associated with two fronts producing heavy rain throughout Europe, before separating entirely and transitioning into a cut-off low.<sup id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> The same day, the NOAA began monitoring the system, designating it as 01M, marking the first time it officially monitored a medicane. A distinct <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature developed while spiral banding and intense convection became evident. At its highest, the <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">Dvorak technique</a> classified the system as T3.0. Convection gradually decreased, and a misalignment of the mid- and upper- level centers was noted. The system continued to rapidly weaken on 9&#160;November, before advisories were halted on the system later that day,<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA-bulletins_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA-bulletins-2">[2]</a></sup> and FU Berlin followed suit by 10&#160;November, removing the name Rolf from its weather maps and declaring its dissipation.<sup id="cite_ref-FUBerlin_63-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUBerlin-63">[63]</a></sup> The deep warm core of this cyclone persisted for a longer time compared to other tropical-like cyclones in the Mediterranean.<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2404_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2404-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="November_2014_(Qendresa)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="November_2014_.28Qendresa.29">November 2014 (Qendresa)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: November 2014 (Qendresa)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/220px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/330px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg/440px-Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="8000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Qendresa_2014-11-07_1215Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCyclone Qendresa on 7 November</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Qendresa" title="Cyclone Qendresa">Cyclone Qendresa</a></div>\n<p>On 6 November 2014, the low-level circulation centre of Qendresa formed near <a href="/wiki/Kerkennah_Islands" title="Kerkennah Islands">Kerkennah Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FUB_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUB-64">[64]</a></sup> As the system was moving north-northeastwards and combining with an upper-level low from <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> early on 7 November, the system <a href="/wiki/Occluded_front" title="Occluded front">occluded</a> quickly and intensified dramatically with an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>-like feature, thanks to favourable conditions. Qendresa directly hit <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> when it had lost its <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">fronts</a> with a more well-defined eye, with ten-minute sustained winds at 110.9&#160;km/h (68.9&#160;mph) and the gust at 153.7&#160;km/h (95.5&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-EUMETSAT_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EUMETSAT-65">[65]</a></sup> The central pressure was presumed to be 978 <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> (28.88 <a href="/wiki/Inch_of_mercury" title="Inch of mercury">inHg</a>). Interacting with <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, the cyclone turned northeastwards and started to make an anticlockwise loop. On 8 November, Qendresa crossed <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Syracuse" title="Province of Syracuse">Syracuse</a> in the morning and then significantly weakened.<sup id="cite_ref-FUB_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FUB-64">[64]</a></sup> Turning southeastwards then moving eastwards,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> Qendressa moved over <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, before dissipating over the island on November 11.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span id="October_2016_(90M)"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="October_2016_.2890M.29">October 2016 (90M)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: October 2016 (90M)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/200px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/300px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg/400px-90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5400" data-file-height="4050" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:90M_2016-10-30_0930Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n90M on 30 October 2016</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>On early 28 October 2016, 35&#160;mph (60&#160;km/h) winds began to form into an extratropical cyclone south of <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">Calabria</a>, in the Ionian sea. It quickly intensified, attaining wind speeds of 50&#160;mph (80&#160;km/h) as it slowly moved to the west, causing high waves and minor damage to cars near the <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Maltese</a> city of <a href="/wiki/Valletta" title="Valletta">Valletta</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2016cyclone_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2016cyclone-68">[68]</a></sup> weakening the following day and beginning to move eastwards. However, later that day it began to re-intensify, becoming a tropical storm on 31 October. After passing over <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> it then began to quickly weaken, with the storm degenerating into an extratropical low on 1 November.<sup id="cite_ref-medicane2016_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medicane2016-69">[69]</a></sup> Tropical Storm 90M was also nicknamed "<i>Medicane Trixi</i>" by some media outlets in Europe during its duration.<sup id="cite_ref-Trixi_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trixi-70">[70]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_storms">Other storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_tropical-like_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Other storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Numerous other Mediterranean cyclones have occurred, but few have been as well-documented as those in 1969, 1982, 1995, 1996, 2006, 2011, and 2014. On 27&#160;September 1983, a medicane was observed at sea between <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">Tunisia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, looping around <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">Sardinia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>, coming ashore twice on the islands, before making landfall at <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">Tunis</a> early on 2&#160;October and dissipating. The development of the system was not encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Baroclinity" title="Baroclinity">baroclinic</a> instability; rather, convection was incited by abnormally high <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperatures (SSTs)</a> at the time of its formation. It also featured a definitive <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, tall <a href="/wiki/Cumulonimbus_cloud" title="Cumulonimbus cloud">cumulonimbus clouds</a>, intense sustained winds, and a warm core. For most of its duration, it maintained a diameter of 200 to 300&#160;km (120 to 190&#160;mi), though it shrank just before landfall on <a href="/wiki/Ajaccio" title="Ajaccio">Ajaccio</a> to a diameter of 100&#160;km (62&#160;mi). Four other notable cyclones occurred in the late twentieth century, on 26&#160;March&#160;1983, 29\xe2\x80\x9330&#160;December&#160;1984, 30\xe2\x80\x9331&#160;October&#160;1997, and 5\xe2\x80\x938&#160;December&#160;1997, all exhibiting characteristics closely resembling tropical cyclones elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-Pytharoulis2000-262_32-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pytharoulis2000-262-32">[32]</a></sup></p>\n<p>A study performed in 2013 revealed several additional storms and additional information on medicanes forming on 7&#160;April&#160;1984, 14&#160;December&#160;1985, 5&#160;December&#160;1991, 15&#160;January&#160;1995, 10&#160;December&#160;1996, 26&#160;January&#160;1998, 19&#160;March&#160;1999, and 27&#160;May&#160;2003.<sup id="cite_ref-TousRomero2013-4_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TousRomero2013-4-71">[71]</a></sup> A second study, conducted in 2007, revealed additional storms forming on 18&#160;October&#160;2003, 27&#160;October&#160;2005, and 15&#160;December&#160;2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Fita2007-45_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fita2007-45-72">[72]</a></sup> A third study revealed several other cyclones, of which developed on 13&#160;September&#160;1999, 10&#160;September&#160;2000, 9&#160;October&#160;2000, 19&#160;September&#160;2004, 3&#160;November 2004, 17&#160;and 26&#160;October&#160;2007, 4&#160;December&#160;2008, 13&#160;April&#160;2012<sup id="cite_ref-Miglietta2013-2402_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miglietta2013-2402-27">[27]</a></sup> and 30 October 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-October_surprise_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-October_surprise-73">[73]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
