class="mw-body-content"><!-- CentralNotice --></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t<div class="mw-indicators mw-body-content">\n</div>\n\t\t\t<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tropical wave</h1>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="contentSub"></div>\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">\n\t\t\t\t\tJump to:\t\t\t\t\t<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, \t\t\t\t\t<a href="#p-search">search</a>\n\t\t\t\t</div>\n\t\t\t\t<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Tropical waves</b>, <b>easterly waves</b>, or <b>tropical easterly waves</b>, also known as <b>African easterly waves</b> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> region, are a type of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric">atmospheric</a> <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a>, an elongated area of relatively <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_area" title="Low-pressure area">low air pressure</a>, oriented <a href="/wiki/North" title="North">north</a> to <a href="/wiki/South" title="South">south</a>, which moves from <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">east</a> to <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">west</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>, causing areas of <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">cloudiness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorms" class="mw-redirect" title="Thunderstorms">thunderstorms</a>. West-moving waves can also form from the tail end of <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal</a> zones in the <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a> and tropics, and may be referred to as easterly waves, but these waves are not properly called tropical waves; they are a form of <a href="/wiki/Inverted_trough" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverted trough">inverted trough</a> sharing many characteristics with fully tropical waves. All tropical waves form in the easterly flow along the equatorward side of the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a> or belt of <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure</a> which lies north and south of the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ). Tropical waves are generally carried westward by the <a href="/wiki/Prevailing_winds" title="Prevailing winds">prevailing easterly winds</a> along the tropics and subtropics near the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. They can lead to the formation of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> in the north Atlantic and eastern north Pacific <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_basin" title="Oceanic basin">basins</a>. Tropical wave study is aided by <a href="/wiki/Hovm%C3%B6ller_diagram" title="Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagram">Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagrams</a>, a graph of meteorological data.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Characteristics"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">Characteristics</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Atlantic"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Atlantic</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#East_Pacific"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">East Pacific</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#Screaming_eagle_waves"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">Screaming eagle waves</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-6"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">6</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-7"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">7</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A tropical wave normally follows an area of sinking, intensely dry air, blowing from the northeast. After the passage of the trough line, the wind veers southeast, the humidity abruptly rises, and the atmosphere <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">destabilizes</a>. This yields widespread showers and thunderstorms, sometimes severe. As the wave moves westward, the showers gradually diminish.</p>\n<p>An exception to this rain is in the Atlantic. Sometimes, a surge of dry air called the <a href="/wiki/Saharan_Air_Layer" title="Saharan Air Layer">Saharan Air Layer</a> (SAL) follows a tropical wave, leaving cloudless skies, as <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convection</a> is capped by the dry layer inversion. Also, any dust in the SAL reflects sunlight, cooling the air below it.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic">Atlantic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Atlantic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="center">\n<div class="thumb tnone">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_waves.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tropical_waves.jpg/700px-Tropical_waves.jpg" width="700" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tropical_waves.jpg/1050px-Tropical_waves.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Tropical_waves.jpg/1400px-Tropical_waves.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2374" data-file-height="944" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_waves.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTropical wave formation.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Tropical waves in the Atlantic basin develop from disturbances which develop as far east as <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> in east <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and drift over the continent into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic ocean">Atlantic ocean</a>. These are generated or enhanced by the <a href="/wiki/African_Easterly_Jet" class="mw-redirect" title="African Easterly Jet">African Easterly Jet</a>. The clockwise circulation of the large transoceanic <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high-pressure cell</a> or <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclone</a> centered near the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> islands (known as the <a href="/wiki/Azores_High" title="Azores High">Azores High</a>) impels easterly waves away from the coastal areas of Africa towards <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.</p>\n<p>Approximately 60% of Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> originate from tropical waves, while approximately 85% of intense Atlantic hurricanes (<a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Category 3</a> and greater) develop from tropical waves.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A4_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A4-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWR_Avila_1995_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWR_Avila_1995-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tropical cyclones can sometimes degenerate back into a tropical wave. This normally occurs if upper-level <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> is too strong. The storm can redevelop if the upper level shear abates.</p>\n<p>If a tropical wave is moving quickly, it can have strong winds over tropical storm force strength, but it is not considered a tropical storm unless it has a closed low-level circulation. An example of this was <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Claudette_(2003)" title="Hurricane Claudette (2003)">Hurricane Claudette</a> in <a href="/wiki/2003_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2003 Atlantic hurricane season">2003</a>, where the original wave had winds of 45&#160;mph (72&#160;km/h) before developing a circulation.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Pacific">East Pacific</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: East Pacific">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>It has been suggested that some eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> tropical cyclones are formed out of tropical easterly waves that originate in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> as well.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A4_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A4-2">[2]</a></sup> During the summer months, tropical waves can extend northward as far as the desert southwest of the United States, producing spells of intensified shower activity embedded within the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoon</a> regime.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Screaming_eagle_waves">Screaming eagle waves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Screaming eagle waves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A screaming eagle is a tropical wave whose convective pattern loosely resembles the head of an eagle. This phenomenon is caused by <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">shearing</a> from either westerly winds aloft or strong easterly winds at the surface. These systems are typically located within 25&#160;degrees latitude of the equator.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">Rain</a> showers and surface <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">winds</a> gusting to 29&#160;mph (47&#160;km/h) are associated with these waves. They move across the ocean at a rate of 15&#160;mph (24&#160;km/h). Strong thunderstorm activity can be associated with the features when located east of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_upper_tropospheric_trough" title="Tropical upper tropospheric trough">tropical upper tropospheric trough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> The term was first publicly seen in an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellite</a> interpretation handbook written by Hank Brandli in 1976. In 1969, Brandli discovered that a storm of this type threatened the original splashdown site for <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Hovm%C3%B6ller_diagram" title="Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagram">Hovm\xc3\xb6ller diagram</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">Tropical cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclogenesis</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_hurricane" title="Cape Verde hurricane">Cape Verde hurricane</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_wave&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">\n<div class="mw-references-wrap">\n<ol class="references">\n<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><cite class="citation web"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://cimss.ssec.wisc.edu/tropic/real-time/marti/2008_09L/webManager/mainpage.html">"Morphed Integrated Microwave Imagery at CIMSS (MIMIC)"</a>.
