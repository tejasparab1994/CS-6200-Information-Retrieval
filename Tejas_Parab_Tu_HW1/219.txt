style="display:none">\n<td colspan="2"></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p><b>Severe Cyclonic Storm Agni</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2004_North_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season" title="2004 North Indian Ocean cyclone season">2004 North Indian Ocean cyclone season</a> notable for its record proximity to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>. It was the second North Indian Ocean cyclone to receive a name, after Onil earlier in the year. Agni formed on November&#160;28 well to the southwest of India in the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>, and steadily intensified as it tracked northwestward. The <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a> (JTWC) estimated peak 1&#160;minute <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained winds</a> of 120&#160;km/h (75&#160;mph), while the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> (IMD) estimated peak 3&#160;minute sustained winds of 100&#160;km/h (65&#160;mph); the IMD is the official <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Centre">warning center</a> for the north <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>. After peaking, it weakened due to <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, dry air, and cooler waters, and the JTWC issued its final advisory on December&#160;3 as it approached the coast of <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>. The remnants of Agni moved along the Somalian coastline until dissipating on December&#160;5.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Agni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Agni_2004_track.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Agni_2004_track.png/220px-Agni_2004_track.png" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Agni_2004_track.png/330px-Agni_2004_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Agni_2004_track.png/440px-Agni_2004_track.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="633" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Agni_2004_track.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A tropical disturbance was observed on November&#160;19 about 800&#160;km (500&#160;mi) southeast of <a href="/wiki/Colombo" title="Colombo">Colombo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">Bay of Bengal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mmm_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mmm-1">[1]</a></sup> The disturbance tracked westward, gradually organizing and prompting the JTWC to issue a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone_Formation_Alert" title="Tropical Cyclone Formation Alert">Tropical Cyclone Formation Alert</a> (TCFA) on November&#160;22. After passing south of Sri Lanka, it became disorganized and was no longer considered likely to <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">develop</a> into a tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-gp_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gp-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation" title="Atmospheric circulation">circulation</a> associated with the system continued westward, reorganizing on November&#160;26 in the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Sea" title="Arabian Sea">Arabian Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mmm_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mmm-1">[1]</a></sup> Despite being located unusually close to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, the disturbance maintained <a href="/wiki/Convection_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Convection (meteorology)">convection</a>, or thunderstorms, which was becoming organized around the weak low-level circulation. With low <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(meteorology)" title="Deformation (meteorology)">diffluence</a> aloft, the JTWC remarked the system had a fair chance of developing.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While the system was organizing, the center crossed the equator to reach about 0.5\xc2\xb0&#160;<a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">S</a>, thus becoming an <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic</a> circulation in the southern hemisphere. This was unusual, as the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolis effect">Coriolis effect</a> is nonexistent along the equator\xe2\x80\x94the Coriolis effect refers to planetary <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a>, which provides the spin in a cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-mmm_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mmm-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wmo_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wmo-4">[4]</a></sup> The JTWC later assessed the system as remaining in the northern hemisphere, reaching as far south as 0.7\xc2\xb0&#160;N, or about 80&#160;km (50&#160;mi) from the equator.<sup id="cite_ref-jtwc_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-5">[5]</a></sup> After the circulation organized further beneath the convection, the JTWC issued another TCFA at 0300&#160;<a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">UTC</a> on November&#160;28.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Three hours later, the agency classified the system as Tropical Cyclone 05A about 1300&#160;km (800&#160;mi) southwest of the southern tip of India.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In a post-season analysis, the JTWC estimated the cyclone became a tropical storm six hours earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-jtwc_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-5">[5]</a></sup> At around the same time, the IMD classified it as a "low pressure area... likely to become well marked."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Upon being classified, the tropical cyclone was moving northwestward under the influence of a <a href="/wiki/Ridge_(meteorology)" title="Ridge (meteorology)">ridge</a> over India. Its <a href="/wiki/Outflow_(meteorology)" title="Outflow (meteorology)">outflow</a> to the north and south became better defined, although its proximity to the equator limited its southerly outflow somewhat due to <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Early on November&#160;29, the IMD classified the system as a depression,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and throughout the day it rapidly intensified to become a cyclonic storm; a cyclonic storm is a tropical cyclone with at least 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph) <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">winds</a> sustained for 3&#160;minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Around that time, the JTWC assessed the cyclone as reaching peak winds of 120&#160;km/h (75&#160;mph), sustained for 1&#160;minute, along with gusts to 150&#160;km/h (90&#160;mph). Late on November&#160;29, the cyclone weakened slightly, only to regain its peak intensity by November&#160;30;<sup id="cite_ref-jtwc_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-5">[5]</a></sup> at that time, it developed a <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">banding eye</a>, and was located about 1500&#160;km (900&#160;mi) southeast of the coast of <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> At 1200&#160;UTC on November&#160;30, the IMD issued its first complete advisory on the cyclone, naming it Severe Cyclonic Storm Agni and estimating peak winds at 100&#160;km/h (65&#160;mph); the agency predicted the storm would continue northwestward and strengthen slightly before weakening.<sup id="cite_ref-imd1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imd1-13">[13]</a></sup> At its peak, the cyclone had a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of 985&#160;<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bt_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bt-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<div and the convection decreased significantly.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On December&#160;1, the IMD estimated Agni weakened to cyclonic storm status,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> around which time the center became exposed from the convection.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The cyclone continued its steady weakening continued due to the wind shear, the presence of dry air, and cooler <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">water temperatures</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and by December&#160;2, the IMD downgraded Agni to depression status, which was its final warning on the system.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As it approached the coast of Somalia, it turned westward due to the building of a ridge over <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Despite unfavorable conditions, thunderstorms briefly reformed over the circulation, which allowed the storm to maintain its intensity.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> By late on December&#160;3, Agni weakened to below tropical storm status, and the JTWC issued its final warning while the storm was about 450&#160;km (275&#160;mi) south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Cape_Guardafui" title="Cape Guardafui">Cape Guardafui</a>\xe2\x80\x94the easternmost point of the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The circulation became difficult to locate by early on December&#160;4, by which time the system had little remaining convection.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It turned southwestward and later to the south, moving ashore on eastern Somalia before dissipating on December&#160;5 near <a href="/wiki/Ceel_Huur" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceel Huur">Ceel Huur</a> over water.<sup id="cite_ref-jtwc_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="Records,_naming,_and_impact"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Records.2C_naming.2C_and_impact">Records, naming, and impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclone_Agni&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Records, naming, and impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>According to RSMC New Delhi, Cyclone Agni developed into a depression at 1.5\xc2\xb0&#160;N, which is the same latitude as where according to RSMC Tokyo, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Vamei" title="Tropical Storm Vamei">Tropical Storm Vamei</a> formed during December 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IMD_BT_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_BT-26">[26]</a></sup> However, according to RSMC La Reunion, the precursor low to Cyclone Agni moved into the Southern Hemisphere and became an anticyclone as it had kept its counter-clockwise circulation.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Agni was the second storm in the north Indian Ocean to receive a name, after the IMD began <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">tropical cyclone naming</a> in the middle of 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-naming_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-naming-28">[28]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As the cyclone was weak when it moved over Somalia, no casualties or significant damage was reported.<sup id="cite_ref-jtwc_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtwc-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h2><span
