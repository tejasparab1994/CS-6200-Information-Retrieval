2014</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>nor\'easter</b> (also <b>northeaster</b>; see <a href="#Etymology_and_usage">below</a>) is a macro-scale <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclone</a>. The name derives from the direction of the strongest winds\xe2\x80\x94as an offshore air mass rotates <a href="/wiki/Counterclockwise" class="mw-redirect" title="Counterclockwise">counterclockwise</a>, winds tend to blow northeast-to-southwest over the region covered by the northwest quadrant of the cyclone. Use of the term in North America is associated with several different types of storms, some of which can form in the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> and some of which form as far south as the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>. The term is most often used in the coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic states</a>. Typically, such storms originate as a <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">low-pressure</a> area that forms within 100 miles (160&#160;km) of the shore between <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> and <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>. The precipitation pattern is similar to that of other <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical storms</a>. Nor\'easters are usually accompanied by very heavy <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> or <a href="/wiki/Snow" title="Snow">snow</a>, and can cause severe <a href="/wiki/Coastal_flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Coastal flooding">coastal flooding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Beaufort Scale">hurricane-force</a> winds, or <a href="/wiki/Blizzard" title="Blizzard">blizzard conditions</a>. Nor\'easters are usually most intense during winter in New England and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a>. They thrive on converging air masses\xe2\x80\x94the cold polar air mass and the warmer air over the water\xe2\x80\x94and are more severe in winter when the difference in temperature between these air masses is greater.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-noaafeature_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaafeature-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-disc2_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disc2-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Nor\'easters tend to develop most often and most powerfully between the months of November and March, although they can (much less commonly) develop during other parts of the year as well. The susceptible regions are generally impacted by Nor\'easters a few times each winter.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:239px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Compass_thumbnail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Compass_thumbnail.jpg/237px-Compass_thumbnail.jpg" width="237" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Compass_thumbnail.jpg/356px-Compass_thumbnail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/25/Compass_thumbnail.jpg/474px-Compass_thumbnail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="475" data-file-height="475" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Compass_thumbnail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCompass card (1607), featuring the spelling "Noreast"</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The term <i>nor\'easter</i> came to <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> by way of <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, the first recorded use in the English language of the term "nore" ("north") in association with the points of the compass and wind direction was by Dekker in 1612: <i>"How blowes the winde Syr?" "Wynde! is Nore-Nore-West."</i> However, this is incorrect, as John Lyly uses the term the same way in his play of 1585, <i><a href="/wiki/Gallathea" title="Gallathea">Gallathea</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Similar uses occurred in 1688 (<i>Nore and Nore-West</i>) and in 1718 (<i>Nore-west or Nore-nore-west</i>) These recorded uses are predated by use of the term "noreast", first recorded as used by Davis in 1594 (<i>Noreast by North raiseth a degree in sayling 24 leagues.</i>) and shown, for instance, on a <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">compass card</a> published in 1607. Thus, the manner of pronouncing from memory the 32 <a href="/wiki/Points_of_the_compass" title="Points of the compass">points of the compass</a>, known in maritime training as "boxing the compass", is described by Ansted<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> with pronunciations "Nor\'east (or west)," "Nor\' Nor\'-east (or west)," "Nor\'east b\' east (or west)," and so forth. According to the OED, the first recorded use of the term "nor\'easter" occurs in 1836 in a translation of <a href="/wiki/Aristophanes" title="Aristophanes">Aristophanes</a>. The term "nor\'easter" naturally developed from the historical spellings and pronunciations of the compass points and the direction of wind or sailing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>As noted in a January 2006 editorial by William Sisson, editor of <i>Soundings</i> magazine,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> use of "nor\'easter" to describe the storm system is common along the U.S. East Coast. Yet it has been asserted by linguist Mark Liberman (see below) that "nor\'easter" as a contraction for "northeaster" has no basis in regional New England dialect; the <a href="/wiki/Boston_accent" title="Boston accent">Boston accent</a> would elide the "R": <i>no\'theastuh\'</i>. He describes nor\'easter as a "fake" word. However, this view neglects the little-known etymology and the historical maritime usage described above.</p>\n<p>Nineteenth-century Downeast mariners pronounced the compass point "north northeast" as "no\'nuth-east", and so on.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For decades, Edgar Comee, of <a href="/wiki/Brunswick,_Maine" title="Brunswick, Maine">Brunswick, Maine</a>, waged a determined battle against use of the term "nor\'easter" by the press, which usage he considered "a pretentious and altogether lamentable affectation" and "the odious, even loathsome, practice of landlubbers who would be seen as salty as the sea itself". His efforts, which included mailing hundreds of postcards, were profiled, just before his death at the age of 88, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Despite the efforts of Comee and others, use of the term continues by the press. According to <i>Boston Globe</i> writer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jan_Freeman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jan Freeman (page does not exist)">Jan Freeman</a>, "from 1975 to 1980, journalists used the nor\xe2\x80\x99easter spelling only once in five mentions of such storms; in the past year (2003), more than 80 percent of northeasters were spelled nor\'easter".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania" title="University of Pennsylvania">University of Pennsylvania</a> linguistics professor <a href="/wiki/Mark_Liberman" title="Mark Liberman">Mark Liberman</a> has pointed out that while the Oxford English Dictionary cites examples dating back to 1837, these examples represent the contributions of a handful of non-New England poets and writers. Liberman posits that "nor\'easter" may have originally been a <a href="/wiki/Syncope_(phonology)" title="Syncope (phonology)">literary affectation</a>, akin to "e\'en" for "even" and "th\'only" for "the only", which is an indication in spelling that two syllables count for only one position in metered verse, with no implications for actual pronunciation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<p>However, despite these assertions, the term can be found in the writings of New Englanders, and was frequently used by the press in the 19th century.</p>\n<ul>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Hartford_Times" title="The Hartford Times">The Hartford Times</a></i> reported on a storm striking New York in December 1839, and observed, "We Yankees had a share of this same "noreaster," but it was quite moderate in comparison to the one of the 15h inst."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Bailey_Aldrich" title="Thomas Bailey Aldrich">Thomas Bailey Aldrich</a>, in his semi-autobiographical work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_a_Bad_Boy" title="The Story of a Bad Boy">The Story of a Bad Boy</a></i> (1870), wrote "We had had several slight flurries of hail and snow before, but this was a regular nor\'easter".<sup id="cite_ref-Aldrich1911_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aldrich1911-14">[14]</a></sup></li>\n<li>In her story "In the Gray Goth" (1869) <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Stuart_Phelps_Ward" title="Elizabeth Stuart Phelps Ward">Elizabeth Stuart Phelps Ward</a> wrote "...and there was snow in the sky now, setting in for a regular nor\'easter".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></li>\n<li>John H. Tice, in <i>A new system of meteorology, designed for schools and private students</i> (1878), wrote "During this battle, the dreaded, disagreeable and destructive Northeaster rages over the New England, the Middle States, and southward. No nor\'easter ever occurs except when there is a high barometer headed off and driven down upon Nova Scotia and Lower Canada."<sup id="cite_ref-Tice1878_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tice1878-16">[16]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Usage existed into the 20th century in the form of:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Current event description, as the Publication Committee of the New York Charity Organization Society wrote in <i>Charities and the commons: a weekly journal of philanthropy and social advance, Volume 19</i> (1908): "In spite of a heavy "nor\'easter," the worst that has visited the New England coast in years, the hall was crowded."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></li>\n<li>Historical reference, as used by Mary Rogers Bangs in <i>Old Cape Cod</i> (1917): "In December of 1778, the Federal brig General Arnold, Magee master and twelve Barnstable men among the crew, drove ashore on the Plymouth flats during a furious nor\'easter, the "Magee storm" that mariners, for years after, used as a date to reckon from."<sup id="cite_ref-Bangs1920_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bangs1920-18">[18]</a></sup></li>\n<li>A "common contraction for "northeaster"", as listed in Ralph E. Huschke\'s <i>Glossary of Meteorology</i> (1959).<sup id="cite_ref-Huschke1959_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huschke1959-19">[19]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_formation_characteristics">Geography and formation characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography and formation characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg/200px-Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg" width="200" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg/300px-Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg/400px-Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="566" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gulf_Stream_water_temperature.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSurface temperature of the sea off the east coast of North America. The corridor in yellow gives the position of the Gulf Stream</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Nor\'easters develop in response to the sharp contrast in the warm <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">Gulf Stream</a> ocean current coming up from the tropical Atlantic and the cold air masses coming down from Canada. When the very cold and dry air rushes southward and meets up with the warm Gulf stream current, which is often near 70&#160;\xc2\xb0F (21&#160;\xc2\xb0C) even in mid-winter, intense low pressure develops.</p>\n<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Upper_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper atmosphere">upper atmosphere</a>, the strong winds of the <a href="/wiki/Jet_stream" title="Jet stream">jet stream</a> remove and replace rising air from the Atlantic more rapidly than the Atlantic air is replaced at lower levels; this and the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> help develop a strong storm. The storm tracks northeast along the East Coast past the Carolinas, the mid-Atlantic, and the New England coastal states. Counterclockwise winds around the <a href="/wiki/Low-pressure_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-pressure system">low-pressure system</a> blow the moist air over land. The relatively warm, moist air meets cold air coming southward from Canada. The low increases the surrounding pressure difference, which causes the very different air masses to collide at a faster speed. When the difference in temperature of the air masses is larger, so is the storm\'s instability, turbulence, and thus severity.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-disc1_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disc1-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The nor\'easters taking the East Coast track usually indicates the presence of a <a href="/wiki/High_pressure" title="High pressure">high-pressure</a> area in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-disc4_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disc4-21">[21]</a></sup> Sometimes a nor\'easter will move slightly inland and bring rain to the cities on the coastal plain (New York City, Philadelphia, Baltimore, etc.) and snow in New England (Boston northward). It can move slightly offshore, bringing a wet snow south of Boston to Richmond, Virginia, or even parts of the Carolinas.<sup id="cite_ref-disc3_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disc3-22">[22]</a></sup> Such a storm will rapidly intensify, tracking northward and following the topography of the East Coast, sometimes continuing to grow stronger during its entire existence. A nor\'easter usually reaches its peak intensity while off the <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canadian coast</a>. The storm then reaches Arctic areas, and can reach intensities equal to that of a weak <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">hurricane</a>. It then meanders throughout the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a> and can last for several weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-disc3_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-disc3-22">[22]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Nor\'easters are usually formed by an area of <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> associated with an upper-level disturbance or from a kink in a frontal surface that causes a surface low-pressure area to develop. Such storms are very often formed from the merging of several weaker storms, a "parent storm", and a polar jet stream mixing with the tropical jet stream.</p>\n<p>Until the nor\'easter passes, thick, dark, low-level clouds often block out the sun. Temperatures usually fall significantly due to the presence of the cooler air from winds that typically come from a northeasterly direction. During a single storm, the precipitation can range from a torrential downpour to a fine mist. All precipitation types can occur in a nor\'easter. High wind gusts, which can reach hurricane strength, are also associated with a nor\'easter. On very rare occasions, such as in the <a href="/wiki/North_American_blizzard_of_2006" title="North American blizzard of 2006">North American blizzard of 2006</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States_Blizzard_of_1978" class="mw-redirect" title="Northeastern United States Blizzard of 1978">nor\'easter in 1978</a>, the center of the storm can take on the circular shape more typical of a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> and have a small "dry slot" near the center, which can be mistaken for an <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a>, although it is not an eye.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_from_tropical_cyclones">Difference from tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Difference from tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Often, people mistake nor\'easters for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical cyclones">tropical cyclones</a> and do not differentiate between the two weather systems. Nor\'easters differ from tropical cyclones in that nor\'easters are cold-core low-pressure systems, meaning that they thrive on cold air. Tropical cyclones are warm-core low-pressure systems, which means they thrive on warm temperatures.</p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_from_other_extratropical_storms">Difference from other extratropical storms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Difference from other extratropical storms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>A nor\'easter is formed in a strong <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a>, usually experiencing <a href="/wiki/Bombogenesis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombogenesis">bombogenesis</a>. While this formation occurs in many places around the world, nor\'easters are unique for their combination of northeast winds and moisture content of the swirling clouds. Nearly similar conditions sometimes occur during winter in the Pacific Northeast (northern Japan and northwards) with winds from NW-N. In Europe, similar weather systems with such severity are hardly possible; the moisture content of the clouds is usually not high enough to cause flooding or heavy snow, though NE winds can be strong.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nor%27easter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The eastern United States, from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> to Maine, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Canada" title="Eastern Canada">Eastern Canada</a> can experience nor\'easters, though most often they affect the areas from northern New Jersey northward. States of Emergencies were declared in 12 States in advance of the storm as well as by the Mayor of <a href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Washington D.C.">Washington D.C.</a>. The blizzard also caused significant storm surge in New Jersey and Delaware that was equal to or worse than Hurricane Sandy. Sustained damaging winds over 50&#160;mph were recorded in many coastal communities, with a maximum gust to 85&#160;mph on <a href="/wiki/Assateague_Island" title="Assateague Island">Assateague Island</a>, Virginia. A total of 55 people died due to the storm.</li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/February_2017_United_States_blizzard" class="mw-redirect" title="February 2017 United States blizzard">February 2017 blizzard</a> (also known as Winter Storm Niko and The Blizzard of 2017 by media outlets) \xe2\x80\x93 Forming as an Alberta clipper in the northern United States on February 6, the system initially produced light snowfall from the Midwest to the Ohio Valley as it tracked southeastwards. It eventually reached the East Coast of the United States on February 9 and began to rapidly grow into a powerful nor\'easter, dumping 1 to near 2 feet across the Northeast Megapolis. The storm also produced prolific thunder and lightning across <a href="/wiki/Southern_New_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern New England">Southern New England</a>. Prior to the blizzard, unprecedented and record-breaking warmth had enveloped the region, with record highs of above 60&#160;\xc2\xb0F (16&#160;\xc2\xb0C) recorded in several areas, including Central Park in New York City.
