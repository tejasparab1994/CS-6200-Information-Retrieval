subtropical cyclones since 2004</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>South Atlantic <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a></b> are unusual weather events that occur in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. Strong <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, which disrupts the formation of cyclones, as well as a lack of weather disturbances favorable for development in the South <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> make any strong tropical system extremely rare, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Catarina</a> in 2004 was the only recorded South <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">Atlantic hurricane</a> in history. Those storms have only developed during the months from November through to April in this basin. Since 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center</a> has started to assign <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names#South_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">names</a> to tropical and subtropical systems in western side of this <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">basin</a> near Brazil, when they have sustained wind speeds of at least 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph), the generally accepted minimum sustained wind velocity for a disturbance to be designated as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> in the North Atlantic basin. occurrence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Theories concerning infrequency of occurrence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Until April 1991, it was thought that tropical cyclones did not develop within the South Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-1">[1]</a></sup> Very strong vertical wind shear in the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> is considered a deterrent.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_G6_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_G6-2">[2]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> drops one to two degrees south of the equator,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> not far enough from the equator for the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_force" title="Coriolis force">Coriolis force</a> to aid development. Water temperatures in the tropics of the southern Atlantic are cooler than those in the tropical north Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During April 1991, these assertions were proven false, when the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> reported that a tropical cyclone had developed over the Eastern South Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC-5">[5]</a></sup> In subsequent years, a few systems were suspected to have the characteristics needed to be classified as a tropical cyclone including in March 1994 and January 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> During March 2004, an <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclone</a> formally transitioned into a tropical cyclone and made landfall on Brazil, after becoming a Category 2 hurricane on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale</a>. While the system was threatening the Brazilian state of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a>, a newspaper used the headline "Furac\xc3\xa3o Catarina," which was originally presumed to mean "furac\xc3\xa3o (hurricane) threatening (Santa) Catarina (the state)".<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-1">[1]</a></sup> After international presses started monitoring the system, "Hurricane Catarina" has formally been adopted.</p>\n<p>At the Sixth WMO International Workshop on Tropical Cyclones (IWTC-VI) in 2006, it was questioned if any subtropical or tropical cyclones had developed within the South Atlantic before Catarina.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> It was noted that suspect systems had developed in January 1970, March 1994, January 2004, March 2004, May 2004, February 2006, and March 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> It was also suggested that an effort should be made to locate any possible systems using satellite imagery and synoptic data; however, it was noted that this effort may be hindered by the lack of any geostationary imagery over the basin before 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> A study was subsequently performed and published during 2012, which concluded that there had been 63 subtropical cyclones in the Southern Atlantic between 1957 and 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> During January 2009, a subtropical storm developed in the basin, and in March 2010, a tropical storm developed, which was named Anita by the Brazilian public and private weather services.<sup id="cite_ref-GPJan2009_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPJan2009-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2010_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2010-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center</a> started to assign <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names#South_Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">names</a> to tropical and subtropical cyclones that develop within its area of responsibility, to the west of 20\xc2\xb0W, when they have sustained wind speeds of at least 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-names_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-names-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Known_storms_and_impacts">Known storms and impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span The next day it moved offshore northern <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a> with a curved cloud pattern. It moved westward over an area of warm waters and caused minor damages on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena" title="Saint Helena">Saint Helena</a> while the circulation became better defined. According to the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>, the system was probably either a tropical depression or a tropical storm at its peak intensity. On April 14, the system rapidly dissipated, as it was absorbed into a large <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NC_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NC-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hurricane Catarina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 24em;">\n<tr>\n<td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-weight: bold; 972&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a>&#160;(<a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">mbar</a>)</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Catarina" title="Hurricane Catarina">Hurricane Catarina</a></div>\n<p>Hurricane Catarina was an extraordinarily rare tropical cyclone, forming in the southern <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> in March 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-College_of_Earth_and_Mineral_Sciences_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-College_of_Earth_and_Mineral_Sciences-14">[14]</a></sup> Just after becoming a hurricane, it hit the southern coast of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a> on the evening of March 28, with winds estimated near 155 kilometres per hour (96&#160;mph), making it a Category 2-equivalent on the <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_Hurricane_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale">Saffir-Simpson Hurricane Scale</a>. The cyclone killed 3 to 10 people and caused millions of dollars in damage in Brazil.</p>\n<p>At the time, the Brazilians were taken completely by surprise, and were initially skeptical that an actual tropical cyclone could have formed in the South Atlantic. Eventually, however, they were convinced, and adopted the previously-unofficial name "Catarina" for the storm, after Santa Catarina state. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> also began monitoring the system as Low SL90. possible January 2004 tropical cyclone</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>According to a presentation at the Sixth WMO International Workshop on Tropical Cyclones (IWTC-VI), satellite imagery from January 1970 showed that a system with an eyewall had developed behind a cold front and that the system needed further analysis to determine if it was tropical or subtropical.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> On March 27, 1974, a weak area of low pressure that had originated over the Amazon River started to intensify further.<sup id="cite_ref-Catarina_Analysis_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catarina_Analysis-45">[45]</a></sup> Over the next 48 hours the system quickly developed further and was classified as subtropical, as it developed a banding structure and deep convection near its warm core.<sup id="cite_ref-Catarina_Analysis_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catarina_Analysis-45">[45]</a></sup> On March 29, a north-westerly flow encroached on the systems environment, which caused the system to rapidly move towards 40S and the cold waters that were present to the south of 40\xc2\xb0S.<sup id="cite_ref-Catarina_Analysis_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Catarina_Analysis-45">[45]</a></sup> In March 1994, a system that was thought to be weaker than Catarina was spawned but was located over cool and open waters.<sup id="cite_ref-WWCBH_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWCBH-46">[46]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="2004\xe2\x80\x932009"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2004.E2.80.932009">2004\xe2\x80\x932009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2004\xe2\x80\x932009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>During 2004, the large-scale conditions over the South Atlantic were more conducive than usual for subtropical or tropical systems, with 4 systems noted.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> The first possible tropical cyclone developed within a trough of low pressure, to the southeast of Salvador, Brazil on January 18.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> The system subsequently displayed a small central dense overcast and was suspected to be at the peak of its development as either a tropical depression or a tropical storm during the next day.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup> The system was subsequently affected by some strong shear, before it moved inland and weakened along the coast of Brazil before it was last noted during January 21.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup> Within Brazil the system caused heavy rain and flooding with a state of emergency declared in <a href="/wiki/Aracaju" title="Aracaju">Aracaju</a>, after the river overflowed and burst its banks which flooded homes, destroyed crops and caused parts of the highway to collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup> However, it was noted that not all of the heavy rain and impacts were attributable to the system, as a large monsoon low covered much of Brazil at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_January_2004_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_January_2004-6">[6]</a></sup> The second system was a possible hybrid cyclone that developed near south-eastern Brazil between March 15\xe2\x80\x9316.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup> Hurricane Catarina was the third system, while the fourth system had a well-defined eye like structure, and formed off the coast of Brazil during on March 15, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<div srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/SATL_TC_Feb_23_2006_1705Z.jpg/300px-SATL_TC_Feb_23_2006_1705Z.jpg 1.5x, system with a warm core developed and moved southwards along the South Atlantic Zone.<sup id="cite_ref-IWTCVI_7-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IWTCVI-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Two subtropical cyclones affected both <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">Rio Grande do Sul</a> state in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. On January 28, 2009, a cold-core mid to upper-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> in phase with a low-level warm-core <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low</a> formed a <a href="/wiki/Pressure_system" title="Pressure system">system</a> and moved eastward into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">South Atlantic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> The storm produced rainfall in 24 hours of 300&#160;mm or more in some locations of <a href="/wiki/Rocha_Department" title="Rocha Department">Rocha</a> (Uruguay) and southern Rio Grande do Sul. The weather station owned by MetSul Weather Center in <a href="/wiki/Morro_Redondo" title="Morro Redondo">Morro Redondo</a>, Southern Brazil, recorded 278.2&#160;mm in a 24-hour period. Fourteen deaths and thousands of evacuees are attributed to the storm with an emergency declared in four cities.<sup id="cite_ref-GPJan2009_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPJan2009-9">[9]</a></sup> It lasted until February 1, when the cyclone became extratropical.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMet-50">[50]</a></sup> The system brought locally heavy rains in southern Brazil and northeast of Uruguay that exceeded 200 millimeters in a few hours in some locations of Southern Rio Grande do Sul northwest of Pelotas.<sup id="cite_ref-MetS_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MetS-51">[51]</a></sup> Damages and flooding were observed in Cerrito, id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> A fourth one formed in late January 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup><sup
