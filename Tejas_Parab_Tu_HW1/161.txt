<i>system</i>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a> remains constant: \xce\x94<i>T</i>&#160;=&#160;0. This typically occurs when a system is in contact with an outside thermal reservoir (<a href="/wiki/Heat_bath" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat bath">heat bath</a>), and the change will occur slowly enough to allow the system to continually adjust to the temperature of the reservoir through <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> exchange. In contrast, an <i><a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a></i> is where a system exchanges no heat with its surroundings (<i>Q</i>&#160;=&#160;0). In other words, in an isothermal process, the value \xce\x94<i>T</i>&#160;=&#160;0 and therefore \xce\x94<i>U</i>&#160;=&#160;0 (only for an ideal gas) but Q&#160;\xe2\x89\xa0&#160;0, while in an adiabatic process, \xce\x94<i>T</i>&#160;\xe2\x89\xa0&#160;0 but Q&#160;=&#160;0.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isothermal_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Isothermal processes can occur in any kind of system that has some means of regulating the temperature, including highly structured <a href="/wiki/Machines" class="mw-redirect" title="Machines">machines</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">living</a> cells. Some parts of the cycles of some <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engines</a> are carried out isothermally (for example, in the <a href="/wiki/Carnot_cycle" title="Carnot cycle">Carnot cycle</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In the thermodynamic analysis of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics" title="Chemical thermodynamics">chemical reactions</a>, it is usual to first analyze what happens under isothermal conditions and then consider the effect of temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-:Rock_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:Rock-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Phase_changes" class="mw-redirect" title="Phase changes">Phase changes</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, are also isothermal processes when, as is usually the case, they occur at constant pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Petrucci_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Petrucci-3">[3]</a></sup> Isothermal processes are often used and a starting point in analyzing more complex, non-isothermal processes.</p>\n<p>Isothermal processes are of special interest for ideal gases. This is a consequence of <a href="/wiki/Joule_effect#Joule.27s_second_law" title="Joule effect">Joule\'s second law</a> which states that the <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> of a fixed amount of an ideal gas depends only on its temperature.<sup id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Thus, in an isothermal process the internal energy of an ideal gas is constant. This is a result of the fact that in an ideal gas there are no <a href="/wiki/Intermolecular_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Intermolecular forces">intermolecular forces</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Note that this is true only for ideal gases; the internal energy depends on pressure as well as on temperature for liquids, solids, and real gases.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the isothermal compression of a gas there is work done on the system to decrease the volume and increase the pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Doing work on the gas increases the internal energy and will tend to increase the temperature. To maintain the constant temperature energy must leave the system as heat and enter the environment. If the gas is ideal, the amount of energy entering the environment is equal to the work done on the gas, because internal energy does not change. For details of the calculations, see <a href="/wiki/Isothermal_process#Calculation_of_work" title="Isothermal process">calculation of work</a>.</p>\n<p>For an <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">adiabatic process</a>, in which no heat flows into or out of the gas because its container is well insulated, <i>Q</i>&#160;=&#160;0. If there is also no work done, i.e. a <a href="/wiki/Joule_expansion" title="Joule expansion">free expansion</a>, there is no change in internal energy. For an ideal gas, this means that the process is also isothermal.<sup id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> Thus, specifying that a process is isothermal is not sufficient to specify a unique process.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Details_for_an_ideal_gas">Details for an ideal gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Isothermal_process&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Details for an ideal gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ideal_gas_isotherms.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Ideal_gas_isotherms.png/220px-Ideal_gas_isotherms.png" width="220" height="203" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Ideal_gas_isotherms.png/330px-Ideal_gas_isotherms.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Ideal_gas_isotherms.png/440px-Ideal_gas_isotherms.png 2x" data-file-width="1504" data-file-height="1386" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ideal_gas_isotherms.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<b>Figure 1.</b> Several isotherms of an ideal gas on a p-V diagram</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>For the special case of a gas to which <a href="/wiki/Boyle%27s_law" title="Boyle\'s law">Boyle\'s law</a><sup id="cite_ref-Klotz_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Klotz-4">[4]</a></sup> applies, the product <i>pV</i> is a constant if the gas is kept at isothermal conditions. Such an expansion is also isothermal and may have the same initial and final states as in the reversible expansion. Since entropy is a <a href="/wiki/State_function" title="State function">state function</a>, the change in entropy of the system is the same as in the reversible process and is given by the formulas above. Note that the result <i>Q</i>&#160;=&#160;0 for the free expansion can not be used in the formula for the entropy change since the process is not reversible.</p>\n<p>The difference between the reversible and free expansions is found in the entropy of the surroundings. In both cases, the surroundings are at a constant temperature, <i>T</i>, so that \xce\x94<i>S</i><sub>sur</sub>&#160;=&#160;\xe2\x88\x92<span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>Q</i></span><span class="visualhide">/</span><span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;"><i>T</i></span></span>; the minus sign is used since the heat transferred to the surroundings is equal in magnitude and opposite in sign to the heat, <i>Q</i>, transferred to the system. In the reversible case, the change in entropy of the surroundings is equal and opposite to the change in the system, so the change in entropy of the universe is zero. In the free expansion, <i>Q</i>&#160;=&#160;0, so the entropy of the surroundings does not change and the change in entropy of the universe is equal to \xce\x94<i>S</i> for the system.</p>\n<h2><span
