These fluctuations in <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_temperature" title="Atmospheric temperature">atmospheric temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">sea surface temperature</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> or other parameters can be <a href="/wiki/Quasi-periodic" class="mw-redirect" title="Quasi-periodic">quasi-periodic</a>, often occurring on inter-annual, multi-annual, decadal, multidecadal, century-wide, millennial or longer <a href="/wiki/Dynamical_time_scale" title="Dynamical time scale">timescales</a>. They are not perfectly periodic and a <a href="/wiki/Fourier_analysis" title="Fourier analysis">Fourier analysis</a> of the data does not give a sharp spectrum.</p>\n<p>A prominent example is the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation</a>, involving sea surface temperatures along a stretch of the equatorial Central and East <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and the western coast of tropical South America, but which affects climate worldwide.</p>\n<p>Records of past climate conditions are recovered through <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> examination of <a href="/wiki/Proxy_(climate)" title="Proxy (climate)">proxies</a>, found in <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">glacier ice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sedimentology" title="Sedimentology">sea bed sediment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">tree ring studies</a> or otherwise.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Many oscillations on different time-scales are hypothesized, although the causes may be unknown. (Some of them are more like a <a href="/wiki/Random_walk" title="Random walk">random walk</a> than an oscillation.) Here is a list of known or proposed climatic oscillations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>:</p>\n<p><br /></p>\n<ul>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Quasi-biennial_oscillation" title="Quasi-biennial oscillation">Quasi-biennial oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 about 30 months</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 2 to 7 years</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_decadal_oscillation" title="Pacific decadal oscillation">Pacific decadal oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 8 to 12 years? (not clear)</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">Schwabe Cycle</a> or sunspot cycle \xe2\x80\x93 about 11 years (may be discernible in climate records; see <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a>)</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">Hale Cycle</a> or double sunspot cycle - about 22 years<sup id="cite_ref-GeoSun_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeoSun-1">[1]</a></sup></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Interdecadal_Pacific_Oscillation" title="Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation">Interdecadal Pacific Oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 15 to 30 years? (not clear)</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Multidecadal_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation">Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 around 50 to 70 years, but unpredictable</li>\n<li>a 60-year climate cycle recorded in many ancient calendars<sup id="cite_ref-Scafetta_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scafetta-2">[2]</a></sup></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">Gleissberg Cycle</a> - a solar cycle of about 88 years<sup id="cite_ref-GeoSun_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeoSun-1">[1]</a></sup></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">Suess Cycle</a> or De Vries Cycle - a solar cycle of about 200 years<sup id="cite_ref-GeoSun_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeoSun-1">[1]</a></sup></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">Hallstadtzeit/Hallstatt Cycle</a> - a solar cycle of about 2,200 to 2,400 years<sup id="cite_ref-GeoSun_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GeoSun-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cycle24_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cycle24-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/North_African_climate_cycles" title="North African climate cycles">North African climate cycles</a> \xe2\x80\x93 tens of thousands of years</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">glacial periods</a> of <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">the current ice age</a> \xe2\x80\x93 period around 100 000 years (see <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation#Astronomical_cycles" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation#Astronomical cycles</a> and <a href="/wiki/100,000-year_problem" title="100,000-year problem">100,000-year problem</a>)</li>\n</ul>\n<p><br /></p>\n<ul>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_oscillation" title="Arctic oscillation">Arctic oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 no particular periodicity</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic Oscillation">North Atlantic Oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93 no particular periodicity</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/North_Pacific_Oscillation" title="North Pacific Oscillation">North Pacific Oscillation</a> \xe2\x80\x93&#160;?</li>\n</ul>\n<p><br />\nAnomalies in oscillations sometimes occur when they coincide, as in the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_dipole_anomaly" title="Arctic dipole anomaly">Arctic dipole anomaly</a> (a combination of the Arctic and North Atlantic oscillations) and the longer-term <a href="/wiki/Younger_Dryas" title="Younger Dryas">Younger Dryas</a>, a sudden non-linear cooling event that occurred at the onset of the current <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">interglacial</a>. In the case of volcanoes, large eruptions such as <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tambora" title="Mount Tambora">Mount Tambora</a> in 1816, which led to the <a href="/wiki/Year_Without_a_Summer" title="Year Without a Summer">Year Without a Summer</a>, typically cool the climate, especially when the volcano is located in the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical climate zone">tropics</a>. Around 70 000 years ago the <a href="/wiki/Toba_Volcano" class="mw-redirect" title="Toba Volcano">Toba supervolcano</a> eruption created an especially cold period during the ice age, leading to a possible <a href="/wiki/Toba_bottleneck" class="mw-redirect" title="Toba bottleneck">genetic bottleneck</a> in human populations. However, <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">outgassing</a> from <a href="/wiki/Large_igneous_province" title="Large igneous province">large igneous provinces</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Permian" title="Permian">Permian</a> <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Traps" title="Siberian Traps">Siberian Traps</a> can input <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into the atmosphere, warming the climate. Triggering of other mechanisms, such as <a href="/wiki/Methane_clathrate" title="Methane clathrate">methane clathrate</a> deposits as during the <a href="/wiki/Paleocene-Eocene_Thermal_Maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum">Paleocene-Eocene Thermal Maximum</a>, increased the rate of climatic temperature change and oceanic extinctions.</p>\n<p>Another longer-term near-millennial oscillation involves the <a href="/wiki/Daansgard-Oeschger_cycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Daansgard-Oeschger cycles">Daansgard-Oeschger cycles</a>, occurring on roughly 1,500-year cycles during the <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Last glacial maximum">last glacial maximum</a>. They may be related to the Holocene <a href="/wiki/Bond_event" title="Bond event">Bond events</a>, and may involve factors similar to those responsible for <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_event" title="Heinrich event">Heinrich events</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_causes">Origins and causes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins and causes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>There are close correlations between Earth\'s climate oscillations and astronomical factors (<a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">barycenter</a> changes, <a href="/wiki/Solar_variation" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar variation">solar variation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> flux, <a href="/wiki/Cloud_albedo" title="Cloud albedo">cloud albedo</a> <a href="/wiki/Cloud_feedback" title="Cloud feedback">feedback</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milankovic_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Milankovic cycle">Milankovic cycles</a>), and modes of <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">heat distribution</a> between the ocean-atmosphere climate system. In some cases, current, historical and <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimate" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleoclimate">paleoclimatological</a> natural oscillations may be masked by significant <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcanic eruption">volcanic eruptions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">impact events</a>, irregularities in <a href="/wiki/Climate_proxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate proxy">climate proxy</a> data, <a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">positive feedback</a> processes or <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas#Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas">emissions</a> of substances such as <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Scafetta_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scafetta-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Extreme phases of short-term climate oscillations such as ENSO can result in characteristic patterns of <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">droughts</a> (including <a href="/wiki/Megadrought" title="Megadrought">megadroughts</a>), <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoonal</a> disruption and extreme temperatures in the form of <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat waves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cold_wave" title="Cold wave">cold waves</a>. Shorter-term climate oscillations typically do not directly result in longer-term <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a> in temperatures. However, the effects of underlying climate trends such as recent <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and oscillations can be cumulative to <a href="/wiki/Global_temperature" title="Global temperature">global temperature</a>, producing shorter-term fluctuations in the <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_temperature_record" title="Instrumental temperature record">instrumental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Satellite_temperature_measurements" title="Satellite temperature measurements">satellite</a> <a href="/wiki/Temperature_record" title="Temperature record">temperature records</a>.</p>\n<p>Collapses of past civilizations such as the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya</a> may be related to cycles of precipitation, especially drought, that in this example also correlates to the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere_Warm_Pool" title="Western Hemisphere Warm Pool">Western Hemisphere Warm Pool</a>.</p>\n<p>One example of possible correlations between factors affecting the climate and global events, popular with the media, is a 2003 study on the correlation between <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a> prices and sunspot numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Analysis_and_uncertainties">Analysis and uncertainties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Analysis and uncertainties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Radiative_forcing" title="Radiative forcing">Radiative forcings</a> and other factors in a climate oscillation must obey the laws of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_thermodynamics" title="Atmospheric thermodynamics">atmospheric thermodynamics</a>. However, because Earth\'s climate is inherently a <a href="/wiki/Complex_system" title="Complex system">complex system</a>, simple <a href="/wiki/Fourier_analysis" title="Fourier analysis">Fourier analysis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Climate_model" title="Climate model">climate modelling</a> often does not create a perfect replication of the observed or inferred conditions. No climate cycle is found to be perfectly periodic, although the <a href="/wiki/Milankovich_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Milankovich cycle">Milankovich cycles</a> (based on multiple superimposed orbital cycles and <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession_(astronomy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Axial precession (astronomy)">Earth\'s precession</a>) are quite close to being periodic (perhaps <a href="/wiki/Almost_periodic" class="mw-redirect" title="Almost periodic">almost periodic</a>?).</p>\n<p>One difficulty in detecting climate cycles is that the Earth\'s climate has been changing in non-cyclic ways over most paleoclimatological timescales. For instance, we are now in a period of global warming that appears anthropogenic. In a larger timeframe, the Earth is <a href="/wiki/Holocene_glacial_retreat" title="Holocene glacial retreat">emerging</a> from the latest ice age, cooling from the <a href="/wiki/Holocene_climatic_optimum" title="Holocene climatic optimum">Holocene climatic optimum</a> and warming from the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>", which means that climate has been constantly changing over the last 15,000 years or so. During warm periods, temperature fluctuations are often of a lesser amplitude. The <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">Pleistocene</a> period, dominated by repeated <a href="/wiki/Glaciation" class="mw-redirect" title="Glaciation">glaciations</a>, developed out of more stable conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pliocene" title="Pliocene">Pliocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Pliocene_climate" title="Pliocene climate">climate</a>. Holocene climate has been relatively stable. All of these changes complicate the task of looking for cyclical behavior in the climate.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Positive_feedback" title="Positive feedback">Positive feedback</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negative_feedback" title="Negative feedback">negative feedback</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ecological_inertia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological inertia">ecological inertia</a> from the land-ocean-atmosphere system often attenuate or reverse smaller effects, whether from orbital forcings, solar variations or changes in concentrations of greenhouse gases. Most climatologists recognize the existence of various <a href="/wiki/Tipping_point_(climatology)" title="Tipping point (climatology)">tipping points</a> that push small forcings beyond a certain threshold that makes the change irreversible while the forcings are still in place. Certain feedbacks involvong processes such as clouds are also uncertain; for <a href="/wiki/Contrail" title="Contrail">contrails</a>, natural <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">cirrus</a> clouds, oceanic <a href="/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide" title="Dimethyl sulfide">dimethyl sulfide</a> and a land-based equivalent, competing theories exist concerning effects on climatic temperatures, for example contrasting the <a href="/wiki/Iris_hypothesis" title="Iris hypothesis">Iris hypothesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/CLAW_hypothesis" title="CLAW hypothesis">CLAW hypothesis</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Through_geologic_and_historical_time">Through geologic and historical time</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Climate_oscillation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Through geologic and historical time">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" class="image"><img alt="" data-file-width="650" data-file-height="397" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:65_Myr_Climate_Change.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nClimate change over the past 65 million years, using proxy data including <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-18" title="Oxygen-18">Oxygen-18</a> ratios from <a href="/wiki/Foraminifera" title="Foraminifera">foraminifera</a>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Holocene_Temperature_Variations.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Holocene_Temperature_Variations.png/500px-Holocene_Temperature_Variations.png" width="500" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ca/Holocene_Temperature_Variations.png 1.5x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Holocene_Temperature_Variations.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTemperature change over the past 12 000 years, from various sources. The thick black curve is an average.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate forcing">climate forcings</a> are typically in flux throughout <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time" class="mw-redirect" title="Geologic time">geologic time</a>, and some processes of the Earth\'s temperature may be <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">self-regulating</a>. For example, during the <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">Snowball Earth</a> period, large glacial ice sheets spanned to Earth\'s equator, covering nearly its entire surface, and very high <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a> created extremely low temperatures, while the accumulation of snow and ice likely removed carbon dioxide through <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(chemistry)" title="Deposition (chemistry)">atmospheric deposition</a>. However, the absence of <a href="/wiki/Plant_cover" title="Plant cover">plant cover</a> to absorb atmospheric CO<sub>2</sub> emitted by volcanoes meant that the greenhouse gas could accumulate in the atmosphere. There was also an absence of exposed silicate rocks, which use CO<sub>2</sub> when they undergo weathering. This created a warming that later melted the ice and brought Earth\'s temperature back to equilibrium. During the following eons of the <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">Paleozoic</a>, cosmic ray flux and occasional nearby <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> explosions (one hypothesis for the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Ordovician%E2%80%93Silurian_extinction_event" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordovician\xe2\x80\x93Silurian extinction event">Ordovician\xe2\x80\x93Silurian extinction event</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamma ray burst">gamma ray bursts</a> may have induced ice ages or other sudden climate changes.</p>\n<p>Throughout the <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">Cenozoic</a>, multiple climate forcings led to warming and cooling of the atmosphere, which led to the early formation of the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">Antarctic ice sheet</a>, subsequent melting, and its later reglaciation. The temperature changes occurred somewhat suddenly, at carbon dioxide concentrations of about 600\xe2\x80\x93760 ppm and temperatures approximately 4&#160;\xc2\xb0C warmer than today. During the Pleistocene, cycles of glaciations and interglacials occurred on cycles of roughly 100,000&#160;years, but may stay longer within an interglacial when <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">orbital eccentricity</a> approaches zero, as during the current interglacial. Previous interglacials such as the <a href="/wiki/Eemian_interglacial" class="mw-redirect" title="Eemian interglacial">Eemian</a> phase created temperatures higher than today, higher sea levels, and some partial melting of the <a href="/wiki/West_Antarctic_ice_sheet" class="mw-redirect" title="West Antarctic ice sheet">West Antarctic ice sheet</a>. The warmest part of the current interglacial occurred during the early Holocene Optimum, when temperatures were a few degrees Celsius warmer than today, and a strong <a href="/wiki/African_Monsoon" class="mw-redirect" title="African Monsoon">African Monsoon</a> created grassland conditions in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic_Subpluvial" title="Neolithic Subpluvial">Neolithic Subpluvial</a>. Since that time, several <a href="/wiki/Stadial" title="Stadial">cooling events</a> have occurred, including:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Piora_Oscillation" title="Piora Oscillation">Piora Oscillation</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age_Cold_Epoch" title="Middle Bronze Age Cold Epoch">Middle Bronze Age Cold Epoch</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age_Cold_Epoch" title="Iron Age Cold Epoch">Iron Age Cold Epoch</a></li>\n<li>cooling during the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Sp%C3%B6rer_Minimum" title="Sp\xc3\xb6rer Minimum">Sp\xc3\xb6rer Minimum</a></li>\n<li>the "<a href="/wiki/Little_Ice_Age" title="Little Ice Age">Little Ice Age</a>"</li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Dalton_Minimum" title="Dalton Minimum">Dalton Minimum</a></li>\n<li>volcanic coolings such as from <a href="/wiki/Laki" title="Laki">Laki</a> in Iceland</li>\n<li>the phase of cooling c. 1940-1970, which led to <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">global cooling</a> hypotheses</li>\n</ul>\n<p>In contrast, several warm periods have also taken place, and they include but are not limited to:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Older_Peron" title="Older Peron">Older Peron</a> during the late Holocene optimum</li>\n<li>a warm period during the apex of the <a href="/wiki/Minoan_civilization" title="Minoan civilization">Minoan civilization</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Warm_Period" title="Roman Warm Period">Roman Warm Period</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Warm_Period" title="Medieval Warm Period">Medieval Warm Period</a></li>\n<li>the <a href="/wiki/Retreat_of_glaciers_since_1850" title="Retreat of glaciers since 1850">retreat of glaciers since 1850</a></li>\n<li>the "<a href="/wiki/Modern_Warming" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Warming">Modern Warming</a>" during the 20th century</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Certain effects have occurred during these cycles. For example, during the Medieval Warm Period, the <a href="/wiki/American_Midwest" class="mw-redirect" title="American Midwest">American Midwest</a> was in drought, including the <a href="/wiki/Sand_Hills_(Nebraska)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand Hills (Nebraska)">Sand Hills of Nebraska</a> which were active <a href="/wiki/Sand_dune" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand dune">sand dunes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Black_death" class="mw-redirect" title="Black death">black death</a> plague of <i><a href="/wiki/Yersinia_pestis" title="Yersinia pestis">Yersinia pestis</a></i> also occurred during Medieval temperature fluctuations, and may be related to changing climates.</p>\n<p>Given that records of solar activity are accurate, solar activity may have contributed to part of the modern warming that peaked in the 1930s, in addition to the 60-year temperature cycles that result in roughly 0.5&#160;\xc2\xb0C of warming during the increasing temperature phase. However, solar cycles fail to account for warming observed since the 1980s to the present day<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. Events such as the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> and recent record low ice minima of the modern <a href="/wiki/Arctic_shrinkage" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic shrinkage">Arctic shrinkage</a> have not taken place for at least several centuries, as early explorers were all unable to make an Arctic crossing, even in summer. Shifts in <a href="/wiki/Biome" title="Biome">biomes</a> and habitat ranges are also unprecedented, occurring at rates that do not coincide with known climate oscillations<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>. The extinction of many tropical <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibian</a> species, especially in <a href="/wiki/Cloud_forest" title="Cloud forest">cloud forests</a>, have been attributed to changing global temperatures, fungal disease and possible influence from unusually extreme phases of oceanic climate oscillations.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
