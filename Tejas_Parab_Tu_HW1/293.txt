<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">\xce\xa4\xcf\x85\xcf\x86\xe1\xbf\xb6\xce\xbd</span>, <i>Tuph\xc5\x8dn</i> <span title="Representation in <i>Tuph\xc5\x8ds</i>), was a monstrous snaky giant and the most deadly creature in <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a>. According to <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, Typhon was the son of <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a>. However one source has Typhon as the son of <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> alone, while another makes Typhon the offspring of <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>. Typhon and his mate <a href="/wiki/Echidna_(mythology)" title="Echidna (mythology)">Echidna</a> were the progenitors of many famous monsters.</p>\n<p>Typhon attempted to overthrow <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> for the supremacy of the cosmos. The two fought a cataclysmic battle, which Zeus finally won with the aid of his thunderbolts. Defeated, Typhon was cast into Tartarus, or buried underneath Mount Etna, or the island of <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>. class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth">Birth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Birth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i> (c. 8th \xe2\x80\x93 7th century BC), Typhon was the son of <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a> (Earth) and <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a>: "when <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> had driven the <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">Titans</a> from heaven, huge Earth bore her youngest child Typhoeus of the love of Tartarus, by the aid of golden <a href="/wiki/Aphrodite" title="Aphrodite">Aphrodite</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The mythographer <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Apollodorus</a> (1st or 2nd century AD) adds that Gaia bore Typhon in anger at the gods for their destruction of her offspring the <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">Giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Numerous other sources mention Typhon as being the offspring of Gaia, or simply "earth-born", with no mention of Tartarus.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> However, according to the <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns"><i>Homeric Hymn to Apollo</i></a> (6th century BC), Typhon was the child of <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> alone.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Hera, angry at Zeus for having given birth to <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a> by himself, prayed to Gaia, <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>, and the Titans, to give her a son stronger than Zeus, then slapped the ground and became pregnant. Hera gave the infant Typhon to the serpent <a href="/wiki/Python_(mythology)" title="Python (mythology)">Python</a> to raise, and Typhon grew up to become a great bane to mortals.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg/300px-Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg" width="300" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg/450px-Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg/600px-Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2256" data-file-height="1648" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wenceslas_Hollar_-_The_Greek_gods._Tryphon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nDepiction by <a href="/wiki/Wenceslas_Hollar" class="mw-redirect" title="Wenceslas Hollar">Wenceslas Hollar</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Several sources locate Typhon\'s birth and dwelling place in <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>, and in particular the region in the vicinity of the ancient Cilician coastal city of <a href="/wiki/Corycus" title="Corycus">Corycus</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/K%C4%B1zkalesi" title="K\xc4\xb1zkalesi">K\xc4\xb1zkalesi</a>, Turkey). The poet <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> (c. 470 BC) calls Typhon "Cilician,"<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and says that Typhon was born in Cilicia and nurtured in "the famous Cilician cave",<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> an apparent allusion to the <a href="/wiki/Corycus#Corycian_Cave" title="Corycus">Corycian cave</a> in Turkey.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a>\' <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Bound" title="Prometheus Bound">Prometheus Bound</a></i>, Typhon is called the "dweller of the Cilician caves",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and both Apollodorus and the poet <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a> (4th or 5th century AD) have Typhon born in Cilicia.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The b scholia to <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> 2.783, preserving a possibly <a href="/wiki/Orphic" class="mw-redirect" title="Orphic">Orphic</a> tradition, has Typhon born in Cilicia, as the offspring of <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>. Gaia, angry at the destruction of the Giants, slanders Zeus to Hera. So Hera goes to Zeus\' father Cronus (whom Zeus had overthrown) and Cronus gives Hera two eggs smeared with his own semen, telling her to bury them, and that from them would be born one who would overthrow Zeus. Hera, angry at Zeus, buries the eggs in Cilicia "under Arimon", but when Typhon is born, Hera, now reconciled with Zeus, informs him.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions">Descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>, Typhon was "terrible, outrageous and lawless",<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> immensely powerful, and on his shoulders were one hundred snake heads, that emitted fire and every kind of noise:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>Strength was with his hands in all that he did and the feet of the strong god were untiring. From his shoulders grew a hundred heads of a snake, a fearful dragon, with dark, flickering tongues, and from under the brows of his eyes in his marvellous heads flashed fire, and fire burned from his heads as he glared. And there were voices in all his dreadful heads which uttered every kind of sound unspeakable; for at one time they made sounds such that the gods understood, but at another, the noise of a bull bellowing aloud in proud ungovernable fury; and at another, the sound of a lion, relentless of heart; and at another, sounds like whelps, wonderful to hear; and again, at another, he would hiss, so that the high mountains re-echoed.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Homeric_Hymns" title="Homeric Hymns"><i>Homeric Hymn to Apollo</i></a> describes Typhon as "fell" and "cruel", and neither like gods nor men.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Three of <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a>\'s poems have Typhon as hundred-headed (as in Hesiod),<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> while apparently a fourth gives him only fifty heads,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> but a hundred heads for Typhon became standard.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Chalcis" title="Chalcis">Chalcidian</a> <a href="/wiki/Hydria" title="Hydria">hydria</a> (c. 540\xe2\x80\x93530 BC), depicts Typhon as a winged humanoid from the waist up, with two snake tails below.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">Aeschylus</a> calls Typhon "fire-breathing".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> For <a href="/wiki/Nicander" title="Nicander">Nicander</a> (2nd century BC), Typhon was a monster of enormous strength, and strange appearance, with many heads, hands, and wings, and with huge snake coils coming from his thighs.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Apollodorus describes Typhon as a huge winged monster, whose head "brushed the stars", human in form above the waist, with snake coils below, and fire flashing from his eyes:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>In size and strength he surpassed all the offspring of Earth. As far as the thighs he was of human shape and of such prodigious bulk that he out-topped all the mountains, and his head often brushed the stars. One of his hands reached out to the west and the other to the east, and from them projected a hundred dragons\' heads. From the thighs downward he had huge coils of vipers, which when drawn out, reached to his very head and emitted a loud hissing. His body was all winged: unkempt hair streamed on the wind from his head and cheeks; and fire flashed from his eyes.</p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The most elaborate description of Typhon is found in <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dionysiaca" title="Dionysiaca">Dionysiaca</a></i>. Nonnus makes numerous references to Typhon\'s serpentine nature,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> giving him a "tangled army of snakes",<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> snaky feet,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> and hair.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> According to Nonnus, Typhon was a "poison-spitting viper",<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> whose "every hair belched viper-poison",<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and Typhon "spat out showers of poison from his throat; the mountain torrents were swollen, as the monster showered fountains from the viperish bristles of his high head",<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> and "the water-snakes of the monster\'s viperish feet crawl into the caverns underground, spitting poison!".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Following Hesiod and others, Nonnus gives Typhon many heads (though untotaled), but in addition to snake heads,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Nonnus also gives Typhon many other animal heads, including leopards, lions, bulls, boars, bears, cattle, wolves, and dogs, which combine to make \'the cries of all wild beasts together\',<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> and a "babel of screaming sounds".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Nonnus also gives Typhon "legions of arms innumerable",<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> and where Nicander had only said that Typhon had "many" hands, and Ovid had given Typhon a hundred hands, Nonnus gives Typhon two hundred.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Offspring">Offspring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Offspring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i>, Typhon "was joined in love" to <a href="/wiki/Echidna_(mythology)" title="Echidna (mythology)">Echidna</a>, a monstrous half-woman and half-snake, who bore Typhon "fierce offspring".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> First, according to Hesiod, there was <a href="/wiki/Orthrus" title="Orthrus">Orthrus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup> the two-headed dog who guarded the Cattle of <a href="/wiki/Geryon" title="Geryon">Geryon</a>, second <a href="/wiki/Cerberus" title="Cerberus">Cerberus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> the multiheaded dog who guarded the gates of <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">Hades</a>, and third the <a href="/wiki/Lernaean_Hydra" title="Lernaean Hydra">Lernaean Hydra</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> the many-headed serpent who, when one of its heads was cut off, grew two more. The <i>Theogony</i> next mentions an ambiguous "she", which might refer to Echidna, as the mother of the <a href="/wiki/Chimera_(mythology)" title="Chimera (mythology)">Chimera</a> (a fire-breathing beast that was part lion, part goat, and had a snake-headed tail) with Typhon then being the father.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While mentioning Cerberus and "other monsters" as being the offspring of Echidna and Typhon, the mythographer <a href="/wiki/Acusilaus" title="Acusilaus">Acusilaus</a> (6th century BC) adds the <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_Eagle" class="mw-redirect" title="Caucasian Eagle">Caucasian Eagle</a> that ate the liver of <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">Prometheus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> the mythographer <a href="/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Leros" title="Pherecydes of Leros">Pherecydes of Leros</a> (5th century BC), also names Prometheus\' eagle,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> and adds <a href="/wiki/Ladon_(mythology)" title="Ladon (mythology)">Ladon</a> (though Pherecydes does not use this name), and the dragon that guarded the <a href="/wiki/Golden_apple" title="Golden apple">golden apples</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Garden_of_the_Hesperides" class="mw-redirect" title="Garden of the Hesperides">Garden of the Hesperides</a> (according to Hesiod, the offspring of Ceto and Phorcys).<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> The lyric poet Lasus of Hermione (6th century BC) adds the Sphinx.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Later authors mostly retain these offspring of Typhon by Echidna, while adding others. <a href="/wiki/Bibliotheca_(Pseudo-Apollodorus)" title="Bibliotheca (Pseudo-Apollodorus)">Apollodorus</a>, in addition to naming as their offspring Orthrus, the Chimera (citing Hesiod as his source) the Caucasian Eagle, Ladon, and the Sphinx, also adds the Nemean lion (no mother is given), and the <a href="/wiki/Crommyonian_Sow" title="Crommyonian Sow">Crommyonian Sow</a>, killed by the hero <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> (unmentioned by Hesiod).<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Hyginus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a> (1st century BC),<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[45]</a></sup> in his list of offspring of Typhon (all by Echidna), retains from the above: Cerberus, the Chimera, the Sphinx, the Hydra and Ladon, and adds "Gorgon" (by which Hyginus means the mother of <a href="/wiki/Medusa" title="Medusa">Medusa</a>, whereas Hesiod\'s three <a href="/wiki/Gorgons" class="mw-redirect" title="Gorgons">Gorgons</a>, of which Medusa was one, were the daughters of Ceto and Phorcys), the <a href="/wiki/Colchian_Dragon" class="mw-redirect" title="Colchian Dragon">Colchian Dragon</a> that guarded the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Fleece" title="Golden Fleece">Golden Fleece</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Scylla" title="Scylla">Scylla</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Harpies" class="mw-redirect" title="Harpies">Harpies</a>, in Hesiod the daughters of <a href="/wiki/Thaumas" title="Thaumas">Thaumas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Oceanid</a> <a href="/wiki/Oceanid" title="Oceanid">Electra</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[48]</a></sup> in one source, are said to be the daughters of Typhon.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The sea serpents which attacked the Trojan priest <a href="/wiki/Laoco%C3%B6n" title="Laoco\xc3\xb6n">Laoco\xc3\xb6n</a>, during the <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">Trojan War</a>, were perhaps supposed to be the progeny of Typhon and Echidna.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to Hesiod, the defeated Typhon is the source of destructive storm winds.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Battle_with_Zeus">Battle with Zeus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Battle with Zeus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Typhon challenged <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> for rule of the cosmos.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The earliest mention of Typhon, and his only occurrence in <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>, is a passing reference in the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> to Zeus striking the ground around where Typhon lies defeated.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Theogony" title="Theogony">Theogony</a></i> gives us the first account of their battle. According to Hesiod, without the quick action of Zeus, Typhon would have "come to reign over mortals and immortals".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> In the <i>Theogony</i> Zeus and Typhon meet in cataclysmic conflict:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>[Zeus] thundered hard and mightily: and the earth around resounded terribly and the wide heaven above, and the sea and Ocean\'s streams and the nether parts of the earth. Great Olympus reeled beneath the divine feet of the king as he arose and earth groaned thereat. And through the two of them heat took hold on the dark-blue sea, through the thunder and lightning, and through the fire from the monster, and the scorching winds and blazing thunderbolt. The whole earth seethed, and sky and sea: and the long waves raged along the beaches round and about at the rush of the deathless gods: and there arose an endless shaking. Hades trembled where he rules over the dead below, and the Titans under Tartarus who live with Cronos, because of the unending clamor and the fearful strife.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Zeus with his thunderbolt easily overcomes Typhon,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> who is thrown down to earth in a fiery crash:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>So when Zeus had raised up his might and seized his arms, thunder and lightning and lurid thunderbolt, he leaped from Olympus and struck him, and burned all the marvellous heads of the monster about him. But when Zeus had conquered him and lashed him with strokes, Typhoeus was hurled down, a maimed wreck, so that the huge earth groaned. And flame shot forth from the thunderstricken lord in the dim rugged glens of the mount, when he was smitten. A great part of huge earth was scorched by the terrible vapor and melted as tin melts when heated by men\'s art in channelled crucibles; or as iron, which is hardest of all things, is shortened by glowing fire in mountain glens and melts in the divine earth through the strength of Hephaestus. Even so, then, the earth melted in the glow of the blazing fire.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Defeated, Typhon is cast into <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a> by an angry Zeus.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Epimenides" title="Epimenides">Epimenides</a> (7th or 6th century BC) seemingly knew a different version of the story, in which Typhon enters Zeus\' palace while Zeus is asleep, but Zeus awakes and kills Typhon with a thunderbolt.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> apparently knew of a tradition which had the gods, in order to escape from Typhon, transform themselves into animals, and flee to Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> calls Typhon the "enemy of the gods",<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup> and says that he was defeated by Zeus\' thunderbolt.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> In one poem Pindar has Typhon being held prisoner by Zeus under Etna, <sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> and in another says that Typhon "lies in dread Tartarus", stretched out underground between Mount Etna and <a href="/wiki/Cumae" title="Cumae">Cumae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> In Aeschylus\' <i>Prometheus Bound</i>, a "hissing" Typhon, his eyes flashing, "withstood all the gods", but "the unsleeping bolt of Zeus" struck him, and "he was burnt to ashes and his strength blasted from him by the lightning bolt."<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup></p>\n<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Leros" title="Pherecydes of Leros">Pherecydes of Leros</a>, during his battle with Zeus, Typhon first flees to the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, which begins to burn, then to the volcanic island of Pithecussae (modern <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>), off the coast of Cumae, where he is buried under the island.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Apollonius_of_Rhodes" title="Apollonius of Rhodes">Apollonius of Rhodes</a> (3rd century BC), like Pherecydes, presents a multi-stage battle, with Typhon being struck by Zeus\' thunderbolt on mount <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a>, before fleeing to the mountains and plain of Nysa, and ending up (as already mentioned by the fifth-century BC Greek historian <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">Herodotus</a>) buried under <a href="/wiki/Lake_Bardawil" title="Lake Bardawil">Lake Serbonis</a> in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">[67]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Like Pindar, <a href="/wiki/Nicander" title="Nicander">Nicander</a> has all the gods but Zeus and <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, transform into animal forms and flee to Egypt: <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> became a hawk, <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> an ibis, <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a> a fish, <a href="/wiki/Artemis" title="Artemis">Artemis</a> a cat, <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a> a goat, <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> a fawn, <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a> an ox, and <a href="/wiki/Leto" title="Leto">Leto</a> a mouse.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[68]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The geographer <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> (c. 20 AD) gives several locations which were associated with the battle. According to Strabo, Typhon was said to have cut the serpentine channel of the <a href="/wiki/Orontes_River" title="Orontes River">Orontes River</a>, which flowed beneath the <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a> Mount Kasios (modern <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Aqra" title="Jebel Aqra">Jebel Aqra</a>), while fleeing from Zeus,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup> and some placed the battle at <a href="/wiki/Catacecaumene" class="mw-redirect" title="Catacecaumene">Catacecaumene</a> ("Burnt Land"),<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">[70]</a></sup> a volcanic plain, on the upper <a href="/wiki/Gediz_River" title="Gediz River">Gediz River</a>, between the ancient kingdoms of <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mysia" title="Mysia">Mysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">Phrygia</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Mount_Tmolus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mount Tmolus">Mount Tmolus</a> (modern Bozda\xc4\x9f) and <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a> the ancient capital of Lydia.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In the versions of the battle given by Hesiod, Aeschylus and Pindar, Zeus\' defeat of Typhon is straightforward, however a more involved version of the battle is given by Apollodorus.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> No early source gives any reason for the conflict, but Apollodorus\' account seemingly implies that Typhon had been produced by Gaia to avenge the destruction, by Zeus and the other gods, of the Giants, a previous generation of offspring of Gaia. According to Apollodorus, Typhon, "hurling kindled rocks", attacked the gods, "with hissings and shouts, spouting a great jet of fire from his mouth." Seeing this, the gods transformed into animals and fled to Egypt (as in Pindar and Nicander). However "Zeus pelted Typhon at a distance with thunderbolts, and at close quarters struck him down with an adamantine sickle"<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> Wounded, Typhon fled to the Syrian Mount Kasios, where Zeus "grappled" with him. But Typhon, twining his snaky coils around Zeus, was able to wrest away the sickle and cut the sinews from Zeus\' hands and feet. Typhon carried the disabled Zeus across the sea to the <a href="/wiki/Corycian_cave_(Turkey)" class="mw-redirect" title="Corycian cave (Turkey)">Corycian cave</a> in Cilicia where he set the she-serpent <a href="/wiki/Delphyne" title="Delphyne">Delphyne</a> to guard over Zeus and his severed sinews, which Typhon had hidden in a bearskin. But <a href="/wiki/Hermes" title="Hermes">Hermes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aegipan" title="Aegipan">Aegipan</a> (possibly another name for <a href="/wiki/Pan_(god)" title="Pan (god)">Pan</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> stole the sinews and gave them back to Zeus. His strength restored, Zeus chased Typhon to mount Nysa, where the <a href="/wiki/Moirai" title="Moirai">Moirai</a> tricked Typhon into eating "ephemeral fruits" which weakened him. Typhon then fled to <a href="/wiki/Thrace" title="Thrace">Thrace</a>, where he threw mountains at Zeus, which were turned back on him by Zeus\' thunderbolts, and the mountain where Typhon stood, being drenched with Typhon\'s blood, became known as Mount Haemus (Bloody Mountain). Typhon then fled to <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, where Zeus threw <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a> on top of Typhon burying him, and so finally defeated him.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Oppian" title="Oppian">Oppian</a> (2nd century AD) says that Pan helped Zeus in the battle by tricking Typhon to come out from his lair, and into the open, by the "promise of a banquet of fish", thus enabling Zeus to defeat Typhon with his thunderbolts.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span id="Nonnus\'s_Dionysiaca"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonnus.27s_Dionysiaca">Nonnus\'s <i>Dionysiaca</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nonnus\'s Dionysiaca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>The longest and most involved version of the battle appears in <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Dionysiaca" title="Dionysiaca">Dionysiaca</a></i> (late 4th or early 5th century AD).<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">[76]</a></sup> Zeus hides his thunderbolts in a cave, so that he might seduce the maiden <a href="/wiki/Plouto_(mother_of_Tantalus)" title="Plouto (mother of Tantalus)">Plouto</a>, and so produce <a href="/wiki/Tantalus" title="Tantalus">Tantalus</a>. But smoke rising from the thunderbolts, enables Typhon, under the guidance of Gaia, to locate Zeus\'s weapons, steal them, and hide them in another cave.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> Immediately Typhon extends "his clambering hands into the upper air" and begins a long and concerted attack upon the heavens.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> Then "leaving the air" he turns his attack upon the seas.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup> Finally Typhon attempts to wield Zeus\' thunderbolts, but they "felt the hands of a novice, and all their manly blaze was unmanned."<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Now Zeus\' sinews had somehow \xe2\x80\x93 Nonnus does not say how or when \xe2\x80\x94 fallen to the ground during their battle, and Typhon had taken them also.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[81]</a></sup> But Zeus devises a plan with <a href="/wiki/Cadmus" title="Cadmus">Cadmus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pan_(god)" title="Pan (god)">Pan</a> to beguile Typhon.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup> Cadmus, desguised as a shepherd, enchants Typhon by playing the panpipes, and Typhon entrusting the thuderbolts to Gaia, sets out to find the source of the music he hears.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[83]</a></sup> Finding Cadmus, he challenges him to a contest, offering Cadmus any goddess as wife, excepting Hera whom Typhon has reserved for himself.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup> Cadmus then tells Typhon that, if he liked the "little tune" of his pipes, then he would love the music of his lyre \xe2\x80\x93 if only it could be strung with Zeus\' sinews.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> So Typhon retrieves the sinews and gives them to Cadmus, who hides them in another cave, and again begins to play his bewitching pipes, so that "Typhoeus yielded his whole soul to Cadmos for the melody to charm".<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With Typhon distracted, Zeus takes back his thunderbolts. Cadmus stops playing, and Typhon, released from his spell, rushes back to his cave to discover the thunderbolts gone. Incensed Typhon unleashes devastation upon the world: animals are devoured, (Typhon\'s many animal heads each eat animals of its own kind), rivers turned to dust, seas made dry land, and the land "laid waste".<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">[87]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The day ends with Typhon yet unchallenged, and while the other gods "moved about the cloudless Nile", Zeus waits through the night for the coming dawn.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> Victory "reproaches" Zeus, urging him to "stand up as champion of your own children!"<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Dawn comes and Typhon roars out a challenge to Zeus.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> And a cataclysmic battle for "the sceptre and throne of Zeus" is joined. Typhon piles up mountains as battlements and with his "legions of arms innumerable", showers volley after volley of trees and rocks at Zeus, but all are destroyed, or blown aside, or dodged, or thrown back at Typhon. Typhon throws torrents of water at Zeus\' thunderbolts to quench them, but Zeus is able to cut off some of Typhon\'s hands with "frozen volleys of air as by a knife", and hurling thunderbolts is able to burn more of typhon\'s "endless hands", and cut off some of his "countless heads". Typhon is attacked by the four winds, and "frozen volleys of jagged hailstones."<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Gaia tries to aid her burnt and frozen son.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> Finally Typhon falls, and Zeus shouts out a long stream of mocking taunts, telling Typhon that he is to be buried under Sicily\'s hills, with a <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> over him which will read "This is the barrow of Typhoeus, son of Earth, who once lashed the sky with stones, and the fire of heaven burnt him up".<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial_and_cause_of_volcanic_activity">Burial and cause of volcanic activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Burial and cause of volcanic activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Etna_and_Ischia">Etna and Ischia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Etna and Ischia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Most accounts have the defeated Typhon buried under either <a href="/wiki/Mount_Etna" title="Mount Etna">Mount Etna</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">Sicily</a>, or the volcanic island of <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>, the largest of the <a href="/wiki/Phlegraean_Islands" title="Phlegraean Islands">Phlegraean Islands</a> off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>, with Typhon being the cause of volcanic eruptions and earthquakes.</p>\n<p>Though Hesiod has Typhon simply cast into <a href="/wiki/Tartarus" title="Tartarus">Tartarus</a> by Zeus, some have read a reference to Mount Etna in Hesiod\'s description of Typhon\'s fall:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>And flame shot forth from the thunderstricken lord in the dim rugged glens of the mount when he was smitten. A great part of huge earth was scorched by the terrible vapor and melted as tin melts when heated by men\'s art in channelled crucibles; or as iron, which is hardest of all things, is shortened by glowing fire in mountain glens and melts in the divine earth through the strength of Hephaestus. Even so, then, the earth melted in the glow of the blazing fire.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>The first certain references to Typhon buried under Etna, as well as being the cause of its eruptions, occur in Pindar:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>Son of Cronus, you who hold Aetna, the wind-swept weight on terrible hundred-headed Typhon, <sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>and:</p>\n<blockquote>\n<p>among them is he who lies in dread Tartarus, that enemy of the gods, Typhon with his hundred heads. Once the famous Cilician cave nurtured him, but now the sea-girt cliffs above Cumae, and Sicily too, lie heavy on his shaggy chest. And the pillar of the sky holds him down, snow-covered Aetna, year-round nurse of bitter frost, from whose inmost caves belch forth the purest streams of unapproachable fire. In the daytime her rivers roll out a fiery flood of smoke, while in the darkness of night the crimson flame hurls rocks down to the deep plain of the sea with a crashing roar. That monster shoots up the most terrible jets of fire; it is a marvellous wonder to see, and a marvel even to hear about when men are present. Such a creature is bound beneath the dark and leafy heights of Aetna and beneath the plain, and his bed scratches and goads the whole length of his back stretched out against it.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup></p>\n</blockquote>\n<p>Thus Pindar has Typhon in Tartarus, and buried under not just Etna, but under a vast volcanic region stretching from Sicily to <a href="/wiki/Cumae" title="Cumae">Cumae</a> (in the vicinity of modern <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>), a region which presumably also included <a href="/wiki/Mount_Vesuvius" title="Mount Vesuvius">Mount Vesuvius</a>, as well as Ischia.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Many subsequent accounts mention either Etna<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> or Ischia.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> In <i><a href="/wiki/Prometheus_Bound" title="Prometheus Bound">Prometheus Bound</a></i>, Typhon is imprisoned underneath Etna, while above him <a href="/wiki/Hephaestus" title="Hephaestus">Hephaestus</a> "hammers the molten ore", and in his rage, the "charred" Typhon causes "rivers of fire" to pour forth. <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">Ovid</a> has Typhon buried under all of Sicily, with his left and right hands under <a href="/wiki/Punta_del_Faro" title="Punta del Faro">Pelorus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pachynus" class="mw-redirect" title="Pachynus">Pachynus</a>, his feet under <a href="/wiki/Lilybaeum" class="mw-redirect" title="Lilybaeum">Lilybaeus</a>, and his head under Etna; where he "vomits flames from his ferocious mouth". And <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Valerius_Flaccus" title="Gaius Valerius Flaccus">Valerius Flaccus</a> has Typhon\'s head under Etna, and all of Sicily shaken when Typhon "struggles". <a href="/wiki/Lycophron" title="Lycophron">Lycophron</a> has both Typhon and <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">Giants</a> buried under the island of Ischia. <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silius_Italicus" title="Silius Italicus">Silius Italicus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Claudian" title="Claudian">Claudian</a>, all calling the island "Inarime", have Typhon buried there. Strabo, calling Ischia "Pithecussae", reports the "myth" that Typhon lay buried there, and that when he "turns his body the flames and the waters, and sometimes even small islands containing boiling water, spout forth."<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">[100]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In addition to Typhon, other mythological beings were also said to be buried under Mount Etna and the cause of its volcanic activity. Most notably the Giant <a href="/wiki/Enceladus_(mythology)" title="Enceladus (mythology)">Enceladus</a> was said to be entombed under Etna, the volcano\'s eruptions being the breath of Enceladus, and its tremors caused by the Giant rolling over from side to side beneath the mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Also said to be buried under Etna were the Hundred-hander <a href="/wiki/Briareus" class="mw-redirect" title="Briareus">Briareus</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup> and Asteropus who was perhaps one of the <a href="/wiki/Cyclops" title="Cyclops">Cyclopes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup></p>\n<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Boeotia">Boeotia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Boeotia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>\n<p>Typhon\'s final resting place was apparently also said to be in <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Hesiodic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hesiodic">Hesiodic</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Shield_of_Heracles" title="Shield of Heracles">Shield of Heracles</a></i> names a mountain near <a href="/wiki/Thebes,_Greece" title="Thebes, Greece">Thebes</a> Typhaonium, perhaps reflecting an early tradition which also had Typhon buried under a <a href="/wiki/Boeotia" title="Boeotia">Boeotian</a> mountain.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">[105]</a></sup> And some apparently claimed that Typhon was buried beneath a mountain in Boeotia, from which came exhaltations of fire.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span id="&quot;Couch_of_Typhoeus&quot;"></span><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Couch_of_Typhoeus.22">"Couch of Typhoeus"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: &quot;Couch of Typhoeus&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Homer describes a place he calls the "couch [or bed] of Typhoeus", which he locates in the land of the Arimoi (<i>\xce\xb5\xe1\xbc\xb0\xce\xbd \xe1\xbc\x88\xcf\x81\xce\xaf\xce\xbc\xce\xbf\xce\xb9\xcf\x82</i>), where Zeus lashes the land about Typhoeus with his thunderbolts.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Presumably this is the same land where, according to Hesiod, Typhon\'s mate Echidna keeps guard "in Arima" (<i>\xce\xb5\xe1\xbc\xb0\xce\xbd \xe1\xbc\x88\xcf\x81\xce\xaf\xce\xbc\xce\xbf\xce\xb9\xcf\x83\xce\xb9\xce\xbd</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup></p>\n<p>But neither Homer nor Hesiod say anything more about where these Arimoi or this Arima might be. The question of whether an historical place was meant, and its possible location, has been, since ancient times, the subject of speculation and debate.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[109]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">Strabo</a> discusses the question in some detail.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[110]</a></sup> Several locales, <a href="/wiki/Cilicia" title="Cilicia">Cilicia</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Syria" title="History of Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lydia" title="Lydia">Lydia</a>, and the island of <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>, all places associated with Typhon, are given by Strabo as possible locations for Homer\'s "Arimoi".</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Pindar" title="Pindar">Pindar</a> has his Cilician Typhon slain by Zeus "among the Arimoi",<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup> and the historian <a href="/wiki/Callisthenes" title="Callisthenes">Callisthenes</a> (4th century BC), located the Arimoi and the Arima mountains in Cilicia, near the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6ksu" title="G\xc3\xb6ksu">Calycadnus</a> river, the Corycian cave and the Sarpedon promomtory.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">[112]</a></sup> The b scholia to <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i> 2.783, mentioned above, says Typhon was born in Cilicia "under Arimon",<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a> mentions Typhon\'s "bloodstained cave of Arima" in Cilicia.<sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">[114]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Just across the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Issus" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf of Issus">Gulf of Issus</a> from <a href="/wiki/Corycus" title="Corycus">Corycus</a>, in ancient Syria, was Mount Kasios (modern <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Aqra" title="Jebel Aqra">Jebel Aqra</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Orontes_River" title="Orontes River">Orontes River</a>, sites associated with Typhon\'s battle with Zeus,<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> and according to Strabo, the historian <a href="/wiki/Posidonius" title="Posidonius">Posidonius</a> (c. 2nd century BC) identified the Arimoi with the <a href="/wiki/Aramaeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaeans">Aramaeans</a> of Syria.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[116]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Alternatively, according to Strabo, some placed the Arimoi at Catacecaumene,<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[117]</a></sup> while <a href="/wiki/Xanthus_(historian)" title="Xanthus (historian)">Xanthus of Lydia</a> (5th century BC) added that "a certain Arimus" ruled there.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[118]</a></sup> Strabo also tells us that for "some" Homer\'s "couch of Typhon" was located "in a wooded place, in the fertile land of Hyde", with Hyde being another name for <a href="/wiki/Sardis" title="Sardis">Sardis</a> (or its acropolis), and that <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_of_Scepsis" title="Demetrius of Scepsis">Demetrius of Scepsis</a> (2nd century BC) thought that the Arimoi were most plausibly located "in the Catacecaumene country in Mysia".<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> The 3rd-century BC poet <a href="/wiki/Lycophron" title="Lycophron">Lycophron</a> placed the lair of Typhons\' mate Echidna in this region.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Another place, mentioned by Strabo, as being associated with Arima, is the island of <a href="/wiki/Ischia" title="Ischia">Ischia</a>, where according to <a href="/wiki/Pherecydes_of_Leros" title="Pherecydes of Leros">Pherecydes of Leros</a>, Typhon had fled, and in the area where Pindar and others had said Typhon was buried. The connection to Arima, comes from the island\'s Greek name Pithecussae, which derives from the Greek word for monkey, and according to Strabo, residents of the island said that "arimoi" was also the Etruscan word for monkeys.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Typhon\'s name has a number of variants.<sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> The earliest forms of Typhoeus and Typhaon, occur prior to the 5th century BC. Homer uses Typhoeus,<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> Hesiod and the <i>Homeric Hymn to Apollo</i> use both Typhoeus and Typhaon.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> The later forms Typhos and Typhon occur from the 5th century BC onwards, with Typhon becoming the standard form by the end of that century.</p>\n<p>Though several possible derivations of the name Typhon have been suggested, the derivation remains uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-125" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup> Consistent with Hesiod\'s making storm winds Typhon\'s offspring, some have supposed that Typhon was originally a wind-god, and ancient sources associated him with the Greek words <i>tuphon, tuphos</i> meaning "whirlwind".<sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Other theories include derivation from a Greek root meaning "smoke" (consistent with Typhon\'s identification with volcanoes),<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> from an <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_root" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-European root">Indo-European root</a> meaning "abyss" (making Typhon a "Serpent of the Deep"),<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">[128]</a></sup> and from Sap\xc3\xb5n the <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_language" title="Phoenician language">Phoenician</a> name for the <a href="/wiki/Ugaritic" title="Ugaritic">Ugaritic</a> god <a href="/wiki/Baal" title="Baal">Baal</a>\'s holy mountain <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Aqra" title="Jebel Aqra">Jebel Aqra</a> (the classical Mount Kasios) associated with the epithet <a href="/wiki/Baal-zephon" title="Baal-zephon">Ba\xca\xbfal Sap\xc5\x8dn</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-129" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_mythology">Related mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Related mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Succession_myth">Succession myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Succession myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Typhonomachy, Zeus\' battle and defeat of Typhon, is just one part of a larger "Succession Myth" given in Hesiod\'s <i>Theogony</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">[130]</a></sup> The Hesiodic succession myth describes how <a href="/wiki/Uranus_(mythology)" title="Uranus (mythology)">Uranus</a>, the original ruler of the cosmos, hid his offspring away inside <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">Gaia</a>, but was overthrown by his <a href="/wiki/Titans_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Titans (mythology)">Titan</a> son <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">Cronus</a>, who castrated Uranus, and how in turn, Cronus, who swallowed his children as they were born, was himself overthrown by his son Zeus, whose mother had given Cronus a stone wrapped in swaddling clothes to swallow, in place of Zeus. However Zeus is then confronted with one final adversary, Typhon, which he quickly defeats. Now clearly the supreme power in the cosmos, Zeus is elected king of gods. Zeus then establishes and secures his realm through the apportionment of various functions and responsibilities to the other gods, and by means of marriage. Finally, by swallowing his first wife <a href="/wiki/Metis_(mythology)" title="Metis (mythology)">Metis</a>, who was destined to produce a son stronger than himself, Zeus is able to put an end to the cycle of succession.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Python">Python</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Python">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Typhon\'s story seems related to that of another monstrous offspring of Gaia: <a href="/wiki/Python_(mythology)" title="Python (mythology)">Python</a>, the serpent killed by <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> at <a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">Delphi</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup> suggesting a possible common origin.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup> Besides the similarity of names, their shared parentage, and the fact that both were snaky monsters killed in single combat with an Olympian god, there are other connections between the stories surrounding Typhon, and those surrounding <a href="/wiki/Python_(mythology)" title="Python (mythology)">Python</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">[133]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Although the Delphic monster killed by <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> is usually said to be the male serpent Python, in the <i>Homeric Hymn to Apollo</i>, the earliest account of this story, the god kills a nameless she-serpent (<i><a href="/wiki/Drakaina_(mythology)" title="Drakaina (mythology)">drakaina</a></i>), subsequently called <a href="/wiki/Delphyne" title="Delphyne">Delphyne</a>, who had been Typhon\'s foster-mother.<sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Delphyne and Echidna, besides both being intimately connected to Typhon\xe2\x80\x94one as mother, the other as mate\xe2\x80\x94share other similarities.<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">[135]</a></sup> Both were half-maid and half-snake,<sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup> a plague to men,<sup id="cite_ref-137" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup> and associated with the Corycian cave in Cilicia.<sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Python was also perhaps connected with a different <a href="/wiki/Corycian_Cave" title="Corycian Cave">Corycian Cave</a> than the one in Cilicia, this one on the slopes of <a href="/wiki/Parnassus" class="mw-redirect" title="Parnassus">Parnassus</a> above Delphi, and just as the Corcian cave in Cilicia was thought to be Typhon and Echidna\'s lair, and associated with Typhon\'s battle with Zeus, there is evidence to suggest that the Corycian cave above Delphi was supposed to be Python\'s (or Delphyne\'s) lair, and associated with his (or her) battle with Apollo.<sup id="cite_ref-139" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-139">[139]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Near_Eastern_influence">Near Eastern influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Near Eastern influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Mythologist <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Campbell" title="Joseph Campbell">Joseph Campbell</a> makes parallels to the slaying of <a href="/wiki/Leviathan" title="Leviathan">Leviathan</a> by <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">YHWH</a>, about which YHWH boasts to <a href="/wiki/Job_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Job (Bible)">Job</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-140" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> Ogden calls the Typhon myth "the only Graeco-Roman <a href="/wiki/Dragons_in_Greek_mythology" title="Dragons in Greek mythology"><i>drak\xc5\x8dn</i></a>-slaying myth that can seriously be argued to exhibit the influence of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Near Eastern</a> antecedents", connecting it in particular with Ba\xca\xbfal Zaphon\'s slaying of <a href="/wiki/Yammu" class="mw-redirect" title="Yammu">Yammu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lotan" title="Lotan">Lotan</a>, as well as with the <a href="/wiki/Hittite_mythology" title="Hittite mythology">Hittite myth</a> of <a href="/wiki/Illuyankas" class="mw-redirect" title="Illuyankas">Illuyankas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-141" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup> From its first reappearance, this latter myth has been seen as a prototype of the battle of Zeus and Typhon.<sup id="cite_ref-142" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Walter_Burkert" title="Walter Burkert">Walter Burkert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Watkins" title="Calvert Watkins">Calvert Watkins</a> each note the close agreements.</p>\n<p>Comparisons can also be drawn with the Mesopotamian monster <a href="/wiki/Tiamat" title="Tiamat">Tiamat</a> and her slaying by Babylonian chief god <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-143" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> The similarities between the Greek myth and its earlier Mesopotamian counterpart do not seem to be merely accidental. A number of west Semitic (<a href="/wiki/Ras_Shamra" class="mw-redirect" title="Ras Shamra">Ras Shamra</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Hittites" title="Hittites">Hittite</a> sources appear to corroborate the theory of a genetic relationship between the two myths.<sup id="cite_ref-144" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Set">Set</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Set">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>From apparently as early as <a href="/wiki/Hecataeus_of_Miletus" title="Hecataeus of Miletus">Hecataeus of Miletus</a> (c. 550 BC \xe2\x80\x93 c. 476 BC), Typhon was identified with <a href="/wiki/Set_(mythology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Set (mythology)">Set</a>, the Egyptian god of destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-145" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> This syncretization with Egyptian mythology can also be seen in the story, apparently known as early as Pindar, of Typhon chasing the gods to Egypt, and the gods transforming themselves into animals.<sup id="cite_ref-146" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-146">[146]</a></sup> Such a story arose perhaps as a way for the Greeks to explain Egypt\'s animal-shaped gods.<sup id="cite_ref-147" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-147">[147]</a></sup> Herodotus also identified Typhon with Set, making him the second to last divine king of Egypt. Herodotus says that Typhon was deposed by <a href="/wiki/Osiris" title="Osiris">Osiris</a>\' son <a href="/wiki/Horus" title="Horus">Horus</a>, whom Herodutus equates with <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> (with Osiris being equated with <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-148" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> and after his defeat by Horus, Typhon was "supposed to have been hidden" in the "<a href="/wiki/Serbonian_Bog" title="Serbonian Bog">Serbonian marsh</a>" (identified with modern <a href="/wiki/Lake_Bardawil" title="Lake Bardawil">Lake Bardawil</a>) in Egypt.<sup id="cite_ref-149" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-149">[149]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Confused_with_the_Giants">Confused with the Giants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Typhon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Confused with the Giants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Typhon bears a close resemblance to an older generation of descendants of Gaia, the <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">Giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-150" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> They, like their younger brother Typhon after them, challenged Zeus for supremacy of the cosmos,<sup id="cite_ref-151" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> were (in later representations) shown as snake-footed,<sup id="cite_ref-152" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> and end up buried under volcanos.<sup id="cite_ref-153" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup></p>\n<p>While distinct in early accounts, in later accounts Typhon was often confused or conflated with the <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">Giants</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-154" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup> The Roman mythographer <a href="/wiki/Hyginus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyginus">Hyginus</a> (64 BC \xe2\x80\x93 17 AD) includes Typhon in his list of Giants,<sup id="cite_ref-155" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> while the Roman poet <a href="/wiki/Horace" title="Horace">Horace</a> (65 \xe2\x80\x93 8 BC), mentions Typhon, along with the Giants <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(Giant)" title="Mimas (Giant)">Mimas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porphyrion" title="Porphyrion">Porphyrion</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Enceladus_(mythology)" title="Enceladus (mythology)">Enceladus</a>, as together battling <a href="/wiki/Athena" title="Athena">Athena</a>, during the Gigantomachy.<sup id="cite_ref-156" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Astronomica_(Manilius)" title="Astronomica (Manilius)">Astronomica</a></i>, attributed to the 1st-century AD Roman poet and astrologer <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Manilius" title="Marcus Manilius">Marcus Manilius</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-157" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> and the late 4th-century early 5th-century Greek poet <a href="/wiki/Nonnus" title="Nonnus">Nonnus</a>, also consider Typhon to be one of the Giants.<sup id="cite_ref-158" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-158">[158]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
