href="/wiki/File:ECL25_06_13.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nAn East Coast Low on 25 June 2013</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><b>Australian east coast lows</b> (known locally as <b>east coast lows</b> and sometimes as east coast cyclones<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup>) are <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The most intense of these systems have many of the characteristics of <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dowdy11_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dowdy11-3">[3]</a></sup> They develop between 25\xcb\x9a south and 40\xcb\x9a south and within 5\xcb\x9a of the Australian coastline,<sup id="cite_ref-Leslie_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leslie-1">[1]</a></sup> typically during the winter months.<sup id="cite_ref-Holland_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lim_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lim-5">[5]</a></sup> Each year there are about ten "significant impact" maritime lows.<sup id="cite_ref-BOMECL_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOMECL-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Prior to the introduction of satellite imagery in the early 1960s, many east coast lows were classified as tropical cyclones. These storms which mostly affect the south east coast should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Australian_region_tropical_cyclone" title="Australian region tropical cyclone">Australian region tropical cyclones</a> which typically affect the northern half of the continent.<sup id="cite_ref-Harper_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_east_coast_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characterisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Explosive_cyclogenesis" title="Explosive cyclogenesis">Explosive cyclogenesis</a> is seen on average just once per year, but these storms cause significant wind and flood damage when they occur.<sup id="cite_ref-Holland_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Holland-4">[4]</a></sup> Australian east coast cyclones vary in size from <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> (approximately 10\xe2\x80\x93100&#160;km (6\xe2\x80\x9362&#160;mi)) to <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_scale_meteorology" title="Synoptic scale meteorology">synoptic scale</a> (approximately 100\xe2\x80\x931,000&#160;km (62\xe2\x80\x93621&#160;mi)).<sup id="cite_ref-GuyCarp_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GuyCarp-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Australian east coast cyclones, although variable in size and intensity, are typically characterised by widespread heavy rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> These storms usually occur along the subtropical east coast of Australia from <a href="/wiki/Gladstone,_Queensland" title="Gladstone, Queensland">Gladstone</a> in Queensland to the Victoria/New South Wales border, and often affect large populated cities such as <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brisbane" title="Brisbane">Brisbane</a>, between which over one-third of the Australian population resides.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Rain associated damages attributed to east coast cyclones are estimated in millions to tens of million dollars annually and are a major contributor to the total weather-associated insurance losses for all of Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Seven per cent of all major Australian disasters since 1967 can be directly attributed to east coast cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hopkins-9">[9]</a></sup> Australian east coast lows often intensify rapidly overnight making them one of the more dangerous weather systems to affect the New South Wales coast.<sup id="cite_ref-BOMECL_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOMECL-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pattern">Pattern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_east_coast_low&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pattern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The incidence of these types of storms can be seen to fluctuate quite widely from one year to the next, with none in some years and the highest incidence being twelve in 1978/79.<sup id="cite_ref-Harper_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup> Another feature of east coast low development is the tendency for clustering of events when conditions remain favourable. For example, near Brisbane, almost one third of events occur within 20 days of a preceding event.<sup id="cite_ref-Harper_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harper-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Correlations of east coast cyclones with the interannual differences of the <a href="/wiki/El_Ni%C3%B1o_Southern_Oscillation" class="mw-redirect" title="El Ni\xc3\xb1o Southern Oscillation">Southern Oscillation Index</a> (SOI) indicate a strong preference for these storms to form just after a large swing from negative to positive Southern Oscillation index values and especially between swings from negative SOI the year before and positive SOI the year after. This suggests a preference for formation of east coast cyclones between extreme events of the Southern Oscillation Index.<sup id="cite_ref-Hopkins_9-5" class="reference"><a
