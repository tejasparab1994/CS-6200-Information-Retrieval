most notable hurricane.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>Hawaiian hurricane</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> that forms in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and affects the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaiian Islands</a>. Hawaii lies in the central Pacific, where about four or five tropical cyclones appear each year, although as many as fifteen have occurred, such as in the <a href="/wiki/2015_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2015 Pacific hurricane season">2015</a> season; rarely do these storms actually affect Hawaii. Tropical cyclone records were not kept before the 1950s. Earlier windstorms that struck Hawaii were not labeled as hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i</a>.</p>\n<h3><span id="Pre\xe2\x80\x931950"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre.E2.80.931950">Pre\xe2\x80\x931950</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pre\xe2\x80\x931950">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul>\n<li>September 1843: The earliest report of a tropical cyclone that could have affected Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i was made in 1843. On September 23, a German ship recorded a cyclone (known later as the "Cyclone of the Lark") near 17\xc2\xb0N and 141\xc2\xb0W. No further records are available, but extrapolation of its forward movement predicted that it would make landfall on the southern coast of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Big Island</a> of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.<sup id="cite_ref-1800s_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1800s-2">[2]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1871: Excerpts from a <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> newsletter known as the <i>Hawaiian Gazette</i> described an August weather event that at least resembled a tropical cyclone. Quotes from captains and civilians describe a severe windstorm that ravaged the islands for several hours. Damage is unrecorded.<sup id="cite_ref-1800s_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1800s-2">[2]</a></sup></li>\n<li>November 1874: In November a possible tropical cyclone may have dropped over 20 inches (510&#160;mm) of rain on <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a> and southerly gales destroyed 23 homes and damaged at least 50.<sup id="cite_ref-1800s_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1800s-2">[2]</a></sup></li>\n<li>December 1902\xe2\x80\x9303: A low pressure system (known later as "The Froc Cyclone") that took a path similar to that of a tropical cyclone, passed through <a href="/wiki/Kaulakahi_Channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaulakahi Channel">Kaulakahi Channel</a> in late December 1902. No records of unusual weather were recorded, so the storm was likely still forming as it crossed Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>October 1906: In October a tropical cyclone passed about 60 miles (97&#160;km) south of <a href="/wiki/South_Point,_Hawaii" class="mw-redirect" title="South Point, Hawaii">South Point</a>. Heavy rains were recorded; "the heaviest in years". A little over 12 inches (300&#160;mm) fell in 4\xc2\xbd&#160;hours.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>November 1906: 90 miles (140&#160;km) south of Honolulu on November 3, 1906, a tropical cyclone was recognized. The storm supposedly tracked northward, passing through the <a href="/wiki/Kauai_channel" class="mw-redirect" title="Kauai channel">Kauai channel</a>. This cyclone must have been abnormally small or very weak, because climatological records show no unusual rainfall, wind, <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">surge</a>, or low pressure. The storm dissipated near <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1925: In August High seas and gusty winds were recorded in Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i from a nearby tropical cyclone.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1938: Again in August a possible tropical cyclone produced heavy wind and rain in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s">1950s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1950s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul>\n<li>August 1950: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hiki" title="Hurricane Hiki">Hurricane Hiki</a> passed north of the islands, bringing gale winds. 68&#160;mph (110&#160;km/h) winds were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a>. Additionally, over 52&#160;inches (1321&#160;mm) of rain fell over 4&#160;days in Kauai. One person died from Hiki.<sup id="cite_ref-C1950_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1950-3">[3]</a></sup></li>\n<li>July 1957: <a href="/wiki/1957_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Kanoa" title="1957 Pacific hurricane season">Hurricane Kanoa</a>, after taking a long journey across the eastern Pacific, became a non-tropical circulation a few miles east of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i. The remnants of Kanoa brought welcomed rain.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1957_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1957-4">[4]</a></sup></li>\n<li>November 1957: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Nina_(1957)" title="Hurricane Nina (1957)">Hurricane Nina</a> was a <a href="/wiki/Saffir-Simpson_hurricane_wind_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir-Simpson hurricane wind scale">Category 1 hurricane</a> that formed in November south of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i. Nina moved north and took a sharp turn to the west without actually striking the state. Nina\'s closest approach to land, which was relatively far off the coast, was only about 120 miles (190&#160;km) southwest of Kauai. Nina caused about $100,000 damage in Kauai and dropped over 20 inches (510&#160;mm) of rain in 14&#160;hours. Nina set a record for the highest wind gust ever recorded in <a href="/wiki/Honolulu" title="Honolulu">Honolulu</a>: 82 miles per hour (132&#160;km/h). Nina killed four people throughout the islands.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1957_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1957-4">[4]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1958: On August 7, a tropical storm seemed to rapidly appear directly off the coast of <a href="/wiki/Hilo,_Hawaii" title="Hilo, Hawaii">Hilo</a>. It moved across the islands, dropping considerable rainfall and causing about $500,000 of damage, before dissipating.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1958_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1958-5">[5]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1959: <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dot_(1959)" title="Hurricane Dot (1959)">Hurricane Dot</a> was another powerful August arrival. Dot entered the Central Pacific as a Category&#160;4 hurricane just south of Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i. On August 5, Dot seemed to turn more northwest, aiming it directly at Kauai. On the 6th, Dot began weakening, and at its landfall in Kauai, Dot was only a Category 1 hurricane. Despite being greatly weakened, the hurricane brought gusts of over 100 miles per hour (160&#160;km/h). In some areas of the island, <a href="/wiki/Palm_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm tree">palm trees</a> snapped in half, suggesting winds were locally over 125 miles per hour (201&#160;km/h). Damages from Dot cost no more than a few million dollars, and rainfall was relatively light; around 4 inches (100&#160;mm). No Dot-related deaths were recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1959_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1959-6">[6]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<ul>\n<li>September 1963: <a href="/wiki/1963_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Irah" title="1963 Pacific hurricane season">Tropical Storm Irah</a> crossed the islands as a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_depression" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical depression">tropical depression</a>, bringing 36 miles per hour (58&#160;km/h) winds, but caused little damage.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1963_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1963-7">[7]</a></sup></li>\n<li>August 1966: <a href="/wiki/1966_Pacific_hurricane_season#Hurricane_Connie" title="1966 Pacific hurricane season">Hurricane Connie</a> brought heavy rain to <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Big Island</a> and <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">Maui</a> without making landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1966_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1966-8">[8]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:189px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_Storm_Maggie.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a0/Tropical_Storm_Maggie.JPG" width="187" height="154" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="187" data-file-height="154" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropical_Storm_Maggie.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nTropical Storm Maggie</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li>August 1970: <a href="/wiki/1970_Pacific_hurricane_season#Tropical_Storm_Maggie" title="1970 Pacific hurricane season">Tropical Storm Maggie</a> passed just south of the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">Big Island</a>, dropping nearly 10 inches (250&#160;mm) of rain.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1970_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC_1970-9">[9]</a></sup></li>\n<li>January 1971: Although not having existed in the Central Pacific as a tropical cyclone, the <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical low</a> that was <a href="/wiki/1971_Pacific_typhoon_season#Tropical_Storm_Sarah_.28Auring.29" title="1971 Pacific typhoon season">Tropical Storm Sarah</a> in the West Pacific passed over Hawai<span class="unicode">\xca\xbb</span>i in mid January, causing high winds and heavy rain.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_1971_10-0" class="reference"><a in a northerly direction</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The islands of Hawaii, with <a href="/wiki/Kauai" title="Kauai">Kauai</a> as the notable exception, appear to be remarkably immune from direct hurricane hits. The USGS states that "more commonly, near-misses that generate large swell and moderately high winds causing varying degrees of damage are the hallmark of hurricanes passing close to the islands."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> This has also drawn media attention.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> One notion is that Hawaii\xe2\x80\x99s volcanic peaks slow down or divert storms.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> A partial source of this idea may be the long list of hurricanes in the above paragraphs that dissipated into tropical storms or depressions upon approaching the islands. Satellite images of Hurricane Flossie\'s breakup when approaching Hawaii Island fueled this idea.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> Another example may be Hurricane Felicia which dropped from Category 4 down to a tropical depression with residual winds predicted at only 35 miles per hour (56&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif/150px-Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif" width="150" height="85" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif/225px-Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif/300px-Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="507" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mokuaweoweo_from_the_air.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSnow on Mauna Loa and Mauna Kea after storm passes Hawaii Island.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Flossie_(2013)" title="Tropical Storm Flossie (2013)">Tropical Storm Flossie</a> (not to be confused with Hurricane Flossie in 2007) provides still another example. On July 28, 2013, the storm appeared headed for a direct hit to the <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(Island)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaii (Island)">Big Island</a>, home to <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa" title="Mauna Loa">Mauna Loa</a>. Both mountains rise to elevations in excess of 13,000 feet above sea level, and as Flossie approached the island, its track shifted abruptly overnight and assumed a more northerly alignment, heading instead to the island of <a href="/wiki/Maui" title="Maui">Maui</a> on July 29.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Wind data in particular supports the <a href="/wiki/USGS" class="mw-redirect" title="USGS">USGS</a> assertion that hurricane damage has been low on all islands except for Kauai. Data collected by the Western Regional Climate Center show no hurricane-strength winds on any Hawaii Islands with the exception of Kauai.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup> Despite this data, FEMA classified all of Hawaii as being in a "Wind-Borne Debris Region".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Before Hurricane Iniki in 1992, a standard homeowner\'s <a href="/wiki/Insurance_policy" title="Insurance policy">insurance policy</a> with extended coverage provided hurricane coverage. Since Iniki, many insurance policies exclude hurricane and a separate hurricane policy is required to obtain hurricane coverage.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_hurricanes" title="List of Pacific hurricanes">List of Pacific hurricanes</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_Pacific_hurricane_seasons" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Pacific hurricane seasons">List of Pacific hurricane seasons</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_wettest_tropical_cyclones_in_the_United_States#Hawaii" title="List of wettest tropical cyclones in the United States">List of wettest tropical cyclones to affect Hawaii</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Tropical_Cyclone" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical Cyclone">Tropical Cyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Kona_low" class="mw-redirect" title="Kona low">Kona low</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Hawaii_hurricanes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
