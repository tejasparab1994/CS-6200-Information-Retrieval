Examples of such names are the <a href="/wiki/1928_Okeechobee_hurricane" title="1928 Okeechobee hurricane">1928 Okeechobee hurricane</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1938_New_England_hurricane" title="1938 New England hurricane">1938 New England hurricane</a>. The system currently in place provides identification of tropical cyclones in a brief form that is easily understood and recognized by the public. The credit for the first usage of personal names for weather systems is given to the <a href="/wiki/Queensland_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Queensland Government">Queensland Government</a> Meteorologist <a href="/wiki/Clement_Wragge" class="mw-redirect" title="Clement Wragge">Clement Wragge</a>, who named tropical cyclones and anticyclones between 1887 and 1907. This system of naming fell into disuse for several years after Wragge retired, until it was revived in the latter part of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> for the Western Pacific. Over the following decades formal naming schemes were introduced for several tropical cyclone basins, including the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">North and South Atlantic, Eastern, Central, Western and Southern Pacific basins</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins#Australian_region" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Australian region</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>.</p>\n<p>However, there has been controversy over the names used at various times, with names being dropped for religious and political reasons. Female names were exclusively used in the basins at various times between 1945 and 2000, and were the subject of several protests. At present tropical cyclones are officially named by one of eleven meteorological services and retain their names throughout their lifetimes. Due to the potential for longevity and multiple concurrent storms, the names reduce the confusion about what storm is being described in forecasts, watches and warnings. Names are assigned in order from predetermined <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">lists</a> once storms have one, three, or ten-minute sustained wind speeds of more than 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph), depending on which basin it originates in. Standards vary from basin to basin, with some tropical depressions named in the Western Pacific, while a significant amount of gale-force winds are required in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a>. The names of significant tropical cyclones in the North Atlantic Ocean, Pacific Ocean and Australian region are retired from the naming lists and replaced with another name, at meetings of the World Meteorological Organization\'s various tropical cyclone committees.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Wragge\'s naming was also mentioned within Sir <a href="/wiki/Napier_Shaw" title="Napier Shaw">Napier Shaw</a>\xe2\x80\x99s \xe2\x80\x9cManual of Meteorology\xe2\x80\x9d which likened it to a "child naming waves".<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>After reading about Clement Wragge, <a href="/wiki/George_R._Stewart" title="George R. Stewart">George Stewart</a> was inspired to write a novel, "<a href="/wiki/Storm_(novel)" title="Storm (novel)">Storm</a>", about a storm affecting <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a> which was named Maria.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWL_July_58_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_July_58-7">[7]</a></sup> The book was widely read after it was published in 1941 by <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a>, especially by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Corps" title="United States Army Air Corps">United States Army Air Corps</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> <small>(USN)</small> meteorologists during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup> During 1944, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> forecasters <small>(USAAF)</small> at the newly established <a href="/wiki/Saipan" title="Saipan">Saipan</a> weather center, started to informally name typhoons after their wives and girlfriends.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70th_Anniversary_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70th_Anniversary-8">[8]</a></sup> This practise became popular amongst meteorologists from the United States Airforce and Navy who found that it reduced confusion during map discussions, and in 1945 the United States Armed Services publicly adopted a list of women\'s names for typhoons of the Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWL_July_58_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_July_58-7">[7]</a></sup> However, they were not able to persuade the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> <small>(USWB)</small> to start naming Atlantic hurricanes, as the Weather Bureau wanted to be seen as a serious enterprise, and thus felt that it was "not appropriate" to name tropical cyclones while warning the United States public.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TWC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-9">[9]</a></sup> They also felt that using women\'s names was frivolous and that using the names in official communications would have made them look silly.<sup id="cite_ref-TWC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TWC-9">[9]</a></sup> During 1947 the Air Force Hurricane Office in Miami started using the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Army/Navy_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet">Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet</a> to name significant tropical cyclones in the North Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup> These names were used over the next few years in private/internal communications between weather centres and aircraft, and were not included in public bulletins.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:197px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Cobra,_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/195px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" width="195" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/293px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg/390px-Typhoon_Cobra%2C_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="707" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typhoon_Cobra,_18_December_1944_east_of_Luzon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Cobra_(1944)" class="mw-redirect" title="Typhoon Cobra (1944)">Typhoon Cobra</a>, like other typhoons during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">WWII</a>, was informally assigned a code name</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>During August and September 1950, three tropical cyclones (Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Baker_(1950)" title="Hurricane Baker (1950)">Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Dog_(1950)" title="Hurricane Dog (1950)">Dog</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Easy_(1950)" title="Hurricane Easy (1950)">Easy</a>) occurred simultaneously and impacted the United States during August and September 1950, which led to confusion within the media and the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup> As a result, during the next tropical cyclone (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Fox_(1950)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Fox (1950)">Fox</a>), <a href="/wiki/Grady_Norton" title="Grady Norton">Grady Norton</a> decided to start using the names in public statements and in the seasonal summary.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mwr_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-11">[11]</a></sup> This practice continued throughout the season, until the system was made official before the start of the <a href="/wiki/1951_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1951 Atlantic hurricane season">next season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWL_July_58_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_July_58-7">[7]</a></sup> During 1952, a new <a href="/wiki/ICAO_spelling_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="ICAO spelling alphabet">International Phonetic Alphabet</a> was introduced, as the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Army/Navy_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet">old phonetic alphabet</a> was seen as too <a href="/wiki/Eurocentrism" title="Eurocentrism">Anglocentric</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> This led to some confusion with what names were being used, as some observers referred to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Charlie_(1952)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Charlie (1952)">Hurricane Charlie</a> as "Cocoa."<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> Ahead of the <a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">following season</a> no agreement could be reached over which phonetic alphabet to use, before it was decided to start using a list of female names to name tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> During the season the names were used in the press with only a few objections recorded, and as a result public reception to the idea seemed favourable. The same names were reused during 1954 with only one change: Gilda for Gail.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> However, as Hurricanes <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Carol" title="Hurricane Carol">Carol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Edna" title="Hurricane Edna">Edna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hazel" title="Hurricane Hazel">Hazel</a> affected the populated <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_United_States" title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States</a>, controversy raged with several protests over the use of women\xe2\x80\x99s names as it was felt to be ungentlemanly or insulting to womanhood, or both.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> Letters were subsequently received that overwhelmingly supported the practise, with forecasters claiming that 99% of correspondence received in the Miami Weather Bureau supported the use of women\xe2\x80\x99s names for hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F_WW-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Forecasters subsequently decided to continue with the current practice of naming hurricanes after women, but developed a new set of names ahead of the <a href="/wiki/1955_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1955 Atlantic hurricane season">1955 season</a> with the names Carol, Edna and Hazel retired for the next ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> However, before the names could be written, a tropical storm was discovered on January 2, 1955 and named <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Alice_(December_1954)" title="Hurricane Alice (December 1954)">Alice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> The Representative <a href="/wiki/T._James_Tumulty" title="T. James Tumulty">T. James Tumulty</a> subsequently announced that he intended to introduce legislation that would call on the USWB to abandon its practice of naming hurricanes after women, and suggested that they be named using descriptive terms instead.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Until 1960, forecasters decided to develop a new set of names each year.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_July_07_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_July_07-13">[13]</a></sup> By 1958, the Guam Weather Center had become the Fleet Weather Central/Typhoon Tracking Center on Guam, and had started to name systems as they became tropical storms rather than typhoons.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form-17">[17]</a></sup> Later that year during the 1958\xe2\x80\x9359 cyclone season, the New Caledonia Meteorological Office started to name tropical cyclones within the Southern Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NZ_TC_39-69_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZ_TC_39-69-18">[18]</a></sup> During 1959 the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Pacific_Command#List_of_Combatant_Commanders" title="United States Pacific Command">US Pacific Command Commander in Chief</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> decided that the various US Navy and Air Force weather units would become one unit based on <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> entitled the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Fleet Weather Central/Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>, which continued naming the systems for the Pacific basin.<sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_Form_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC_Form-17">[17]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JTWC_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JTWC-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span id="1960\xe2\x80\x931990s"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1960.E2.80.931990s">1960\xe2\x80\x931990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_tropical_cyclone_naming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1960\xe2\x80\x931990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In January 1960, a formal naming scheme was introduced for the South-West Indian Ocean by the Mauritius and Madagascan Weather Services. with the first cyclone being named Alix.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> Later that year, as <a href="/wiki/Meteorology" title="Meteorology">meteorology</a> entered a new era with the launching of the world\'s first <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">meteorological satellite</a> <a href="/wiki/TIROS-1" title="TIROS-1">TIROS-1</a>, eight lists of tropical cyclone names were prepared for use in the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">Atlantic and Eastern Pacific basins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MWL_Jan_60_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_Jan_60-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWL_July_60_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_July_60-24">[24]</a></sup> In the Atlantic it was decided to rotate these lists every four years, while in the Eastern Pacific the names were designed to be used consecutively before being repeated.<sup id="cite_ref-MWL_Jan_60_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_Jan_60-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MWL_July_60_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MWL_July_60-24">[24]</a></sup> During 1963, the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">Philippine Weather Bureau</a> adopted four sets of female Filipino nicknames ending in "ng" from A to Y for use in its self-defined <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Atmospheric,_Geophysical_and_Astronomical_Services_Administration#Tropical_Cyclones" class="mw-redirect" title="Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration">area of responsibility</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pedring_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pedring-27">[27]</a></sup> Following the international practise of naming tropical cyclones, the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Bureau_of_Meteorology" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Bureau of Meteorology">Australian Bureau of Meteorology</a> decided at a conference in October 1963 that they would start naming tropical cyclones after women at the start of the <a href="/wiki/1963%E2%80%9364_Australian_region_cyclone_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1963\xe2\x80\x9364 Australian region cyclone season">1963\xe2\x80\x9364 cyclone season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> The first <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australian</a> cyclone was subsequently named Bessie on January 6, 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> In 1965, after two of the Eastern Pacific lists of names had been used, it was decided to start recycling the sets of names on an annual basis like in the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-EPAC_Book_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPAC_Book-30">[30]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_Nov_07_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_Nov_07-31">[31]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At its 1969 national conference, the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a> passed a motion that called for the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> <small>(NHC)</small> not to name tropical cyclones using only female names.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> Later that year, during the <a href="/wiki/1969-70_South_Pacific_cyclone_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1969-70 South Pacific cyclone season">1969\xe2\x80\x9370 cyclone season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Meteorological_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="New Zealand Meteorological Service">New Zealand Meteorological Service</a> <small>(NZMS)</small> office in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a> started to name tropical cyclones that developed within the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">South Pacific basin</a>, with the first named Alice on January 4, 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> Within the Atlantic basin the four lists of names were used until 1971, when the newly established United States <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> decided to inaugurate a ten-year list of names for the basin.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roxcy_Bolton" title="Roxcy Bolton">Roxcy Bolton</a> subsequently petitioned the 1971, 1972 and 1973 interdepartmental hurricane conferences to stop the female naming; however, the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a> responded by stating that there was a 20:1 positive response to the usage of female names.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup> In February 1975, the NZMS decided to incorporate male names into the naming lists for the South Pacific, from the following season after a request from the Fiji National Council of Women who considered the practice discriminatory.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> At around the same time the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_Innovation,_Industry,_Science_and_Research_(Australia)#List_of_science_ministers" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister for Innovation, Industry, Science and Research (Australia)">Australian Science Minister</a> ordered that tropical cyclones within the Australian region should carry both men\'s and women\'s names, as the minister thought "that both sexes should bear the odium of the devastation caused by cyclones."<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> Male names were subsequently added to the lists for the Southern Pacific and each of the three Australian tropical cyclone warning centres ahead of the 1975\xe2\x80\x9376 season.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During 1977 the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> decided to form a hurricane committee, which held its first meeting during May 1978 and took control of the <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">Atlantic hurricane naming lists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup> During 1978 the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Commerce">Secretary of Commerce</a> <a href="/wiki/Juanita_Kreps" class="mw-redirect" title="Juanita Kreps">Juanita Kreps</a> ordered <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA administrator</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_M._White_(meteorologist)" title="Robert M. White (meteorologist)">Robert White</a> to cease the sole usage of female names for hurricanes.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup> Robert White subsequently passed the order on to the Director of NHC <a href="/wiki/Neil_Frank" title="Neil Frank">Neil Frank</a>, who attended the first meeting of the hurricane committee and requested that both men\xe2\x80\x99s and women\xe2\x80\x99s names be used for the Atlantic.<sup id="cite_ref-Mahina_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mahina-1">[1]</a></sup> The committee subsequently decided to accept the proposal and adopted five new lists of male and female names to be used the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-1978_NHOP_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1978_NHOP-35">[35]</a></sup> The lists also contained several Spanish and French names, so that they could reflect the cultures and languages used within the Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-ATL_Book_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL_Book-36">[36]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GP_August_07_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_August_07-37">[37]</a></sup> After an agreement was reached between Mexico and the United States, six new sets of male/female names were implemented for the Eastern Pacific basin during 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-Names1978_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Names1978-38">[38]</a></sup> A new list was also drawn up during the year for the Western Pacific and was implemented after Typhoon Bess and the 1979 tropical cyclone conference.<sup id="cite_ref-1978_NHOP_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1978_NHOP-35">[35]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATCR_1979_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATCR_1979-39">[39]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As the dual sex naming of tropical cyclones started in the Northern Hemisphere, the NZMS considered adding ethnic Pacific names to the naming lists rather than the European names that were currently used.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result of the many languages and cultures in the Pacific there was a lot of discussion surrounding this matter, with one name, "Oni," being dropped as it meant "the end of the world" in one language.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> One proposal suggested that cyclones be named from the country nearest to which they formed; however, this was dropped when it was realized that a cyclone might be less destructive in its formative stage than later in its development.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> Eventually it was decided to combine names from all over the South Pacific into a single list at a training course, where each course member provided a list of names that were short, easily pronounced, culturally acceptable throughout the Pacific and did not contain any idiosyncrasies.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> These names were then collated, edited for suitability, and cross-checked with the group for acceptability.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> It was intended that the four lists of names should be alphabetical with alternating male and female names while using only ethnic names. However, it was not possible to complete the lists using only ethnic names.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> As a result, there was a scattering of European names in the final lists, which have been used by the <a href="/wiki/Fiji_Meteorological_Service" title="Fiji Meteorological Service">Fiji Meteorological Service</a> and NZMS since the <a href="/wiki/1980-81_South_Pacific_cyclone_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1980-81 South Pacific cyclone season">1980\xe2\x80\x9381 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Whats_in_a_name.3F_4-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Whats_in_a_name.3F-4">[4]</a></sup> During October 1985 the Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center had to request an additional list, after the names preselected for that season was used up.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup> As a result, the names Xina, York, Zelda, Xavier, Yolanda, Zeke were subsequently added to the naming lists, while a contingency plan of using the Greek alphabet if all of the names were used up was introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_Feb_02_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_Feb_02-41">[41]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHOP_1987_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHOP_1987-42">[42]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_millennium">New millennium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_tropical_cyclone_naming&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: New millennium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>During the 30th session of the ESCAP/WMO Typhoon Committee in November 1997, a proposal was put forward by Hong Kong to give Asian typhoons local names and to stop using the European and American names that had been used since 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_Newsletter_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_Newsletter-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup> The committee\'s Training and Research Coordination Group was subsequently tasked to consult with members and work out the details of the scheme in order to present a list of names for approval at the 31st session.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_Newsletter_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_Newsletter-43">[43]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup> During August 1998, the group met and decided that each member of the committee would be invited to contribute ten names to the list and that five principles would be followed for the selection of names.<sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup> It was also agreed that each name would have to be approved by each member and that a single objection would be enough to veto a name.<sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup> A list of 140 names was subsequently drawn up and submitted to the Typhoon Committee\'s 32nd session, who after a lengthy discussion approved the list and decided to implement it on January 1, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PAGASA_NWP_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAGASA_NWP-45">[45]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TC_Newsletter_11_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_Newsletter_11-46">[46]</a></sup> It was also decided that the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Meteorological_Agency" title="Japan Meteorological Agency">Japan Meteorological Agency</a> would name the systems rather than the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Typhoon_Warning_Center" title="Joint Typhoon Warning Center">Joint Typhoon Warning Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Retired_typhoons_44-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Retired_typhoons-44">[44]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During its annual session in 2000, the WMO/ESCAP Panel on North Indian Tropical Cyclones agreed in principle to start assigning names to <a href="/wiki/Super_cyclonic_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Super cyclonic storm">cyclonic storms</a> that developed within the North Indian Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_2014_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_2014-48">[48]</a></sup> As a result of this, the panel requested that each member country submit a list of ten names to a rapporteur by the end of 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> At the 2001 session, the rapporteur reported that of the eight countries involved, only India had refused to submit a list of names, as it had some reservations about assigning names to tropical cyclones.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> The panel then studied the names and felt that some of the names would not be appealing to the public or the media and thus requested that members submit new lists of names.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> During 2002 the rapporteur reported that there had been a poor response by member countries in resubmitting their lists of names. Over the next year, each country except India submitted a fresh list.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> By the 2004 session, India had still not submitted its list despite promising to do so. However, the rapporteur presented the lists of names that would be used with a gap left for India\'s names.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> The rapporteur also recommended that the naming lists be used on an experimental basis during the season, starting in May or June 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-31st_49-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31st-49">[49]</a></sup> The naming lists were then completed in May 2004, after India submitted their names.<sup id="cite_ref-32nd_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32nd-50">[50]</a></sup> However, the lists were not used until September 2004, when the first tropical cyclone was named <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Onil" title="Cyclone Onil">Onil</a> by the <a href="/wiki/India_Meteorological_Department" title="India Meteorological Department">India Meteorological Department</a> <small>(IMD)</small>.<sup id="cite_ref-IMD_2014_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IMD_2014-48">[48]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32nd_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32nd-50">[50]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:197px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg/195px-Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg" width="195" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg/293px-Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg/390px-Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cyclone_Catarina_2004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\n<a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Catarina" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclone Catarina">Rare South Atlantic cyclone</a> unofficially received the name "Catarina" from media outlets</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>At the 22nd hurricane committee in 2000 it was decided that tropical cyclones that moved from the Atlantic to the Eastern Pacific basin and vice versa would no longer be renamed.<sup id="cite_ref-HC_2000_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HC_2000-51">[51]</a></sup> Ahead of the <a href="/wiki/2000-01_South-West_Indian_Ocean_cyclone_season" class="mw-redirect" title="2000-01 South-West Indian Ocean cyclone season">2000\xe2\x80\x9301 season</a> it was decided to start using male names, as well as female names for tropical cyclones developing in the South-West Indian Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-Names_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Names-52">[52]</a></sup> RSMC La Reunion subsequently proposed to the fifteenth session of the RA I Tropical Cyclone Committee for the South-West Indian Ocean during September 2001, that the basin adopt a single circular list of names.<sup id="cite_ref-15th_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15th-53">[53]</a></sup> Along with the RA V Tropical Cyclone Committee, RSMC La Reunion also proposed to the session that a tropical cyclone have only one name during its lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-15th_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15th-53">[53]</a></sup> However, both of these proposals were rejected in favour of continuing an annual list of names and to rename systems when they moved across 90\xc2\xb0E into the South-West Indian Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-15th_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15th-53">[53]</a></sup> During the 2002 Atlantic hurricane season the naming of <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">subtropical cyclones</a> restarted, with names assigned to systems from the main list of names drawn up for that year.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During March 2004, a rare tropical cyclone developed within the Southern Atlantic, about 1,010&#160;km (630&#160;mi) to the east-southeast of Florian\xc3\xb3polis in southern Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-55">[55]</a></sup> As the system was threatening the Brazilian state of <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">Santa Catarina</a>, a newspaper used the headline "Furac\xc3\xa3o Catarina," which was presumed to mean "furac\xc3\xa3o (hurricane) threatening (Santa) Catarina (the state)".<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-55">[55]</a></sup> However, when the international press started monitoring the system, it was assumed that "Furac\xc3\xa3o Catarina" meant "Cyclone Catarina" and that it had been formally named in the usual way.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2004_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2004-55">[55]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/2005_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="2005 Atlantic hurricane season">2005 Atlantic hurricane season</a> the names pre-assigned for the North Atlantic basin were exhausted and as a result names from the Greek alphabet were used.<sup id="cite_ref-2005_AHS_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2005_AHS-56">[56]</a></sup> There were subsequently a couple of attempts to get rid of the Greek names, as they are seen to be inconsistent with the standard naming convention used for tropical cyclones, and are generally unknown and confusing to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Greek_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greek-57">[57]</a></sup> However, none of the attempts have succeeded and thus the Greek alphabet will be used should the lists ever be used up again.<sup id="cite_ref-Greek_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greek-57">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WMO_05_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_05-58">[58]</a></sup> Ahead of the <a href="/wiki/2007_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2007 Pacific hurricane season">2007 hurricane season</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> <small>(CPHC)</small> and the Hawaii State Civil Defense requested that the hurricane committee retire eleven names from the Eastern Pacific naming lists.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-59">[59]</a></sup> However, the committee declined the request and noted that its criteria for the retirement of names was "well defined and very strict."<sup id="cite_ref-WMO_2007_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_2007-60">[60]</a></sup> It was felt that while the systems may have had a significant impact on the Hawaiian Islands, none of the impacts were major enough to warrant the retirement of the names.<sup id="cite_ref-WMO_2007_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_2007-60">[60]</a></sup> It was also noted that the committee had previously not retired names for systems that had a greater impact than those that had been submitted.<sup id="cite_ref-WMO_2007_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_2007-60">[60]</a></sup> The CPHC also introduced a revised set of Hawaiian names for the Central Pacific, after they had worked with the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">University of Hawaii</a> <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawaiian Studies">Hawaiian Studies</a> Department to ensure the correct meaning and appropriate historical and cultural use of the names.<sup id="cite_ref-CPHC_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPHC-59">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[61]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On April 22, 2008 the newly established tropical cyclone warning centre in <a href="/wiki/Jakarta,_Indonesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Jakarta, Indonesia">Jakarta, Indonesia</a> named its first system: <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Durga" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyclone Durga">Durga</a>, before two sets of Indonesian names were established for their area of responsibility ahead of the <a href="/wiki/2008-09_Australian_region_cyclone_season" class="mw-redirect" title="2008-09 Australian region cyclone season">2008\xe2\x80\x9309 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Durga_TCR_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Durga_TCR-62">[62]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WMO_RA_V_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WMO_RA_V-63">[63]</a></sup> At the same time the Australian Bureau of Meteorology, merged their three lists into one national list of names.<sup id="cite_ref-Aus_names_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aus_names-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-65">[65]</a></sup> The issue of tropical cyclones being renamed when they moved across 90\xc2\xb0E into the South-West Indian Ocean, was subsequently brought up during October 2008 at the 18th session of the RA I Tropical Cyclone Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-18th_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18th-66">[66]</a></sup> However, it was decided to postpone the matter until the following committee meeting so that various consultations could take place.<sup id="cite_ref-18th_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18th-66">[66]</a></sup> During the 2009 Tropical Cyclone RSMCs/TCWCs Technical Coordination Meeting, it was reaffirmed that a tropical cyclone name should be retained throughout a system\'s lifetime, including when moving from one basin to another, to avoid confusion.<sup id="cite_ref-TCM_6_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCM_6-67">[67]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I-68">[68]</a></sup> As a result, it was proposed at the following year\'s RA I tropical cyclone committee, that systems stopped being renamed when they moved into the South-West Indian Ocean from the Australian region.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I-68">[68]</a></sup> It was subsequently agreed that during an interim period, cyclones that moved into the basin would have a name attached to their existing name, before it was stopped at the start of the 2012\xe2\x80\x9313 season.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I-68">[68]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reunion_FAQ_B4_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reunion_FAQ_B4-69">[69]</a></sup> Tropical Cyclone Bruce was subsequently the first tropical cyclone not to be renamed, when it moved into the South-West Indian Ocean during 2013-14.<sup id="cite_ref-Reunion_FAQ_B4_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reunion_FAQ_B4-69">[69]</a></sup> During March 12, 2010, public and private weather services in Southern Brazil, decided to name a tropical storm Anita in order to avoid confusion in future references.<sup id="cite_ref-GP_March_2010_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GP_March_2010-70">[70]</a></sup> A naming list was subsequently set up by the Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center with the names Arani, Bapo and Cari subsequently taken from that list during 2011 and 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-brazil_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-71">[71]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JM_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JM-72">[72]</a></sup></p>\n<p>At its twenty-first session in 2015, the RA I Tropical Cyclone Committee reviewed the arrangements for naming tropical storms and decided that the procedure was in need of a "very urgent change".<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_21st_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I_21st-73">[73]</a></sup> In particular it was noted that the procedure did not take into account, any of the significant improvements in the science surrounding tropical cyclones and that it was biased due to inappropriate links with some national warning systems.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_21st_73-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I_21st-73">[73]</a></sup> As a result the committee decided to keep the current naming procedure, for the next few years and form a task force, in order to develop an alternative cyclone naming procedure.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_21st_73-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I_21st-73">[73]</a></sup> The taskforce could not meet during the intersessional pierod, but planned to meet during the twenty-second session during 2017.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_day">Modern day</span><span title="Meteorological Service of New Zealand">Meteorological Service of New Zealand</a></td>\n<td>Equator&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 25\xc2\xb0S, 160\xc2\xb0E&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 120\xc2\xb0W<br href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-65">[65]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td><a href="/wiki/South_Atlantic_tropical_cyclone" title="South Atlantic tropical cyclone">South Atlantic</a></td>\n<td><a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Navy" title="Brazilian Navy">Brazilian Navy Hydrographic Center</a> (Unofficial)</td>\n<td>Equator&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 35\xc2\xb0S, Brazilian Coast&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 20\xc2\xb0W</td>\n<td><sup id="cite_ref-brazil_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-71">[71]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n</center>\n<p>At present tropical cyclones are officially named by one of eleven warning centres and retain their names throughout their lifetimes to provide ease of communication between forecasters and the general public regarding forecasts, watches, and warnings.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup> Due to the potential for longevity and multiple concurrent storms, the names are thought to reduce the confusion about what storm is being described.<sup id="cite_ref-TC_FAQ_B1_5-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TC_FAQ_B1-5">[5]</a></sup> Names are assigned in order from predetermined <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_tropical_cyclone_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Lists of tropical cyclone names">lists</a> once storms have one, three, or ten-minute sustained wind speeds of more than 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph) depending on which basin it originates in.<sup id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_74-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-77">[77]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NIO_TCOP_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NIO_TCOP-78">[78]</a></sup> However, standards vary from basin to basin, with some tropical depressions named in the Western Pacific, while tropical cyclones have to have gale-force winds occurring near the center before they are named within the Southern Hemisphere.<sup id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_65-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SWIO_TCOP_77-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SWIO_TCOP-77">[77]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Any member of the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="World Meteorological Organisation">World Meteorological Organisation</a>\'s hurricane, typhoon and tropical cyclone committees can request that the name of a tropical cyclone be retired or withdrawn from the various <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">tropical cyclone naming lists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_65-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-65">[65]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_74-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-75">[75]</a></sup> A name is retired or withdrawn if a consensus or majority of members agree that the tropical cyclone has acquired a special notoriety, such as causing a large number of deaths and amounts of damage, impacts or for other special reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_74-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-74">[74]</a></sup> Any tropical cyclone names assigned by the Papua New Guinea National weather Service are automatically retired regardless of any damage caused.<sup id="cite_ref-SPAC_TCOP_65-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SPAC_TCOP-65">[65]</a></sup> A replacement name is then submitted to the committee concerned and voted upon, but these names can be rejected and replaced for various reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_74-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-74">[74]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WPAC_TCOP_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPAC_TCOP-75">[75]</a></sup> These reasons include the spelling and pronunciation of the name, its similarity to the name of a recent tropical cyclone or on another list of names, and the length of the name for modern communication channels such as social media.<sup id="cite_ref-Aus_names_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aus_names-64">[64]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP_74-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATL.2FEPAC_TCOP-74">[74]</a></sup> PAGASA also retires the names of significant tropical cyclones, when they have caused at least <span style="display:none" class="sortkey">7009100000000000000\xe2\x99\xa0</span>\xe2\x82\xb11&#160;billion in damage and/or have caused at least 300&#160;deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-PAGASA_2014_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAGASA_2014-79">[79]</a></sup> There are no names retired within the South-West Indian Ocean, as names that are used are automatically removed from the three naming lists used in that basin.<sup id="cite_ref-RA_I_21st_73-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RA_I_21st-73">[73]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
