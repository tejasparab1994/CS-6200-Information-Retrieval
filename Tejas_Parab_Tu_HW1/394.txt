Rippey Stewart</b> (May 31, 1895 \xe2\x80\x93 August 22, 1980) was an American historian, <a href="/wiki/Toponymist" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponymist">toponymist</a>, novelist, and a professor of English at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>. His 1959 book, <i>Pickett\'s Charge</i>, a detailed history of the final attack at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Gettysburg</a>, was called "essential for an understanding of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gettysburg" title="Battle of Gettysburg">Battle of Gettysburg</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> His 1949 <a href="/wiki/Apocalyptic_and_post-apocalyptic_fiction" title="Apocalyptic and post-apocalyptic fiction">post-apocalyptic novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_Abides" title="Earth Abides">Earth Abides</a></i> won the first <a href="/wiki/International_Fantasy_Award" title="International Fantasy Award">International Fantasy Award</a> in 1951.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div id="Early_life_and_university_career">Early life and university career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_R._Stewart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and university career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Sewickley,_Pennsylvania" title="Sewickley, Pennsylvania">Sewickley, Pennsylvania</a>, George Rippey Stewart, Jr. was the son of engineer George Rippey Stewart Sr. (died 1937), who designed gasworks and electric railways and later became a citrus "rancher" in Southern California, and Ella Wilson Stewart (died 1937). The younger Stewart earned a bachelor\'s degree from <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in 1917, an MA from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a>, and his Ph.D. in English literature from <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a> in 1922. He accepted a position in the English department at Berkeley in 1923.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> After his father died, he dropped the "Jr." from his name.</p>\n<p>Stewart was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/American_Name_Society" title="American Name Society">American Name Society</a> in 1956-57, and he once served as an expert witness in a <a href="/wiki/Murder" title="Murder">murder</a> trial as a specialist in family names. His best-known academic work is <i>Names on the Land: A Historical Account of Place-Naming in the United States</i> (1945; reprinted, New York Review Books, 2008). He wrote three other books on names: <i>A Concise Dictionary of American Place-Names</i> (1970), <i>Names on the Globe</i> (1975), and <i>American Given Names</i> (1979). His scholarly works on the poetic meter of ballads (published under the name George R. Stewart, Jr.), beginning with his 1922 Ph.D. dissertation at Columbia, remain important in their field.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_R._Stewart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As an author, Stewart\'s output was at once diverse, original, and important. <i>Ordeal by Hunger</i>, <i>Pickett\'s Charge</i>, and other works are examinations of American history, but are unusual in their probing of the interaction of human beings with their physical and social environments. His greatest achievement as a novelist, <i>Earth Abides</i>, takes somewhat the same perspective, but in the context of a collapse of civilization, in which everything formerly taken for granted about civilization and the situation of human beings in their environment can no longer be assumed. This radically altered circumstance permits Stewart to raise and examine issues rarely, if ever, tackled by other novelists. <i>East of Giants</i> is historical fiction. <i>Man, An Autobiography</i> is one of the very few works of speculative anthropology, in which he attempts to deduce how key developments in prehistorical civilization must have unfolded, and also offers a wealth of original and interesting insights into the character of early civilization. <i>Good Lives</i> provides a series of biographical sketches with the end in view of determining what it is that makes for a good life, an undertaking having much in common with, say, Aristotle\'s <i>Nichomachean Ethics</i>. <i>Not So Rich as You Think</i> (1968) was a prescient early essay in environmentalism (Rachel Carson\'s <i>Silent Spring</i> had been published only a few years earlier, in 1962, and had a much narrower focus). <i>Storm</i> (1941) takes an immense storm as its protagonist, an extraordinary departure in itself, and again teases out the consequences for human beings of this large-scale environmental disruption. Other works, such as <i>Names on the Land</i> and <i>American Ways of Life</i> offer other unique insights and perspectives not found anywhere else.</p>\n<p>Taken together, this enlightening body of work provides a breadth and depth of perspective found elsewhere only in authors like Toynbee, the Durants, and Carroll Quigley, but in a far more palatable and accessible form. Achievements of this stature should have earned Stewart a lasting reputation as one of America\'s greatest writers and men of letters.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> However, the significance of his output was largely overlooked during his lifetime, and is now almost forgotten.</p>\n<p>He is today known primarily for his only science fiction novel <i><a href="/wiki/Earth_Abides" title="Earth Abides">Earth Abides</a></i> (1949), a <a href="/wiki/Post-apocalyptic" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-apocalyptic">post-apocalyptic</a> novel, for which he won the inaugural <a href="/wiki/International_Fantasy_Award" title="International Fantasy Award">International Fantasy Award</a> for fiction in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-SFE_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFE-1">[1]</a></sup> It was dramatized on radio\'s <i><a href="/wiki/Escape_(radio_program)" title="Escape (radio program)">Escape</a></i> and served as an inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>\'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Stand" title="The Stand">The Stand</a></i>, as King has stated.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <i>The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction</i> calls it "one of the finest of all Post-Holocaust/Ruined Earth novels".<sup id="cite_ref-SFE_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFE-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>His 1941 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Storm_(novel)" title="Storm (novel)">Storm</a></i>, featuring as its protagonist a Pacific storm called "Maria," prompted the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> to use personal names to designate storms<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and inspired <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jay_Lerner" title="Alan Jay Lerner">Alan Jay Lerner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Loewe" title="Frederick Loewe">Frederick Loewe</a> to write the song "<a href="/wiki/They_Call_the_Wind_Maria" title="They Call the Wind Maria">They Call the Wind Maria</a>" for their 1951 musical <i><a href="/wiki/Paint_Your_Wagon_(musical)" title="Paint Your Wagon (musical)">Paint Your Wagon</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p><i>Storm</i> was dramatized as <i>A Storm Called Maria</i> on the November 2, 1959 episode of ABC\'s <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_anthology_television_series" title="Walt Disney anthology television series"><i>Walt Disney Presents</i></a>. Co-produced by Ken Nelson Productions, it blended newsreel footage of several different storms to represent the mega-storm in the novel and traced the storm from its origins in Japan to the coast of California. The cast included non-actors, among them the dam superintendent George Kritsky, the telephone lineman Walt Bowen, and the highway superintendent Leo Quinn.</p>\n<p>Another novel, <i>Fire</i> (1948), and an historical work, <i>Ordeal by Hunger</i> (1936), also evoked environmental catastrophes.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bibliography">Bibliography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_R._Stewart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bibliography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:American_Given_Names.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/American_Given_Names.jpg/175px-American_Given_Names.jpg" width="175" height="257" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/b4/American_Given_Names.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="260" data-file-height="382" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:American_Given_Names.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nCover of the 1979 <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">Oxford University Press</a> paperback edition of <i>American Given Names</i>.</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li><i>The Technique of English Verse</i> (1930)</li>\n<li><i><a href="/wiki/Bret_Harte" title="Bret Harte">Bret Harte</a>: Argonaut and Exile</i> (1931)</li>\n<li><i>English Composition, A Laboratory Course,</i> (1936)</li>\n<li><i>Ordeal by Hunger: The Story of the Donner Party</i> (1936;
