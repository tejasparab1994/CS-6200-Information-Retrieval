world with tropical climates</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>tropics</b> are a region of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a> surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">Equator</a>. They are delimited in <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">Northern Hemisphere</a> at 23\xc2\xb026\xe2\x80\xb213.1\xe2\x80\xb3&#160;(or&#160;23.43698\xc2\xb0)&#160;N and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">Southern Hemisphere</a> at 23\xc2\xb026\xe2\x80\xb213.1\xe2\x80\xb3&#160;(or&#160;23.43698\xc2\xb0)&#160;S; these latitudes correspond to the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">axial tilt</a> of the Earth. The tropics are also referred to as the <b>tropical zone</b> and the <b>torrid zone</b> (see <a href="/wiki/Geographical_zone" title="Geographical zone">geographical zone</a>). The tropics include all the areas on the Earth where the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a> contacts the <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">zenith</a>, a point directly overhead, at least once during the <a href="/wiki/Solar_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Solar year">solar year</a> (which is a <a href="/wiki/Subsolar_point" title="Subsolar point">subsolar point</a>).</p>\n<p>The tropics are distinguished from the other climatic and biomatic regions of Earth, which are the <a href="/wiki/Middle_latitudes" title="Middle latitudes">middle latitudes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">polar regions</a> on either side of the equatorial zone.</p>\n<p>The Tropics comprise 40% of the Earth\'s surface area<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and contain 36% of the Earth\'s <a href="/wiki/Landmass" class="mw-redirect" title="Landmass">landmass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup>As of 2014<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tropics&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the region is home to 40% of the <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">world population</a>, and this figure is projected to reach 50% by the late 2030s.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> to mean warm to hot and moist year-round, often with the sense of lush vegetation.</p>\n<p>Many tropical areas have a dry and wet season. The <a href="/wiki/Wet_season" title="Wet season">wet season</a>, rainy season or green season is the time of year, ranging from one or more months, when most of the average annual <a href="/wiki/Rainfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainfall">rainfall</a> in a region falls.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Areas with wet seasons are disseminated across portions of the tropics and <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">subtropics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification</a>, for <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climates</a>, a wet-season month is defined as a month where average precipitation is 60 millimetres (2.4&#160;in) or more.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">Tropical rainforests</a> technically do not have dry or wet seasons, since their rainfall is equally distributed through the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Hyde_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hyde-7">[7]</a></sup> Some areas with pronounced rainy seasons see a break in rainfall during mid-season when the <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_convergence_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Intertropical convergence zone">intertropical convergence zone</a> or <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_trough" title="Monsoon trough">monsoon trough</a> moves poleward of their location during the middle of the warm season;<sup id="cite_ref-JS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JS-8">[8]</a></sup> typical vegetation in these areas ranges from moist <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_tropical_forest" title="Seasonal tropical forest">seasonal tropical forests</a> to <a href="/wiki/Savannah" class="mw-redirect" title="Savannah">savannahs</a>.</p>\n<p>When the wet season occurs during the warm season, or <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">summer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> falls mainly during the late afternoon and early evening hours. The wet season is a time when <a href="/wiki/Air_quality" class="mw-redirect" title="Air quality">air quality</a> improves, freshwater quality improves and vegetation grows significantly, leading to crop yields late in the season. Floods cause rivers to overflow their banks, and some animals to retreat to higher ground. <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">Soil</a> nutrients diminish and erosion increases. The incidence of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> increases in areas where the rainy season coincides with high temperatures. Animals have adaptation and survival strategies for the wetter regime. Unfortunately, the previous dry season leads to food shortages into the wet season, as the crops have yet to mature.</p>\n<p>Regions within the tropics may well not have a tropical climate. There are <a href="/wiki/Alpine_tundra" title="Alpine tundra">alpine tundra</a> and snow-capped peaks, including <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Kea" title="Mauna Kea">Mauna Kea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> as far south as the northernmost parts of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>. Under the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification">K\xc3\xb6ppen climate classification</a>, much of the area within the geographical tropics is classed not as "tropical" but as "dry" (<a href="/wiki/Desert_climate" title="Desert climate">arid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Semi-arid_climate" title="Semi-arid climate">semi-arid</a>) including the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara Desert</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Atacama_Desert" title="Atacama Desert">Atacama Desert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Outback" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Outback">Australian Outback</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystems">Ecosystems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Fonds-Saint-Denis" title="Fonds-Saint-Denis">Fonds-Saint-Denis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Tropical_plants" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical plants">Tropical plants</a> and animals are those species native to the tropics. Tropical ecosystems may consist of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_tropical_forests" class="mw-redirect" title="Seasonal tropical forests">seasonal tropical forests</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_and_subtropical_dry_broadleaf_forests" title="Tropical and subtropical dry broadleaf forests">dry (often deciduous) forests</a>, spiny forests, <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">desert</a> and other habitat types. There are often significant areas of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, and species <a href="/wiki/Endemism" title="Endemism">endemism</a> present, particularly in rainforests and seasonal forests. Some examples of important biodiversity and high endemism ecosystems are <a href="/wiki/El_Yunque_National_Forest" title="El Yunque National Forest">El Yunque National Forest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rican</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaraguan</a> rainforests, <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a> territories of several <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South American</a> countries, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar_dry_deciduous_forests" title="Madagascar dry deciduous forests">Madagascar dry deciduous forests</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Waterberg_Biosphere" title="Waterberg Biosphere">Waterberg Biosphere</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, and eastern <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> rainforests. Often the soils of tropical forests are low in <a href="/wiki/Nutrient" title="Nutrient">nutrient</a> content, making them quite vulnerable to <a href="/wiki/Slash-and-burn" title="Slash-and-burn">slash-and-burn</a> <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a> techniques, which are sometimes an element of <a href="/wiki/Shifting_cultivation" title="Shifting cultivation">shifting cultivation</a> agricultural systems.</p>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">biogeography</a>, the tropics are divided into <a href="/wiki/Paleotropics" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleotropics">Paleotropics</a> (Africa, Asia and Australia) and <a href="/wiki/Neotropics" class="mw-redirect" title="Neotropics">Neotropics</a> (Caribbean, Central America, and South America). Together, they are sometimes referred to as the Pantropic. The Neotropical region should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Ecozone" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecozone">ecozone</a> of the same name; in the Old World, there is no such ambiguity, as the Paleotropics correspond to the Afrotropical, Indomalayan, and partly the Australasian and Oceanic ecozones.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropicality">Tropicality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tropicality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>"Tropicality" refers to the geographic imagery that many people outside the tropics have of that region. The idea of tropicality gained renewed interest in modern geographical discourse when French geographer <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gourou" title="Pierre Gourou">Pierre Gourou</a> published <i>Les Pays Tropicaux</i> (<i>The Tropical World</i>, in English), in the late 1940s.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tropicality encompasses at least two contradictory imageries. One is that the tropics represent a Garden of Eden, a heaven on Earth;<sup id="cite_ref-Arnold.2C_David_p._7_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold.2C_David_p._7-10">[10]</a></sup> the alternative is that the tropics are primitive and essentially lawless. The latter view was often discussed in Western literature\xe2\x80\x94more so than the first.<sup id="cite_ref-Arnold.2C_David_p._7_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arnold.2C_David_p._7-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Western scholars also theorized about the reasons that tropical areas were deemed "inferior" to regions in the Northern Hemisphere. A popular explanation focused on the differences in climate\xe2\x80\x94tropical regions typically have much warmer weather than northern regions. This theme led some scholars, including Gourou, to argue that warmer climates correlate to primitive indigenous populations lacking control over nature, compared to northern populations having "mastered nature".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span
