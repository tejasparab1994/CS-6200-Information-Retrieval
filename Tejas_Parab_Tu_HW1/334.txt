hurricane">Pacific hurricanes</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>. The sixteenth tropical storm, eighth hurricane, and seventh major hurricane of the <a href="/wiki/1997_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="1997 Pacific hurricane season">1997 Pacific hurricane season</a>, Pauline developed out of a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> from Africa on September 16, 1997, moving across South America and into the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. On October 5, the depression intensified into a tropical storm early the next day and by October 7, Pauline had reached hurricane intensity. It initially moved eastward, then turned northwestward and quickly strengthened to reach peak winds of 135&#160;mph (215&#160;km/h). It paralleled the Mexican coastline a short distance offshore before weakening and making landfall near <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Angel" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Angel">Puerto Angel</a>, on October&#160;9, and dissipated the next day.</p>\n<p>Hurricane Pauline produced torrential rainfall along the <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexican</a> coastline, peaking at 32.62&#160;inches (930&#160;mm) in Puente Jula. Intense flooding and mudslides in some of the poorest areas of Mexico killed between 230 and 500&#160;people, making it one of the deadliest Eastern Pacific storms in recorded history. The passage of the hurricane destroyed or damaged tens of thousands of houses, leaving around 300,000&#160;people homeless and causing $447.8&#160;million in damage (1997&#160;<a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">USD</a>).</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 id="Meteorological_history">Meteorological history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Meteorological history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pauline_1997_track.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Pauline_1997_track.png/220px-Pauline_1997_track.png" width="220" height="136" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Pauline_1997_track.png/330px-Pauline_1997_track.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Pauline_1997_track.png/440px-Pauline_1997_track.png 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="633" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pauline_1997_track.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMap plotting the track and intensity of the storm, according to the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_scale" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Tropical_wave" title="Tropical wave">tropical wave</a> exited the coast of Africa on September 16. It traversed steadily westward, with the southern portion of the wave axis moving across northern South America. On September 26 the wave entered the eastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> near <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, and slowly organized. A weak low-level <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">trough</a> extended from the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> to south of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, disrupting the normal flow of westward steering currents. On October 3, the tropical wave developed a distinct area of deep convection, and began to drift eastward to the south of Mexico. Two days later a low-level circulation formed, and midday on October 5 the system developed into Tropical Depression Eighteen-E while located about 250&#160;miles (410&#160;km) south-southwest of <a href="/wiki/Huatulco" title="Huatulco">Huatulco</a> in the state of <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With an absence of vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>, the depression drifted just south of due east and steadily organized. The system developed <a href="/wiki/Rainband" title="Rainband">banding features</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)#Structure" title="Eye (cyclone)">central dense overcast</a>, and early on October 6 the depression intensified into a tropical storm and was named <i>Pauline</i> while located about 295&#160;miles (475&#160;km) south-southeast of <a href="/wiki/Salina_Cruz" title="Salina Cruz">Salina Cruz, Oaxaca</a>. A strong <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">high pressure system</a> eroded the trough over southeastern Mexico, which turned Pauline to the northeast. An <a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye feature</a> developed late on October 6, and early the next day Pauline intensified into a hurricane about 265&#160;miles (425&#160;km) southeast of Salina Cruz after turning to the north and northwest.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif/220px-Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif/330px-Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif/440px-Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="588" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hurr-pauline-vis1.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nHurricane Pauline off the Mexican coast on October 7</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Pauline <a href="/wiki/Rapid_deepening" class="mw-redirect" title="Rapid deepening">rapidly intensified</a> after becoming a hurricane with favorable conditions for continued development, and 18&#160;hours after becoming a hurricane it attained a peak intensity of 135&#160;mph (215&#160;km/h). The winds of the hurricane weakened slightly to 115&#160;mph (185&#160;km/h), but on October 8 Pauline re-strengthened to reach winds of 135&#160;mph (215&#160;km/h) a short distance off the coast of Mexico. The hurricane turned more to the west-northwest while paralleling the southern coast of Oaxaca, and Pauline quickly weakened due to interaction with the mountainous terrain before <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfall</a> near <a href="/wiki/Puerto_%C3%81ngel" title="Puerto \xc3\x81ngel">Puerto \xc3\x81ngel</a> as a 110&#160;mph (175&#160;km/h) hurricane early on October 9. The <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> continued to weaken as it paralleled the coast a short distance inland. On October 10, Pauline dissipated over the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Mexico" title="States of Mexico">state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">Jalisco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations">Preparations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Preparations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Early forecasts underestimated the peak intensity of Pauline by 65&#160;mph (105&#160;km/h). On October 7, about 41&#160;hours before landfall, the government of Mexico issued a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_warnings_and_watches" title="Tropical cyclone warnings and watches">hurricane warning</a> from <a href="/wiki/Tapachula" title="Tapachula">Tapachula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a> to Punta Maldonado in <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a>. Shortly after Pauline made landfall, the warning was extended northwestward to <a href="/wiki/Manzanillo,_Colima" title="Manzanillo, Colima">Manzanillo, Colima</a>, and later to <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Vallarta" title="Puerto Vallarta">Puerto Vallarta, Jalisco</a>. Pauline\'s turn to the west-northwest near landfall was unexpected, resulting in hurricane conditions with only a few hours notice in some areas.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Authorities in <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> declared a national state of alert in response to the potential threat from the hurricane. Residents in flood-prone areas were warned of potential <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn107_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn107-2">[2]</a></sup> As the hurricane turned sharply to the northwest, there are no reports of damage or deaths from Pauline in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> Officials in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Madero" title="Puerto Madero">Puerto Madero</a> closed port facilities to all ships, excluding ships in open seas seeking shelter.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn107_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn107-2">[2]</a></sup> The government ultimately closed six major ports between <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> and Puerto Madero. State authorities in <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a> opened 75&#160;emergency shelters<sup id="cite_ref-cnn108_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn108-3">[3]</a></sup> and href="#cite_note-emdat-19">[19]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>Few surface observations were taken during the passage of the hurricane, though officials reported that southern Mexico experienced the brunt of the storm. <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Escondido,_Oaxaca" title="Puerto Escondido, Oaxaca">Puerto Escondido, Oaxaca</a>, near where Pauline made landfall, reported a peak wind gust of 70&#160;mph (115&#160;km/h) several hours before the hurricane moved through the area; no reports were available after that time. An <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> in <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a> reported a wind gust of 59&#160;mph (95&#160;km/h) with sustained winds of 46&#160;mph (75&#160;km/h). However, officials estimate Pauline might have been a hurricane while passing through the area.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> The hurricane produced very heavy rainfall along its path,<sup id="cite_ref-cnn108a_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn108a-4">[4]</a></sup> with many areas receiving more than 15&#160;inches (381&#160;mm). According to the <a href="/wiki/Servicio_Meteorol%C3%B3gico_Nacional_(Mexico)" title="Servicio Meteorol\xc3\xb3gico Nacional (Mexico)">Comision Nacional del Agua</a>, precipitation was recorded at 2,132 sites. The two highest reported rainfall totals are 27.1&#160;inches (688&#160;mm) at San Luis Actlan, and 32.62&#160;inches (930&#160;mm) at Puente Jula, near Paso Overjas.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn108a_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn108a-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> This made Pauline the wettest <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> in the history of <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Roth_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roth-28">[28]</a></sup> In Acapulco, the hurricane dropped 16.9&#160;in (430&#160;mm) of rainfall in 24 hours. This broke the city precipitation record set originally in 1974;<sup id="cite_ref-your_dad_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-your_dad-29">[29]</a></sup> the 1997 total represented about 25% of the city\'s annual rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> Seas of about 30&#160;ft (9&#160;m) were reported along the <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a> coastline while the hurricane made landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn108a_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn108a-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Hurricane Pauline lightly affected the state of <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>, but severely affected Oaxaca and Guerrero, two of the poorest regions of Mexico. The area most impacted was the region in and around Acapulco.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1-31">[31]</a></sup> Throughout the country, Hurricane Pauline resulted in $447.8&#160;million in damage (1997&#160;USD).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> A report issued by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> Department of Humanitarian Affairs reported 137&#160;deaths three days after Hurricane Pauline.<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> Four days after the passage of the hurricane, a <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">Reuters</a> news report stated there were 173&#160;dead with about 200&#160;missing,<sup id="cite_ref-rwplague_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwplague-34">[34]</a></sup> while the government of Mexico issued a statement reporting 149&#160;deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-rw5_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw5-35">[35]</a></sup> Ultimately, media reports indicated a death toll of at least 230&#160;people, and the Mexican <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> estimated 400&#160;dead<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-1">[1]</a></sup> and at least 1,900&#160;missing. The <a href="/wiki/Church_World_Service" title="Church World Service">Church World Service</a> estimated at least 500&#160;people were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Relief_Web" class="mw-redirect" title="Relief Web">Relief Web</a> suggests that 217 fatalities were reported and 600,000 people were impacted.<sup id="cite_ref-Interragate.com_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Interragate.com-37">[37]</a></sup> Approximately 300,000 people were left homeless due to the storm.<sup id="cite_ref-Relief_Web_reports_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Relief_Web_reports-38">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The hurricane caused severe damage to the environment; 200&#160;square miles (700&#160;km\xc2\xb2) of low-lying <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a> and pine and evergreen oak woodlands were greatly damaged in southern Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-fao_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fao-39">[39]</a></sup> Strong waves produced severe beach erosion in some locations. The erosion affected two nesting cycles for the <a href="/wiki/Olive_Ridley" class="mw-redirect" title="Olive Ridley">Olive Ridley</a> turtle,<sup id="cite_ref-turtle_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-turtle-40">[40]</a></sup> destroying about 40&#160;million eggs. Nearly 806,000 nests were affected, and about 50% of these were wiped out.<sup id="cite_ref-egg_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-egg-41">[41]</a></sup> The deadliest and most intense hurricane to hit southern Mexico since <a href="/wiki/1959_Mexico_Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="1959 Mexico Hurricane">1959</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-rw2_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw2-42">[42]</a></sup> the hurricane was the first documented hurricane strike on Pacific <a href="/wiki/Coral_reef" title="Coral reef">coral reefs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> Crop damage was extreme, and 400,000 bags of coffee were lost. In the days after the hurricane, soybean and wheat prices increased.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[44]</a></sup> In all, Hurricane Pauline had a large effect on fauna in Southern Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-your_dad_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-your_dad-29">[29]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Oaxaca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a> was declared for the state of <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a> shortly after Pauline made landfall.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn109_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn109-45">[45]</a></sup> Abundant rainfall caused the River Los Perros to overflow its capacity, flooding 50&#160;<a href="/wiki/Municipalities_of_Oaxaca" title="Municipalities of Oaxaca">municipalities in Oaxaca</a>. The flooding damaged 12&#160;bridges,<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> of which two were destroyed,<sup id="cite_ref-rw1013_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1013-46">[46]</a></sup> and cut off some areas of electricity, drinking water, and telecommunications for several days.<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> The passage of the hurricane affected thousands of houses, leaving roughly 250,000&#160;homeless in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-table_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-47">[47]</a></sup> At least 110&#160;people died in the state, with hundreds of thousands of residents and 1,278&#160;communities being affected.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Strong winds from the hurricane downed trees and power lines throughout southern Oaxaca. The storm temporarily isolated <a href="/wiki/Puerto_%C3%81ngel" title="Puerto \xc3\x81ngel">Puerto \xc3\x81ngel, Oaxaca</a> and a navy base thereby cutting off communications from the rest of Mexico. In <a href="/wiki/Huatulco" title="Huatulco">Huatulco</a>, the winds blew down antennas at the local television station and destroyed at least 30&#160;cardboard houses.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn108a_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn108a-4">[4]</a></sup> A community near the airport of the city was hit hard, with several people left homeless. Heavy rainfall from the storm caused severe flooding in portions of Oaxaca and neighboring <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn109_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn109-45">[45]</a></sup> A total of about 500&#160;entire communities were destroyed in Oaxaca; the areas worst affected were <a href="/wiki/Zapotecos" class="mw-redirect" title="Zapotecos">Zapotecos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chatino" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatino">Chatino</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mixtecos" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixtecos">Mixtecos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guerrero">Guerrero</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Guerrero">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Heavy rainfall led to severe mudslides and flooding throughout southern <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a>. Entire communities were nearly destroyed, with some remaining flooded for a week after the hurricane. The flooding washed out or destroyed thousands of acres of crops, and killed thousands of cattle. The flooding and mudslides isolated more than 45,000&#160;people from the outside world.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup> The passage of the hurricane resulted in damage to houses, bridges, and electrical and water supply.<sup id="cite_ref-table_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-47">[47]</a></sup> About 400&#160;mm (16&#160;in) fell in the city in a three-hour span, resulting in rivers overflowing its banks. About 1,100 vessels were stranded at port, and 35 ships sunk. Damage to the coffee industry was $80 million (1997 USD).<sup id="cite_ref-your_mom_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-your_mom-48">[48]</a></sup> One environmental agency remarked that it will take 15 years for coffee crops to recover. According to one preliminary estimate, 123&#160;people died in Guerrero,<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> primarily in <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a>. Over 200&#160;were missing by four days after the hurricane due to being washed out to sea or buried in mudslides.<sup id="cite_ref-rw5_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw5-35">[35]</a></sup> A total of 50,000&#160;people were left homeless across the state.<sup id="cite_ref-table_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-table-47">[47]</a></sup> Striking the week after <a href="/wiki/Tropical_Storm_Olaf_(1997)" title="Tropical Storm Olaf (1997)">Tropical Storm Olaf</a>, previously wet grounds combined with heavy rainfall from Pauline resulted in severe mudslides and <a href="/wiki/Flash_flood" title="Flash flood">flash flooding</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shanty_town" title="Shanty town">shanty towns</a> around <a href="/wiki/Acapulco_Bay" class="mw-redirect" title="Acapulco Bay">Acapulco Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rw3_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw3-49">[49]</a></sup> There, around 5,000&#160;homes were destroyed with another 25,000&#160;damaged,<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup> with 10,000&#160;people left homeless in and around the city.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1-31">[31]</a></sup> The luxury resort hotels near the beach were largely unaffected by the hurricane, though residents in the shanty towns lost what little they had. Much of the city was covered in mud, and 70&#160;percent of Acapulco was without water as a result of the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-rwplague_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwplague-34">[34]</a></sup> Most of the city\'s one million residents were left without power or telephone service.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1-31">[31]</a></sup> Overall, total damage was nearly $300 million <a href="/wiki/Mexican_pesos" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican pesos">pesos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-your_mom_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-your_mom-48">[48]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span Pauline</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Volunteers from the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Red_Cross" title="Mexican Red Cross">Mexican Red Cross</a> quickly went to disaster areas with search and rescue teams,<sup id="cite_ref-rw3_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw3-49">[49]</a></sup> including using specially trained dogs to search for hurricane victims trapped under muddy areas of <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">Acapulco</a>. By four days after the storm each team was finding one or two corpses per day, with officials stating the search could take weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-rwplague_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwplague-34">[34]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Guerrero" title="Guerrero">Guerrero</a>, the teams rescued a total of 35&#160;people from hazard.<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> Hours after the hurricane passed through the area, relief works traveled by boat through flooded areas to assist the worst-hit areas. The Red Cross provided food, water, clothing, blankets, water purification supplies, milk powder and other non-perishable foods, and medical supplies to those staying in government shelters in <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">Oaxaca</a>, Guerrero, and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>. The Mexican Red Cross also set up shelters to house and feed hundreds of displaced people, and by four days after the storm, the Red Cross distributed 100 <a href="/wiki/Tonnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonnes">tonnes</a> of relief supplies to hurricane victims. Medical workers were also deployed to the area to aid the injured.<sup id="cite_ref-rw3_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw3-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Officials set up emergency water purification plants in Acapulco, though water remained largely unavailable. Water trucks were sent to the city, with thousands standing in line for fresh water. Tourists in luxury hotels of Acapulco, for the most part generally unaffected by the hurricane,<sup id="cite_ref-rwplague_34-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwplague-34">[34]</a></sup> and rapidly recovered while other parts of the city remained devastated.<sup id="cite_ref-usa_today_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usa_today-50">[50]</a></sup> Hotels were forced to use bottled water and ration their available water to as little as possible to provide water for the rest of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1015_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1015-51">[51]</a></sup> Extreme <a href="/wiki/Price_gouging" title="Price gouging">price gouging</a> occurred in the city following the hurricane, with one consumer protection official reporting shopkeepers charging 200&#160;percent more for milk, 500&#160;percent more for tortillas, and 1000&#160;percent more than usual for water.<sup id="cite_ref-rw5_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw5-35">[35]</a></sup> Even though the government was blamed for lack of warnings and water shortages, <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Zedillo" title="Ernesto Zedillo">Ernesto Zedillo</a>, the president of Mexico at that time, cut his vacation in Europe short to respond to the catastrophe.<sup id="cite_ref-Economst.com_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Economst.com-52">[52]</a></sup> Government officials set up 39&#160;aid centers for Acapulco citizens, though some residents were unable to get food and water. Some residents suspected than the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Mexico" title="President of Mexico">President</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Institutional_Revolutionary_Party" title="Institutional Revolutionary Party">Institutional Revolutionary Party</a> of taking aid supplies for their own purposes. The president promised to seek charges and decided to close aid centers in favor of opening soup kitchens. Despite having the food, the Mexican army did not set up the kitchens, nor was aid distributed at the aid centers.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1015_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1015-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Most of Acapulco remained closed for at least a week after the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1015_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1015-51">[51]</a></sup> Initially, authorities around Acapulco gave preference to clean up tourist areas, which resulted in the scenic highway from the hotels to the airport being quickly fixed. Tourism greatly decreased following the hurricane, causing some hotels to charge 40&#160;percent less than normal in an attempt to bring people back. One airline offered two plane tickets for the price of one from <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> to Acapulco. Most hotels were almost completely back to normal around a month after the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes119_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes119-53">[53]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Donations">Donations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Donations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The governments of Oaxaca and Guerrero asked <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Children%27s_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Children\'s Fund">UNICEF</a> for assistance, specifically water tanks, water pumps, and construction material.<sup id="cite_ref-rw4_33-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw4-33">[33]</a></sup> International aid initially focused almost solely on the damage in Acapulco. By a week after the hurricane, 500&#160;communities in Oaxaca remained isolated and without assistance, with several large communities in Guerrero not receiving any material aid by a week after the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-rw1016_36-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw1016-36">[36]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Adventist_Development_and_Relief_Agency" title="Adventist Development and Relief Agency">Adventist Development and Relief Agency</a> organized about 7&#160;tons of food and clothing, and sent a bus of 40&#160;people to help isolated villages in southern Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-adra_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adra-54">[54]</a></sup> Around ten days after the hurricane struck, 20,000&#160;people were still isolated from emergency crews and relief works, causing the president to suspect people could begin starving to death. Helicopters were initially sent to the remote areas, though severe fog and heavy rainfall after the hurricane grounded the operations. The government worked to bring food to remote mountain communities, though officials noted the serious risk in doing so.<sup id="cite_ref-abc1019_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abc1019-55">[55]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Three days after the hurricane, the <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">American Red Cross</a> sent an initial donation of $25,000 (1997&#160;USD), and also sent plastic sheets for temporary roofing and cleaning supplies such as mops, brooms, buckets, sponges, bleach, and cleaning chemicals.<sup id="cite_ref-rw3_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw3-49">[49]</a></sup> Local chapters also offered assistance. The chapter in <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a> sent cleaning kits, and the chapter in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a> delivered 2,000&#160;comfort kits containing hygiene supplies and crossword puzzles for children.<sup id="cite_ref-arc_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arc-56">[56]</a></sup> The German Red Cross also offered assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-rw3_49-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rw3-49">[49]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases">Diseases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diseases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The floodwaters from the hurricane combined with raw sewage in many poor areas of southwestern Mexico, leading to a widespread threat for a spread of tropical diseases. As a result, government health workers opened vaccination centers in several cities along the Guerrero and Oaxaca coasts. Thousands were inoculated for <a href="/wiki/Typhoid_fever" title="Typhoid fever">typhoid fever</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tetanus" title="Tetanus">tetanus</a>. Officials noted a potential threat for <a href="/wiki/Dengue" class="mw-redirect" title="Dengue">dengue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> as a result of the spoiled water. Health workers also stated <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">mosquitos</a> possessing <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dengue_fever" title="Dengue fever">dengue fever</a> were likely to breed in large areas of leftover water. In Acapulco, about two days after the hurricane passed, the first day of sun in a week evaporated the areas of leftover water, spreading dust across the region with the deadly diseases. Residents were warned to boil their food and water for 30&#160;minutes due to the threat for contamination by the dust.<sup id="cite_ref-rwplague_34-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rwplague-34">[34]</a></sup> At least twenty cases of cholera<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes119_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes119-53">[53]</a></sup> and at least six cases of dengue fever were reported.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1014_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1014-57">[57]</a></sup> Most significantly, there were 14,630&#160;cases of malaria in 616&#160;villages in Oaxaca due to Pauline; this represented about 80% of the malaria cases in Mexico during 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[58]</a></sup> Army soldiers distributed <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a> tablets to disinfect water pools and wheelbarrows to remove rotting mud and sewage from their damaged homes. Two <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules" title="Lockheed C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> planes and twenty helicopters airlifted food and water to smaller villages south of Acapulco that were stranded for nearly a week after the hurricane.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters1015_51-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters1015-51">[51]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_Pauline&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Less than a month after Pauline, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Rick_(1997)" title="Hurricane Rick (1997)">Hurricane Rick</a> struck the same general region, which brought additional rainfall and hampered relief efforts. Compared to Pauline, however, damage was minimal.<sup id="cite_ref-Australia_Severe_Weather_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Australia_Severe_Weather-59">[59]</a></sup> Because of the high death tolls and the extent of damage in Mexico, The name <a href="/wiki/Pauline_(given_name)" title="Pauline (given name)">Pauline</a> was retired in the spring of 1998 by the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> and will never again be used for a <a href="/wiki/Pacific_hurricane" title="Pacific hurricane">Pacific hurricane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[60]</a></sup> It was replaced by Patricia in the <a href="/wiki/2003_Pacific_hurricane_season" title="2003 Pacific hurricane season">2003 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ATWC_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATWC-61">[61]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
