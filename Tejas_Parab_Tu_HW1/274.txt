class="mw-redirect" title="ATR-72">ATR-72</a>, a typical modern turboprop aircraft</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <b>turboprop</b> engine is a <a href="/wiki/Turbine_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Turbine engine">turbine engine</a> that drives an aircraft <a href="/wiki/Propeller_(aircraft)" class="mw-redirect" title="Propeller (aircraft)">propeller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In contrast to a <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a>, the engine\'s <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">exhaust gases</a> do not contain enough energy to create significant thrust, since almost all of the engine\'s power is used to drive the propeller.</p>\n<p>In its simplest form a turboprop consists of an intake, <a href="/wiki/Gas_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas compressor">compressor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combustor" title="Combustor">combustor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Propelling_nozzle" title="Propelling nozzle">propelling nozzle</a>. Air is drawn into the intake and compressed by the compressor. Fuel is then added to the compressed air in the combustor, where the fuel-air mixture then <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combusts</a>. The hot combustion gases expand through the turbine. Some of the power generated by the turbine is used to drive the compressor. The rest is transmitted through the reduction gearing to the propeller. Further expansion of the gases occurs in the propelling nozzle, where the gases exhaust to atmospheric pressure. The propelling nozzle provides a relatively small proportion of the thrust generated by a turboprop.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Technological aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flowtp.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Flowtp.gif/220px-Flowtp.gif" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Flowtp.gif/330px-Flowtp.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Flowtp.gif/440px-Flowtp.gif 2x" data-file-width="482" data-file-height="436" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flowtp.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nFlow past a turboprop engine in operation</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Exhaust thrust in a turboprop is sacrificed in favor of shaft power, which is obtained by extracting additional power (up to that necessary to drive the compressor) from turbine expansion. Owing to the additional expansion in the turbine system, the residual energy in the exhaust jet is low.<sup id="cite_ref-glennTur_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennTur-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-glennThr_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennThr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lyle_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lyle-4">[4]</a></sup> Consequently, the exhaust jet typically produces around or less than 10% of the total thrust.<sup id="cite_ref-srm_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srm-5">[5]</a></sup> A higher proportion of the thrust comes from the propeller at low speeds and less at higher speeds.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Turboprops can have <a href="/wiki/Bypass_ratio" title="Bypass ratio">bypass ratios</a> up to 50-100<sup id="cite_ref-kroo_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kroo-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Spak_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spak-8">[8]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nag_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nag-9">[9]</a></sup> although the propulsion airflow is less clearly defined for propellers than for fans.<sup id="cite_ref-glennProp_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennProp-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The propeller is coupled to the turbine through a <a href="/wiki/Reduction_gear" class="mw-redirect" title="Reduction gear">reduction gear</a> that converts the high <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">RPM</a>/low <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">torque</a> output to low RPM/high torque. The propeller itself is normally a <a href="/wiki/Constant-speed_propeller" title="Constant-speed propeller">constant speed (variable pitch) type</a> similar to that used with larger <a href="/wiki/Reciprocating_engine" title="Reciprocating engine">reciprocating aircraft engines</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Unlike the small diameter fans used in <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> jet engines, the propeller has a large diameter that lets it accelerate a large volume of air. This permits a lower airstream velocity for a given amount of thrust. As it is more efficient at low speeds to accelerate a large amount of air by a small degree than a small amount of air by a large degree,<sup id="cite_ref-bevil_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bevil-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bensen_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bensen-12">[12]</a></sup> a low <a href="/wiki/Disc_loading" class="mw-redirect" title="Disc loading">disc loading</a> (thrust per disc area) increases the aircraft\'s energy efficiency, and this reduces the fuel use.<sup id="cite_ref-wayne_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wayne-13">[13]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-step_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-step-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Propellers lose efficiency as aircraft speed increases, so turboprops are normally not used on high-speed aircraft<sup id="cite_ref-glennTur_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennTur-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-glennThr_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glennThr-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lyle_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lyle-4">[4]</a></sup> above <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 0.6-0.7.<sup id="cite_ref-srm_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-srm-5">[5]</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Propfan" title="Propfan">propfan</a> engines, which are very similar to turboprop engines, can cruise at flight speeds approaching <a href="/wiki/Mach_number" title="Mach number">Mach</a> 0.75. To increase propeller efficiency, a mechanism can be used to alter their pitch relative to the airspeed. A variable-pitch propeller, also called a <a href="/wiki/Controllable-pitch_propeller" class="mw-redirect" title="Controllable-pitch propeller">controllable-pitch propeller</a>, can also be used to generate negative thrust while decelerating on the runway. Additionally, in the event of an engine outage, the pitch can be adjusted to a vaning pitch (called <a href="/wiki/Feathering_(propeller)#Feathering" class="mw-redirect" title="Feathering (propeller)">feathering</a>), thus minimizing the drag of the non-functioning propeller.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>While most modern <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofan</a> engines use <a href="/wiki/Axial-flow_compressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Axial-flow compressor">axial-flow compressors</a>, turboprop engines usually contain at least one stage of <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_compressor" title="Centrifugal compressor">centrifugal compression</a>. Centrifugal compressors have the advantage of being simple and lightweight, at the expense of a streamlined shape.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>While the power turbine may be integral with the gas generator section, many turboprops today feature a free power turbine on a separate coaxial shaft. This enables the propeller to rotate freely, independent of compressor speed.<sup id="cite_ref-pt6nation_2_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pt6nation_2-15">[15]</a></sup> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Residual_thrust&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Residual thrust (page does not exist)">Residual thrust</a> on a turboshaft is avoided by further expansion in the turbine system and/or truncating and turning the exhaust 180&#160;degrees, to produce two opposing jets. Apart from the above, there is very little difference between a turboprop and a turboshaft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-nag_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nag-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" engine</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Alan_Arnold_Griffith" title="Alan Arnold Griffith">Alan Arnold Griffith</a> had published a paper on turbine design in 1926. <a href="/wiki/Turbojet_development_at_the_RAE" title="Turbojet development at the RAE">Subsequent work at the Royal Aircraft Establishment</a> investigated axial turbine designs that could be used to supply power to a shaft and thence a propeller. From 1929, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Whittle" title="Frank Whittle">Frank Whittle</a> began work on centrifugal turbine designs that would deliver pure jet thrust.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The world\'s first turboprop was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_engineering" title="Mechanical engineering">mechanical engineer</a> <a href="/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Jendrassik" title="Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Jendrassik">Gy\xc3\xb6rgy Jendrassik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> Jendrassik published a turboprop idea in 1928, and on 12 March 1929 he patented his invention. In 1938, he built a small-scale (100 Hp; 74.6&#160;kW) experimental gas turbine.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The larger <a href="/wiki/Jendrassik_Cs-1" title="Jendrassik Cs-1">Jendrassik Cs-1</a>, with a predicted output of 1,000 bhp, was produced and tested at the <a href="/wiki/Ganz_Works" title="Ganz Works">Ganz Works</a> in <a href="/wiki/Budapest" title="Budapest">Budapest</a> between 1937 and 1941. It was of axial-flow design with 15 compressor and 7 turbine stages, annular combustion chamber and many other modern features. First run in 1940, combustion problems limited its output to 400&#160;bhp. In 1941,the engine was abandoned due to war, and the factory was turned over to conventional engine production. The world\'s first turboprop engine that went into mass production was designed by a German engineer, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Adolf_Mueller&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Max Adolf Mueller (page does not exist)">Max Adolf Mueller</a>, in 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-airent1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-airent1-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The first mention of turboprop engines in the general public press was in the February 1944 issue of the British aviation publication <i><a href="/wiki/Flight_International" title="Flight International">Flight</a></i>, which included a detailed cutaway drawing of what a possible future turboprop engine could look like. The drawing was very close to what the future Rolls-Royce Trent would look like.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> The first British turboprop engine was the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Limited" title="Rolls-Royce Limited">Rolls-Royce</a> <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Trent_(turboprop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rolls-Royce Trent (turboprop)">RB.50 Trent</a>, a converted <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Derwent" title="Rolls-Royce Derwent">Derwent II</a> fitted with reduction gear and a <a href="/wiki/Rotol" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotol">Rotol</a> 7&#160;ft 11&#160;in (2.41&#160;m) five-bladed propeller. Two Trents were fitted to <a href="/wiki/Gloster_Meteor" title="Gloster Meteor">Gloster Meteor</a> <i>EE227</i> \xe2\x80\x94 the sole "Trent-Meteor" \xe2\x80\x94 which thus became the world\'s first turboprop-powered aircraft, albeit a test-bed not intended for production.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> It first flew on 20 September 1945. From their experience with the Trent, Rolls-Royce developed the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Clyde" title="Rolls-Royce Clyde">Rolls-Royce Clyde</a>, the first turboprop engine to be fully <a href="/wiki/Type_certificate" title="Type certificate">type certificated</a> for military and civil use,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Dart" title="Rolls-Royce Dart">Dart</a>, which became one of the most reliable turboprop engines ever built. Dart production continued for more than fifty years. The Dart-powered <a href="/wiki/Vickers_Viscount" title="Vickers Viscount">Vickers Viscount</a> was the first turboprop aircraft of any kind to go into production and sold in large numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> It was also the first four-engined turboprop. Its first flight was on 16 July 1948. The world\'s first single engined turboprop aircraft was the <a href="/wiki/Armstrong_Siddeley_Mamba" title="Armstrong Siddeley Mamba">Armstrong Siddeley Mamba</a>-powered <a href="/wiki/Boulton_Paul_Balliol" title="Boulton Paul Balliol">Boulton Paul Balliol</a>, which first flew on 24 March 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Soviet Union built on German World War II development by Junkers Motorenwerke, while BMW, <a href="/wiki/Heinkel" title="Heinkel">Heinkel-Hirth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daimler-Benz" title="Daimler-Benz">Daimler-Benz</a> also developed and partially tested designs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> While the Soviet Union had the technology to create the airframe for a jet-powered strategic bomber comparable to Boeing\'s <a href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-52 Stratofortress">B-52 Stratofortress</a>, they instead produced the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-95" title="Tupolev Tu-95">Tupolev Tu-95</a> Bear, powered with four <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_NK-12" title="Kuznetsov NK-12">Kuznetsov NK-12</a> turboprops, mated to eight <a href="/wiki/Contra-rotating_propellers" title="Contra-rotating propellers">contra-rotating propellers</a> (two per nacelle) with supersonic tip speeds to achieve maximum cruise speeds in excess of 575&#160;mph, faster than many of the first jet aircraft and comparable to jet cruising speeds for most missions. The Bear would serve as their most successful long-range combat and surveillance aircraft and symbol of Soviet power projection throughout the end of the 20th century. The USA would incorporate contra-rotating turboprop engines, such as the ill-fated twin-turbine <a href="/wiki/Allison_T40" title="Allison T40">Allison T40</a> \xe2\x80\x94 essentially a twinned up pair of <a href="/wiki/Allison_T38" title="Allison T38">Allison T38</a> turboprop engines driving contra-rotating propellers \xe2\x80\x94 into a series of experimental aircraft during the 1950s, with aircraft powered with the T40, like the <a href="/wiki/Convair_R3Y_Tradewind" title="Convair R3Y Tradewind">Convair R3Y Tradewind</a> flying boat never entering U.S. Navy service.</p>\n<p>The first American turboprop engine was the <a href="/wiki/GE_Aviation" title="GE Aviation">General Electric</a> <a href="/wiki/General_Electric_T31" title="General Electric T31">XT31</a>, first used in the experimental <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Vultee_XP-81" title="Consolidated Vultee XP-81">Consolidated Vultee XP-81</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> The XP-81 first flew in December 1945, the first aircraft to use a combination of turboprop and <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> power. The technology of the Allison\'s earlier T38 design evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Allison_T56" title="Allison T56">Allison T56</a>, with quartets of the T56s being used to power the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-188_Electra" title="Lockheed L-188 Electra">Lockheed Electra</a> airliner, its military maritime patrol derivative the <a href="/wiki/P-3_Orion" class="mw-redirect" title="P-3 Orion">P-3 Orion</a>, and the widely produced <a href="/wiki/C-130_Hercules" class="mw-redirect" title="C-130 Hercules">C-130 Hercules</a> military transport aircraft. One of the most produced turboprop engines used in civil aviation is the <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada">Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada</a> <a href="/wiki/Pratt_%26_Whitney_Canada_PT6" title="Pratt &amp; Whitney Canada PT6">PT6</a> engine.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>The first turbine-powered, shaft-driven helicopter was the <a href="/wiki/Kaman_K-225" title="Kaman K-225">Kaman K-225</a>, a development of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kaman" title="Charles Kaman">Charles Kaman</a>\'s K-125 <a href="/wiki/Synchropter" class="mw-redirect" title="Synchropter">synchropter</a>, which used a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_T50" title="Boeing T50">Boeing T50</a> turboshaft engine to power it on 11 December 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turboprop&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/220px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/330px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Gas_turbine_efficiency.png/440px-Gas_turbine_efficiency.png 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="500" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gas_turbine_efficiency.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nPropulsive efficiency comparison for various gas turbine engine configurations</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Compared to <a href="/wiki/Turbofan" title="Turbofan">turbofans</a>, turboprops are most efficient at flight speeds below 725&#160;km/h (450&#160;mph; 390 knots) because the jet velocity of the propeller (and exhaust) is relatively low. Modern turboprop airliners operate at nearly the same speed as small <a href="/wiki/Regional_jet" title="Regional jet">regional jet</a> airliners but burn two-thirds of the fuel per passenger.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> However, compared to a <a href="/wiki/Turbojet" title="Turbojet">turbojet</a> (which can fly at high altitude for enhanced speed and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_efficiency" title="Fuel efficiency">fuel efficiency</a>) a propeller aircraft has a lower ceiling.</p>\n<p>The most common application of turboprop engines in civilian aviation is in small <a href="/wiki/Regional_airliner" title="Regional airliner">commuter aircraft</a>, where their greater power and reliability offsets their higher initial cost and fuel consumption. Turboprop-powered aircraft have become popular for <a href="/wiki/Bush_airplane" class="mw-redirect" title="Bush airplane">bush airplanes</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Cessna_Caravan" class="mw-redirect" title="Cessna Caravan">Cessna Caravan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quest_Kodiak" title="Quest Kodiak">Quest Kodiak</a> as <a href="/wiki/Jet_fuel" title="Jet fuel">jet fuel</a> is easier to obtain in remote areas than <a href="/wiki/Avgas" title="Avgas">avgas</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Due to the high price of turboprop engines, they are mostly used where high-performance short-takeoff and landing (<a href="/wiki/STOL" title="STOL">STOL</a>) capability and efficiency at modest flight speeds are required.</p>\n<p>Turboprop engines are generally used on small subsonic aircraft, but the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-114" title="Tupolev Tu-114">Tupolev Tu-114</a> can reach 470 <a href="/wiki/Knot_(unit)" title="Knot (unit)">kt</a> (870&#160;km/h, 541&#160;mph). Large <a href="/wiki/Military_aviation" title="Military aviation">military</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_aviation" title="Civil aviation">civil</a> aircraft, such as the <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_L-188_Electra" title="Lockheed L-188 Electra">Lockheed L-188 Electra</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tupolev_Tu-95" title="Tupolev Tu-95">Tupolev Tu-95</a>, have also used turboprop power. The <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A400M" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus A400M">Airbus A400M</a> is powered by four <a href="/wiki/Europrop_TP400" title="Europrop TP400">Europrop TP400</a> engines, which are the third most powerful turboprop engines ever produced, after the eleven megawatt-output <a href="/wiki/Kuznetsov_NK-12" title="Kuznetsov NK-12">Kuznetsov NK-12</a> and 10.4 MW-output <a href="/wiki/Progress_D-27" title="Progress D-27">Progress D-27</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Some commercial aircraft with turboprop engines include the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Bombardier Dash 8</a>, <a href="/wiki/ATR_42" title="ATR 42">ATR 42</a>, <a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">ATR 72</a>, <a href="/wiki/Handley_Page_Jetstream" title="Handley Page Jetstream">BAe Jetstream 31</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_1900" title="Beechcraft 1900">Beechcraft 1900</a>, <a href="/wiki/Embraer_EMB_120_Brasilia" title="Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia">Embraer EMB 120 Brasilia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fairchild_Swearingen_Metroliner" title="Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner">Fairchild Swearingen Metroliner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dornier_328" title="Dornier 328">Dornier 328</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saab_340" title="Saab 340">Saab 340</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saab_2000" title="Saab 2000">2000</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA60" title="Xian MA60">Xian MA60</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA600" title="Xian MA600">Xian MA600</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Xian_MA700" title="Xian MA700">Xian MA700</a>, Fokker 27, <a href="/wiki/Fokker_50" title="Fokker 50">50</a> and 60.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
