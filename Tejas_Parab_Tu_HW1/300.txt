religion</a></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The <b>Maya people</b> (sometimes <b>Mayans</b>) are a group of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Indigenous peoples</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">Mesoamerica</a>. They inhabit southern <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>. The overarching term "Maya" is a collective designation to include the peoples of the region that share some degree of cultural and linguistic heritage; however, the term embraces many distinct populations, societies, and ethnic groups that each have their own particular traditions, cultures, and historical identity.</p>\n<p>The pre-Columbian Maya population was approximately eight million.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> There were an estimated seven million Maya living in this area at the start of the 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-intercontinentalcry.org_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-intercontinentalcry.org-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">southern Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula">Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a>, and western <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> have managed to maintain numerous remnants of their ancient cultural heritage. Some are quite integrated into the majority <a href="/wiki/Hispanicized" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanicized">hispanicized</a> <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">Mestizo</a> cultures of the nations in which they reside, while others continue a more traditional, culturally distinct life, often speaking one of the <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a> as a primary <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>.</p>\n<p>The largest populations of contemporary Maya inhabit Guatemala, Belize, and the western portions of Honduras and El Salvador, as well as large segments of population within the <a href="/wiki/Political_divisions_of_Mexico" class="mw-redirect" title="Political divisions of Mexico">Mexican states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yucat\xc3\xa1n (state)">Yucat\xc3\xa1n</a>, <a href="/wiki/Campeche" title="Campeche">Campeche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">Quintana Roo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabasco_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tabasco (state)">Tabasco</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">Chiapas</a>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Yucatec_Maya"><span title="Yucat\xc3\xa1n (state)">Yucat\xc3\xa1n State</a> and the neighboring states of Campeche, Quintana Roo and in Belize. These peoples commonly identify themselves simply as "Maya" with no further ethnic subdivision (unlike in the Highlands of Western Guatemala). They speak the language which anthropologists term "<a href="/wiki/Yucatec_Maya_language" title="Yucatec Maya language">Yucatec Maya</a>", but is identified by speakers and Yucatecos simply as "Maya". Among Maya speakers, Spanish is commonly spoken as a second or first language.</p>\n<p>There is a significant amount of confusion as to the correct terminology to use\xe2\x80\x94Maya or Mayan\xe2\x80\x94and the meaning of these words with reference to contemporary or pre-Columbian peoples, to Maya peoples in different parts of Mexico, Guatemala, Belize, and to languages or peoples.</p>\n<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:300px; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">\n<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">\n<div style="margin:0px 2em;"><i>oxlahun ahau u katunil u 13 he\xe2\x80\xbacob cah mayapan: maya uinic u kabaob: uaxac ahau paxci u cabobi: ca uecchahi ti peten tulacal: uac katuni paxciob ca haui u maya-bulub ahau u kaba u katunil hauci u maya kabaob maya uinicob: christiano u kabaob</i><br />\n<p>"Ahau was the katun when they founded the cah of Mayapan; they were [thus] called Maya men. In 8 Ahau their lands were destroyed and they were scattered through out the peninsula. Six katun after they were destroyed they ceased to be called Maya; 11 Ahau was</p>\nthe name of the katun when the Maya men ceased to be called Maya [and] were called Christians."</div>\n<div style="position: absolute; left: 0px; top: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:\'Times New Roman\',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">\xe2\x80\x9c</div>\n<div style="position: absolute; right: 0px; bottom: 0px; text-align: left; font-family:\'Times New Roman\',serif; font-weight:bold; font-size: large; color: gray;">\xe2\x80\x9d</div>\n</div>\n<cite style="display: block; text-align: right;"><a href="/wiki/Chilam_Balam" title="Chilam Balam">Chilam Balam</a> Chumayel<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERestall200467_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERestall200467-4">[4]</a></sup></cite></div>\n<p>Linguists refer to the Maya language as Yucatec or Yucatec Maya to distinguish it from other <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">Mayan languages</a>. This norm has often been misinterpreted to mean that the people are also called Yucatec Maya; that term refers only to the language, and the correct name for the people is simply Maya (not Mayans). Maya is one language in the Mayan language family. Thus, to refer to Maya as Mayans would be similar to referring to Spanish people as Romantics because they speak a language belonging to the Romance language family.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Confusion of the term Maya/Mayan as an ethnic label occurs because Maya women who use traditional dress identify by the ethnic term <i>mestiza</i> and not Maya.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Persons use a strategy of ethnic identification that Juan Castillo Cocom refers to as "ethnoexodus"\xe2\x80\x94meaning that ethnic self-identification as Maya is quite variable, situational, and articulated not to processes of producing group identity, but of escaping from discriminatory processes of sociocultural marginalization.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Yucat\xc3\xa1n\'s indigenous population was first exposed to Europeans after a party of Spanish shipwreck survivors came ashore in 1511. One of the sailors, <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Guerrero" title="Gonzalo Guerrero">Gonzalo Guerrero</a>, is reported to have taken up with a local woman and started a family; he became a counselor among a local polity near present-day <a href="/wiki/Chetumal" title="Chetumal">Chetumal</a>. Later Spanish expeditions to the region were led by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Hern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_(discoverer_of_Yucat%C3%A1n)" class="mw-redirect" title="Francisco Hern\xc3\xa1ndez de C\xc3\xb3rdoba (discoverer of Yucat\xc3\xa1n)">C\xc3\xb3rdoba</a> in 1517, <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Grijalva" title="Juan de Grijalva">Grijalva</a> in 1518, and <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hern\xc3\xa1n Cort\xc3\xa9s">Cort\xc3\xa9s</a> in 1519. From 1528 to 1540, several attempts by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_de_Montejo" title="Francisco de Montejo">Francisco Montejo</a> to conquer the Yucat\xc3\xa1n failed. His son, Francisco de Montejo the Younger, fared almost as badly when he first took over: while desperately holding out at Chichen Itza, he lost 150 men in a single day.<sup id="cite_ref-ambconq_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ambconq-9">[9]</a></sup> European diseases, massive recruitment of native warriors from Campeche and Champoton, and internal hatred between the Xiu Maya and the lords of Cocom eventually turned the tide for Montejo the Younger. <a href="/wiki/Chichen_Itza" title="Chichen Itza">Chichen Itza</a> was conquered by 1570.<sup id="cite_ref-ambconq_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ambconq-9">[9]</a></sup> In 1542, the western Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula also surrendered to him.</p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chichen_Itza_3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chichen_Itza_3.jpg/220px-Chichen_Itza_3.jpg" width="220" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chichen_Itza_3.jpg/330px-Chichen_Itza_3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Chichen_Itza_3.jpg/440px-Chichen_Itza_3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="9771" data-file-height="5197" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chichen_Itza_3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nChichen Itza\'s <a href="/wiki/El_Castillo,_Chichen_Itza" title="El Castillo, Chichen Itza"><i>El Castillo</i></a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Historically, the population in the eastern half of the peninsula was less affected by and less integrated with Hispanic culture than the western half. In the 21st century in the Yucat\xc3\xa1n Peninsula (Mexican states of Campeche, Yucat\xc3\xa1n and Quintana Roo), between 750,000 and 1,200,000 people speak Mayan. However, three times more than that are of Maya origins, hold ancient Maya surnames, and do not speak Mayan languages as their first language.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Matthew_Restall" title="Matthew Restall">Matthew Restall</a>, in his book <i>The Maya Conquistador</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> mentions a series of letters sent to the King of Spain in the 16th and 17th centuries. people">K\'iche\'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mam_people" title="Mam people">Mam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poqomam_people" title="Poqomam people">Poqomam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poqomchi%27" class="mw-redirect" title="Poqomchi\'">Poqomchi\'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Q%27anjob%27al_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Q\'anjob\'al people">Q\'anjob\'al</a>, <a href="/wiki/Q%27eqchi%27_people" title="Q\'eqchi\' people">Q\'eqchi\'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tz%27utujil_people" title="Tz\'utujil people">Tz\'utujil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uspantek_people" title="Uspantek people">Uspantek</a>.</p>\n<p>The Q\'eqchi\' live in lowland areas of Alta Vera Paz, Peten, and Western Belize. Over the course of the succeeding centuries a series of land displacements, re-settlements, persecutions and migrations resulted in a wider dispersal of Q\'eqchi\' communities, into other regions of Guatemala (Izabal, Pet\xc3\xa9n, El Quich\xc3\xa9). They are the 2nd largest ethnic Maya group in Guatemala (after the <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_people" title="K\'iche\' people">K\'iche\'</a>) and one of the largest and most widespread throughout Central America.</p>\n<p>In Guatemala, the Spanish colonial pattern of keeping the native population legally separate and subservient continued well into the 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> This resulted in many traditional customs being retained, as the only other option than traditional Maya life open to most Maya was entering the Hispanic culture at the very bottom rung. Because of this many Guatemalan Maya, especially women, continue to wear traditional clothing, that varies according to their specific local identity.</p>\n<p>The southeastern region of Guatemala (bordering with <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>) includes groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Ch%27orti%27_people" title="Ch\'orti\' people">Ch\'orti\'</a>. The northern lowland Pet\xc3\xa9n region includes the <a href="/wiki/Itza" class="mw-redirect" title="Itza">Itza</a>, whose language is near extinction but whose agro-forestry practices, including use of dietary and medicinal plants may still tell us much about pre-colonial management of the Maya lowlands.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genocide_in_Guatemala">Genocide in Guatemala</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Genocide in Guatemala">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_genocide" title="Guatemalan genocide">Guatemalan genocide</a></div>\n<p>During the 1980s the indigenous Maya community made up about two-thirds of Guatemala\xe2\x80\x99s 10 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> During his presidency (1982-1983), <a href="/wiki/Efra%C3%ADn_R%C3%ADos_Montt" title="Efra\xc3\xadn R\xc3\xados Montt">Efra\xc3\xadn R\xc3\xados Montt</a> instituted a campaign of state terror intended to destroy the Mayas in the name of countering \xe2\x80\x9ccommunist subversion\xe2\x80\x9d and ridding the country of its indigenous culture.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>In some areas, government forces killed about 40% of the total population; the campaign destroyed at least 440 Mayan villages. The army attacked inhabitants, and burned down their homes and fields.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The 36 year long <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Civil_War" title="Guatemalan Civil War">Guatemalan Civil War</a> left more than 200,000 people dead and at least 100,000 women raped; most of the victims were Maya.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>\n<p>On January 26, 2012 former president R\xc3\xados Montt was formally indicted in Guatemala and appeared in court for genocide and crimes against humanity<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> for which he was then sentenced to a sentence of 80 years in prison on May 10, 2013<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>. This ruling was overturned by the constitutional court on May 20, 2013 over alleged irregularities in the handling of the case<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>. The ex-president appeared in court again on January 5, 2015 amongst protest form his lawyers regarding his health conditions<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> and on August 25, 2015 it was deliberated that a re-trial of the 2013 proceedings could find R\xc3\xados Montt guilty or not, but that the sentence would be suspended<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup>.</p>\n<h3><span Each village has its own distinctive pattern, making it possible to distinguish a person\'s home town. Women\'s clothing consists of a shirt and a long skirt.</p>\n<p>The Maya religion is <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a> combined with the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Maya_religion" title="Maya religion">Maya religion</a> to form the unique syncretic religion which prevailed throughout the country and still does in the rural regions prior to 2010s of "orthodoxing" the western rural areas by Christian Orthodox missionaries. Beginning from negligible roots prior to 1960s, however, <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Pentecostalism" title="Pentecostalism">Pentecostalism</a> has grown to become the predominant religion of <a href="/wiki/Guatemala_City" title="Guatemala City">Guatemala City</a> and other urban centers, later to 2010s that almost of all Maya of several rural areas of West Guatemala, living rural areas were mostly mass converted from Catholicism or possibly Maya religion due of various reasons to either <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern</a> or <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodoxy" title="Oriental Orthodoxy">Oriental Orthodoxy</a> by late Fr. Andres Giron and some other Orthodox missionaries, and also smaller to mid-sized towns also slowly converted as well since 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-theorthodoxchurch.info_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-theorthodoxchurch.info-28">[28]</a></sup> The unique religion is reflected in the local saint, <a href="/wiki/Maxim%C3%B3n" title="Maxim\xc3\xb3n">Maxim\xc3\xb3n</a>, who is associated with the subterranean force of masculine fertility and prostitution. Always depicted in black, he wears a black hat and sits on a chair, often with a cigar placed in his mouth and a gun in his hand, with offerings of tobacco, alcohol, and Coca-Cola at his feet. The locals know him as San Simon of Guatemala.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maximon,_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c2/Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg/275px-Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg" width="275" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c2/Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg/413px-Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c2/Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg/550px-Maximon%2C_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="463" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maximon,_in_Santiago_Atitlan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nMaxim\xc3\xb3n, a Maya deity</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">Popol Vuh</a> is the most significant work of Guatemalan literature in the <a href="/wiki/K%27iche%27_language" title="K\'iche\' language">K\'iche\' language</a>, and one of the most important of <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian_era" title="Pre-Columbian era">Pre-Columbian</a> American literature. It is a compendium of Maya stories and legends, aimed to preserve Maya traditions. The first known version of this text dates from the 16th century and is written in Quich\xc3\xa9 transcribed in Latin characters. It was translated into Spanish by the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> priest <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Xim%C3%A9nez" title="Francisco Xim\xc3\xa9nez">Francisco Xim\xc3\xa9nez</a> in the beginning of the 18th century. Due to its combination of historical, mythical, and religious elements, it has been called the Maya Bible. It is a vital document for understanding the culture of Pre-Columbian America. The <a href="/wiki/Rabinal_Ach%C3%AD" title="Rabinal Ach\xc3\xad">Rabinal Ach\xc3\xad</a> is a dramatic work consisting of dance and text that is preserved as it was originally represented. It is thought to date from the 15th century and narrates the mythical and dynastic origins of the Toj K\'iche\' rulers of Rabinal, and their relationships with neighboring K\'iche\' of <a href="/wiki/Q%27umarkaj" title="Q\'umarkaj">Q\'umarkaj</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The Rabinal Ach\xc3\xad is performed during the Rabinal festival of January 25, the day of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Paul">Saint Paul</a>. It was declared a masterpiece of oral tradition of humanity by UNESCO in 2005. The 16th century saw the first native-born Guatemalan writers that wrote in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Maya_cultural_heritage_tourism">Maya cultural heritage tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_peoples&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Maya cultural heritage tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">\n<div id="mwe_player_0" class="PopUpMediaTransform" style="width:220px;" videopayload="&lt;div class=&quot;mediaContainer&quot; In the case of the Maya, the many national identities have been constructed because of the growing demands placed on them by cultural tourism. By focusing on lifeways through costumes, rituals, diet, handicrafts, language, housing, or other features, the identity of the economy shifts from the sale of labor to that of the sale of culture.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Global tourism is now considered one of the largest scale movement of goods, services, and people in history and a significant catalyst for economic development and sociopolitical change.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[31]</a></sup> Estimated that between 35 and 40 percent of tourism today is represented by cultural tourism or heritage tourism, this alternative to mass tourism offers opportunities for place-based engagement that frames context for interaction by the lived space and everyday life of other peoples, as well as sites and objects of global historical significance.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup> In this production of tourism the use of historic symbols, signs, and topics form a new side that characterizes a nation and can play an active role in nation building.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With this type of tourism, people argue that ethno-commerce may open unprecedented opportunities for creating value of various kinds. Tourists travel with cultural expectations, which has created a touristic experience sometimes faced with the need to invent traditions of artificial and contrived attractions, often developed at the expense of local tradition and meanings.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p>\n<p>An example of this can be seen in \xe2\x80\x9cMayanizing Tourism on Roatan Island, Honduras: Archaeological Perspectives on Heritage, Development, and Indignity.\xe2\x80\x9d Alejandro j. Figueroa et al., combine archaeological data and ethnographic insights to explore a highly contested tourism economy in their discussion of how places on Roatan Island, Honduras, have become increasingly \xe2\x80\x9cMayanized\xe2\x80\x9d over the past decade. As tour operators and developers continue to invent an idealized Maya past for the island, non-Maya archaeological remains and cultural patrimony are constantly being threatened and destroyed. While heritage tourism provides economic opportunities for some, it can devalue contributions made by less familiar groups.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a
