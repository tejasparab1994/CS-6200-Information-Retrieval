For the videogame, see <a href="/wiki/Violent_Storm" title="Violent Storm">Violent Storm</a>.</div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_scale_12_notext.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Beaufort_scale_12_notext.jpg/300px-Beaufort_scale_12_notext.jpg" width="300" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b5/Beaufort_scale_12_notext.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="399" data-file-height="282" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Beaufort_scale_12_notext.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nForce 12 at sea</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <b>Beaufort scale</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="\'b\' in \'buy\'">b</span><span title="/o\xca\x8a/: \'o\' in \'code\'">o\xca\x8a</span><span title="\'f\' in \'find\'">f</span><span title="/\xc9\x99r/: \'er\' in \'letter\'">\xc9\x99r</span><span title="\'t\' in \'tie\'">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> is an <a href="/wiki/Empirical" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical">empirical</a> measure that relates <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> to observed conditions at sea or on land. Its full name is the <b>Beaufort wind force scale</b>.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#History"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span class="toctext">History</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-2"><a href="#Modern_scale"><span class="tocnumber">2</span> <span class="toctext">Modern scale</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-3"><a href="#See_also"><span class="tocnumber">3</span> <span class="toctext">See also</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-4"><a href="#References"><span class="tocnumber">4</span> <span class="toctext">References</span></a></li>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-5"><a href="#External_links"><span class="tocnumber">5</span> <span class="toctext">External links</span></a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Beaufort_scale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The scale was devised in 1805 by Irish-born <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaufort" title="Francis Beaufort">Francis Beaufort</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Rear_admiral_(Royal_Navy)" title="Rear admiral (Royal Navy)">Rear Admiral</a> Sir Francis Beaufort), a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> officer, while serving on <a href="/wiki/HMS_Woolwich" title="HMS Woolwich">HMS <i>Woolwich</i></a>. The scale that carries Beaufort\'s name had a long and complex evolution from the previous work of others (including <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a> the century before) to when Beaufort was Hydrographer of the Navy in the 1830s when it was adopted officially and first used during the voyage of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Beagle" title="HMS Beagle">HMS <i>Beagle</i></a> under Captain <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzRoy" title="Robert FitzRoy">Robert FitzRoy</a>, later to set up the first <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorological Office">Meteorological Office</a> (Met Office) in Britain giving regular weather forecasts.<sup id="cite_ref-met_office_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-met_office-1">[1]</a></sup> In the early 19th century, naval officers made regular weather observations, but there was no standard scale and so they could be very subjective&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 one man\'s "stiff breeze" might be another\'s "soft breeze". Beaufort succeeded in standardising the scale.</p>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FrancisBeaufort.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/FrancisBeaufort.jpg/220px-FrancisBeaufort.jpg" width="220" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/FrancisBeaufort.jpg/330px-FrancisBeaufort.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/FrancisBeaufort.jpg/440px-FrancisBeaufort.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1018" data-file-height="1280" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FrancisBeaufort.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nSir Francis Beaufort</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The initial scale of thirteen classes (zero to twelve) did not reference <a href="/wiki/Wind_speed" title="Wind speed">wind speed</a> numbers but related qualitative wind conditions to effects on the sails of a <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigate</a>, then the main ship of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a>, from "just sufficient to give steerage" to "that which no canvas sails could withstand".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The scale was made a standard for ship\'s log entries on Royal Navy vessels in the late 1830s and was adapted to non-naval use from the 1850s, with scale numbers corresponding to cup <a href="/wiki/Anemometer" title="Anemometer">anemometer</a> rotations. In 1916, to accommodate the growth of steam power, the descriptions were changed to how the sea, not the sails, behaved and extended to land observations. Rotations to scale numbers were standardized only in 1923. <a href="/wiki/George_Simpson_(meteorologist)" title="George Simpson (meteorologist)">George Simpson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">C.B.E.</a> (later Sir George Simpson), director of the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a> <a href="/wiki/Meteorological_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorological Office">Meteorological Office</a>, was responsible for this and for the addition of the land-based descriptors.<sup id="cite_ref-met_office_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-met_office-1">[1]</a></sup> The measure was slightly altered some decades later to improve its utility for <a href="/wiki/Meteorologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteorologist">meteorologists</a>. Today, many countries have abandoned the scale and use the <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">metric system</a> based units, m/s or km/h, instead,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> but the <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather" title="Severe weather">severe weather</a> warnings given to the public are still approximately the same as when using the Beaufort scale.</p>\n<p>The Beaufort scale was extended in 1946, when forces 13 to 17 were added.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> However, forces 13 to 17 were intended to apply only to special cases, such as <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a>. Nowadays, the extended scale is only used in Taiwan and mainland China, which are often affected by <a href="/wiki/Typhoon" title="Typhoon">typhoons</a>. Internationally, WMO Manual on Marine Meteorological Services (2012 edition) defined the Beaufort Scale only up to force 12 and there was no recommendation on the use of the extended scale.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Wind speed on the 1946 Beaufort scale is based on the <a href="/wiki/Empirical_relationship" title="Empirical relationship">empirical relationship</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<dl>\n<dd><i>v</i> = 0.836 <i>B</i><sup>3/2</sup> <a href="/wiki/Metres_per_second" class="mw-redirect" title="Metres per second">m/s</a></dd>\n</dl>\n<p>Where <i>v</i> is the equivalent wind speed at 10 metres above the sea surface and <i>B</i> is Beaufort scale number. For example, <i>B</i> = 9.5 is related to 24.5&#160;m/s which is equal to the lower limit of "10 Beaufort". Using this formula the highest winds in hurricanes would be 23 in the scale.</p>\n<p>Today, hurricane-force winds are sometimes described as Beaufort scale 12 through 16, very roughly related to the respective category speeds of the <a href="/wiki/Saffir%E2%80%93Simpson_hurricane_scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane scale">Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson hurricane scale</a>, by which actual <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricanes</a> are measured, where Category 1 is equivalent to Beaufort 12. However, the extended Beaufort numbers above 13 do not match the Saffir\xe2\x80\x93Simpson scale. Category 1 <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Fujita_scale" title="Fujita scale">Fujita</a> and <a href="/wiki/TORRO_scale" title="TORRO scale">TORRO</a> scales also begin roughly at the end of level 12 of the Beaufort scale, but are independent scales&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 although the TORRO scale wind values are based on the 3/2 power law relating wind velocity to Beaufort force.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Wave_height" title="Wave height">Wave heights</a> in the scale are for conditions in the open ocean, not along the shore.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_scale">Modern scale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a <i>Encyclop\xc3\xa6dia Britannica</i><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></th>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>The wind speeds in different units are not mathematically equivalent; e.g. 12\xe2\x80\x9319&#160;km/h is not equivalent to 8\xe2\x80\x9312&#160;mph, and both are not equivalent to 7\xe2\x80\x9310 knots. The reason is that the Beaufort scale is not an exact nor an objective scale. It was based on visual and subjective observation of a ship and of the sea. The corresponding integral wind speeds were determined later, but the values in different units were never made equivalent.</p>\n<p>The scale is used in the <a href="/wiki/Shipping_Forecast" title="Shipping Forecast">Shipping Forecasts</a> broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> in the United Kingdom, and in the Sea Area Forecast from <a href="/wiki/Met_%C3%89ireann" title="Met \xc3\x89ireann">Met \xc3\x89ireann</a>, the Irish Meteorological Service. Met \xc3\x89ireann issues a "Small Craft Warning" if winds of Beaufort force 6 (mean wind speed exceeding 22 knots) are expected up to 10 nautical miles offshore. Other warnings are issued by Met \xc3\x89ireann for Irish coastal waters, which are regarded as extending 30 miles out from the coastline, and the Irish Sea or part thereof: "Gale Warnings" are issued if winds of Beaufort force 8 are expected; "Strong Gale Warnings" are issued if winds of Beaufort force 9 or frequent gusts of at least 52 knots are expected.; "Storm Force Warnings" are issued if Beaufort force 10 or frequent gusts of at least 61 knots are expected; "Violent Storm Force Warnings" are issued if Beaufort force 11 or frequent gusts of at least 69 knots are expected; "Hurricane Force Warnings" are issued if winds of greater than 64 knots are expected.</p>\n<p>This scale is also widely used in the Netherlands, Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Greece, China, Taiwan, Hong Kong, Malta and Macau, although with some differences between them. Taiwan uses the Beaufort scale with the extension to 17 noted above. China also switched to this extended version without prior notice on the morning of 15 May 2006,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> and the extended scale was immediately put to use for <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Chanchu" title="Typhoon Chanchu">Typhoon Chanchu</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> retain force 12 as the maximum.</p>\n<p>In the United States, winds of force 6 or 7 result in the issuance of a <a href="/wiki/Small_craft_advisory" title="Small craft advisory">small craft advisory</a>, with force 8 or 9 winds bringing about a <a href="/wiki/Gale_warning" title="Gale warning">gale warning</a>, force 10 or 11 a <a href="/wiki/Storm_warning" title="Storm warning">storm warning</a> ("a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm_warning" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm warning">tropical storm warning</a>" being issued instead of the latter two if the winds relate to a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a>), and force 12 a <a href="/wiki/Storm_warning" title="Storm warning">hurricane-force wind warning</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_warning" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane warning">hurricane warning</a> if related to a tropical cyclone). A set of red warning flags (daylight) and red warning lights (night time) is displayed at shore establishments which coincide with the various levels of warning.</p>\n<p>In Canada, maritime winds forecast to be in the range of 6 to 7 are designated as "strong"; 8 to 9 "gale force"; 10 to 11 "storm force"; 12 "hurricane force". Appropriate wind warnings are issued by Environment Canada\'s Meteorological Service of Canada: strong wind warning, gale (force wind) warning, storm (force wind) warning and hurricane-force wind warning. These designations were standardized nationally in 2008, whereas "light wind" can refer to 0 to 12 or 0 to 15 knots and "moderate wind" 12 to 19 or 16 to 19 knots, depending on regional custom, definition or practice. Prior to 2008, a "strong wind warning" would have been referred to as a "small craft warning" by Environment Canada, similar to US terminology.
