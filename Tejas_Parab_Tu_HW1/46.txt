<a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a></div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>A <i><b>derecho</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="\'d\' in \'dye\'">d</span><span title="/\xc9\x99/: \'a\' in \'about\'">\xc9\x99</span><span title="/\xcb\x88/: primary stress follows">\xcb\x88</span><span title="\'r\' in \'rye\'">r</span><span title="/e\xc9\xaa/: \'a\' in \'face\'">e\xc9\xaa</span><span title="/t\xca\x83/: \'ch\' in \'China\'">t\xca\x83</span><span title="/o\xca\x8a/: \'o\' in \'code\'">o\xca\x8a</span></span>/</a></span></span>, from <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <span lang="es" xml:lang="es"><i>derecho</i></span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[de\xcb\x88\xc9\xbeet\xca\x83o]</a></span>, "straight") is a widespread, long-lived, <a href="/wiki/Straight-line_wind" class="mw-redirect" title="Straight-line wind">straight-line wind</a> storm that is associated with a land-based, fast-moving group of <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather#Categories" title="Severe weather">severe thunderstorms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-noaafacts_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaafacts-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Derechos can cause hurricane-force winds, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a>, heavy rains, and flash floods. <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_convective_system" title="Mesoscale convective system">Convection-induced</a> winds take on a <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a> (backward "C") form of <a href="/wiki/Squall_line" title="Squall line">squall line</a>, forming in an area of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wind_divergence&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wind divergence (page does not exist)">wind divergence</a> in upper levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a>, within a region of low-level <a href="/wiki/Thermal_wind#Advection_Turning" title="Thermal wind">warm air advection</a> and rich low-level moisture. They travel quickly in the direction of movement of their associated storms, similar to an <a href="/wiki/Outflow_boundary" title="Outflow boundary">outflow boundary</a> (gust front), except that the wind is <a href="/wiki/Maximum_sustained_wind" title="Maximum sustained wind">sustained</a> and increases in strength behind the front, generally exceeding <a href="/wiki/Beaufort_scale" title="Beaufort scale">hurricane-force</a>.</p>\n<p>A warm-weather phenomenon, derechos occur mostly in summer, especially during June, July, and August in the Northern Hemisphere, within areas of moderately strong <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_instability" title="Atmospheric instability">instability</a> and moderately strong vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a>. They may occur at any time of the year and occur as frequently at night as during the daylight hours.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div adjective form for "straight" (or "direct"), in contrast with a <i><a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornado</a></i> which is a "twisted" wind.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The word was first used in the <i>American Meteorological Journal</i> in 1888 by <a href="/wiki/Gustavus_Detlef_Hinrichs" title="Gustavus Detlef Hinrichs">Gustavus Detlef Hinrichs</a> in a paper describing the phenomenon and based on a significant derecho event that crossed Iowa on 31 July 1877.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Organized areas of <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> activity reinforce pre-existing frontal zones, and can outrun <a href="/wiki/Cold_front" title="Cold front">cold fronts</a>. The resultant <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_Convective_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesoscale Convective System">mesoscale convective system</a> (MCS) forms at the point of the best upper level divergence in the wind pattern in the area of best low level inflow.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> The convection then moves east and toward the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a> into the warm sector, parallel to low-level thickness lines with the mean tropospheric flow. When the convection is strong linear or curved, the MCS is called a squall line, with the feature placed at the leading edge of the significant wind shift and pressure rise.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Derechos are squall lines that are bow- or spearhead-shaped on <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">weather radar</a> and, thus, also are called <i>bow echoes</i> or <i>spearhead radar echoes</i>. Squall lines typically bow out due to the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Mesoscale_meteorology" title="Mesoscale meteorology">mesoscale</a> <a href="/wiki/High-pressure_area" title="High-pressure area">high pressure system</a> which forms within the <a href="/wiki/Stratiform" class="mw-redirect" title="Stratiform">stratiform</a> rain area behind the initial line. This high pressure area is formed due to strong descending motion behind the squall line, and could come in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downburst</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-blowmedown_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blowmedown-6">[6]</a></sup> The size of the bow may vary, and the storms associated with the bow may die and redevelop.</p>\n<p>During the cold season within the Northern Hemisphere, derechos generally develop within a pattern of southwesterly winds at mid levels of the troposphere in an environment of low to moderate atmospheric instability (caused by heat and moisture near ground level or cooler air moving in aloft and measured by <a href="/wiki/Convective_available_potential_energy" title="Convective available potential energy">convective available potential energy</a>) and high values of vertical wind shear (20&#160;m/s (45&#160;mph) within the lowest 5 kilometres (16,000&#160;ft) of the atmosphere). Warm season derechos in the Northern Hemisphere form in west to northwesterly flow at mid levels of the <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">troposphere</a> with moderate to high levels of instability. Derechos form within environments of low-level warm air advection and significant low-level moisture.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_and_criteria">Classification and criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification and criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Its common definition is a thunderstorm complex that produces a damaging wind swath of at least 250 miles (400&#160;km),<sup id="cite_ref-Ashley_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ashley-8">[8]</a></sup> featuring a concentrated area of convectively-induced wind gusts exceeding 50 knots (60&#160;mph; 90&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-noaafacts_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaafacts-1">[1]</a></sup> According to the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> criterion, a derecho is classified as a band of storms that have winds of at least 50 knots (90&#160;km/h; 60&#160;mph) along the entire span of the storm front, maintained over a time span of at least six hours. Some studies add a requirement that no more than two or three hours separate any two successive wind reports.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Derechos typically possess a high or rapidly increasing forward speed. They have a distinctive appearance on radar (known as a bow echo) with several unique features, such as the rear <a href="/wiki/Inflow_notch" class="mw-redirect" title="Inflow notch">inflow notch</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bookend_vortices&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bookend vortices (page does not exist)">bookend vortices</a>, and usually they manifest two or more <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a>.</p>\n<p>There are four types of derechos:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><b>Serial derecho</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 This type of derecho is usually associated with a very deep <a href="/wiki/Low_pressure_area" class="mw-redirect" title="Low pressure area">low</a>.\n<ul>\n<li><b>Single-bow</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 A very large <a href="/wiki/Bow_echo" title="Bow echo">bow echo</a> around or upwards of 250 miles (400&#160;km) long. This type of serial derecho is less common than the multi-bow kind. An example of a single-bow serial derecho is the derecho that occurred in association with the <a href="/wiki/October_2010_North_American_storm_complex" title="October 2010 North American storm complex">October 2010 North American storm complex</a>.</li>\n<li><b>Multi-bow</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 Multiple bow derechos are embedded in a large squall line typically around 250 miles (400&#160;km) long. One example of a multi-bow serial derecho is a derecho that occurred during the <a href="/wiki/1993_Storm_of_the_Century" title="1993 Storm of the Century">1993 Storm of the Century</a> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> Because of embedded <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadoes</a> can spin out of these types of derechos. This is a much more common type of serial derecho than the single-bow kind. Multi-bow serial derechos can be associated with <a href="/wiki/Line_echo_wave_pattern" title="Line echo wave pattern">line echo wave patterns</a> on weather radar.</li>\n</ul>\n</li>\n<li><b>Progressive derecho</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 A line of thunderstorms take the bow-shape and may travel for hundreds of miles along stationary fronts. Examples of this include "<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Elvis" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Elvis">Hurricane Elvis</a>" in 2003 and the <a href="/wiki/Boundary_Waters-Canadian_Derecho" class="mw-redirect" title="Boundary Waters-Canadian Derecho">Boundary Waters-Canadian Derecho</a> of 4\xe2\x80\x935 July 1999. Tornado formation is less common in a progressive than serial type.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="(July 2012)">why?</span></a></i>]</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>\n<li><b>Hybrid derecho</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 A derecho with characteristics of both a serial and progressive derecho. Similar to serial derechos and progressive derechos, these types of derechos are associated with a deep low, but are relatively small in size. An example is the <a href="/wiki/Late-May_1998_tornado_outbreak_and_derecho" title="Late-May 1998 tornado outbreak and derecho">Late-May 1998 tornado outbreak and derecho</a> that moved through the central Northern Plains and the Southern Great Lakes on 30\xe2\x80\x9331 May 1998.</li>\n<li><b>Low dewpoint derecho</b>&#160;\xe2\x80\x93 A derecho that occurs in an environment of comparatively limited low-level moisture, with appreciable moisture confined to the mid-levels of the atmosphere. Such derechos most often occur between late fall and early spring in association with strong low pressure systems. Low dewpoint derechos are essentially organized bands of successive, dry <a href="/wiki/Downburst" title="Downburst">downbursts</a>. The Utah-Wyoming derecho of 31 May 1994 was an event of this type. It produced a 105&#160;mph wind gust at Provo, Utah, where sixteen people were injured, and removed part of the roof of the Saltair Pavilion on the Great Salt Lake. Surface dewpoints along the path of the derecho were in the mid-40s \xc2\xb0F.<sup id="cite_ref-noaaUW_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaaUW-11">[11]</a></sup></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Winds in a derecho can be enhanced by downburst clusters embedded inside the storm. These straight-line winds may exceed 100 miles per hour (160&#160;km/h), reaching 130 miles per hour (210&#160;km/h) in past events.<sup id="cite_ref-RAH_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RAH-12">[12]</a></sup> Tornadoes sometimes form within derecho events, although such events are often difficult to confirm due to the additional damage caused by straight-line winds in the immediate area.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>\n<p>With the average tornado in the United States and Canada rating in the low end of the F/<a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Fujita_Scale" class="mw-redirect" title="Enhanced Fujita Scale">EF</a>1 classification at 85 to 100 miles per hour (137 to 161&#160;km/h) peak winds and most or all of the rest of the world even lower, derechos tend to deliver the vast majority of extreme wind conditions over much of the territory in which they occur.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Datasets compiled by the United States <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a> and other organizations show that a large swath of the north-central United States, and presumably at least the adjacent sections of Canada and much of the surface of the Great Lakes, can expect winds above 85&#160;mph to as high as 120&#160;mph (135 to 190&#160;km/h) over a significant area at least once in any 50-year period, including both <a href="/wiki/Convective" class="mw-redirect" title="Convective">convective</a> events and extra-tropical cyclones and other events deriving power from <a href="/wiki/Baroclinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Baroclinic">baroclinic</a> sources. Only in 40 to 65&#160;percent or so of the United States resting on the coast of the Atlantic basin, and a fraction of the Everglades, are derechos surpassed in this respect&#160;\xe2\x80\x94 by <a href="/wiki/Landfall_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Landfall (meteorology)">landfalling</a> <a href="/wiki/Hurricanes" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricanes">hurricanes</a>, which at their worst may have winds as severe as EF3 tornadoes.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Certain derecho situations are the most common instances of severe weather outbreaks which may become <i>less</i> favorable to tornado production as they become more violent;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the height of 30\xe2\x80\x9331 May 1998 upper Middle West-Canada-New York State derecho and the latter stages of significant tornado and severe weather outbreaks in 2003 and 2004 are only three examples of this. Some upper-air measurements used for severe-weather forecasting may reflect this <a href="/wiki/Point_of_diminishing_return" class="mw-redirect" title="Point of diminishing return">point of diminishing return</a> for tornado formation, and the mentioned three situations were instances during which the rare <a href="/wiki/Particularly_Dangerous_Situation" title="Particularly Dangerous Situation">Particularly Dangerous Situation</a> <a href="/wiki/Severe_thunderstorm_watch" title="Severe thunderstorm watch">severe thunderstorm</a> variety of <a href="/wiki/Severe_weather_terminology_(United_States)" title="Severe weather terminology (United States)">severe weather watches</a> were issued from the <a href="/wiki/Storm_Prediction_Center" title="Storm Prediction Center">Storm Prediction Center</a> of the U.S. <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_%26_Atmospheric_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="National Oceanic &amp; Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic &amp; Atmospheric Administration</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Some derechos develop a radar signature resembling that of a <a href="/wiki/Hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane">hurricane</a> in the low levels. They may have a central <i><a href="/wiki/Eye_(cyclone)" title="Eye (cyclone)">eye</a></i> free of precipitation, with a minimum central pressure and surrounding bands of strong convection, but are really associated with an MCS developing multiple squall lines, and are not tropical in nature. These storms have a warm core, like other mesoscale convective systems. One such derecho occurred across the midwestern U.S. on 21 July 2003. An area of convection developed across eastern <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a> near a weak stationary/warm front and ultimately matured, taking on the shape of a wavy squall line across western <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>. The system re-intensified after leaving the Ohio Valley, starting to form a large hook, with occasional hook echoes appearing along its eastern side. A surface low pressure center formed and became more impressive later in the day.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb tleft">\n<div navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_derecho_events" title="List of derecho events">List of derecho events</a></div>\n<p>Derechos in North America form predominantly from April to August, peaking in frequency from May into July.<sup id="cite_ref-RAH_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RAH-12">[12]</a></sup> During this time of year, derechos are mostly found in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a> most commonly from <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a> and across the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio Valley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ashley_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ashley-8">[8]</a></sup> During mid-summer when a hot and muggy airmass covers the north-central U.S., they will often develop farther north into <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> or <a href="/wiki/Northwestern_Ontario" title="Northwestern Ontario">Northwestern Ontario</a>, sometimes well north of the Canada\xe2\x80\x93US border. <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> and upper <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a> are also vulnerable to derecho storms when such conditions are in place. They often occur along <a href="/wiki/Stationary_front" title="Stationary front">stationary fronts</a> on the northern periphery of where the most intense heat and humidity bubble exists. Late-year derechos are normally confined to <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Deep_South" title="Deep South">Deep South</a>, although a <a href="/wiki/New_York_State_Labor_Day_Derechos" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State Labor Day Derechos">late-summer derecho</a> struck upper parts of the <a href="/wiki/New_York_State" class="mw-redirect" title="New York State">New York State</a> area after midnight on 7 September 1998. Warm season derechos have greater instability than their cold season counterpart, while cool season derechos have greater shear than their warm season counterpart.</p>\n<p>Although these storms most commonly occur in North America, derechos can occur elsewhere in the world, although infrequently. Outside North America, they sometimes are called by different names. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a> and adjacent portions of India, a type of storm known as a "Nor\'wester" may be a progressive derecho.<sup id="cite_ref-noaafacts_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-noaafacts-1">[1]</a></sup> One such event occurred on 10 July 2002 in Germany: a serial derecho killed eight people and injured 39 near Berlin. They have occurred in Argentina and South Africa as well, and on rarer occasions, close to or north of the <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_north" title="60th parallel north">60th parallel</a> in northern Canada. Primarily a <a href="/wiki/Mid-latitudes" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid-latitudes">mid-latitudes</a> phenomenon, derechos do occur in the Amazon Basin of Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> On 8 August 2010, a derecho struck Estonia and tore off the tower of V\xc3\xa4ike-Maarja Church.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Damage risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Unlike other thunderstorms, which typically can be heard in the distance when approaching, a derecho seems to strike suddenly. Within minutes, extremely high winds can arise, strong enough to knock over highway signs and topple large trees. These winds are accompanied by spraying rain and frequent lightning from all directions. It is dangerous to drive under these conditions, especially at night, because of blowing debris and obstructed roadways. Downed wires and widespread power outages are likely but not always a factor. A derecho moves through quickly, but can do much damage in a short time.</p>\n<p>Since derechos occur during warm months and often in places with cold winter climates, people who are most at risk are those involved in outdoor activities. Campers, hikers, and motorists are most at risk because of falling trees toppled over by straight-line winds. Wide swaths of forest have been felled by such storms. People who live in mobile homes are also at risk; mobile homes that are not anchored to the ground may be overturned from the high winds. Across the United States, Michigan and New York have incurred a significant portion of the fatalities from derechos. Prior to <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a>, the death tolls from derechos and hurricanes were comparable for the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Ashley_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ashley-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Derechos may also severely damage an urban area\'s electrical distribution system, especially if these services are routed above ground. The derecho that struck <a href="/wiki/July_2011_Midwest_Derecho" title="July 2011 Midwest Derecho">Chicago, Illinois on 11 July 2011</a> left more than 860,000 people without electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/June_2012_North_American_derecho" title="June 2012 North American derecho">June 2012 North American derecho</a> took out electrical power to more than 3.7 million customers starting in the Midwestern United States, across the central Appalachians, into the Mid-Atlantic States during a <a href="/wiki/Heat_wave" title="Heat wave">heat wave</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-reut630_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reut630-19">[19]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See also">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Convective_storm_detection" title="Convective storm detection">Convective storm detection</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Extreme_weather" title="Extreme weather">Extreme weather</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">Mesocyclone</a></li>\n<li><a href="/wiki/List_of_derecho_events" title="List of derecho events">List of derecho events</a></li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="References">References</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Derecho&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: References">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><b>Notes</b></p>\n<div class="reflist columns references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em;
