Scientific study is ongoing, as some aspects of tornado formation remain a mystery.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornadogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Supercellular tornadoes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Classical tornadoes are <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercellular</a> tornadoes, which have a recognizable pattern of formation.<sup id="cite_ref-Advanced_Spotter_Guide_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Advanced_Spotter_Guide-1">[1]</a></sup> The cycle begins when a strong <a href="/wiki/Thunderstorm" title="Thunderstorm">thunderstorm</a> develops a rotating <a href="/wiki/Mesocyclone" title="Mesocyclone">mesocyclone</a> a few miles up in the atmosphere. As rainfall in the storm increases, it drags with it an area of quickly descending air known as the <a href="/wiki/Rear_flank_downdraft" title="Rear flank downdraft">rear flank downdraft</a> (RFD). This downdraft accelerates as it approaches the ground, and drags the rotating mesocyclone towards the ground with it. Storm relative helicity (SRH) has been shown to play a role in tornado development and strength. SRH is horizontal vorticity that is parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Inflow_(meteorology)" title="Inflow (meteorology)">Inflow</a> of the storm and is tilted upwards when it is taken up by the updraft, thus creating vertical vorticity.</p>\n<p>As the mesocyclone lowers below the cloud base, it begins to take in cool, moist air from the downdraft region of the storm. This convergence of warm air in the updraft, and this cool air, causes a rotating wall cloud to form. The RFD also focuses the mesocyclone\'s base, causing it to siphon air from a smaller and smaller area on the ground. As the updraft intensifies, it creates an area of low pressure at the surface. This pulls the focused mesocyclone down, in the form of a visible condensation funnel. As the funnel descends, the RFD also reaches the ground, creating a gust front that can cause severe damage a good distance from the tornado. Usually, the funnel cloud begins causing damage on the ground (becoming a tornado) within a few minutes of the RFD reaching the ground.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>\n<p>Field studies have shown that in order for a supercell to produce a tornado the RFD needs to be no more than a few Kelvin cooler than the updraft. Also the FFD (<a href="/wiki/Forward_flank_downdraft#Forward_Flank_Downdraft_.28FFD.29" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward flank downdraft">Forward Flank Downdraft</a>) seems to be warmer within tornadic supercells than it is the case in non-tornadic supercells.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landspouts">Landspouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornadogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Landspouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Landspout" title="Landspout">Landspouts</a> are tornadoes which form from an entirely different process. They are similar to waterspouts in appearance and structure, analogous to a fair weather waterspout over land. Known officially as "dust-tube tornadoes" by the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service" title="National Weather Service">National Weather Service</a>, they are thought to form in a manner similar to that of weaker waterspouts. They form during the growth stage of convective clouds by the ingestion and tightening of <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_boundary_layer" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric boundary layer">boundary layer</a> <a href="/wiki/Vorticity" title="Vorticity">vorticity</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Cumulus_cloud" title="Cumulus cloud">cumuliform</a> tower\'s updraft.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterspouts">Waterspouts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornadogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Waterspouts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Waterspout" title="Waterspout">Waterspouts</a> are defined as tornadoes over water. However, while some waterspouts are supercellular (also known as "tornadic waterspouts"), forming in a process similar to the one above, most are much weaker and caused by different processes of atmospheric dynamics. They normally develop in <a href="/wiki/Moisture" title="Moisture">moisture</a>-laden environments with little vertical <a href="/wiki/Wind_shear" title="Wind shear">wind shear</a> in areas where wind comes together (convergence), such as <a href="/wiki/Land_breeze" class="mw-redirect" title="Land breeze">land breezes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake-effect_snow" title="Lake-effect snow">lake effect</a> bands, lines of frictional convergence from nearby landmasses, or surface troughs. Waterspouts normally develop as their parent clouds are in the process of development. It is theorized that they spin upward as they move up the surface boundary from the horizontal shear near the surface, and then stretch upward to the cloud once the low level shear vortex aligns with a developing cumulus or thunderstorm.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Their parent cloud can be as innocuous as a moderate cumulus, or as significant as a <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercell</a>.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Continuing_research">Continuing research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tornadogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Continuing research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Though these are widely accepted hypotheses for how most tornadoes form, they do not explain the formation of long-lived tornadoes, or tornadoes with multiple vortices. These each have different mechanisms which influence their development\xe2\x80\x94however, most tornadoes follow a pattern similar to these ones.<sup id="cite_ref-downdraft_transport_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-downdraft_transport-3">[3]</a></sup> There are still many aspects about the formation of tornadoes which remain a mystery.<sup id="cite_ref-VORTEX_book_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VORTEX_book-4">[4]</a></sup> Research programs, including <a href="/wiki/VORTEX" class="mw-redirect" title="VORTEX">VORTEX</a>, deployment of <a href="/wiki/TOtable_Tornado_Observatory" title="TOtable Tornado Observatory">TOTO</a> (the TOtable Tornado Observatory), and dozens of other programs, hope to solve many questions that still plague meteorologists about this topic.<sup id="cite_ref-tornado_forecasting_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tornado_forecasting-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline"
