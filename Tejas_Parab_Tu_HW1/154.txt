These terms are used in descriptions of engineering, physics, and other sciences, as well as ordinary day to day discourse.</p>\n<p>Though these terms by themselves may be somewhat ambiguous, they are usually used in a context in which their meaning is clear. For example, when referring to a <a href="/wiki/Drive_shaft" title="Drive shaft">drive shaft</a> it is clear what is meant by axial or radial directions. Or, in a <a href="/wiki/Free_body_diagram" title="Free body diagram">free body diagram</a>, one may similarly infer a sense of orientation by the forces or other vectors represented.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geometric_terms_of_location&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Common geometric terms of location are:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><b>Adjacent</b> - next to</li>\n<li><b>Axial</b> \xe2\x80\x93 along the center of a round body, or the <a href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis of rotation">axis of rotation</a> of a body</li>\n<li><b>Azimuthal</b> or <b>circumferential</b> \xe2\x80\x93 following around a curve or <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">circumference</a> of an object. For instance, the pattern of cells in <a href="/wiki/Taylor%E2%80%93Couette_flow" title="Taylor\xe2\x80\x93Couette flow">Taylor\xe2\x80\x93Couette flow</a> varies along the azimuth of the experiment.</li>\n<li><b>Collinear</b> - in the same line</li>\n<li><b>Degree of freedom</b> - axis direction, see <a href="/wiki/Six_degrees_of_freedom" title="Six degrees of freedom">six degrees of freedom</a></li>\n<li><b>Lateral</b> \xe2\x80\x93 spanning the width of a body.</li>\n<li><b>Lineal</b> \xe2\x80\x93 following along a given path. The shape of the path is not necessarily straight (compare to <a href="/wiki/Linear" class="mw-redirect" title="Linear">linear</a>). For instance, a length of rope might be measured in lineal <a href="/wiki/Meters" class="mw-redirect" title="Meters">meters</a> or <a href="/wiki/Foot_(unit)" title="Foot (unit)">feet</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Arc_length" title="Arc length">arc length</a>.</li>\n<li><b>Longitudinal</b> \xe2\x80\x93 spanning the length of a body. The distinction between width and length may be unclear out of context.</li>\n<li><b>Orthogonal</b> \xe2\x80\x93 at right angles to a line, or more generally, on a different axis.</li>\n<li><b>Parallel</b> - in the same direction</li>\n<li><b>Perpendicular</b> - at right angles to, synonym to orthogonal</li>\n<li><b>Radial</b> \xe2\x80\x93 along a direction pointing along a <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a> from the center of an object, or perpendicular to a curved path.</li>\n<li><b>Tangential</b> \xe2\x80\x93 intersecting a curve at a point and parallel to the curve at that point.</li>\n<li><b>Transverse</b> \xe2\x80\x93 orthogonal to a specified direction, such as a particle trajectory or an axis of rotation.</li>\n</ul>\n<h2><span
