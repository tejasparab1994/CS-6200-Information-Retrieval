href="/wiki/Pseudovector" title="Pseudovector">pseudovector</a> <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">field</a> that describes the <i>local</i> <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">spinning</a> motion of a continuum near some point (the tendency of something to rotate <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup>), as would be seen by an observer located at that point and traveling along with the flow.</p>\n<p>Conceptually, vorticity could be determined by marking the part of continuum in a small neighborhood of the point in question, and watching their <i>relative</i> displacements as they move along the flow. The vorticity vector would be twice the mean <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a> vector of those particles relative to their <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of mass</a>, oriented according to the <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">right-hand rule</a>. This quantity must not be confused with the angular velocity of the particles relative to some other point.</p>\n<p>More precisely, the vorticity is a <a href="/wiki/Pseudovector" title="Pseudovector">pseudovector</a> <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">field</a> <i>\xcf\x89</i><span style="position:relative; margin-right:-0.75em; right:0.75em; bottom:0.75em;;"><small>\xe2\x86\x92</small></span>, defined as the <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a> (rotational) of the <a href="/wiki/Flow_velocity" title="Flow velocity">flow velocity</a> <i>u</i><span style="position:relative; margin-right:-0.75em; right:0.75em; bottom:0.75em;;"><small>\xe2\x86\x92</small></span> vector. class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>In a mass of continuum that is rotating like a rigid body, the vorticity is twice the <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">angular velocity</a> vector of that rotation. This is the case, for example, of water in a tank that has been spinning for a while around its vertical axis, at a constant rate.</p>\n<p>The vorticity may be nonzero even when all particles are flowing along straight and parallel <a href="/wiki/Pathline" class="mw-redirect" title="Pathline">pathlines</a>, if there is <a href="/wiki/Shear_(fluid)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shear (fluid)">shear</a> (that is, if the flow speed varies across <a href="/wiki/Streamlines,_streaklines,_and_pathlines" title="Streamlines, streaklines, and pathlines">streamlines</a>). For example, in the <a href="/wiki/Laminar_flow" title="Laminar flow">laminar flow</a> within a pipe with constant <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">cross section</a>, all particles travel parallel to the axis of the pipe; but faster near that axis, and practically stationary next to the walls. The vorticity will be zero on the axis, and maximum near the walls, where the shear is largest.</p>\n<p>Conversely, a flow may have zero vorticity even though its particles travel along curved trajectories. An example is the ideal <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">irrotational vortex</a>, where most particles rotate about some straight axis, with speed inversely proportional to their distances to that axis. A small parcel of continuum that does not straddle the axis will be rotated in one sense but sheared in the opposite sense, in such a way that their mean angular velocity <i>about their center of mass</i> is zero.</p>\n<table border="0">\n<tr>\n<td style="text-align:center;" colspan="3">Example flows:</td>\n</tr>\n<tr>\n<td valign="top"><a href="/wiki/File:Vorticity_Figure_01_a-m.gif" class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The evolution of the vorticity field in time is described by the <a href="/wiki/Vorticity_equation" title="Vorticity equation">vorticity equation</a>, which can be derived from the <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier\xe2\x80\x93Stokes equations">Navier\xe2\x80\x93Stokes equations</a>.</p>\n<p>In many real flows where the viscosity can be neglected (more precisely, in flows with high <a href="/wiki/Reynolds_number" title="Reynolds number">Reynolds number</a>), the vorticity field can be modeled well by a collection of discrete vortices, the vorticity being negligible everywhere except in small regions of space surrounding the axes of the vortices. This is clearly true in the case of 2-D <a href="/wiki/Potential_flow" title="Potential flow">potential flow</a> (i.e. 2-D zero viscosity flow), in which case the flowfield can be modeled as a <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex-valued</a> field on the <a href="/wiki/Complex_plane" title="Complex plane">complex plane</a>.</p>\n<p>Vorticity is a useful tool to understand how the ideal potential flow solutions can be perturbed to model real flows. In general, the presence of viscosity causes a <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">diffusion</a> of vorticity away from the vortex cores into the general flow field. This flow is accounted for by the diffusion term in the vorticity transport equation. Thus, in cases of very viscous flows (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Couette_Flow" class="mw-redirect" title="Couette Flow">Couette Flow</a>), the vorticity will be diffused throughout the flow field and it is probably simpler to look at the velocity field than at the vorticity.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vortex_lines_and_vortex_tubes">Vortex lines and vortex tubes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vortex lines and vortex tubes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <b>vortex line</b> or <b>vorticity line</b> is a line which is everywhere tangent to the local vorticity vector. class="mw-headline" id="Specific_sciences">Specific sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Specific sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aeronautics">Aeronautics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aeronautics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamics" title="Aerodynamics">aerodynamics</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lift_(force)" title="Lift (force)">lift</a> distribution over a finite wing may be approximated by assuming that each segment of the wing has a semi-infinite trailing vortex behind it. It is then possible to solve for the strength of the vortices using the criterion that there be no flow induced through the surface of the wing. This procedure is called the vortex panel method of <a href="/wiki/Computational_fluid_dynamics" title="Computational fluid dynamics">computational fluid dynamics</a>. The strengths of the vortices are then summed to find the total approximate <a href="/wiki/Circulation_(fluid_dynamics)" title="Circulation (fluid dynamics)">circulation</a> about the wing. According to the <a href="/wiki/Kutta%E2%80%93Joukowski_theorem" title="Kutta\xe2\x80\x93Joukowski theorem">Kutta\xe2\x80\x93Joukowski theorem</a>, lift is the product of circulation, airspeed, and air density.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_sciences">Atmospheric sciences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vorticity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Atmospheric sciences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <b>relative vorticity</b> is the vorticity of the air velocity field relative to the Earth. This is often modeled as a two-dimensional flow parallel to the ground, so that the relative vorticity vector is generally perpendicular to the ground, and can then be viewed as a scalar quantity, positive when the vector points upward, negative when it points downwards. Therefore, vorticity is positive when the wind turns counter-clockwise (looking down onto the Earth\'s surface). In the Northern Hemisphere, positive vorticity is called <a href="/wiki/Cyclonic_rotation" title="Cyclonic rotation">cyclonic rotation</a>, and negative vorticity is <a href="/wiki/Anticyclone" title="Anticyclone">anticyclonic rotation</a>; the nomenclature is reversed in the Southern Hemisphere.</p>\n<p>The <b>absolute vorticity</b> is computed from the air velocity relative to an inertial frame, and therefore includes a term due to the Earth\'s rotation, the <a href="/wiki/Coriolis_parameter" class="mw-redirect" title="Coriolis parameter">Coriolis parameter</a>.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a> is absolute vorticity divided by the vertical spacing between levels of constant <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Potential_temperature" title="Potential temperature">potential temperature</a>). The absolute vorticity of an air mass will change if the air mass is stretched (or compressed) in the <i>z</i> direction, but the potential vorticity is <a href="/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation law (physics)">conserved</a> in an <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Adiabatic">adiabatic</a> flow, which predominates in the atmosphere. The potential vorticity is therefore useful as an approximate <a href="/wiki/Flow_tracer" title="Flow tracer">tracer</a> of air masses over the timescale of a few days, particularly when viewed on levels of constant entropy.</p>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/Barotropic_vorticity_equation" title="Barotropic vorticity equation">barotropic vorticity equation</a> is the simplest way for forecasting the movement of <a href="/wiki/Rossby_wave" title="Rossby wave">Rossby waves</a> (that is, the <a href="/wiki/Trough_(meteorology)" title="Trough (meteorology)">troughs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">ridges</a> of 500&#160;<a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">hPa</a> <a href="/wiki/Geopotential_height" title="Geopotential height">geopotential height</a>) over a limited amount of time (a few days). In the 1950s, the first successful programs for <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_forecasting" class="mw-redirect" title="Numerical weather forecasting">numerical weather forecasting</a> utilized that equation.</p>\n<p>In modern <i>numerical weather forecasting models</i> and <a href="/wiki/General_circulation_model" title="General circulation model">general circulation models</a> (GCM\'s), vorticity may be one of the predicted variables, in which case the corresponding time-dependent equation is a <a href="/wiki/Prognostic_equation" title="Prognostic equation">prognostic equation</a>.</p>\n<p>Helicity of the air motion is important in forecasting <a href="/wiki/Supercell" title="Supercell">supercells</a> and the potential for <a href="/wiki/Tornado" title="Tornado">tornadic</a> activity.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
