href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclones</a> in the North <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> and Northeastern <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> to gather weather data. Currently, the US organizations that fly these missions are the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Air Force Reserve">United States Air Force Reserve</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a>\'s <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters" title="NOAA Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a>. Such missions have also been flown by Navy units and other Air Force and NOAA units.</p>\n<p>Manned flights into hurricanes began in 1943 when, on a bet, pilot-trainer Colonel Joseph Duckworth flew a single-engine plane into a category 1 storm near <a href="/wiki/Galveston" class="mw-redirect" title="Galveston">Galveston</a>, Texas. Since then, six military weather reconnaissance planes have gone down, at a cost of fifty-three lives.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-fghh_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fghh-2">[2]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-huntinghugo_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huntinghugo-3">[3]</a></sup> In one such incident, six of the seven crew members of the Navy PB4Y-2 (BuNo 59415) were killed on October 1, 1945 when their plane went down in a Category 1 typhoon over the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Before <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">satellites</a> were used to find storms, military aircraft flew routine weather reconnaissance tracks to detect formation of tropical cyclones. Today, satellites have improved weather forecasters\' ability to detect signs of such cyclones before they form, yet they cannot determine the interior <a href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" class="mw-redirect" title="Barometric pressure">barometric pressure</a> of a hurricane nor provide accurate wind speed information--data needed to accurately predict hurricane development and movement.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li class="toclevel-1 tocsection-1"><a href="#Units"><span class="tocnumber">1</span> <span href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Units">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="USAFR_53d_WRS">USAFR 53d WRS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: USAFR 53d WRS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a></div>\n<p>The Air Force Reserve <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a>, the world\'s only operational military weather reconnaissance unit, is based at <a href="/wiki/Keesler_Air_Force_Base" title="Keesler Air Force Base">Keesler Air Force Base</a> in <a href="/wiki/Biloxi,_Mississippi" title="Biloxi, Mississippi">Biloxi, Mississippi</a>; most weather recon flights originate there. The term "hurricane hunters" was first applied to its missions in 1946.</p>\n<p>The USAFR hurricane hunters fly weather missions in an area midway through the Atlantic Ocean to the <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_Islands" title="Hawaiian Islands">Hawaiian Islands</a>, and have on occasion flown into typhoons in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and gathered data in winter storms.</p>\n<p>The 53d WRS hurricane hunters operate ten <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_WC-130J" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed WC-130J">Lockheed WC-130J</a> aircraft, which fly directly into hurricanes, typically penetrating the hurricane\'s eye several times per mission at altitudes between 500 feet (150&#160;m) and 10,000 feet (3,000&#160;m).</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters">NOAA Hurricane Hunters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: NOAA Hurricane Hunters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters" title="NOAA Hurricane Hunters">NOAA Hurricane Hunters</a></div>\n<p>The civilian and <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA Corps">NOAA Corps</a> crew members of the <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Hurricane_Hunters" title="NOAA Hurricane Hunters">NOAA Hurricane Hunters</a>, until recently based at the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_Operations_Center" title="Aircraft Operations Center">Aircraft Operations Center</a> at <a href="/wiki/MacDill_AFB" class="mw-redirect" title="MacDill AFB">MacDill AFB</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Tampa" class="mw-redirect" title="Tampa">Tampa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, mainly perform surveillance, research, and reconnaissance with highly instrumented aircraft including airborne <a href="/wiki/Weather_radar" title="Weather radar">Doppler weather radar</a> measurements in both Atlantic and Pacific storms. In June 2017<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://wusfnews.wusf.usf.edu/post/noaa-hurricane-hunters-have-new-home-lakeland#stream/0">[1]</a><a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.tampabay.com/news/noaa-hurricane-center-once-housed-at-macdill-opens-friday-in-lakeland/2325954">[2]</a>, the Hunters moved in to a new facility at Lakeland Linder Airport in Lakeland, Florida, after being at MacDill since 1993. They fly two <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_WP-3D_Orion" title="Lockheed WP-3D Orion">Lockheed WP-3D Orion</a> aircraft, heavily instrumented flying laboratories modified to take atmospheric and radar measurements within tropical cyclones and winter storms, and a <a href="/wiki/Gulfstream_IV" title="Gulfstream IV">G-IV Gulfstream</a> high-altitude jet above 41,000 feet (12&#160;km) to document upper- and lower-level winds that affect cyclone movement. The computer models that forecast hurricane tracks and intensity mainly use G-IV <a href="/wiki/Dropsonde" title="Dropsonde">dropsonde</a> data collected day and night in storms affecting the United States.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a in <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Ginny" title="Hurricane Ginny">Hurricane Ginny</a> during the <a href="/wiki/1963_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1963 Atlantic hurricane season">1963 Atlantic hurricane season</a>. Other types include the <a href="/wiki/A-20_Havoc" class="mw-redirect" title="A-20 Havoc">A-20 Havoc</a>, 1944; <a href="/wiki/B-24" class="mw-redirect" title="B-24">B-24</a>, 1944\xe2\x80\x931945; <a href="/wiki/B-17_Flying_Fortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-17 Flying Fortress">B-17</a>, 1945\xe2\x80\x931947; <a href="/wiki/B-25" class="mw-redirect" title="B-25">B-25</a>, 1946\xe2\x80\x931947; <a href="/wiki/B-29" class="mw-redirect" title="B-29">B-29</a>, 1946\xe2\x80\x931947. W<a href="/wiki/B-29" class="mw-redirect" title="B-29">B-29</a>, 1951\xe2\x80\x931956; W<a href="/wiki/B-50_Superfortress" class="mw-redirect" title="B-50 Superfortress">B-50</a>, 1956\xe2\x80\x931963; W<a href="/wiki/B-47" class="mw-redirect" title="B-47">B-47</a>, 1963\xe2\x80\x931969; W<a href="/wiki/C-121" class="mw-redirect" title="C-121">C-121</a>N 1954\xe2\x80\x931973; <a href="/wiki/WC-130" class="mw-redirect" title="WC-130">WC-130</a>A,B,E,H, 1965\xe2\x80\x932012.</p>\n<p>The idea of aircraft reconnaissance of hurricane storm trackers was put forth by Captain W. L. Farnsworth of the Galveston Commercial Association in the early 1930s. Supported by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a>, the "storm patrol bill" passed both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a> on June 15, 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1943_Surprise_Hurricane">1943 Surprise Hurricane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1943 Surprise Hurricane">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/1943_Surprise_Hurricane" title="1943 Surprise Hurricane">1943 Surprise Hurricane</a>, which struck <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a>, during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, marked the first intentional meteorological flight into a hurricane. It started with a bet.</p>\n<p>That summer, British pilots were being trained in instrument flying at <a href="/wiki/Campus_of_Texas_A%26M_University#Riverside_Campus" title="Campus of Texas A&amp;M University">Bryan Field</a>. When they saw that the Americans were evacuating their <a href="/wiki/T-6_Texan" class="mw-redirect" title="T-6 Texan">AT-6 Texan</a> trainers in the face of the storm, they began questioning the construction of the aircraft. Lead instructor <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">Colonel</a> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Duckworth" title="Joseph Duckworth">Joe Duckworth</a> took one of the trainers out and flew it straight into the eye of the storm. After he returned safely with <a href="/wiki/Flight_officer" title="Flight officer">navigator</a> Lt. Ralph O\'Hair, the base\'s weather officer, Lt. William Jones-Burdick, took over the navigator\'s seat and Duckworth flew into the storm a second time.</p>\n<p>This flight showed that hurricane reconnaissance flights were possible, and further flights continued occasionally. In 1946, the moniker "Hurricane Hunters" was first used, and the Air Force and now Air Force Reserve have used it ever since.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="VW-4">VW-4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: VW-4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg/220px-Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg/330px-Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg/440px-Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="686" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lockheed_P-3_WP-3A_150496_VW-4_JAX_06.74_edited-2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nLockheed WP-3A Orion weather reconnaissance aircraft of VW-4 Squadron at its <a href="/wiki/NAS_Jacksonville" class="mw-redirect" title="NAS Jacksonville">NAS Jacksonville</a> Florida base in 1974</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>\'s VW-4 / WEARECORON FOUR Weather Reconnaissance Squadron Four, "Hurricane Hunters" was the seventh U.S. Navy squadron dedicated to weather reconnaissance. They flew several types of aircraft, but the <a href="/wiki/EC-121_Warning_Star" class="mw-redirect" title="EC-121 Warning Star">WC-121N "Willy Victor"</a> was the aircraft most often associated with flying into the "eye of the storm." The squadron operated WC-121s between late 1954 and 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> VW-4 lost one aircraft and crew in a penetration of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Janet" title="Hurricane Janet">Hurricane Janet</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> and another to severe damage in a storm, but the severely damaged Willy Victor (MH-1) brought her crew home, though it never flew again. During 1973\xe2\x80\x931975, VW-4 operated the turbine-propeller <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-3" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockheed P-3">Lockheed WP-3A Orion</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swan_38">Swan 38</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Swan 38">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Swan_38" class="mw-redirect" title="Swan 38">Swan 38</a></div>\n<p>In 1974, a newly converted WC-130 (serial number 65-0965) was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/54th_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="54th Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">54th Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a>, the "Typhoon Chasers", at <a href="/wiki/Andersen_Air_Force_Base" title="Andersen Air Force Base">Andersen Air Force Base</a> on <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a>. The aircraft was sent to investigate <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Bess_(1974)" title="Typhoon Bess (1974)">Typhoon Bess</a>. The crew departed <a href="/wiki/Clark_Air_Base" title="Clark Air Base">Clark Air Base</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> with the callsign "Swan 38".</p>\n<p>Radio contact with the aircraft was lost on 12 October 1974, apparently as the aircraft was heading into the typhoon\'s eye to make a second position fix. There were no radio transmissions indicating an emergency on board, and search teams could not locate the aircraft or its crew. All six crew members were listed as killed in action.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Swan 38 was the only <a href="/wiki/WC-130" class="mw-redirect" title="WC-130">WC-130</a> lost in a storm.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hurricane Katrina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The landfall of <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Katrina" title="Hurricane Katrina">Hurricane Katrina</a> on 29 August 2005 devastated <a href="/wiki/Keesler_Air_Force_Base" title="Keesler Air Force Base">Keesler Air Force Base</a>, home of the 53d WRS. The equipment and personnel of the squadron were flying out of <a href="/wiki/Dobbins_Air_Reserve_Base" title="Dobbins Air Reserve Base">Dobbins Air Reserve Base</a> near <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>. Despite heavy losses,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2015)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> the squadron never missed a mission from the <a href="/wiki/National_Hurricane_Center" title="National Hurricane Center">National Hurricane Center</a>. The 53d has since returned to Keesler.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hurricane_hunters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>A <a href="/wiki/Reality_television" title="Reality television">reality television</a> series featuring the USAFR 53d, entitled <i>Hurricane Hunters</i>, debuted on <a href="/wiki/The_Weather_Channel" title="The Weather Channel">The Weather Channel</a> in July 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See
