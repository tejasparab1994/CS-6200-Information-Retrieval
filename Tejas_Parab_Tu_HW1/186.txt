The agency, which is co-located with the <a href="/wiki/National_Weather_Service_Miami,_Florida" title="National Weather Service Miami, Florida">Miami branch of the National Weather Service</a>, is situated on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a> in <a href="/wiki/University_Park,_Florida" title="University Park, Florida">University Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Its Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch (TAFB) routinely issues marine forecasts, in the form of graphics and high seas forecasts, for this area year round, with the <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Prediction_Center" title="Ocean Prediction Center">Ocean Prediction Center</a> having backup responsibility for this unit. The Technology and Science Branch (TSB) provides technical support for the center, which includes new infusions of technology from abroad. The Chief, Aerial Reconnaissance Coordination, All Hurricanes (CARCAH) unit tasks planes, for research and operational purposes, to tropical cyclones during the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic hurricane season</a> and significant weather events, including snow storms, during winter and spring. Research to improve operational forecasts is done through the Hurricane Forecast Improvement Project (HFIP) and Joint Hurricane Test Bed (JHT) initiatives.</p>\n<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="Atlantic hurricane season">Atlantic</a> and northeast Pacific hurricane seasons, the Hurricane Specialists Unit (HSU) issues routine tropical weather outlooks for the northeast Pacific and northern Atlantic oceans. When <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storm</a> or hurricane conditions are expected within 48&#160;hours, the center issues watches and warnings via the news media and <a href="/wiki/NOAA_Weather_Radio" title="NOAA Weather Radio">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) Weather Radio</a>.</p>\n<p>Although the NHC is an agency of the United States, the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> has designated it as <a href="/wiki/Regional_Specialized_Meteorological_Center" title="Regional Specialized Meteorological Center">Regional Specialized Meteorological Center</a> for the North Atlantic and eastern Pacific, making it the clearinghouse for tropical cyclone forecasts and observations occurring in these areas. If the center loses power or becomes incapacitated, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Central Pacific Hurricane Center">Central Pacific Hurricane Center</a> backs tropical cyclone advisories and tropical weather outlooks for the northeast Pacific Ocean while the <a href="/wiki/Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Weather Prediction Center">Weather Prediction Center</a> backs up tropical cyclone advisories and tropical weather outlooks for the north Atlantic Ocean.</p>\n<p></p>\n<div id="toc" class="toc">\n<div class="toctitle">\n<h2>Contents</h2>\n</div>\n<ul>\n<li title="Tropical cyclone naming">Tropical cyclone naming</a></div>\n<p>The first hurricane warning service was set up in the 1870s from <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> with the work of Father Benito Vi\xc3\xb1es. After his death, hurricane warning services were assumed by the <a href="/wiki/Signal_Corps_(United_States_Army)" title="Signal Corps (United States Army)">United States Signal Corps</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> over the next decade, first based in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> in 1898 and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> in 1899 before shifting to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> in 1902.<sup id="cite_ref-Simpson_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Simpson-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wb59_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wb59-4">[4]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The central office in Washington, which evolved into the National Meteorological Center and Weather Prediction Center (formally known as the Hydrometeorological Prediction Center), assumed hurricane warning/advisory responsibility at that time. This responsibility passed to regional hurricane offices in 1935, and the concept of the Atlantic hurricane season was established to keep a vigilant lookout for tropical cyclones during certain times of the year. Hurricane advisories issued every six hours by the regional hurricane offices began at this time.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<p>The Jacksonville hurricane warning office moved to <a href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" class="mw-redirect" title="Miami, Florida">Miami, Florida</a> in 1943. Tropical cyclone naming began for Atlantic tropical cyclones using the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Army/Navy_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet">Joint Army/Navy Phonetic Alphabet</a> by 1947.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1950, the Miami Hurricane Warning Office began to prepare the annual hurricane season summary articles.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/1953_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1953 Atlantic hurricane season">1953 Atlantic season</a>, the United States Weather Bureau began naming storms which reach tropical storm intensity with human names.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>\n<div class="thumb Project">National Hurricane Research Project</a>, begun in the 1950s, used aircraft to study tropical cyclones and carry out experiments on mature hurricanes through its <a href="/wiki/Project_Stormfury" title="Project Stormfury">Project Stormfury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HRDhistory_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HRDhistory-9">[9]</a></sup> On July 1, 1956, a National Hurricane Information Center was established in Miami, Florida which became a warehouse for all hurricane-related information from one United States Weather Bureau office.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Miami Hurricane Warning Office (HWO) moved from Lindsey Hopkins Hotel to the Aviation Building 4 miles (6.4&#160;km) to the northwest on July 1, 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Forecasts within the hurricane advisories were issued one day into the future in 1954 before being extended to two days into the future in 1961, three days into the future in 1964, and five days into the future in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-verify_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-verify-12">[12]</a></sup> The Miami HWO moved to the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Miami" title="University of Miami">University of Miami</a> in 1964,<sup id="cite_ref-stormfury_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stormfury-13">[13]</a></sup> and was referred to as the NHC in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> The Miami HWO tropical cyclone reports were done regularly and took on their modern format in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KnabbRetire-26">[25]</a></sup></td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<p>Beginning in 1973, the National Meteorological Center duties (renamed the Hydrometeorological Prediction Center; renamed for a second time in 2013)<sup id="cite_ref-HPChistory_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HPChistory-27">[26]</a></sup> gained advisory responsibility for tracking and publicizing inland tropical depressions. The <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a> assumed control of the Atlantic hurricane naming list in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[27]</a></sup> In 1978, the NHC\'s offices moved off the campus of the University of Miami across <a href="/wiki/U.S._Highway_1" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Highway 1">U.S. Highway 1</a> to the IRE Financial Building.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[28]</a></sup> Male names were added into the hurricane list beginning in the <a href="/wiki/1979_Atlantic_hurricane_season" title="1979 Atlantic hurricane season">1979 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> The hurricane warning offices remained active past 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-WJ_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WJ-31">[30]</a></sup></p>\n<p>In 1984, the NHC was separated from the Miami Weather Service Forecast Office, which meant the meteorologist in charge at Miami was no longer in a position above the hurricane center director.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> By 1988, the NHC gained responsibility for eastern Pacific tropical cyclones as the former <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific_Hurricane_Center" title="Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center">Eastern Pacific Hurricane Center</a> in San Francisco was decommissioned.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup> In 1992, <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Andrew" title="Hurricane Andrew">Hurricane Andrew</a> blew the <a href="/wiki/WSR-57" title="WSR-57">WSR-57</a> weather radar and the anemometer off the roof of NHC\'s/the Miami State Weather Forecast offices.<sup id="cite_ref-tcr_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tcr-34">[33]</a></sup> The radar was replaced with a WSR-88D <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">NEXRAD</a> system in April 1993 installed near <a href="/wiki/Zoo_Miami" title="Zoo Miami">Metro Zoo</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup> near where Hurricane Andrew made landfall.</p>\n<p>In 1995, the NHC moved into a new hurricane-resistant facility on the campus of <a href="/wiki/Florida_International_University" title="Florida International University">Florida International University</a>, capable of withstanding 130&#160;mph (210&#160;km/h) winds.<sup id="cite_ref-NHC_bunker_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHC_bunker-36">[35]</a></sup> Its name was changed to the Tropical Prediction Center in 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-TPC_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPC-37">[36]</a></sup> After the name change to TPC, the Hurricane Specialists were grouped as a separate NHC unit under the Tropical Prediction Center,<sup id="cite_ref-TPC_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPC-37">[36]</a></sup> separating themselves from the Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch. On October 1, 2010, the Tropical Prediction Center was renamed the NHC,<sup id="cite_ref-nhc.noaa_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhc.noaa-38">[37]</a></sup> and the group formerly known as the NHC became known as the Hurricane Specialists Unit (HSU).<sup id="cite_ref-Staff_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Staff-39">[38]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Tropical cyclone forecasting is now done using statistical methods based on tropical cyclone climatology, as well as methods of <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> where computers use mathematical equations of motion to determine their movement.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-advance_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advance-41">[40]</a></sup> The World Meteorological Organization continues to create and maintain the annual hurricane naming lists. Naming lists use a six-year rotation, with the deadliest or most notable storm names retired from the rotation.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">[41]</a></sup> The current acting director of the National Hurricane Center is Edward Rappaport following the retirement of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Knabb" title="Richard Knabb">Richard Knabb</a> on May&#160;15, 2017. A permanent replacement director has not been announced.<sup id="cite_ref-KnabbRetire_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KnabbRetire-26">[25]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>For the <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> of 2008, the budget for the NHC was $6.8&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-advance_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advance-41">[40]</a></sup> A total of 66&#160;people work for the NHC, with 12 of the staff composing their management.<sup id="cite_ref-Staff_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Staff-39">[38]</a></sup> The NHC has been one of the nine national centers which compose the National Centers for Environmental Prediction (NCEP).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hurricane_Specialists_Unit">Hurricane Specialists Unit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit <i>(pictured)</i> from which the tropics are monitored</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>Known as the NHC from 1995 through 2010, the hurricane specialists within the hurricane specialists unit (HSU) are the chief meteorologists that predict the actions of tropical storms. The specialists work rotating eight-hour shifts from May through November, monitoring weather patterns in the Atlantic and Eastern Pacific oceans. Whenever a tropical or subtropical cyclone forms, they issue advisories every six hours until the storm is over. Public advisories are issued more often when the storm expected to be of tropical storm or hurricane intensity threatens land.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup> The specialists coordinate with officials in each country likely to be affected. They forecast and recommend watches and warnings.<sup id="cite_ref-about_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-45">[44]</a></sup></p>\n<p>During the hurricane season, the HSU routinely issues their Tropical Weather Outlook product, which identifies areas of concern within the tropics which could develop into tropical cyclones. If systems occur outside the defined hurricane season, the HSU issues special Tropical Weather Outlooks.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> Backup responsibility for their northeast Pacific area resides at the Central Pacific Hurricane Center (CPHC),<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup> and vice versa if CPHC were to have communication issues.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">[47]</a></sup> North Atlantic responsibilities are backed up by the Weather Prediction Center (WPC).<sup id="cite_ref-advance_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-advance-41">[40]</a></sup> Routine coordination occurs at 1700 UTC each day between the Weather Prediction Center and National Hurricane Center to identify systems for the pressure maps three to seven days into the future within the tropics, and points for existing tropical cyclones six to seven days into the future.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> Outside of the hurricane season, the specialists concentrate on public education efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-50" Specialist</center>\n</td>\n</tr>\n</table>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Analysis_and_Forecast_Branch">Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div class="center">\n<div class="thumb tnone">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:807px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NHC_TAFB.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/NHC_TAFB.jpg/805px-NHC_TAFB.jpg" width="805" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/NHC_TAFB.jpg/1208px-NHC_TAFB.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/NHC_TAFB.jpg/1610px-NHC_TAFB.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5948" data-file-height="720" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NHC_TAFB.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nA panoramic view of TAFB\'s operations at the NHC prior to being remodeled</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<div style="clear:both;"></div>\n<div class="thumb tright">\n<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NWSmarinezones.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/NWSmarinezones.gif/220px-NWSmarinezones.gif" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/NWSmarinezones.gif/330px-NWSmarinezones.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/NWSmarinezones.gif/440px-NWSmarinezones.gif 2x" data-file-width="1095" data-file-height="619" /></a>\n<div class="thumbcaption">\n<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NWSmarinezones.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>\nThe National Weather Service areas of <a href="/wiki/Marine_weather_forecasting" title="Marine weather forecasting">marine weather forecasting</a> responsibility</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The Tropical Analysis and Forecast Branch (TAFB, formerly the Tropical Satellite Analysis and Forecast unit and the Tropical Analysis Center) is a part of the National Hurricane Center and was created in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> The TAFB is responsible for <a href="/wiki/High_seas" class="mw-redirect" title="High seas">high seas</a> analyses and forecasts for tropical portions of the Atlantic and Pacific between the <a href="/wiki/IERS_Reference_Meridian" title="IERS Reference Meridian">Prime Meridian</a> and the 140th meridian west poleward to the 30th parallel north in the northeast Pacific Ocean and the 31st parallel north in the northern Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[51]</a></sup> Unlike the Hurricane Specialists Unit (HSU), TAFB is staffed full-time around the year. Other responsibilities of the TAFB include satellite-derived <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical cyclone</a> position and <a href="/wiki/Dvorak_technique" title="Dvorak technique">intensity</a> estimates, <a href="/wiki/NEXRAD" title="NEXRAD">WSR-88D radar</a> fixes for tropical cyclones, tropical cyclone forecast support, media support, and general operational support.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup> The Ocean Prediction Center backs up TAFB in the event of a communications outage, and vice versa.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology_and_Science_Branch">Technology and Science Branch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Technology and Science Branch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Technology &amp; Science Branch (TSB) develops and transitions new tools and techniques into operations for tropical weather prediction in conjunction with other government and academic entities. TSB created and continues development of the <a href="/wiki/Automated_Tropical_Cyclone_Forecasting_System" title="Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting System">Automated Tropical Cyclone Forecasting (ATCF) system</a>, used to incorporate various data and model outputs, create and update <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a>, and to generate tropical cyclone forecasts. The TSB provides support for NHC computer and communications systems including its website. TSB maintains a number of statistical and dynamical models used in predicting both tropical cyclone behavior and associated weather conditions. The <a href="/wiki/Storm_surge" title="Storm surge">Storm Surge</a> Unit, which develops and maintains software to forecast the storm surge of tropical cyclones, is part of this branch.<sup id="cite_ref-about_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-45">[44]</a></sup> The Techniques Development and Applications Unit (TDAU) is part of TSB.<sup id="cite_ref-abouttsb_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-abouttsb-55">[54]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="CARCAH">CARCAH</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: CARCAH">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Chief, Aerial Reconnaissance Coordination, All Hurricanes (CARCAH) is a subunit of the <a href="/wiki/53d_Weather_Reconnaissance_Squadron" title="53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron">53d Weather Reconnaissance Squadron</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Hunters" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Hunters">Hurricane Hunters</a>). CARCAH\'s mission is to provide a point-of-contact and to coordinate all tropical cyclone operational reconnaissance requirements at NHC and the Central Pacific Hurricane Center for the North Atlantic, Caribbean, Gulf of Mexico, and the North Pacific basin east of the International Date Line in accordance with the National Hurricane Operations Plan (NHOP). During the winter, CARCAH coordinates the Atlantic and Pacific winter storm requirements in support of the National Winter Storms Operations Plan (NWSOP). Missions are flown in advance of the high-impact weather events forecast to affect the U.S., such as heavy snowfall,<sup id="cite_ref-about_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-45">[44]</a></sup> and at times when there is significant uncertainty within/between <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> output.<sup id="cite_ref-nhopappf_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhopappf-56">[55]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="HLT">HLT</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: HLT">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>The Hurricane Liaison Team (HLT) supports hurricane response through information exchange between the NHC, the National Weather Service (NOAA/NWS), and the emergency management community. The HLT is composed of federal, state, and local emergency managers, as well as NWS meteorologists and hydrologists, who maintain open lines of communication about the progress and threat level of the storm with appropriate Federal, state, and local officials. The team establishes and facilitates video and/or teleconferences with the NHC, FEMA and other Federal agencies, state Emergency Operations Centers (EOCs), Weather Prediction Center (HPC), Storm Prediction Center (SPC), and River Forecast Centers (RFCs).<sup id="cite_ref-about_45-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-about-45">[44]</a></sup> During significant landfalling hurricanes, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> as well as affected city <a href="/wiki/Mayoralty_in_the_United_States" title="Mayoralty in the United States">mayors</a> and state <a href="/wiki/Governor_(United_States)" title="Governor (United States)">governors</a> join the daily briefing call, which occurs at noon <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Daylight_Time" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Daylight Time">Eastern Daylight Time</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[56]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Hurricane_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>As part of their annual tropical cyclone activity, the agency issues a report on every tropical cyclone in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic">Atlantic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Pacific" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Pacific">Eastern Pacific</a> Ocean basins, which are available since 1958 and 1988, respectively. The report summarizes the synoptic history, meteorological statistics, casualties and damages, and the post-analysis best track of a storm.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup> The reports were formally known as Preliminary Reports up until 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">[58]</a></sup> The agency maintains archives and climatological statistics on Atlantic and Pacific hurricane history, including annual reports on every tropical cyclone, a complete set of tropical cyclone advisories, digitized copies of related materials on older storms, season summaries published as the <a href="/wiki/Monthly_Weather_Review" title="Monthly Weather Review">Monthly Weather Review</a>, and <a href="/wiki/HURDAT" title="HURDAT">HURDAT</a>, which is the official tropical cyclone database.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Programs are dedicated to improving the accuracy of tropical cyclone forecasts from the center. The Joint Hurricane Testbed (JHT) is a joint operation between the <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> (NOAA) and United States Weather Research Program to speed up the transfer of tropical cyclone-related research into forecast operations. Since 2001, with its annual budget of between $1.0 and $1.5&#160;million, the JHT has funded 62&#160;initiatives, with most of them being implemented operationally. The projects have had varied success, ranging from minor to significant advances in the way the NHC operates.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> The Hurricane Forecast Improvement Program\'s (HFIP) five-year goal is to lead to a 20&#160;percent improvement within the <a href="/wiki/Numerical_weather_prediction" title="Numerical weather prediction">numerical weather prediction</a> models provided by the National Centers for Environmental Prediction to NHC by 2015 and a 50&#160;percent improvement within <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_track_forecasting" title="Tropical cyclone track forecasting">tropical cyclone track forecasting</a> and intensity guidance by 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">[61]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="See_also">See also</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
