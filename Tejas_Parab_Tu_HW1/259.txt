<b>estuary</b> is a partially enclosed coastal body of <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish water</a> with one or more rivers or streams flowing into it, and with a free connection to the open sea.<sup id="cite_ref-james_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-james-1">[1]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Estuaries form a transition zone between river environments and maritime environments. They are subject both to marine influences\xe2\x80\x94such as <a href="/wiki/Tides" class="mw-redirect" title="Tides">tides</a>, waves, and the influx of saline water\xe2\x80\x94and to riverine influences\xe2\x80\x94such as flows of fresh water and sediment. The inflows of both sea water and fresh water provide high levels of nutrients both in the water column and in sediment, making estuaries among the most productive natural habitats in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-McLusky_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLusky-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Most existing estuaries formed during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> epoch with the flooding of river-eroded or glacially scoured valleys when the sea level began to rise about 10,000\xe2\x80\x9312,000 years ago.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolanski_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolanski-3">[3]</a></sup> Estuaries are typically classified according to their <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphological</a> features or to water-circulation patterns. They can have many different names, such as bays, harbors, lagoons, inlets, or sounds, although some of these water bodies do not strictly meet the above definition of an estuary and may be fully saline.</p>\n<p>The banks of many estuaries are amongst the most heavily populated areas of the world, with about 60% of the world\'s population living along estuaries and the coast.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> As a result, many estuaries suffer <a href="/wiki/Habitat_destruction" title="Habitat destruction">degradation</a> by many factors, including sedimentation from soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> from <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, overgrazing, and other <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">poor farming practices</a>; <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>; drainage and filling of wetlands; <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> due to excessive nutrients from sewage and animal wastes; pollutants including <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_biphenyl" title="Polychlorinated biphenyl">polychlorinated biphenyls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">radionuclides</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> from sewage inputs; and diking or damming for flood control or water diversion.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolanski_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolanski-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a title="Amazon river">Amazon</a> estuary</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n</div>\n<p>The word "estuary" is derived from the Latin word <i>aestuarium</i> meaning tidal inlet of the sea, which in itself is derived from the term <i>aestus</i>, meaning tide. There have been many definitions proposed to describe an estuary. The most widely accepted definition is: "a semi-enclosed coastal body of water, which has a free connection with the open sea, and within which sea water is measurably diluted with freshwater derived from land drainage".<sup id="cite_ref-james_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-james-1">[1]</a></sup> However, this definition excludes a number of coastal water bodies such as coastal lagoons and <a href="/wiki/Brackish_water" title="Brackish water">brackish</a> seas. A more comprehensive definition of an estuary is "a semi-enclosed body of water connected to the sea as far as the <a href="/wiki/Tidal_limit" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal limit">tidal limit</a> or the salt intrusion limit and receiving freshwater runoff; however the freshwater inflow may not be perennial, the connection to the sea may be closed for part of the year and tidal influence may be negligible".<sup id="cite_ref-Wolanski_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolanski-3">[3]</a></sup> This broad definition also includes <a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">fjords</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lagoon" title="Lagoon">lagoons</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_mouth" title="River mouth">river mouths</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tidal_creek" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal creek">tidal creeks</a>. An estuary is a dynamic <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> having a connection to the open sea through which the <a href="/wiki/Sea_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea water">sea water</a> enters with the rhythm of the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>. The sea water entering the estuary is diluted by the <a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a> flowing from rivers and streams. The pattern of dilution varies between different estuaries and depends on the volume of fresh water, the tidal range, and the extent of evaporation of the water in the estuary.<sup id="cite_ref-McLusky_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLusky-2">[2]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_based_on_geomorphology">Classification based on geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification based on geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drowned_river_valleys">Drowned river valleys</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Drowned river valleys">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ria" title="Ria">Ria</a></div>\n<p>Drowned river valleys are also known as coastal plain estuaries. In places where the sea level is rising relative to the land, sea water progressively penetrates into river valleys and the topography of the estuary remains similar to that of a river valley. This is the most common type of estuary in temperate climates. Well-studied estuaries include the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Estuary" title="Severn Estuary">Severn Estuary</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ems_Dollart_Region" title="Ems Dollart Region">Ems Dollard</a> along the Dutch-German border.</p>\n<p>The width-to-depth ratio of these estuaries is typically large, appearing wedge-shaped (in cross-section) in the inner part and broadening and deepening seaward. Water depths rarely exceed 30&#160;m (100&#160;ft). <a href="/wiki/Ria#Examples" title="Ria">Examples of this type of estuary</a> in the U.S. are the <a href="/wiki/Hudson_River" title="Hudson River">Hudson River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Mid-Atlantic_states" title="Mid-Atlantic states">Mid-Atlantic</a> coast, and <a href="/wiki/Galveston_Bay" title="Galveston Bay">Galveston Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tampa_Bay" title="Tampa Bay">Tampa Bay</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Coast" class="mw-redirect" title="Gulf Coast">Gulf Coast</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lagoon-type_or_bar-built">Lagoon-type or bar-built</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lagoon-type or bar-built">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Bar-built estuaries are found in place where the deposition of sediment has kept pace with rising sea level so that the estuaries are shallow and separated from the sea by sand spits or barrier islands. They are relatively common in tropical and subtropical locations.</p>\n<p>These estuaries are semi-isolated from ocean waters by barrier beaches (<a href="/wiki/Barrier_island" title="Barrier island">barrier islands</a> and barrier <a href="/wiki/Spit_(landform)" title="Spit (landform)">spits</a>). Formation of barrier beaches partially encloses the estuary, with only narrow inlets allowing contact with the ocean waters. Bar-built estuaries typically develop on gently sloping plains located along tectonically stable edges of continents and marginal sea coasts. They are extensive along the Atlantic and Gulf coasts of the U.S. in areas with active coastal deposition of sediments and where tidal ranges are less than 4&#160;m (13&#160;ft). The barrier beaches that enclose bar-built estuaries have been developed in several ways:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>building up of offshore bars by wave action, in which sand from the sea floor is deposited in elongated bars parallel to the shoreline,</li>\n<li>reworking of sediment discharge from rivers by wave, current, and wind action into beaches, overwash flats, and dunes,</li>\n<li>engulfment of mainland beach ridges (ridges developed from the erosion of coastal plain sediments around 5000 years ago) due to sea level rise and resulting in the breaching of the ridges and flooding of the coastal lowlands, forming shallow lagoons, and</li>\n<li>elongation of barrier spits from the erosion of headlands due to the action of <a href="/wiki/Longshore_current" class="mw-redirect" title="Longshore current">longshore currents</a>, with the spits growing in the direction of the littoral drift.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Barrier beaches form in shallow water and are generally parallel to the shoreline, resulting in long, narrow estuaries. The average water depth is usually less than 5&#160;m (16&#160;ft), and rarely exceeds 10&#160;m (33&#160;ft). Examples of bar-built estuaries are <a href="/wiki/Barnegat_Bay" title="Barnegat Bay">Barnegat Bay</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>; <a href="/wiki/Laguna_Madre" class="mw-redirect" title="Laguna Madre">Laguna Madre</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Pamlico_Sound" title="Pamlico Sound">Pamlico Sound</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a>.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fjord-type">Fjord-type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fjord-type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Fjords were formed where pleistocene glaciers deepened and widened existing river valleys so that they become U-shaped in cross sections. At their mouths there are typically rocks, bars or <a href="/wiki/Aquatic_sill" title="Aquatic sill">sills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">glacial deposits</a>, which have the effects of modifying the estuarine circulation.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Fjord" title="Fjord">Fjord</a>-type estuaries are formed in deeply eroded valleys formed by <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">glaciers</a>. These U-shaped estuaries typically have steep sides, rock bottoms, and underwater sills contoured by glacial movement. The estuary is shallowest at its mouth, where terminal glacial <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">moraines</a> or rock bars form sills that restrict water flow. In the upper reaches of the estuary, the depth can exceed 300&#160;m (1,000&#160;ft). The width-to-depth ratio is generally small. In estuaries with very shallow sills, tidal oscillations only affect the water down to the depth of the sill, and the waters deeper than that may remain stagnant for a very long time, so there is only an occasional exchange of the deep water of the estuary with the ocean. If the sill depth is deep, water circulation is less restricted, and there is a slow but steady exchange of water between the estuary and the ocean. Fjord-type estuaries can be found along the coasts of <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">Puget Sound</a> region of western <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington state</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, eastern Canada, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, New Zealand, and Norway.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tectonically_produced">Tectonically produced</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tectonically produced">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>These estuaries are formed by subsidence or land cut off from the ocean by land movement associated with <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">faulting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">volcanoes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Landslide" title="Landslide">landslides</a>. <a href="/wiki/Inundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Inundation">Inundation</a> from eustatic sea level rise during the <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">Holocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Epoch_(geology)" title="Epoch (geology)">Epoch</a> has also contributed to the formation of these estuaries. There are only a small number of <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">tectonically</a> produced estuaries; one example is the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay" title="San Francisco Bay">San Francisco Bay</a>, which was formed by the crustal movements of the <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_fault" class="mw-redirect" title="San Andreas fault">San Andreas fault</a> system causing the inundation of the lower reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Sacramento_River" title="Sacramento River">Sacramento</a> and <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_River" title="San Joaquin River">San Joaquin rivers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennish_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennish-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_based_on_water_circulation">Classification based on water circulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classification based on water circulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Estuarine_water_circulation" title="Estuarine water circulation">Estuarine water circulation</a></div>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salt_wedge">Salt wedge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Salt wedge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>In this type of estuary, river output greatly exceeds marine input and tidal effects have a minor importance. Fresh water floats on top of the seawater in a layer that gradually thins as it moves seaward. The denser seawater moves landward along the bottom of the estuary, forming a wedge-shaped layer that is thinner as it approaches land. As a velocity difference develops between the two layers, shear forces generate internal waves at the interface, mixing the seawater upward with the freshwater. An example of a salt wedge estuary is the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennish_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennish-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Partially_mixed">Partially mixed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Partially mixed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>As tidal forcing increases, river output becomes less than the marine input. Here, current induced turbulence causes mixing of the whole water column such that salinity varies more longitudinally rather than vertically, leading to a moderately stratified condition. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Narragansett_Bay" title="Narragansett Bay">Narragansett Bay</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennish_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennish-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Well-mixed">Well-mixed</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Well-mixed">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Tidal mixing forces exceed river output, resulting in a well mixed water column and the disappearance of the vertical salinity gradient. The freshwater-seawater boundary is eliminated due to the intense turbulent mixing and eddy effects. The lower reaches of <a href="/wiki/Delaware_Bay" title="Delaware Bay">Delaware Bay</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Raritan_River" title="Raritan River">Raritan River</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> are examples of vertically homogenous estuaries.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennish_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennish-7">[7]</a></sup></p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inverse">Inverse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Inverse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Inverse estuaries occur in dry climates where evaporation greatly exceeds the inflow of fresh water. A salinity maximum zone is formed, and both riverine and oceanic water flow close to the surface towards this zone.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> This water is pushed downward and spreads along the bottom in both the seaward and landward direction.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolanski_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolanski-3">[3]</a></sup> An example of an inverse estuary is <a href="/wiki/Spencer_Gulf" title="Spencer Gulf">Spencer Gulf</a>, South Australia.</p>\n<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Intermittent">Intermittent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Intermittent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>\n<p>Estuary type varies dramatically depending on freshwater input, and is capable of changing from a wholly marine <a href="/wiki/Embayment" class="mw-redirect" title="Embayment">embayment</a> to any of the other estuary types.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Day_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Day-10">[10]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physiochemical_variation">Physiochemical variation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Physiochemical variation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>The most important variable characteristics of estuary water are the concentration of dissolved oxygen, <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> load. There is extreme spatial variability in salinity, with a range of near zero at the <a href="/wiki/Tidal_limit" class="mw-redirect" title="Tidal limit">tidal limit</a> of tributary rivers to 3.4% at the estuary mouth. At any one point the salinity will vary considerably over time and seasons, making it a harsh environment for organisms. Sediment often settles in intertidal <a href="/wiki/Mudflats" class="mw-redirect" title="Mudflats">mudflats</a> which are extremely difficult to colonize. No points of attachment exist for <a href="/wiki/Algae" title="Algae">algae</a>, so vegetation based habitat is not established.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2012)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Sediment can also clog feeding and respiratory structures of species, and special adaptations exist within mudflat species to cope with this problem. Lastly, <a href="/wiki/Dissolved_oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="Dissolved oxygen">dissolved oxygen</a> variation can cause problems for life forms. Nutrient-rich sediment from man-made sources can promote primary production life cycles, perhaps leading to eventual decay removing the dissolved oxygen from the water; thus <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">anoxic</a> zones can develop.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications_for_marine_life">Implications for marine life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Implications for marine life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Estuaries provide habitats for a large number of organisms and support very high productivity. Estuaries provide habitats for many fish nurseries, depending upon their locations in the world, such as <a href="/wiki/Salmon" title="Salmon">salmon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_trout" title="Sea trout">sea trout</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Also, <a href="/wiki/Bird_migration" title="Bird migration">migratory</a> bird populations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_godwit" title="Black-tailed godwit">black-tailed godwit</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> make essential use of estuaries.</p>\n<p>Two of the main challenges of estuarine life are the variability in <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">sedimentation</a>. Many species of <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> have various methods to control or conform to the shifts in salt concentrations and are termed <a href="/wiki/Osmoconformer" title="Osmoconformer">osmoconformers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osmoregulator" class="mw-redirect" title="Osmoregulator">osmoregulators</a>. Many animals also <a href="/wiki/Burrowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Burrowing">burrow</a> to avoid <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> and to live in the more stable sedimental environment. However, large numbers of bacteria are found within the sediment which have a very high oxygen demand. This reduces the levels of oxygen within the sediment often resulting in partially <a href="/wiki/Anoxic_waters" title="Anoxic waters">anoxic</a> conditions, which can be further exacerbated by limited water flux.</p>\n<p><a href="/wiki/Phytoplankton" title="Phytoplankton">Phytoplankton</a> are key primary producers in estuaries. They move with the water bodies and can be flushed in and out with the <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">tides</a>. Their productivity is largely dependent upon the <a href="/wiki/Turbidity" title="Turbidity">turbidity</a> of the water. The main phytoplankton present are <a href="/wiki/Diatoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Diatoms">diatoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dinoflagellates" class="mw-redirect" title="Dinoflagellates">dinoflagellates</a> which are abundant in the sediment.</p>\n<p>It is important to remember that a primary source of food for many organisms on estuaries, including <a href="/wiki/Bacteria" title="Bacteria">bacteria</a>, is <a href="/wiki/Detritus" title="Detritus">detritus</a> from the settlement of the sedimentation.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_impact">Human impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Estuary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Human impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>\n<p>Of the thirty-two largest cities in the world in the early 1990s, twenty-two were located on estuaries.<sup id="cite_ref-RossDA_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RossDA-14">[14]</a></sup></p>\n<p>As ecosystems, estuaries are under threat from human activities such as <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Overfishing" title="Overfishing">overfishing</a>. They are also threatened by sewage, coastal settlement, land clearance and much more. Estuaries are affected by events far upstream, and concentrate materials such as pollutants and sediments.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Land run-off and industrial, agricultural, and domestic waste enter rivers and are discharged into estuaries. Contaminants can be introduced which do not disintegrate rapidly in the marine environment, such as <a href="/wiki/Marine_debris" title="Marine debris">plastics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Furan" title="Furan">furans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polychlorinated_dibenzodioxins" title="Polychlorinated dibenzodioxins">dioxins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">phenols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_(chemistry)" class="mw-redirect" title="Heavy metal (chemistry)">heavy metals</a>.</p>\n<p>Such toxins can accumulate in the tissues of many species of aquatic life in a process called <a href="/wiki/Bioaccumulation" title="Bioaccumulation">bioaccumulation</a>. They also accumulate in <a href="/wiki/Benthic" class="mw-redirect" title="Benthic">benthic</a> environments, such as estuaries and <a href="/wiki/Bay_muds" class="mw-redirect" title="Bay muds">bay muds</a>: a geological record of human activities of the last century. The elemental composition of <a href="/wiki/Biofilm" title="Biofilm">biofilm</a> reflect areas of the estuary impacted by human activities, and over time may shift the basic composition of the ecosystem, and the reversible or irreversible changes in the abiotic and biotic parts of the systems from the bottom up.<sup id="cite_ref-R.C3.ADo_de_la_Plata_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-R.C3.ADo_de_la_Plata-16">[16]</a></sup></p>\n<p>For example, Chinese and Russian industrial pollution, such as phenols and heavy metals, has devastated fish stocks in the <a href="/wiki/Amur_River" title="Amur River">Amur River</a> and damaged its estuary soil.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>\n<p>Estuaries tend to be naturally <a href="/wiki/Eutrophic" class="mw-redirect" title="Eutrophic">eutrophic</a> because <a href="/wiki/Land_runoff" class="mw-redirect" title="Land runoff">land runoff</a> discharges nutrients into estuaries. With human activities, land run-off also now includes the many chemicals used as fertilizers in agriculture as well as waste from livestock and humans. Excess oxygen-depleting chemicals in the water can lead to <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental)" title="Hypoxia (environmental)">hypoxia</a> and the creation of <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">dead zones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> This can result in reductions in water quality, fish, and other animal populations. Overfishing also occurs. <a href="/wiki/Chesapeake_Bay" title="Chesapeake Bay">Chesapeake Bay</a> once had a flourishing <a href="/wiki/Oyster" title="Oyster">oyster</a> population that has been almost wiped out by overfishing. Oysters filter these pollutants, and either eat them or shape them into small packets that are deposited on the bottom where they are harmless. Historically the oysters filtered the estuary\'s entire water volume of excess nutrients every three or four days. Today that process takes almost a year,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> and sediment, nutrients, and algae can cause problems in local waters.</p>\n<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a
